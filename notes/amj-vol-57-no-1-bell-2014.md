# Corporate Governance and Investors' Perceptions of Foreign IPO Value: An Institutional Perspective

**Abstract**
This article investigates stock market responses to different constellations of firm-level corporate governance mechanisms by focusing on foreign initial public offerings (IPOs) in the United States. We build on sociology-grounded research on financial market behavior and use a “nested” legitimacy framework to explore US investor perceptions of foreign IPO value. Using a fuzzy set theoretic methodology, we demonstrate how different combinations of monitoring and incentive-based corporate governance mechanisms lead to the same level of investor valuation of firms. Moreover, institutional factors related to the strength of minority shareholder protection in a foreign IPO’s home country represent a boundary condition that affects the number of governance mechanisms required to achieve high value perceptions among US investors. Our findings contribute to the sociological perspective on comparative corporate governance and the dependencies between organizations and institutions.

**Research Question**
Can different bundles of governance mechanisms in foreign IPO firms lead to the same perceived valuation outcomes, and how do differences between a foreign IPO firm's home and host country institutional contexts affect this process of gaining legitimacy through governance mechanisms?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1: The effect of a foreign IPO's governance mechanisms on investor value perceptions is contingent on the legitimacy of its home country regulatory institutions, and the value of multiple mechanisms does not accumulate for foreign IPOs originating from countries with strong legal protection for minority investors.
H2: To achieve high investor value perceptions, foreign IPOs from countries with weak legal protection for minority investors must employ a larger number of mechanisms in their governance bundles than IPOs from countries with strong protection for minority investors.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm-level governance conditions — board independence, CEO stock options (incentive alignment), and venture capital backing — analyzed configurationally as governance bundles rather than as independent net effects
- DV(s): US investor value perceptions of foreign IPO firms, operationalized as membership in the set of highly valued foreign IPOs (price premium)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Strength of home country legal protection of minority investors (regulatory legitimacy) as a boundary condition; contextual conditions include high-tech industry, mature IPO firm, and prestigious underwriter

Investor valuation of foreign IPOs is theorized as a socially constructed legitimation process rather than a purely rational, efficiency-centered assessment. Firm-level governance mechanisms confer cognitive legitimacy, but this process is nested within US investors' perceptions of the regulatory legitimacy of the firm's home country institutions, so overall legitimacy falls at the intersection of the cognitive and regulatory institutional domains. Foreign IPOs from jurisdictions that surpass a minimal regulatory legitimacy threshold (strong minority shareholder protection) can obtain legitimacy flexibly from different, limited governance combinations, because under conditions of "cognitive economy" additional signals become redundant and their marginal effect declines. Firms from weak-protection countries face divergent institutional logics, institutional duality, and heightened investor uncertainty, so investors demand a wider range of standard, institutionalized governance practices before granting the same level of legitimacy and valuation.

**Data & Measures**
Sample: 198 foreign firms from 36 countries that made first-time "firm commitment" IPOs in the US between 1996 and 2006, identified from the Security Data Corporation (SDC) New Issues database, with each firm's S-1 registration filing and final prospectus obtained from the SEC; listings from mergers/acquisitions or spin-offs, unit trusts, warrants, rights offerings, utilities, and firms incorporated in Bermuda, the Bahamas, or the Cayman Islands were excluded. Outcome condition: price premium = (offer price - book value)/offer price, calibrated with full non-membership below a 66 percent premium, full membership at a 95 percent premium (the sample's highest decile, following Fama and French, 2002), and the midpoint at the average of the two breakpoints; a variant using the first-day closing price controls for underpricing and does not change results. Governance conditions: board independence (independent directors identified from the prospectus; fully in at 70 percent independent members, fully out at 30 percent), CEO stock (crisp set: stock options offered to the issuing firm's CEO prior to IPO), and venture capital backing (crisp set: VCs among a firm's principal pre-IPO shareholders). Country condition: strong home country investor protection = the product of the standardized Djankov et al. (2008) revised antidirector index and the International Country Risk Guide law and order index, with countries above the sample median fully in (97 weak-protection vs. 101 strong-protection firms). Contextual conditions: prestigious underwriter (Carter and Manaster index of 8 or higher fully in, below 5 fully out), mature IPO (at least 20 years old fully in, 1 year or less fully out, 5-year crossover), and high-tech industry (Internet-related, electronics, and software firms). Analysis: fs/QCA truth-table reduction with a consistency cutoff of .80 and a frequency threshold of two (capturing 84 percent of cases); the design is set-theoretic and configurational (sufficiency analysis), not a causal-identification design.

**Key Findings**
Six solution configurations showed acceptable consistency (consistency >= .80; overall solution consistency = .88, overall coverage = .54) for high foreign IPO price premiums. In line with Hypothesis 1, foreign IPOs from countries with strong legal protection of minority investors (solutions 1-3) achieved high price premiums with only one governance mechanism: incentive alignment (with prestigious underwriters) for mature technology firms, venture capital monitoring for younger technology firms, and an independent board for non-technology firms. Supporting Hypothesis 2, IPOs from countries without strong legal protection (solutions 4-6) needed multiple governance mechanisms — incentive alignment combined with venture backing, incentive alignment combined with an independent board, or all three — to reach comparably high perceived values. The asymmetric analysis of low perceived value complements these results: strong-protection IPOs suffer low perceived value when they adopt multiple governance mechanisms, while weak-protection IPOs experience poor perceived value when they adopt very few. Robustness analyses adding insider retained ownership, Big Five auditor backing, and founder-CEO status are consistent with both hypotheses; the presence of an international auditor appears in all configurations leading to high perceived value, and a hot IPO market was a necessary condition for high foreign IPO perceived value.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study shows there is no universal governance bundle leading to high levels of investors' value perceptions: different and limited combinations of monitoring and incentive-related mechanisms are equifinal, challenging agency-grounded assumptions that governance mechanisms act independently, additively, and cumulatively. It offers a nested model of legitimacy in which investor perceptions of a foreign IPO's overall legitimacy fall at the intersection of the cognitive and regulatory institutional domains, with home country minority investor protection acting as a first-order regulatory legitimacy threshold that shapes the size and composition of firm-level governance bundles. Methodologically, the paper introduces fs/QCA to IPO governance research, relaxing assumptions of permanent causality, additivity, and causal symmetry inherent in most IPO research.

**Practical Implication**
For foreign firms going public on US exchanges, IPOs originating from countries with weak minority investor protection must deploy both monitoring and incentive-related governance to bolster US investor confidence in their governance quality and their potential to achieve high valuations. IPOs from strong-protection countries can achieve high perceived value with a single salient governance mechanism, and having too many governance practices may actually undermine their IPO valuations.

**Limitations**
fs/QCA is constrained by the number of variables researchers can include, so the analysis does not utilize all possible controls typically used in IPO research. Investor protection is a formal-institution proxy for institutional differences between home countries, so informal institutional characteristics (e.g., networks, trust relationships) that US investors may also weigh are not captured by the operationalization. The price premium measure reflects the commingling of the value perceived by institutional investors and underwriters, and the study does not tease out how different groups of investors perceive foreign IPO quality; normative sources of legitimacy are likewise not isolated.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research isolating which governance bundles are associated with prestigious underwriters and auditors, which bundles attract "dedicated" institutional investors, and whether institutional factors affect these bundles. They suggest applying the configurational framework to IPO survival studies, to host capital markets whose institutional environments differ from the US, and to firms listing on two or more national capital markets. They also point to developing a broader theoretical approach to nested legitimacy, including its formative and boundary conditions and normative and other institutional aspects of legitimation.
