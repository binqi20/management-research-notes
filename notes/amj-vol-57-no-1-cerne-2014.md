# What Goes Around Comes Around: Knowledge Hiding, Perceived Motivational Climate, and Creativity

**Abstract**
Knowledge hiding prevents colleagues from generating creative ideas, but it may also have negative consequences for the creativity of a knowledge hider. Drawing on social exchange theory, we propose that when employees hide knowledge, they trigger a reciprocal distrust loop in which coworkers are unwilling to share knowledge with them. We further suggest that these effects are contingent on motivational climate, in such a way that the negative effects of an individual’s hiding knowledge on his/her own creativity are enhanced in a performance climate and attenuated in a mastery climate. A field study of 240 employees nested in 34 groups revealed a negative relationship between knowledge hiding and knowledge hiders’ creativity as well as a moderating role of a mastery climate. Study 2 replicated these findings in an experimental study of 132 undergraduate students, testing a reciprocal distrust loop and comparing it with an alternative intrapsychic explanatory process based on situational regulatory focus. Implications for practice and future research are discussed.

**Research Question**
How does knowledge hiding affect the knowledge hider's own creativity, and how do perceived mastery and performance climates moderate this relationship? The study proposes a reciprocal distrust loop, grounded in social exchange theory and the norm of reciprocity, as the interpersonal mechanism explaining why hiding knowledge backfires on the hider.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: Knowledge hiding is negatively related to a knowledge hider's creativity.
- H2: A coworker's distrust mediates the negative relationship between knowledge hiding and a knowledge hider's creativity.
- H3: A perceived mastery climate moderates the relationship between knowledge hiding and creativity; the higher the perceived mastery climate, the less negative the relationship.
- H4: A perceived performance climate moderates the relationship between knowledge hiding and creativity; the higher the perceived performance climate, the more negative the relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Knowledge hiding by the focal employee (self-reported in Study 1; manipulated in Study 2)
- DV(s): The knowledge hider's own creativity (supervisor-rated in Study 1; rated by two independent experts in Study 2)
- Mediators: Coworker's distrust and reciprocated knowledge hiding (the reciprocal distrust loop, tested in Study 2); prevention focus examined as an alternative intrapsychic mediator
- Moderators: Perceived mastery climate; perceived performance climate

Drawing on social exchange theory and the norm of reciprocity, the authors argue that when employee A intentionally hides knowledge that employee B has requested, distrust is created and B retaliates by hiding knowledge from A. This reciprocated hiding denies the initial knowledge hider access to the alternatives, example solutions, and relevant ideas needed for creative work, so knowledge hiding backfires on the hider's own creativity. A mastery climate (valuing effort, cooperation, learning, and skill development) overrides these reciprocity norms and attenuates the creativity penalty, whereas a performance climate (emphasizing normative ability, social comparison, and intrateam competition) strengthens adherence to reciprocity norms and makes the negative relationship stronger.

**Data & Measures**
Study 1: field survey of 240 employees and their 34 direct supervisors in two Slovenian companies (an aluminum manufacturer and a metal-processing firm), with data collected in August, September, and October 2011. Knowledge hiding was self-reported on Connelly et al.'s (2012) 12-item scale (alpha = .89); creativity was supervisor-rated on Zhou and George's (2001) 13-item scale (alpha = .95); perceived mastery and performance climates used Nerstad et al.'s 14-item instrument (alpha = .79 and .84), aggregated to the group level; the creative requirement of each position, rated by an independent HRM expert blind to the study, served as a control. Estimation used hierarchical linear (random coefficient) modeling on employees nested in groups. Study 2: a 3 (mastery climate / performance climate / no climate induced) x 2 (knowledge hiding low / high) between-persons factorial experiment with 132 second-year undergraduates working in dyads on a two-stage marketing scenario; creativity was rated by two independent expert raters (ICC2 = .83); distrust was rated by the other dyad member; prevention focus was measured with nine adapted items from Lockwood et al.'s (2002) general regulatory focus measure; analyses used random coefficient modeling (HLM 7, MPlus 6) and multilevel moderated mediation procedures (Bauer et al., 2006). Study 1 is cross-sectional and associational; the randomized manipulations in Study 2 were added to strengthen causal inferences.

**Key Findings**
Study 1: H1 supported, knowledge hiding was negatively and significantly related to creativity (γ = -.21, p < .01). H3 supported: a significant positive knowledge hiding x mastery climate interaction (γ = .17, p < .05); knowledge hiding predicted lower creativity when the mastery climate was low (γ = -.39, p < .01) but not when it was high (γ = -.01, n.s.). H4 received only marginal support: the knowledge hiding x performance climate interaction was marginally significant (γ = -.12, p = .07), with the slope more negative in groups perceiving a higher performance climate. Study 2: the knowledge-hiding manipulation significantly decreased a respondent's own creativity (γ = -.26, p < .01); knowledge hiding decreased creativity in the performance climate and control conditions but not in the mastery climate condition (supporting H3). Three-path mediation analysis supported the reciprocal distrust loop (H2), with distrust mediating the knowledge hiding-creativity relationship in all motivational climate conditions; prevention focus also uniquely mediated the relationship, so interpersonal (social) and intrapsychic mechanisms represent different sides of the same coin.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study's first contribution to the creativity literature is establishing the existence of a distrust loop between knowledge hiding and creativity: the very actions deemed by knowledge hiders as self-protective can backfire by undermining their own creativity, answering calls to enrich knowledge about social influences on creativity. Second, it introduces the moderating role of motivational climate: a mastery climate overrides the reciprocity norms underlying the social exchange in the knowledge hiding-creativity relationship, shifting explanation from personal dispositions to context. The findings also extend research on help seeking and help giving (Mueller & Kamdar, 2011) by showing positive gains, in the form of personal creativity, from giving knowledge and information to others.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can mitigate the negative influence of knowledge hiding on a knowledge hider's creativity by facilitating a mastery climate, which fosters trust rather than distrust; stimulating a performance climate (emphasizing competition and goal achievement at any cost) leads to even worse scenarios, so providing incentives for employees to "betray" their coworkers should be avoided. For employees who intentionally hide knowledge, the two studies show that "what goes around comes around": hiders receive reciprocated selfish treatment from coworkers that ultimately decreases their own creativity, so they should engage in less knowledge-hiding behavior to facilitate their own creative processes.

**Limitations**
Knowledge hiding was self-reported and, as a low-base-rate and undesirable behavior, may be underreported; Study 1 is cross-sectional, so reverse causality cannot be excluded (less creative employees may be more inclined to hide knowledge), and sequential data on the distrust loop could not be collected. In Study 2, the mastery climate manipulation may have confounded cooperation and learning with the knowledge-hiding manipulation, rating distrust after the first stage of the task may have affected behavior in the second stage, and participants were not randomly assigned to dyads. The student sample poses a potential threat to generalizability, although the authors argue that knowledge hiding and creativity are relevant for all workers.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research exploring work situations and factors beyond motivational climate (for example, perceived supervisor support, decision autonomy, and goal orientation) that stimulate or hinder the negative relationship between knowledge hiding and creativity. They ask future studies to rule out potential confounds by producing cleaner manipulations of the three mastery climate dimensions (cooperation, the important role each individual plays, and learning) and to vary the measurement order of distrust. Because the performance climate findings were not consistent across the two studies, that moderation should be interpreted with caution and warrants further research.
