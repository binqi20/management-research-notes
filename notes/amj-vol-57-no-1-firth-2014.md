# Newcomers Abroad: Expatriate Adaptation during Early Phases of International Assignments

**Abstract**
Integrating work from the expatriate adjustment and newcomer socialization literatures within a motivational framework, we propose that motivational states and stress cognitions impact expatriates’ work adjustment patterns over time, which in turn influence important assignment attitudes. In accordance with our theorizing, analyses of longitudinal data collected from 70 expatriates during their first four months of international assignment indicated that cross-cultural motivation and psychological empowerment related positively to initial levels of adjustment, and indirectly and negatively to work adjustment change. Challenge stressors positively related to changes in work adjustment over time. In turn, changes in work adjustment significantly related to expatriates’ assignment satisfaction and premature return intention, explaining variance above and beyond that explained by average levels of work adjustment. These findings extend understanding of how and why expatriate work adjustment evolves over time, as well as the unique influence that differences in adjustment change have on important expatriate outcomes.

**Research Question**
The paper examines the nature, antecedents, and outcomes of changes in expatriate work adjustment: why and how motivational states (cross-cultural motivation, psychological empowerment) and stress cognitions (challenge and hindrance stressors) relate to initial levels of, and changes in, expatriates' work adjustment during the first months of an international assignment, and how adjustment change in turn relates to assignment satisfaction and premature return intention.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: Expatriates' (a) psychological empowerment and (b) cross-cultural motivation positively relate to initial levels of work adjustment.
- H2: Expatriates' initial work adjustment mediates a negative relationship between expatriates' (a) psychological empowerment and (b) cross-cultural motivation and changes in work adjustment over time.
- H3: Challenge stressors positively relate to changes in expatriates' work adjustment over time (expatriates with higher challenge stressors exhibit more positive, or less negative, adjustment change).
- H4: Hindrance stressors negatively relate to (a) initial levels of and (b) changes in expatriates' work adjustment over time.
- H5a: Changes in work adjustment positively relate to assignment satisfaction.
- H5b: Changes in work adjustment negatively relate to premature return intention.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Psychological empowerment; cross-cultural motivation; challenge stressors; hindrance stressors (all measured at time 1, between-individual level)
- DV(s): Initial work adjustment (time 1); work adjustment change (times 1-3); assignment satisfaction and premature return intention (time 4)
- Mediators: Initial work adjustment (between motivational states and work adjustment change)
- Moderators: None hypothesized (time interactions capture within-individual trajectories)

Building on Kanfer and Heggestad's (1997) work motivation framework integrated with control theory (Carver & Scheier, 1982), the paper argues that motivational states and stress-related cognitions trigger motivation control and emotion control strategies that direct and sustain effort toward assignment goals. Expatriates higher in cross-cultural motivation and psychological empowerment immediately devote effort toward work goals and therefore experience higher initial work adjustment; per control theory's negative feedback cycle, the resulting positive discrepancies between actual and desired adjustment lead them to divert subsequent effort elsewhere, producing steeper declines in work adjustment over time. Challenge stressors lead expatriates to gradually increase effort to attain associated benefits, mitigating this decline, whereas hindrance stressors were expected to drain regulatory resources away from adjustment. Because adjustment perceptions reflect a sensemaking process, the rate and direction of adjustment change inform expatriates' appraisals of their assignment, shaping assignment satisfaction and premature return intention.

**Data & Measures**
Data came from expatriates of a large multinational Global 100 company in the energy industry; for 18 months the firm provided monthly contact lists of expatriates who had recently begun a new international assignment, and participants completed four confidential web surveys one month apart (times 1-4). Of 118 expatriates, 70 (59 percent) provided complete times 1-3 data (Hypotheses 1-4) and 61 provided complete data at all four time points (Hypothesis 5). Measures (1 "strongly disagree" to 5 "strongly agree" unless noted): cross-cultural motivation = Ang et al.'s (2007) five-item scale (time 1); psychological empowerment = Spreitzer's (1995) 12-item scale reworded to the current assignment (time 1); challenge and hindrance stressors = six- and five-item scales from Cavanaugh et al. (2000), rated 1 "produces no stress" to 5 "produces a great deal of stress" (time 1); work adjustment = three-item Black and Stephens (1989) scale (times 1-3); assignment satisfaction = Hackman and Oldham's (1976) four-item job satisfaction scale reworded to the assignment (time 4); premature return intention = three-item Kraimer and Wayne (2004) scale (time 4). Controls: organizational tenure, host-country language skills, prior international experience, and days on assignment before time 1. Analyses used two-level random coefficient models in HLM 6 (with Bayes-estimated work adjustment change scores), mediated regression with indirect-effect tests (MacKinnon et al., 2004), and hierarchical regressions; the design is an associational longitudinal self-report survey, with no causal identification strategy claimed.

**Key Findings**
- H1a and H1b supported: psychological empowerment (beta = 0.73, p < .05) and cross-cultural motivation (beta = 0.23, p < .05) related positively to initial levels of work adjustment.
- H2a and H2b supported: initial work adjustment negatively and uniquely predicted work adjustment change (b = -.17, p < .05), and the indirect effects of cross-cultural motivation (-.04) and psychological empowerment (-.09) on adjustment change through initial adjustment were both statistically significant.
- H3 supported: challenge stressors related positively to work adjustment trajectories over time (beta = 0.12, p < .05); the challenge stressor x time interaction accounted for 3 percent of the variance in adjustment change.
- H4a and H4b not supported: hindrance stressors related to neither initial levels of work adjustment (beta = -0.08, n.s.) nor adjustment trajectories (beta = -0.02, n.s.).
- H5a and H5b supported: work adjustment change positively predicted assignment satisfaction (beta = 2.76, p < .05; delta R-squared = .14) and negatively predicted premature return intention (beta = -1.98, p < .05; delta R-squared = .08), above and beyond controls, antecedents, and initial work adjustment; results held when controlling for average adjustment levels instead.
- On average, work adjustment decreased over the study period (beta = -0.08, p < .05), with significant individual differences in linear change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
First, the study shows that the impact of expatriates' motivational states and stressor experiences on work adjustment is time dependent, and it is among the first to demonstrate control theory's utility in explaining adjustment patterns over time, providing a motivational explanation for why expatriates with higher initial adjustment experience subsequent decreases. Second, it provides theoretical rationale and empirical evidence for the unique role of changes in work adjustment as antecedents of subsequent expatriate attitudes, above and beyond initial and average adjustment levels. Third, by viewing expatriates as newcomers, it integrates the expatriate adjustment and newcomer socialization literatures into a more complete account of how and why employees adapt to novel and challenging work demands.

**Practical Implication**
Managers might survey potential expatriates to select those exhibiting higher cross-cultural motivation and psychological empowerment, and organizations can take actions to improve cross-cultural motivation prior to assignment and empower expatriates in their new assignments. Because expatriates with higher initial motivation are more likely to experience decreases in work adjustment, managers should take care to maintain high levels of expatriate motivation over time. The paper also identifies challenge stressors, which are largely under the control of organizational leadership (e.g., time pressures, responsibility for multiple challenging projects, making international assignments prerequisites for promotion), as a lever for maintaining higher rates of work adjustment and keeping expatriates satisfied and focused on completing their assignments.

**Limitations**
The sample was limited to four well-developed, Western host countries, so generalizability to more culturally diverse settings is untested. The study did not directly measure the underlying motivation and emotion control processes posited to account for the hypothesized relationships, and it measured only initial levels of the motivational and stress-related antecedents, which may fluctuate over time. The authors also caution against overgeneralizing findings from the first several months of assignment to adjustment processes that unfold over longer periods, and the study relies on longitudinal self-report data capturing only expatriates' perceptions.

**Future Research**
The authors call for process-oriented research that formally tests the posited motivation and emotion control mechanisms, including intrapersonal processes such as effort, goal setting, and goal striving. They recommend collecting repeated measures of motivation and stress-related variables to model how fluctuations dynamically impact adjustment (using mixed-effects or latent growth modeling), examining whether findings generalize to more culturally diverse countries and to longer or varying assignment durations, and studying other adjustment forms (e.g., interaction, general) and behavioral effectiveness criteria (e.g., job performance, citizenship behavior, turnover, career success) longitudinally. They also suggest measuring adjustment from multiple sources and incorporating additional stakeholders such as spouses and local coworkers.
