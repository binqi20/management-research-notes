**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: rebalance relevance with rigor by promoting strong empirical work that informs practice, bringing the context center stage and moving the organizational problem to the forefront.
- Problem focus: manuscripts should be motivated by clearly articulated problem statements or research questions rooted in managerial challenges, rather than by smaller literature "gaps" or by adding untested moderators, mediators, and moderated mediators.
- Context: context shapes the boundary conditions and assumptions of the theories being examined; authors should integrate the setting (e.g., an organization's culture and policies, or industry characteristics) into the motivation and framing of the study.
- Embedding practices: a new reviewer evaluation criterion ("Engages an important problem for organizations"), reviewer education, editorials coauthored with a senior executive, thought leader, or scholar from a different field, and experimentation with the "house style."
- Workflow changes: shorter first-round reviews (2-3 pages with 4-6 major points), guidance to keep "Response to Reviewers" documents to about 20 pages, earlier "conditional accept" decisions, and a goal of keeping the first-submission-to-in-press cycle under 500 days.

This inaugural editorial by the 20th editorial team of the Academy of Management Journal argues that the dominant paradigm of motivating studies through a rather elusive "theoretical contribution," often achieved by adding moderators and mediators without regard to an organizational problem or phenomenon, has shifted the field's focus away from relevance and its applied nature. The team's proposed remedy is to make organizational problems and contexts the starting point of empirical research, and to embed that philosophy through reviewer criteria, author guidance, coauthored editorials, and workflow reforms.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial reframes what counts as a theoretical contribution: rather than deftness of theoretical manipulation using constructs, moderators, and moderated mediators, contribution should be a coherent explanation of how, when, and why phenomena occur. It positions problem-focused framing and context, which shapes theories' boundary conditions and assumptions, as the route to theoretical and empirical contribution, shifting discussion from smaller "gaps" in the literature to managerial, organizational, and societal problems that need to be addressed.

**Practical Implication**
For authors submitting to AMJ, the editorial signals that manuscripts should engage important organizational problems and integrate the context into their framing; authors may suggest action editors and five potential reviewers from the editorial review board, should disclose prior use of a dataset and individuals who commented on earlier versions, and are advised to keep "Response to Reviewers" documents to roughly 20 pages. Reviewers are asked to score manuscripts on the new criterion of engaging an important problem for organizations and to write shorter, more focused first-round reviews (2-3 pages with 4-6 major points).

**Limitations**
The editorial acknowledges that an incoming editor is not the reviewer: without consensus and guidance, reviewers will likely revert to habit, so the team's efforts in informing and educating reviewers will only slowly take hold. It also notes that it is difficult to define turnaround targets given author response time, reviewer speed, and editorial engagement.

**Future Research**
The editorial encourages pursuing bold ideas and "Grand Challenges" in management, adopting less conventional approaches to address significant, unresolved problems. It announces editorials coauthored with senior executives, thought leaders, or scholars from different fields as stage setters for opening up fresh new areas of inquiry, with the stated aim of making AMJ less formulaic, more interesting, and decidedly relevant in tackling managerial, organizational, and societal problems.
