# Courage as Identity Work: Accounts of Workplace Courage

**Abstract**
This study focuses on the intersection of identity dynamics and workplace courage. I examine accounts of courage submitted by business professionals and articulate the identity processes underlying the accounts. The narratives illustrate four storylines that reflect four distinct forms of courage and one storyline that reflects a lack of courage. In the accounts, identity tensions precipitate courageous acts, and courage-based identity work is used to reconcile the tensions. The findings suggest that in accounts of workplace courage, courageous behavior is viewed as an important form of identity work that helps individuals to minimize incongruities between their self- and social identities.

**Research Question**
The study asks three questions: (1) How do identity tensions influence acts of workplace courage? (2) How does courage-based identity work unfold in the storylines reflected in courage narratives? (3) How does courage-based identity work help to reconcile identity tensions in accounts of courage?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering conditions: identity tensions, i.e., incompatibilities among identities (self-identity vs. position/role identity, self-identity vs. relational identity, self-identity vs. group/organizational identity, one social identity vs. a different social identity, and opposing values within one's self-identity) that produce uncertainty, dissonance, anxiety, distress, and sometimes excitement (sensebreaking).
- Process / storylines: five storylines ordered by the courageous actor's level of proactivity, from low to high: endurance, reaction, opposition, creation, plus a no-courage storyline.
- Identity work forms: preserving an identity by withstanding unchangeable situations (endurance); repairing an identity by admitting fault or strengthening it by responding to a challenge or crisis (reaction); strengthening, revising, asserting, or reaffirming an identity by opposing more powerful individuals or organizational forces (opposition); creating a new identity or strengthening one by seizing risky opportunities (creation); no identity work performed (no courage).
- Contextual forces: relationships, power imbalances, and organizational norms, rules, and standards both foster and inhibit courage-based identity work.
- Emergent outcomes: reconciliation of identity tension, in which current identities are verified or modified, new identities are adopted, self- and social identities become coherent, and the actor's authentic self is revealed, accompanied by a sense of integrity, pride, joy, relief, and confidence; in no-courage accounts there is no reconciliation, and shame, regret, and frustration follow.

In the proposed model, identity tension operates as a sensebreaking mechanism: incompatibilities among a person's self- and social identities create dissonance and lead individuals to question which of their identities are most central. Courage-based identity work is the corresponding sensemaking mechanism, a courageous act through which individuals weigh contrasting alternatives, select the most salient identity, and reduce the tension. The process works as an identity distillation in which more salient identities are separated from less salient ones in the pursuit of a coherent identity; acts of no courage involve no identity work and leave the tension unreconciled.

**Data & Measures**
Data are 89 written narrative accounts of workplace courage (one per respondent; 92 to 2,672 words, mean 470 words) submitted anonymously through an online data collection tool by business professionals enrolled in an evening MBA program at a western US liberal arts college; respondents described an incident of workplace courage they personally experienced or observed, rated the degree of courage demonstrated, and explained how they defined courage. The narrative was the unit of analysis. Analysis combined narrative analytic techniques with grounded theory coding and constant comparison across four phases: microanalysis and open coding of the first 25 accounts; development of preliminary categories (storylines, identity tensions, characters, organizational context); recoding of all 89 accounts using operational definitions, with an independent reliability check by a colleague holding a Ph.D. in business administration; and storyline-by-storyline analysis using story skeletons.

**Key Findings**
Five storylines organize the accounts: opposition (56%), reaction (19%), no courage (17%), creation (5%), and endurance (4%). The three components of courage (morally worthy goal; risk, threat, or obstacle; and intentional action) were present in all but two narratives. Identity tensions precipitated the courageous acts, and courage-based identity work reconciled them; the four forms of identity work varied by the actor's level of proactivity, from low (endurance) to high (creation). In courage accounts, protagonists were described as having strong, salient personal values (93%), identifying strongly with their occupation, work group, or company (85%), and being high in self-efficacy (96%). References to relationships appeared in 93% of narratives, power imbalances in 81%, and organizational norms, rules, and standards in 77%; these forces both fostered and inhibited courage, and in 80% of accounts the act was intended to benefit others rather than just the actor. Courageous acts were followed by a sense of integrity, pride, joy, relief, and confidence, whereas no-courage accounts involved no identity work, left identity tensions unreconciled, and were followed by shame, regret, and frustration.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The main contribution is a model depicting the identity dynamics reflected in accounts of workplace courage: a three-step identity distillation process in which identity tension is aroused (sensebreaking), courage-based identity work is performed (sensemaking), and identity tension is reconciled. The study conceptualizes courage as a form of identity work, a conceptualization not proposed in prior research, adding a distinctive and more intense tactic to the identity work repertoire for situations in which two or more highly valued identities conflict, and showing how courageous acts help individuals manage discrepancies among self- and social identities. It also extends theory on the process of courage by proposing self- and social identity as crucial constructs in a courageous act and identifies four forms of courage-based identity work (endurance, reaction, opposition, creation) that broaden the oppositional focus of prior workplace courage research.

**Practical Implication**
For organizational leaders, courageous acts can serve as a signal that identity boundaries have been crossed; when those boundaries were crossed significantly or repeatedly, perceived negative outcomes followed, including individuals seeking new employment, no longer identifying with the organization, or actively disidentifying with it. Because respondents generally viewed courage-based identity work as a healthy response to organizational demands perceived as inappropriate, the paper's stated implication is that organizational leaders should welcome, support, and even promote courageous acts at work.

**Limitations**
All participants had college degrees, so low-skilled and uneducated workers are not represented; the average age was 30, ethnicity mirrored a predominately Caucasian community, and males were overrepresented. Written accounts may lack the depth of a lengthy interview, preclude follow-up questions, and may emphasize what respondents believed the researcher or other readers wanted to hear. Because the accounts are retrospective narratives, storytellers may have omitted, added, or changed details; the findings reflect how people make sense of courageous acts rather than how courageous acts occur objectively, some respondents may have fabricated identity tensions to explain courageous acts, and some positive feelings may have been self-enhancing rationalizations.

**Future Research**
The paper states that the endurance and creation forms of workplace courage deserve closer examination, and that courage focused on creation is likely an important type of workplace courage that warrants more attention given the importance of innovation and the attention entrepreneurship is receiving in business. It also notes that narratives offer promise as a method of gaining insight about both courage and identity, since sensemaking is integral to understanding both phenomena.
