# Power to the Principals! An Experimental Look at Shareholder Say-On-Pay Voting

**Abstract**
With recent legislation mandating that publicly traded corporations submit their CEOs' compensation for a nonbinding shareholder vote, a systematic understanding of shareholder preferences has never been so important. In spite of this, relatively little is known about what impacts shareholders' preferences and, subsequently, their ultimate voting behavior. We integrate two theories to help frame the question and to help predict shareholder behavior. Per agency theory, shareholders, as principals, will disapprove of high CEO rewards and poor firm performance, symmetrically assessing gains and losses. Per prospect theory, shareholders will be loss averse, responding much more strongly to being in a loss position than to being in a gain or neutral position. We combine these theories' predictions in two lab experiments in which we simulate a shareholder "say-on-pay" vote, hypothesizing that shareholders will be concerned with agency costs, but only when they are in a loss position. The results of these simulated votes suggest that shareholders do value "pay for performance," in keeping with agency theory. However, shareholders exhibit this focus on agency-normative prescriptions asymmetrically, showing loss aversion in keeping with prospect theory. This finding has significant implications for both theory and practice as shareholder votes become a regular and high-profile occurrence.

**Research Question**
What impacts shareholders' preferences and ultimate voting behavior in nonbinding say-on-pay votes on CEO compensation? The authors propose that shareholders' frame of reference, determined jointly by CEO rewards and firm performance, determines their preferences regarding the link between CEO pay and firm performance.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: With firm performance held constant, higher CEO rewards decrease the probability that shareholders vote to approve CEO pay.
- H2: With CEO rewards held constant, poorer firm performance decreases the probability that shareholders vote to approve CEO pay.
- H3: Higher CEO rewards decrease the probability that shareholders vote to approve CEO pay only when firm performance is poor.
- H4: The effect of high CEO rewards on the probability shareholders vote to approve CEO pay is mediated by shareholders' agency-normative assessment.
- H5: The effect of poor firm performance on the probability shareholders vote to approve CEO pay is mediated by shareholders' agency-normative assessment.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO rewards (Study 1: 20 percent CEO pay increase vs. decrease; Study 2: high vs. low CEO pay at the 95th vs. 5th percentile of Fortune 500 CEO compensation); firm performance (shareholder return deficit vs. surplus, 20 percentage points below vs. above the S&P 500 average in Study 1 and the industry average in Study 2)
- DV(s): Probability that a shareholder votes to approve the CEO's compensation (approval)
- Mediators: Shareholders' agency-normative assessment (the extent to which shareholders judge CEO pay to be justified, given firm performance)
- Moderators: Shareholders' decision frame (loss vs. neutral vs. gain position), modeled through the fully crossed interaction of the two treatments; per H3, CEO rewards matter only in the loss vs. neutral contrast

Shareholders, as principals, are assumed to want the strongest firm performance for the lowest CEO compensation (agency theory), but following Tversky and Kahneman's (1991) model of loss aversion in riskless choice, agency losses (high CEO rewards paired with poor firm performance) loom larger in shareholders' minds than commensurate agency gains. Firm performance and CEO rewards jointly determine whether shareholders occupy a loss, neutral, or gain position, and CEO rewards reduce approval only when shareholders vote from a loss position. The proximal mechanism is shareholders' agency-normative assessment, an evaluation of the appropriateness of the CEO's rewards from an agency perspective that is disproportionately impacted by a loss position and mediates the effects of CEO rewards and firm performance on the vote.

**Data & Measures**
Two fully crossed two-by-two between-subjects lab experiments with random assignment simulated a say-on-pay vote using a notice modeled after a Fortune 500 firm's actual say-on-pay notice (Aflac Incorporated, 2010). Study 1: 93 of 107 full-time students in a first-year MBA strategy course at a large research university with a BusinessWeek "Top 20" MBA program (results validated on a convenience sample of 63 experienced investors). Study 2: 88 full-time first- and second-year MBA students prompted to act as mutual fund managers whose fund held 15 percent of its assets in the focal firm. DV: approval, coded 1 for a vote to approve and 0 for a vote to reject (logistic regression; marginal effects reported). Mediator: agency-normative assessment, measured in Study 1 with the single item "To what extent do you agree that the CEO's pay is justified, given the performance of the firm?" (7-point scale, standardized) and in Study 2 with a three-item average. Manipulations: CEO pay increase (pay rising from about $9 million to $11 million vs. falling from $13 million to $11 million) in Study 1, high vs. low CEO pay (95th vs. 5th percentile of Fortune 500 CEO pay) in Study 2, and shareholder return deficit (return 20 percentage points below vs. above the S&P 500 average in Study 1, the industry average in Study 2). Mediation was tested with Baron and Kenny's (1986) three-step method (logit and OLS); the authors note that experiments enable researchers to draw conclusions about the causal direction between related variables.

**Key Findings**
Study 1: H1 not supported (an increase in CEO pay had no significant marginal effect on approval); H2 supported (a shareholder return deficit decreased the probability of approval by 20 percentage points, p < .05); H3 supported (a CEO pay increase decreased approval by 27 percentage points when shareholder return was below the S&P 500, p < .05, but had no significant effect when shareholder value was above the S&P 500); H4 and H5 supported (agency-normative assessment, marginal effect 0.23, p < .001, accounted for all of the overall effects of both treatments). Study 2: all five hypotheses supported; high CEO pay reduced approval by 18 percentage points (p < .05), a shareholder return deficit reduced it by 36 percentage points (p < .001), high CEO pay decreased approval by 29 percentage points only in the loss vs. neutral frame (p < .05), and including agency-normative assessment (marginal effect 0.27, p < .001) eliminated any effect of either treatment. Overall, shareholders value pay for performance but exhibit loss aversion, reacting to CEO rewards only when facing a potential loss, not when facing a potential gain.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates agency theory and prospect theory, via Tversky and Kahneman's (1991) model of loss aversion in riskless choice, to explain shareholder voting behavior more completely than either theory alone, applying prospect theory to shareholders' side of the agency contract rather than to CEOs' response to incentives. It introduces a new construct, shareholders' agency-normative assessment, as the mediating mechanism, and contributes theory-based, experimental evidence on causal mechanisms to a shareholder voting literature that remains underdeveloped and dominated by archival studies.

**Practical Implication**
Boards of directors should take shareholders' frame of reference into account when presenting CEO pay for a say-on-pay vote: shareholders react very negatively to raising CEO pay in times of poor firm performance, so boards should be wary of doing so. Because a decrease in CEO pay, or simply low CEO pay, only alters shareholders' behavior in times of poor performance, symbolically low compensation should be adopted with caution, since raising it later may be difficult if performance is weak. Boards can also proactively educate shareholders and justify CEO compensation ahead of annual shareholder meetings, especially since a no-vote may register displeasure with the board's compensation committee rather than the CEO.

**Limitations**
Lab experiments are less generalizable because they are conducted in controlled settings, and the study offers no indication of how the individual-level loss aversion identified maps onto communities of investors, who routinely exhibit herding behavior. Only a few variables could be manipulated in any given study, and common method variance may still be present given the high correlations between the mediator and outcome, especially in Study 2. Because several conditions changed between Study 1 and Study 2 (the institutional investor prompt, extreme CEO pay values, the industry performance benchmark, and the high-salience 15 percent portfolio stake), the authors cannot identify which altered condition explains the support for H1 found only in Study 2.

**Future Research**
The authors call for analysis of actual say-on-pay vote outcomes once data become available, testing the hypotheses on institutional investors, and joining work on collective investor biases to understand how individual-level loss aversion aggregates into shareholder vote outcomes. They suggest replicating the studies while altering the pay mix (stock options, restricted stock, bonus pay, salary), using alternative reference points and conditions (past firm performance, environmental conditions, CEO tenure, board monitoring activity, the CEO's past compensation), and shifting the decision frame to relative deprivation by contrasting CEO pay with an average worker's pay. They also encourage techniques such as measuring variables at different times to study agency-normative assessment without common method variance.
