# Abusive Supervision and Retaliation: A Self-Control Framework

**Abstract**
There are conflicting perspectives on whether subordinates will or will not aggress against an abusive supervisor. To address this paradox we develop a self-control model of retaliatory behavior, wherein subordinates’ self-control capacity and motivation to self-control influence emotional and retaliatory reactions to provocations by enabling individuals to override their hostile impulses. In Study 1, we demonstrate that self-control capacity, motivation to self-control (supervisor coercive power), and abusive supervision interact in such a way that the strongest association between abusive supervision and supervisor-directed aggression occurs when subordinates are low in self-control capacity and perceive their supervisor to be low in coercive power. In Study 2, we extend this finding, testing a moderated mediation model, wherein hostility toward a supervisor represents the hostile impulse resulting in retaliatory behavior, mediating the relation between abusive supervision and supervisor-directed aggression. Results from Study 2 indicate that self-control capacity allows individuals to regulate the hostile feelings experienced following abusive supervision, while self-control capacity and supervisor coercive power jointly moderate the tendency to act on one’s hostile feelings toward an abusive supervisor. We discuss implications for retaliatory behaviors at work.

**Research Question**
The paper addresses the paradox of whether subordinates will or will not aggress against an abusive supervisor. It asks how subordinates' self-control capacity and motivation to self-control (perceived supervisor coercive and reward power) jointly determine hostile emotional reactions and supervisor-directed aggression following abusive supervision.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1a: In a significant three-way interaction, the relation between abusive supervision and supervisor-directed aggression is strongest when subordinates neither have self-control capacity nor perceive that their supervisor has coercive power.
- H1b: In a significant three-way interaction, the relation between abusive supervision and supervisor-directed aggression is strongest when subordinates neither have self-control capacity nor perceive that their supervisor has reward power.
- H1c: The three-way interaction between abusive supervision, self-control capacity, and supervisor coercive power (H1a) is stronger than the three-way interaction between abusive supervision, self-control capacity, and supervisor reward power (H1b) in the prediction of supervisor-directed aggression.
- H2: Hostility toward supervisors mediates the relation between abusive supervision and supervisor-directed aggression.
- H3: The relation between abusive supervision and hostility toward supervisors is moderated by self-control capacity: the relation is weaker when self-control capacity is high rather than low.
- H4: The relation between hostility toward supervisors and supervisor-directed aggression is strongest when subordinates neither have self-control capacity nor perceive their supervisor as having coercive power.
- H5: The indirect relation between abusive supervision and supervisor-directed aggression through the mediating role of hostility toward supervisors is strongest when subordinates neither have self-control capacity nor perceive their supervisor as having coercive power.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Abusive supervision
- DV(s): Supervisor-directed aggression
- Mediators: Hostility toward supervisor (Study 2)
- Moderators: Self-control capacity; supervisor coercive power and supervisor reward power (operationalizing motivation to self-control)

Abusive supervision is theorized as an interpersonal provocation that elicits hostility toward the supervisor, a hostile impulse that must be controlled. Whether that impulse becomes supervisor-directed aggression depends jointly on self-control capacity (the ability to restrain retaliatory urges, which also mitigates the extent to which hostile feelings are experienced) and motivation to self-control, operationalized as perceived supervisor power to punish (coercive power) or reward. Subordinates who possess neither the capacity nor the motivation to self-control reciprocate supervisory abuse with abuse in kind, whereas possessing either ingredient enables them to override their natural inclination to retaliate. Following the principle that bad is stronger than good, punishment-based coercive power is expected to outweigh reward power as a motivator of self-control.

**Data & Measures**
Two multi-wave online surveys of full-time employees who interacted regularly with their supervisors, each with three measurement points separated by approximately one week (Study 1: n = 196, recruited through advertisements on online forums; Study 2: n = 188, recruited through University of Waterloo alumni offices and public advertisements). Measures: abusive supervision = Tepper's (2000) 15-item scale; supervisor coercive and reward power = Hinkin and Schriesheim's (1989) four-item scales; self-control capacity = 25-item scale (Ciarocco, Twenge, Muraven, & Tice, 2007); supervisor-directed aggression = Mitchell and Ambrose's (2007) ten-item scale; hostility toward supervisor (Study 2) = adapted hostility subscale of the PANAS-X (Watson & Clark, 1994). Controls: age, tenure with supervisor, and gender, plus impression management (Paulhus, 1991) in Study 1. Analyses: confirmatory factor analyses (AMOS 19.0), hierarchical multiple regression (SPSS 19.0), and Edwards and Lambert's (2007) moderated path-analysis approach with 1,000 bootstrapped samples. The design is associational: the authors state that the cross-sectional design does not allow causal inferences.

**Key Findings**
Study 1: the three-way abusive supervision x self-control capacity x supervisor coercive power interaction significantly predicted supervisor-directed aggression (b = .23, p < .01), and the abusive supervision-aggression relation was strongest when subordinates were low in self-control capacity and perceived coercive power was low, supporting H1a. The parallel three-way interaction with supervisor reward power was not significant (b = -.21, n.s.), failing to support H1b; usefulness analyses showed the coercive-power interaction added explained variance (delta R-squared = .02, p < .01) while the reward-power interaction did not (delta R-squared = .01, n.s.), supporting H1c. Study 2: the abusive supervision x self-control capacity interaction predicted hostility toward supervisors (b = -0.14, p < .05, Table 4), the relation being weaker when self-control capacity was high rather than low, supporting H3. The indirect effect of abusive supervision on supervisor-directed aggression through hostility toward supervisor was significant (.19, p < .01), supporting H2. The hostility x self-control capacity x supervisor coercive power interaction was significant (b = .23, p < .01), with the hostility-aggression relation strongest when coercive power was low and self-control capacity was low, supporting H4; the reward-power three-way interaction was again not significant (b = .07, n.s.). The indirect effect was strongest under low coercive power and low self-control capacity (.77, p < .01, significantly stronger than the other three conditions), supporting H5.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper presents a self-control model of retaliation that reconciles theoretical frameworks in which supervisory power mitigates retaliatory responses with empirical results showing abused subordinates frequently do retaliate: aggression toward supervisors is a joint function of self-control capacity, motivation to self-control, and their interaction, a framework that also maps previously disparate and disconnected moderation findings onto common self-control theory constructs. It extends the abusive supervision literature's treatment of power by directly measuring perceived power rather than indirect proxies and by differentiating supervisory reward power from coercive power, showing punishment-based motivation is more effectual than reward-based motivation, in accordance with the principle that bad is stronger than good. By situating hostility toward supervisors as the mediating mechanism, the model is among the first to link the abusive supervision literature to the emotion literature and contributes to understanding of discrete emotions in the workplace.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations and individual employees, the findings suggest selection procedures that directly assess self-control capacity and training that develops it (e.g., self-control exercises such as physical exercise programs or financial management training), as well as mindfulness training to reduce feelings of hostility in response to abusive supervision. Organizations can also maximize motivation to self-control by raising employees' awareness of the negative consequences of retaliatory actions and by strengthening workplace norms for civil behavior through training or formal civility interventions. Because abusive supervisors are provocateurs, the most straightforward route is to remove or alter aggressive supervisor behavior, including selecting individuals with high self-control capacity for supervisory positions, training supervisors' self-control, and informing supervisors of the negative consequences of engaging in abusive acts.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the studies did not employ random population sampling, so generalizability should be viewed with some caution, and that single-source self-report data raise common-source concerns, although interactive effects offer persuasive evidence against common source effects and self-reports are considered valid for perceptual outcomes and internal states. The cross-sectional design does not allow causal inferences, and it is plausible that supervisor-directed aggression leads to abusive supervision rather than the reverse. The studies also did not directly assess the reappraisal and suppression strategies implied by the emotion-regulation account of their findings.

**Future Research**
The paper encourages examining the model in other settings and populations, using multisource data, and using cross-lagged panel designs to untangle the causal relation between abusive supervision and supervisor-directed aggression. It calls for directly testing reappraisal and suppression strategies and related individual differences (e.g., hostile attribution bias), examining alternative motivational factors (e.g., agreeableness, traditionality, negative reciprocity, performance-contingent self-esteem, or directly assessed motivation to self-control), and exploring downsides of the self-control process such as decreased job performance and organizational citizenship behaviors. It also suggests experience-sampling designs to examine the model at the intraindividual level and generalizing the framework to other aggression targets (organizations, coworkers, family members), covert retaliation, and displaced aggression.
