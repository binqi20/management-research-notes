# Falling from Great (and Not-So-Great) Heights: How Initial Status Position Influences Performance after Status Loss

**Abstract**
We investigate how initial status position influences the quality of task performance in the aftermath of status loss. We argue that despite the benefits of having status, high-status individuals experience more “self-threat”—challenges or contradictions to a central view of the self—and, consequently, have more difficulty performing well after status loss than do low-status individuals who experience a comparable loss of status. In a field study of professional baseball players (Study 1), we found that although low-status players’ performance quality was unaffected by status loss, the quality of high-status players’ performance declined significantly after losing status. In a high-involvement group experiment (Study 2), we found that high-status individuals who lost status were less proficient than both high-status individuals who did not lose status and low-status individuals who lost a comparable amount of status. However, supporting self-threat as the proposed psychological process, self-affirmation restored the quality of high-status individuals’ performance (Study 3). We discuss the practical and theoretical implications of these findings.

**Research Question**
How does a person's initial position in a status hierarchy influence the quality of their task performance in the aftermath of status loss? Specifically, the authors ask whether high- or low-status individuals have more difficulty executing tasks proficiently and accurately after losing an objectively comparable amount of status.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1: The negative effect of status loss on performance quality for high-status individuals is stronger than it is for low-status individuals.
H2: Self-affirmation moderates the effect of initial status position on performance quality after status loss: high- (but not low-) status individuals perform better when they are given an opportunity for self-affirmation.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Status loss (losing final-offer salary arbitration in Study 1; losing respect points and a status-based title in Study 2; losing respect points in Study 3)
- DV(s): Task performance quality (standardized composite of on-base percentage and slugging percentage in Study 1; errors on a word-search task in Study 2; low-quality responses on a word generation task in Study 3, with lower error counts indicating higher-quality performance)
- Mediators: Self-threat (the proposed psychological process; not measured directly, but tested indirectly via a moderation-of-process design)
- Moderators: Initial status position (high vs. low); self-affirmation opportunity (Study 3)

Because people strive to maintain and enhance positive self-views, high-status individuals define their superior position as a relatively more central component of the self than low-status individuals do. Losing status therefore triggers more self-threat (challenges or contradictions to a central view of the self) for high-status individuals, and self-threat interrupts the information processing necessary to execute subsequent tasks proficiently and accurately by directing attention toward the self and away from the task. As a result, high-status individuals' performance quality declines after status loss while comparable status loss leaves low-status individuals' performance unaffected. Because self-affirmation protects against threats to the self, giving high-status individuals an opportunity to self-affirm restores the quality of their performance.

**Data & Measures**
Study 1 (field): archival data on all nonpitchers who went through Major League Baseball final-offer salary arbitration once between 1974 and 2011 (n = 199; complete data on 186 players). Initial prearbitration status = composite of All-Star Game selections and four major awards (alpha = .72); status loss = losing the arbitration (dummy: 1 = loss, 0 = win); postarbitration performance quality = standardized composite of on-base percentage and slugging percentage (alpha = .90). Multilevel regressions (players nested in teams; Stata 12 xtmixed) with controls including prior performance (linear and squared), team performance and postseason play, year, player age, injuries, share of games on the current team, and the financial opportunity cost of the loss; the design is associational, and the authors state that causality cannot be determined from these results. Study 2 (lab): 2 (initial status: high vs. low) x 2 (status loss vs. no status loss) between-participants group experiment (86 participants; 77 after excluding suspicious participants) using confederates, respect-point ratings, and role titles; all participants received identical above-average performance feedback so status loss was not confounded with negative performance feedback; performance quality = summed errors on a word-search task (independent-rater reliability r = .90); Poisson regression. Study 3 (lab): 2 (initial status) x 2 (self-affirmation vs. no affirmation) between-participants computer-mediated design (76 participants; 73 after exclusions) in which all participants lost 50 percent of their respect points; affirmation was manipulated by writing about a valued personal relationship versus a trip to the grocery store; performance quality = number of low-quality responses in generating adjectives describing the taste of chocolate chip cookies (independent-rater reliability r = .92); Poisson regression.

**Key Findings**
Study 1: a significant negative interaction between initial status position and status loss on performance quality (gamma = -.19, s.e. = .08, p = .01). Simple slopes showed that when initial status was low (-1 s.d.), status loss had no effect on performance quality (b = -0.08, p = .77), but when initial status was high (+1 s.d.), status loss had a significant negative effect (b = -0.76, p = .002); the main effect of status loss was marginally significant and negative (gamma = -.42, p = .08) and initial status position was positively related to performance quality (gamma = .17, p = .001). H1 supported. Study 2: a significant initial-status-by-status-loss interaction on errors (b = -1.17, s.e. = 0.45, p = .01); high-status participants who lost status performed more poorly (mean errors = 4.21) than high-status participants who had not lost status (mean = 1.53, p = .001) and than low-status participants who lost a comparable amount of status (mean = 0.62, p = .001), while low-status participants' performance quality was unaffected by status loss (p = .69); output quantity did not differ (p = .11), suggesting impaired ability rather than reduced motivation. H1 supported. Study 3: a significant status-by-affirmation interaction (b = -0.15, s.e. = 0.08, p = .05); high-status participants who were not affirmed gave more low-quality responses (mean = 10.67) than affirmed high-status participants (mean = 7.84, p = .005), whereas low-status participants were unaffected by affirmation (means = 9.56 vs. 9.56, p = .99). H2 supported: self-affirmation restored the quality of high-status individuals' performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the status literature by examining the understudied phenomenon of status loss: to the authors' knowledge it is the first series of studies investigating how high- and low-status people behave after actually losing status, rather than reacting to the prospect of losing it. The findings challenge traditional models of status in which more status is equated with greater resources and better outcomes, highlighting status loss as a negative life event in which more status resources are a liability because the self becomes implicated in holding a high-status position. Whereas research on prospective status loss suggests people increase effort to avoid loss, this research shows that those with the most status are least careful and proficient after actual loss, a self-defeating reaction that may produce further status loss and thwart status regain.

**Practical Implication**
For understanding status loss in organizations, the authors note that high-status individuals such as star performers and high-ranking executives are the most likely to experience impaired performance after losing status yet are probably also less likely to admit or show that they feel threatened, so bad decisions and errors made in the immediate aftermath of status loss may go unrecognized by coworkers, clients, and shareholders until they have serious and potentially irreversible consequences. Study 3 gives reason for optimism: thinking about a significant relationship that makes one feel respected and worthy can restore high-quality performance, although the authors question whether those at the top of a hierarchy will seek out the affirmation they need. Because status can often be regained, finding other ways to appear worthy of a high-status position (e.g., helping one's group members) might help high-status individuals regain their position after status loss.

**Limitations**
The authors state that causality cannot be determined from Study 1's field data, and that unobservable factors (e.g., arbitrators being more aware of high-status players' deficits, or the public visibility of high-status players increasing the magnitude of their loss) could have influenced results. In Study 1, status loss was negatively correlated with prior-year performance (r = -.14), so losing arbitration may partly have been perceived as negative performance feedback, and the on-base/slugging composite feeds into team performance; Study 2 addressed these issues with controlled feedback and an individual task. Study 2's hierarchy was zero-sum, so reactions to status loss may also have been reactions to a specific group member gaining status, and its design could not examine the role of self-threat, which the authors describe as difficult to measure accurately and which was therefore assessed only indirectly through Study 3's self-affirmation moderation-of-process design.

**Future Research**
The authors propose investigating how those in the middle of a status hierarchy (e.g., the middle class, midlevel managers) respond to losing status; how different types of status hierarchies (bimodal, flat, zero-sum) and gradual versus abrupt losses of achieved versus ascribed status influence reactions and their downstream consequences; and extending the scope of outcomes to other organizationally relevant behaviors (organizational citizenship behaviors, counterproductive behaviors, adaptive and proactive behaviors) and to behaviors outside the context in which the status loss occurred. They also call for exploring moderators such as the centrality of status to the self and affective commitment to one's role, and alternate processes (e.g., thwarted status-striving motivation, cognitive inconsistency, inequity reduction) that might contribute to performance declines after status loss.
