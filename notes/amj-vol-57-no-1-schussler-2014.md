# On Melting Summits: The Limitations of Field-Configuring Events as Catalysts of Change in Transnational Climate Policy

**Abstract**
Although field-configuring events have been highlighted as catalysts of institutional change, scholars still know little about the specific conditions that allow such change to occur. Using data from a longitudinal study of United Nations climate conferences, we analyze how regular and high-stakes events in an event series interacted in producing and preventing institutional change in the transnational climate policy field. We uncover variations in event structures, processes, and outcomes that explain why climate conferences have not led to effective solutions to combat human-induced global warming. Results in particular highlight that growing field complexity and issue multiplication compromise the change potential of a field-configuring event series in favor of field maintenance. Over time, diverse actors find event participation useful for their own purposes, but their activity is not connected to the institutions at the center of the issue-based field. In discussing how events configuring a field are purposefully staged and enacted but also influenced by developments in the field, our study contributes to a more complete understanding of field-configuring events, particularly in contested transnational policy arenas.

**Research Question**
The paper poses two research questions in its own words: (1) What are the causes and effects of temporal boundedness and interactional openness in a field-configuring event series? (2) How and why do field-configuring events change in the context of an evolving organizational field? Both are asked to explain why the UN climate conferences (COPs) have failed to bring about regulations to combat human-induced climate change.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: temporal boundedness (deadlines, multiyear negotiation cycles, media attention) and interactional openness (overlapping formal and informal discursive spaces among diverse field members)
- Event types: regular COPs (technical, incremental negotiation with more open interaction) versus high-stakes COPs (COP 3 Kyoto, COP 13 Bali, COP 15 Copenhagen: milestone deadlines, high expectations, strong media coverage)
- Aggregate dimensions from the coding: event staging (event calendar; issues and positions; general audience awareness) and event enactment (opportunities and spaces for interaction; negotiation tactics; observer roles)
- Process / phases: Phase 1 (1995-2005), negotiating and implementing the Kyoto Protocol; Phase 2 (2005-2012), defining the future of the UNFCCC amid growing field complexity and issue multiplication
- Triggering conditions: entry into force of the Kyoto Protocol in 2005; US withdrawal from the Kyoto process; entry of diverse new actors; multiplication of negotiation tracks
- Emergent outcomes: the event series shifts from field-endogenous catalyst of institutional change to mechanism of field maintenance; failure of the 2009 Copenhagen summit

Regular events created interactional openness through multiple formal and informal opportunities for participant exchange, supporting trust building and the incremental advancement of rules, whereas the few high-stakes events induced a stronger sense of temporal boundedness through deadlines and media attention, generating momentum. In the early phases of field development, the interaction of regular and high-stakes events facilitated institutional change by allowing for both trust building and momentum creation. After the Kyoto Protocol entered into force, growing field complexity and issue multiplication meant that the COPs ceased to be interactionally open and temporally bounded, and the event series came to maintain the field rather than change it. Deliberately staging the 2009 Copenhagen COP as a high-stakes event under these conditions prevented institutional change and resulted in an ongoing delay of substantive policy decisions.

**Data & Measures**
Longitudinal qualitative data on the UNFCCC COP event series: participant observation (one author has attended 13 major international climate policy events since 2000; two authors attended COP 14 in Poznan in 2008 and the subsequent subsidiary bodies meeting in Bonn in 2009); 39 formal semistructured interviews (28 at events, 11 follow-ups; 34 audio-taped and fully transcribed); and documents comprising 204 COP-related Earth Negotiations Bulletin (ENB) issues (187 daily issues plus 17 COP summaries), 58 academic articles, UNFCCC secretariat electronic archives (participant directories, press releases, official speeches, side-event registration data, budget reports), and New York Times coverage (3 articles sampled per COP plus per-COP article counts). All 204 ENBs, interviews, and observations were coded with ATLAS.ti qualitative data analysis software: a first round of descriptive coding yielded an initial scheme with 60 codes, the data were additionally coded for temporal boundedness and interactional openness, and iterative analysis produced a data structure of six second-order themes under two aggregate dimensions (event staging and event enactment).

**Key Findings**
First, events in the series were staged and enacted in different ways: regular COPs created more interactional openness by providing multiple formal and informal opportunities for participant exchange, whereas the few high-stakes events (Kyoto, Bali, Copenhagen) induced a stronger sense of temporal boundedness through deadlines and media attention; high-stakes events led to highly visible outcomes, either new agreements (Kyoto, Bali) or a widely shared sense of failure (Copenhagen), while regular events favored incremental advancement of rules. Second, in the early phases of field development the interaction of regular and high-stakes events facilitated institutional change by allowing for both trust building and momentum creation. Third, over time the COPs ceased to be interactionally open and temporally bounded as diverse actors with vested interests entered the field, power coalitions shifted, and the events became platforms for issues not strictly related to emission reduction: growing field complexity and issue multiplication compromised the change potential of the event series in favor of field maintenance, and the deliberate staging of the Copenhagen high-stakes event in 2009 prevented institutional change and resulted in an ongoing delay of substantive policy decisions. The authors attribute the Copenhagen failure at least in part to organizers' futile attempt to stage a high-stakes event in a situation of insufficient interactional openness among field constituencies.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study makes two contributions to research on field-configuring events, institutional change, and the structuration of transnational fields. First, it identifies variations among different events within a series (regular versus high-stakes) that allow the authors to define the processes by which the temporal boundedness and interactional openness of field-configuring events can lead to institutional change, extending scholarly work on discursive spaces and field-configuring events (Hardy & Maguire, 2010). Second, it detects field-level conditions that limit the capacity of field-configuring events to bring about institutional change: when an issue-based field fragments into increasingly diverse membership and subissues as a result of the fluid and complex dynamics of transnational field structuration, the role of field-configuring events shifts from field-endogenous catalysts of change to mechanisms of field maintenance.

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest that organizers of field-configuring events need to take field structures into account when designing their events, specifically considering the complexity and diversity of issues at stake and the boundaries and authority structures of a field. Event formats such as "unconferences" or "barcamps" could be integrated into a field-configuring event series to better balance coordination with the creativity needed to imagine possible futures, which also raises the practical question of how effective a template for structuring transnational fields the UN system is. For climate policy, a shift into smaller policy forums and the breaking down of complex issues into more concrete and tangible policy arenas could reintroduce some of the boundaries within which field-configuring events can support the development of a new, encompassing climate agreement.

**Limitations**
The authors state that they focused on one specific and in many ways unique transnational policy field, so the findings may not be directly applicable to other field settings; they note, however, that other fields share characteristics of the climate policy field (formation around a contested issue, absence of clear structures of domination, increasing field complexity and issue multiplication) and suggest their findings will apply to such settings. In addition, their choice to highlight the temporal aspects of boundedness meant that they largely neglected the role of spatial proximity or distance.

**Future Research**
The paper proposes that further research could build on its idea of field maintenance and track national and community activities resulting from and related to field-configuring events, illuminating the trickle-up and trickle-down processes occurring between transnational and local arenas. It also calls for research on the spatial and temporal dimensions of field-configuring events at group and individual levels of analysis (for instance, how high-stakes staging affects objective and subjective representations of time and space among event participants, or how different time frames affect group processes during events), for investigating how elements of creativity and spontaneity could be reinstalled in highly complex and fragmented decision-making processes (for example, through "unconferences" or "barcamps"), and for paying attention to the two-sided role of emotions at field-configuring events to enrich understanding of the emotional dimension of institutional work.
