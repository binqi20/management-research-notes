# It's Not Easy Being Green: The Role of Self-Evaluations in Explaining Support of Environmental Issues

**Abstract**
Using a mixed methods design, we examine the role of self-evaluations in influencing support for environmental issues. In Study 1—an inductive, qualitative study—we develop theory about how environmental issue supporters evaluate themselves in a mixed fashion, positively around having assets (self-assets) and negatively around questioning their performance (self-doubts). We explain how these ongoing self-evaluations, which we label “situated self-work,” are shaped by cognitive, relational, and organizational challenges individuals interpret about an issue from a variety of life domains (work, home, or school). In Study 2—an inductive, quantitative, observational study—we derive three profiles of environmental issue supporters’ mixed selves (self-affirmers, self-critics, and self-equivocators) and relate these profiles to real issue-supportive behaviors. We empirically validate key constructs from Study 1 and show that even among the most dedicated issue supporters, doubts play an important role in their experiences and may be either enabling or damaging, depending on the composition of their mixed selves. Our research offers a richer view of both how contexts shape social issue support and how individuals’ self-evaluations play a meaningful role in understanding the experiences and, ultimately, the issue-supportive behaviors of individuals working on social issues.

**Research Question**
The paper poses two emergent research questions in its own words: Study 1 asks "How do issue supporters' everyday experiences influence their self-evaluations?"; Study 2 asks "How do patterns of self-evaluations relate to levels of issue-supportive behaviors?"

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: issue support challenges (relational, cognitive, organizational); self-assets (value assets, knowledge assets, experience assets); self-doubts (doing questioning, doing constraints); situated self-work; mixed-self profiles (self-affirmers, self-equivocators, self-critics)
- Process / phases: supporters interpret issue support challenges across life domains (work, home, school); challenges prompt ongoing self-evaluation around two questions (Do I have the assets needed to be an issue supporter? How am I performing as an issue supporter?); the resulting mixed self-evaluations combine into profiles that relate to different levels of issue-supportive behavior
- Triggering conditions: complacency and skepticism of others (relational); the issue's scope, complexity, and interconnectivity (cognitive); getting organizational buy-in and the economic challenges of organizational support (organizational)
- Emergent outcomes: real issue-supportive behaviors (composting, interpersonal influence attempts, signing an Earth Hour pledge)

The induced theory of situated self-work holds that being an issue supporter confronts individuals with relational, cognitive, and organizational challenges in multiple contexts, prompting ongoing, effortful evaluation of the self. Supporters respond by claiming self-assets (values, knowledge, experience) that position them as capable of supporting the issue while simultaneously harboring self-doubts about the sufficiency of their actions. Study 2's motivation-based account suggests that self-assets foster self-concordance and self-efficacy; high self-doubts without accompanying assets may function as externally imposed burdens that decrease motivation, whereas low-level doubts combined with high assets might function as reminders of important goals and standards rather than as burdens.

**Data & Measures**
Study 1: 29 hour-long semi-structured interviews with self-identified climate change issue supporters (14 current students and 15 working alumni of the Environment and Business Program at a North American university), supplemented by field observations at two annual student gatherings (2006 and 2007) and program information; grounded-theory analysis moved from open codes to theoretical categories to three aggregate dimensions. Study 2: 91 environmental issue supporters recruited through 19 environmental groups in a major North American city; self-doubts measured with three items (e.g., "I don't do enough to be called a good environmentalist"; alpha = .88) and self-assets with nine items spanning knowledge, experience, and values (alpha = .88), validated in a 58-student pretest and a bootstrapped confirmatory factor analysis; issue-supportive behavior was the sum of three concealed-observation behaviors (composting leftover food, a written or verbal interpersonal influence attempt about the study's environmental practices, and signing a WWF Earth Hour pledge). Profiles were derived inductively with Ward's hierarchical cluster analysis, and behavior differences were tested with one-way ANOVA and Tukey post hoc comparisons; the design is observational and associational, not a causal-identification design.

**Key Findings**
Study 1 finds that informants interpret three categories of issue support challenges (relational, cognitive, organizational) and respond with mixed self-evaluations: positive self-assets (value, knowledge, and experience assets) and negative self-doubts (doing questioning, doing constraints), theorized together as situated self-work. Study 2's cluster analysis yielded three profiles: 38 self-affirmers (low self-doubts, high self-assets), 33 self-equivocators (high self-doubts, high self-assets), and 20 self-critics (high self-doubts, low self-assets). Self-affirmers engaged in the most issue-supportive behaviors (mean = 1.21 actions), followed by self-equivocators (mean = 0.61) and self-critics (mean = 0.45); the ANOVA was significant (F = 10.56, p < .01), with self-affirmers significantly higher than both self-critics (mean difference = 0.76, p < .01) and self-equivocators (mean difference = 0.60, p = .01), while the self-equivocator versus self-critic difference did not reach statistical significance. Even the most dedicated issue supporters had doubts, which the authors argue may be enabling or damaging depending on the composition of the mixed self.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper's theory of situated self-work offers an alternative to the outward-looking, image-risk-focused self of issue-selling research: supporters evaluate whether they have the assets to support an issue and how they are performing on it, rather than how others construe them. It documents a decidedly mixed self of simultaneous self-assets and self-doubts and, in contrast with self-affirmation theory, shows that individuals can generate self-assets in the same domain in which they perceive a threat, with no posited sequential order between doubts and assets. It also embeds self-processes in multiple contexts (work, home, school), explaining how contexts shape ongoing self-evaluations and joining scholarship on spillover between work and nonwork contexts.

**Practical Implication**
The findings help explain why it is difficult to be a social issue supporter and present guidance for strengthening social issue supporters, for whom burnout and depression are a key concern: because self-doubts question the self across multiple contexts, bolstering self-assets may not be a sufficient remedy, and supporters may also need to devise ways to mitigate their self-doubts. The authors also note that supporters can develop self-assets outside their work context, which may be particularly important for those who lack access to tangible resources or who sit in less politically legitimate parts of an organization, such as a corporate social responsibility department.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their studies raise, but do not resolve, the question of how mixed self-evaluations influence social issue support, with existing research split between an ambivalence-resolution view and a view of contradictory beliefs as a foundation for wisdom. They examined social issue support only vis-a-vis the natural environment, so it is not clear the findings will generalize to other social issues, and the profiles represent self-evaluations at a single point in time. Study 2 also faced statistical issues, including low power/sample size and restricted range on the dependent variable.

**Future Research**
The authors call for longitudinal research to unpack how self-evaluations change over time and relate to ongoing issue-supportive behaviors: whether individuals end up with more self-doubts as they burn out, accrue more self-assets as they capture small wins, or move through profiles as stages of working through an ambivalent self. They also note that self-evaluations induced by issue support challenges may vary for different kinds of social issues, and that additional empirical work can help disentangle the relationship between a mixed self and issue-supportive behaviors.
