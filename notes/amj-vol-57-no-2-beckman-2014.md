# Relational Pluralism in De Novo Organizations: Boards of Directors as Bridges or Barriers to Diverse Alliance Portfolios?

**Abstract**
This paper develops relational pluralism as a collective construct whose dimensions are heterogeneity, multiplexity, and asymmetry. Relational pluralism is instantiated in the board of directors, whose network of relationships influence a new venture’s ability to establish external links beyond the networks of the founding team. We argue that relational pluralism speeds the establishment of a diverse alliance portfolio, which, in turn, speeds the attainment of major revenue milestones in a new firm. We examine these ideas in a population of de novo semiconductor firms and find that diverse alliance portfolios emerge faster when a board includes members with heterogeneous, multiplex relationships, as well as central network positions. However, the asymmetric influence of outside board members can have both positive and negative effects: the alliance formation process is aided by outsiders in central network positions, but impeded when central investors dominate the board. We discuss implications for our understanding of relational pluralism as a collective construct.

**Research Question**
How does the board of directors, as a collective instantiation of relational pluralism, shape diverse alliance portfolio emergence among de novo technology organizations? The paper also asks whether forming a diverse alliance portfolio speeds attainment of major revenue milestones.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: The greater the heterogeneity of the board, as indicated by the diversity of board experiences, the greater the rate of diverse alliance portfolio emergence in de novo organizations.
- H2: The greater the multiplexity of the board, as indicated by board members' multiple types of ties, the higher the rate of diverse alliance portfolio emergence in de novo organizations.
- H3: The greater the asymmetry of the board, as indicated by the distribution of board member centrality, the lower the rate of diverse alliance portfolio emergence in de novo organizations.
- H4a: The greater the asymmetry of the board, with outside directors having dominance, the greater the rate of diverse alliance portfolio emergence in de novo organizations.
- H4b: The greater the asymmetry of the board, with investor board members having dominance, the lower the rate of diverse alliance portfolio emergence in de novo organizations.
- H5: Forming a diverse alliance portfolio will speed the attainment of important revenue milestones in a de novo organization.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Board heterogeneity, board multiplexity, overall and tie-type-specific board centrality asymmetry, and cumulative alliance portfolio diversity in the revenue models.
- DV(s): Rate of attaining a diverse alliance portfolio and rates of attaining US$10 million, US$20 million, and US$50 million annual-revenue milestones.
- Mediators: None tested.
- Moderators: None tested.

Heterogeneous board experiences broaden the information, expertise, and alliance opportunities available to a new venture, while multiplex ties deepen information and trust through multiple points of connection. The distribution of board-member centrality allocates attention and influence: central non-investor outsiders provide broad knowledge without discouraging attention to other members, whereas dominant investor directors may narrow alliance choices toward their own networks and preferred strategies. A diverse alliance portfolio is theorized to give the venture access to resources, know-how, innovative capabilities, and external legitimacy that help it attain revenue milestones more quickly.

**Data & Measures**
The study follows 104 de novo merchant semiconductor producers founded in the continental United States between January 1978 and December 1985. Structured interviews, supplementary financial forms, subsequent telephone waves, and archival research provide annual board measures, monthly alliance data through 1990, and annual revenue outcomes through 2002 or firm death. Diverse portfolio emergence is the time to obtaining three alliance types; an alternative specification uses a Blau diversity index, and the revenue models use the monthly cumulative number of alliance types. Board heterogeneity is the geometric mean of Blau indices for outside directors' regions, two-digit industries, and organization types; multiplexity sums investor, customer, and parent board ties; asymmetry uses board-member degree-centrality skewness and differences between the most central outside and investor directors. Semiparametric Cox event history models estimate time to portfolio emergence and to the US$10 million, US$20 million, and US$50 million revenue milestones.

**Key Findings**
- H1 was supported: board heterogeneity increased the rate of diverse alliance portfolio emergence (b = 1.587, p < .05; full model b = 1.500, p < .05).
- H2 was supported in its standalone model: board multiplexity increased portfolio-emergence rates (b = 0.560, p < .05), although the positive effect was only marginally significant in the full model (b = 0.517, p < .10).
- H3 received only partial support and not in the predicted simple direction. Overall centrality skewness was nonsignificant; negative skewness, reflecting multiple highly central members and few low-centrality members, increased portfolio-emergence rates (b = 3.148, p < .001; full model b = 2.702, p < .001), while positive skewness was nonsignificant.
- H4a was supported in Model 6: outside-director dominance increased portfolio-emergence rates (b = 62.060, p < .01), with the positive effect becoming marginally significant in the full model (b = 45.950, p < .10).
- H4b had the predicted negative direction but was nonsignificant in Model 6 (b = -28.680) and only marginally significant in the full model (b = -54.600, p < .10).
- H5 received milestone-specific support: alliance portfolio diversity increased the rate of attaining US$10 million in annual revenue (b = 0.236, p < .05), was positive but marginal for US$20 million (b = 0.229, p < .10), and was nonsignificant for US$50 million (b = 0.023).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper conceptualizes relational pluralism as a collective construct instantiated in the board of directors and develops heterogeneity, multiplexity, and asymmetry as its dimensions, adding asymmetry to prior formulations. It combines connectionist attention to tie content with structuralist attention to network position and power distribution, showing how board relationships shape both the emergence of diverse alliance portfolios and their revenue consequences in de novo organizations.

**Practical Implication**
For de novo ventures, the paper indicates that board composition and the balance of influence matter for developing diverse alliance portfolios: heterogeneous and multiplex experience and central non-investor outsiders can help, whereas dominance by central investor directors can impede portfolio formation. Early alliance portfolio formation is associated with reaching US$10 million and US$20 million revenue milestones more quickly, milestones the paper identifies as providing credibility to retain existing investor support and attract new investors.

**Limitations**
The average board contained only four members, with a maximum of ten, and almost half of the firms had no multiplex board ties, limiting empirical power. The single-industry setting and the concentration of 75 percent of firms in northern California leave open whether the dimensions and effects of relational pluralism depend on an industry's network structure, development stage, and geographic concentration.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine a larger collective to refine analysis of relational pluralism's dimensions and compare settings such as biotechnology, where alliances are more prevalent, and nanotechnology, where board networks may be sparse and span more knowledge domains. The paper also calls for examining how industry network structure, stage of development, geographic agglomeration, and region-specific network path dependencies condition relational pluralism.
