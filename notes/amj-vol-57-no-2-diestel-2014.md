# The Impact of Social Context on the Relationship Between Individual Job Satisfaction and Absenteeism: The Roles of Different Foci of Job Satisfaction and Work-Unit Absenteeism

**Abstract**
Building upon recent conceptualizations of different foci of job satisfaction and theories of social-contextual influence, we develop and test an integrative cross-level model of the individual relationships between both “externally focused satisfaction” (referring to job conditions) and “internally focused satisfaction” (referring to the work unit) and absenteeism. For both of these foci, we hypothesize differential three-way interactive effects of work-unit absenteeism patterns as characterized by their mean and dispersion levels, as well as individual satisfaction levels on subsequent individual absenteeism. Based on two German multi-level samples, our analyses demonstrate that the negative relationship between externally focused satisfaction and individual absenteeism is strongest in the presence of high mean and dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism, whereas this relationship is weaker when either the mean or dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism, or both, are low. In contrast, the negative relationship between internally focused satisfaction and individual absenteeism is strongest under conditions of low mean and dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism, whereas this relationship is weaker when either the mean or dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism, or both, are high. The present findings suggest that simultaneously improving individual internally focused satisfaction and reducing work-unit absenteeism is the most promising approach to reducing individual absenteeism.

**Research Question**
How do the mean and dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism jointly moderate the individual relationships between externally and internally focused job satisfaction and subsequent individual absenteeism?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: The mean and dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism jointly moderate the negative relationship between externally focused job satisfaction and individual absenteeism: the relationship is strongest when both work-unit absenteeism mean and dispersion are high, and weaker when either or both are low.
- H2: The mean and dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism jointly moderate the negative relationship between internally focused job satisfaction and individual absenteeism: the relationship is strongest when both work-unit absenteeism mean and dispersion are low, and weaker when either or both are high.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Externally focused job satisfaction and internally focused job satisfaction.
- DV(s): Subsequent individual short-term absence frequency.
- Mediators: Not tested; the paper theorizes instrumental or calculative responses for externally focused satisfaction and social-adjustive alignment or withdrawal for internally focused satisfaction.
- Moderators: Mean and dispersion levels of work-unit absenteeism, modeled jointly as cross-level contextual conditions.

Externally focused satisfaction concerns job conditions, management, and career opportunities and serves an instrumental function. When work-unit absence mean and dispersion are both high, the context is weak and tolerant enough for employees' own external dissatisfaction to translate into more absence; when absence patterns are uniform, employees instead align with the unit's absence level. Internally focused satisfaction concerns colleagues and supervisors and indicates social integration, so satisfied employees tend to align with work-unit absence patterns, whereas dissatisfied employees tend to withdraw. Accordingly, the negative internal satisfaction-absence relationship is predicted to be strongest when work-unit absence mean and dispersion are both low.

**Data & Measures**
Study 1 included 412 health-care employees in 63 work units at a German residential elderly care organization; Study 2 included 330 employees in 53 work units at a German tax and revenue organization. Externally focused satisfaction was measured with three items on working conditions, organization and management, and career opportunities; internally focused satisfaction was measured with two items on colleagues and the work-unit supervisor. Archival absence frequency counted short-term absence events of fewer than four consecutive workdays during the six months before and after the survey; work-unit absence mean and dispersion were the aggregated mean and within-unit standard deviation of pre-survey absence frequency. The authors estimated HLM cross-level interaction models, controlling for prior individual absenteeism, health disorders, demographics, team size, and local unemployment; the longitudinal design remained correlational.

**Key Findings**
Study 1 supported both hypotheses. For H1, externally focused satisfaction was negatively related to subsequent absence only when work-unit absence mean and dispersion were both high (γ = -0.34, p < .01); for H2, internally focused satisfaction was negatively related to absence only when both were low (γ = -0.42, p < .01), with the other simple slopes nonsignificant. Study 2 strongly supported H1: the high-mean, high-dispersion slope was negative and significant (γ = -0.43, p < .01), while the other slopes were nonsignificant. H2 was not fully supported in Study 2 because the three-way interaction was nonsignificant; internal satisfaction was negatively related to absence when work-unit absence mean was low (γ = -0.20, p < .01) but not when it was high (γ = 0.01, n.s.), and dispersion did not moderate this relationship.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a cross-level, context-based account of when job satisfaction predicts individual absenteeism by combining satisfaction focus with the mean and dispersion of work-unit absence. It shows that contextual strength has different implications for an instrumental attitude toward job conditions and a social-adjustive attitude toward the work unit. This distinction extends social information processing arguments by identifying attitude function and work-unit behavioral patterns as joint boundary conditions of the satisfaction-absenteeism relationship.

**Practical Implication**
The authors recommend that HR practices simultaneously increase internally focused satisfaction and reduce work-unit absenteeism. Managers should foster social integration and supportive relationships among teammates and supervisors while using group-level practices such as clear supervisory instructions, formalized attendance procedures, feedback systems, or unit-level incentives to control work-unit absence patterns.

**Limitations**
Although the data were longitudinal, the design was correlational and did not permit strong causal inferences. The satisfaction measure covered only five job aspects, the absence measure did not explicitly distinguish voluntary from involuntary or excused from unexcused cases, and unmeasured confounding remained possible. The studies also did not test the proposed mediating processes or directly measure the unit-level mechanisms producing work-unit absenteeism.

**Future Research**
Future studies should model changes in satisfaction and absenteeism over time and develop more explicit measures of satisfaction components and attitudinal functions. They should test mediating processes such as calculative judgment and social identification, distinguish voluntary from involuntary and excused from unexcused absences, and consider further relevant controls. The paper also calls for research that integrates behavioral patterns at higher structural levels when explaining relationships between job attitudes and individual behavior.
