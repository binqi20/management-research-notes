**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: the defining feature of big data is its fine-grained nature rather than dataset size alone, enabling granular analysis of individual actions and behaviors.
- Data sources: public data, private data, data exhaust, community data, and self-quantification data.
- Analytical progression: identify correlational patterns, use theory to develop causal arguments, test those arguments in subsequent research, ideally through field experiments, and seek consilience across independent sources.
- Evidence standards: emphasize effect sizes, variance explained, and informative outliers rather than relying on p values that become unhelpful in extremely large datasets.
- Enabling conditions: link data-sharing agreements to data protection and privacy mechanisms, including anonymization, access control, rights management, and data usage control.

The editorial presents big data as fine-grained, high-volume information that can expose variables, relationships, and contextual conditions unavailable in conventional management datasets. Because most big data are unstructured and not designed to establish causality, observed correlations can have multiple causal explanations. The proposed progression combines theory-guided explanation, subsequent field experimentation, and convergence of evidence from independent sources, while data-sharing and privacy arrangements make access possible.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial reframes big data from a question of dataset size to one of fine-grained information and develops an agenda for its use in management scholarship. It argues that such data can reveal overlooked variables, relationships, boundary conditions, microfoundations, and dynamic organizational processes, while theory-guided tests and consilience can strengthen conclusions drawn from initially correlational patterns.

**Practical Implication**
Management scholars entering big-data research should integrate data sharing and privacy into their research methodology, emphasize effect sizes, variance explained, and outliers rather than p values alone, and use theory followed by field experiments to investigate causality. For scholars and managers interested in action research, the editorial presents "management engineering" as an opportunity to shorten the cycle between identifying a promising theoretical insight and testing it through a well-designed intervention.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the editorial is neither a systematic review of big-data applications nor a definitive guide for future research. Specialized analytical techniques are beyond its scope, and the authors caution that unstructured big data are generally not designed for causal identification and can support multiple possible causal explanations.

**Future Research**
The editorial calls for research questions that integrate data sharing and privacy into research methodology, with the aim of refining future models of data sharing and data rights. It urges scholars to use theory to explain causal processes and test those explanations in subsequent research, ideally through field experiments, and to examine how ubiquitous data generate value, the routes through which value is created, and how that value is captured and apportioned. It also identifies opportunities to study microfoundations, organizational processes, individual and team behavior, and network dynamics using granular, near-real-time data.
