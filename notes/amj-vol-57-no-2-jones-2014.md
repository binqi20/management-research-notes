# Why Are Job Seekers Attracted by Corporate Social Performance? Experimental and Field Tests of Three Signal-Based Mechanisms

**Abstract**
Research on employee recruitment has shown that an organization’s corporate social performance (CSP) affects its attractiveness as an employer, but the underlying mechanisms and processes through which this occurs are poorly understood. We propose that job seekers receive signals from CSP that inform three signal-based mechanisms that ultimately affect organizational attractiveness: job seekers’ anticipated pride from being affiliated with the organization, their perceived value fit with the organization, and their expectations about how the organization treats its employees. We hypothesized that these signal-based mechanisms mediate the relationships between CSP and organizational attractiveness, focusing on two aspects of CSP: an organization’s community involvement and pro-environmental practices. In an experiment (n ⴝ 180), we manipulated CSP via a company’s web pages. In a field study (n ⴝ 171), we measured CSP content in the recruitment materials used by organizations at a job fair and job seekers’ perceptions of the organizations’ CSP. Results provided support for the signal-based mechanisms, and we discuss the implications for theory, future research, and practice.

**Research Question**
Why do an organization’s community involvement and pro-environmental practices increase its attractiveness to job seekers, and are these relationships explained by anticipated pride or organizational prestige, perceived value fit, and expectations about favorable employee treatment?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1: The positive relationships between CSP pertaining to (a) community involvement and (b) pro-environmental practices and organizational attractiveness are mediated by anticipated pride (Study 1) and beliefs about organizational prestige (Study 2).
H2: The positive relationships between CSP pertaining to (a) community involvement and (b) pro-environmental practices and organizational attractiveness are mediated by perceived value fit.
H3a: An organization that engages in community involvement is more attractive to individuals higher in communal orientation, and this relationship is mediated by perceived value fit.
H3b: An organization that engages in pro-environmental practices is more attractive to individuals with stronger pro-environmental attitudes, and this relationship is mediated by perceived value fit.
H4: The positive relationships between CSP pertaining to (a) community involvement and (b) pro-environmental practices and organizational attractiveness are mediated by expectations about favorable employee treatment.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CSP pertaining to community involvement and pro-environmental practices, operationalized as experimentally presented web-page content (Study 1), job seekers’ perceived CSP, and independent ratings of CSP content in recruitment materials (Study 2)
- DV(s): Organizational attractiveness
- Mediators: Anticipated pride (Study 1) or organizational prestige (Study 2), perceived value fit, and expected treatment
- Moderators: Communal orientation for community involvement and pro-environmental attitudes for pro-environmental practices as individual-difference contingencies in H3; no interaction term was reported

CSP sends job seekers signals about organizational prestige, organizational values, and prosocial orientation. These signals respectively inform anticipated pride in affiliation, perceived fit between personal and organizational values, and expectations that the organization treats employees favorably; those inferences then shape organizational attractiveness. H3 further proposes that CSP is especially attractive when the relevant personal orientation or attitude aligns with the organization’s community or environmental practices, with perceived value fit transmitting that relationship.

**Data & Measures**
Study 1 was a longitudinal experiment with 180 senior undergraduates at a Western Canadian university. Participants completed two sessions one week apart and were randomly assigned in equal groups to review a fictitious clothing company’s web pages containing community-involvement CSP, pro-environmental CSP, or no CSP information. Communal orientation used Clark et al.’s 14-item measure; pro-environmental attitudes used six adapted items; anticipated pride used three Cable and Turban items; perceived value fit used three Cable and DeRue items; expected treatment used five author-created items; and organizational attractiveness used Highhouse et al.’s five-item measure. Analyses included CFA, ANOVA and contrasts, regression-based multiple mediation with 5,000 bootstrap samples, and a blind content analysis of employment-choice rationales.

Study 2 surveyed 171 active job seekers at two three-hour job fairs held at a Northeastern U.S. university in November 2008 and March 2009; 153 cases had independent recruitment-material ratings. A research assistant rated community and environmental CSP content on four-point scales, while job seekers completed four-item seven-point perceived-CSP measures. Organizational prestige used a five-item Highhouse et al. measure, and perceived value fit, expected treatment, and organizational attractiveness used the Study 1 measures. Bootstrap multiple-mediator models controlled for familiarity with the organization and the other CSP dimension; this field design was associational, whereas Study 1 supported causal inference from the randomized CSP manipulation.

**Key Findings**
In Study 1, organizational attractiveness was significantly higher in both CSP conditions than in the no-CSP condition. H1a/H1b and H2a/H2b were supported: unique indirect effects through anticipated pride were .47 for community involvement and .27 for pro-environmental practices, and those through perceived value fit were .22 and .21, respectively. H4a/H4b were not supported in the simultaneous three-mediator models (expected-treatment indirect effects were .01 and −.02), although post hoc analyses found both expected-treatment effects significant when anticipated pride was removed; H3a/H3b were supported, with indirect effects through perceived value fit of .34 and .11.

In Study 2, both perceived community CSP and independently rated community CSP in recruitment materials had significant total indirect effects on organizational attractiveness (.20 and .17) and significant unique indirect effects through organizational prestige, perceived value fit, and expected treatment, supporting H1a, H2a, and H4a. For environmental CSP, none of the indirect effects through the three mediators was significant for either CSP operationalization, so H1b, H2b, and H4b were not supported in Study 2 despite significant correlations and some significant component paths.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops and tests a signal-based account of why CSP attracts job seekers by specifying the inferences that connect CSP signals to organizational attractiveness rather than treating mediation as assumed. It distinguishes signals about prestige, values, and prosocial orientation from the mechanisms they inform—anticipated pride, perceived value fit, and expected treatment—and provides complementary experimental and field evidence across three CSP operationalizations. The contrast between community involvement and pro-environmental practices also shows that signal-based effects depend on the nature and co-occurrence of CSP practices.

**Practical Implication**
Companies may benefit from communicating CSP initiatives through websites, recruitment materials, and recruiters because job seekers must be aware of CSP before receiving its signals. The paper specifically suggests promoting external CSP recognition to signal prestige, explaining links between company values and CSP practices to inform value fit, and explicitly linking CSP to concern for employees to inform expected treatment.

**Limitations**
The relatively young participants may limit generalizability, and the studies address early recruitment stages, so whether the findings extend to later stages remains unknown. In Study 1, the no-CSP web pages contained less information and fewer positive statements than the CSP pages, making information amount and positivity alternative explanations; analogous differences could also affect Study 2. In the field study, substantial co-occurrence of community and environmental CSP increased the potential for suppression when both dimensions were modeled together, particularly for environmental CSP.

**Future Research**
The authors call for tests of whether the effects differ for older job seekers, persist into later recruitment stages or after hiring, and depend on prior knowledge about the employer. They recommend examining other signals from CSP, whether more people-oriented CSP practices produce stronger expected-treatment signals, and boundary conditions involving social-approval needs, internalized CSP values, fairness sensitivity, and attributions of disingenuous motives. They also propose assessing whether CSP changes applicant-pool size and quality by measuring general mental ability, conscientiousness, and other predictors of job performance.
