# Reputation and Decision Making under Ambiguity: A Study of U.S. Venture Capital Firms' Investments in the Emerging Clean Energy Sector

**Abstract**
This study examines the role of reputation on decision making under ambiguity. Drawing on social cognition and behavioral theories, we propose that a firm’s reputation exerts dual pressures on its decision making under ambiguity. On the one hand, a firm’s reputation increases its aspirations for future performance and promotes its engagement in risky strategies to achieve them. On the other hand, preserving the already established reputation requires a firm to deliver consistent performance over time, which promotes greater use of risk reduction strategies. Our analyses of the U.S. venture capital firms’ investments in the clean energy sector from 1990 to 2008 demonstrate that while reputable firms are more likely to invest in the emerging sector, they also employ risk reduction strategies more extensively. The sector’s legitimation further influences these firms’ investment decisions both directly and through its interaction with firm reputation.

**Research Question**
How does a firm’s reputation affect its decision making under ambiguity through pressures to pursue risky opportunities and to preserve consistent performance, and how does the legitimation of an emerging sector directly and interactively shape these decisions? The study examines these questions in U.S. VC firms’ investment decisions concerning the emerging clean energy sector.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1: The higher the reputation of a VC firm, the higher the likelihood that it will invest in an emerging sector.
H2: The higher the reputation of a VC firm, the greater its use of (a) syndication and (b) later-stage investment, and (c) the lower its levels of commitment when investing in an emerging sector.
H3: Sector legitimation will increase the likelihood of a VC firm investing in an emerging sector.
H4: Sector legitimation will reduce the use of (a) syndication, (b) later-stage investment, and (c) lower levels of commitment among VC firms investing in an emerging sector.
H5: Sector legitimation will strengthen the relationship between a VC firm’s reputation and its likelihood to invest in an emerging sector.
H6: Sector legitimation will weaken the relationship between a VC firm’s reputation and its use of (a) syndication, (b) later-stage investment, and (c) lower levels of commitment when investing in an emerging sector.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): VC firm reputation; sector legitimation, comprising media attention, regulatory approval, and prior investments by peers
- DV(s): Likelihood of investing in the emerging clean energy sector; use of syndication; use of later-stage investment; level of commitment to clean energy
- Mediators: Higher performance aspirations and lower perceived barriers to exploration are theorized to promote entry, while concern for consistent performance and reputation damage is theorized to promote risk reduction; these mechanisms are not directly measured as mediators
- Moderators: Sector legitimation moderates the relationships between VC firm reputation and both sector entry and the three risk-reduction outcomes

Reputation creates dual pressures under ambiguity. High stakeholder expectations raise reputable VC firms’ aspirations, while reputation also supplies resources and access to startups, making these firms more likely to interpret an emerging sector as an opportunity and invest in it. At the same time, the greater reputational cost of being wrong makes reputable firms manage exposure through syndication, later-stage investment, and lower commitment. Sector legitimation attracts attention, supplies information and structure, and provides social proof, thereby increasing entry and altering how reputation relates to both entry and risk reduction.

**Data & Measures**
The archival data cover the population of 3,574 U.S.-based VC firms active from 1990 through 2008, yielding 37,293 firm-year observations for the sector-entry analysis; a second panel follows the 172 firms that invested in 181 clean energy startups, yielding 709 firm-years for the risk-reduction analyses. Investment data came from VentureXpert, with sector data from the Earth Policy Institute, U.S. Department of Energy, LexisNexis Academic, and DSIRE; 13 semi-structured exploratory interviews with clean energy VC investors informed the characterization of ambiguity. Entry equals whether a focal firm first invested in a clean energy startup; syndication is the average number of co-investment partners across clean energy rounds; later-stage investment is the average stage of the clean energy startups funded; and commitment is the number of clean energy startups funded divided by all startups funded by the VC firm in that year. Reputation is a annually updated composite of five standardized five-year indicators—portfolio companies, amount invested, funds raised, number of funds, and portfolio-company IPOs—transformed to a 100-point scale. Sector legitimation combines standardized media attention, federal financial incentives, and cumulative peer investments (Cronbach’s alpha = .97). The authors use observational longitudinal panel models with one-year-lagged predictors, random effects, time-period dummies, and a two-stage selection correction; the design is not a natural or randomized experiment.

**Key Findings**
H1 was supported: VC firm reputation had a positive and significant effect on entry, with a one-standard-deviation increase in reputation multiplying the odds of clean energy investment by 1.25. H2a, H2b, and H2c were supported: reputation positively predicted syndication and later-stage investment and negatively predicted level of commitment, indicating greater use of lower commitment as a risk-reduction strategy. H3 was supported: a one-standard-deviation increase in sector legitimation multiplied the odds of entry by 2.33. H4a was not supported because sector legitimation had no significant effect on syndication; H4b was not supported and the result ran contrary to its prediction because legitimation positively affected later-stage investment; H4c was supported because legitimation positively affected level of commitment, thereby reducing the use of lower commitment. H5 was supported: sector legitimation positively strengthened the reputation–entry relationship. H6a and H6b were not supported and ran contrary to prediction because the interactions of legitimation with reputation were positive and significant for syndication and later-stage investment; H6c was not supported because the interaction for commitment was not significant.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study moves beyond the self-reinforcing reputation–performance relationship by showing that, under ambiguity, reputation simultaneously promotes risky exploration and more extensive risk reduction rather than only conservative persistence. It identifies aspirations and concern for reputation preservation as mechanisms producing these dual pressures and distinguishes decision making under ambiguity from decision making under uncertainty. It also shows that sector legitimation does not affect all firms uniformly: its influence on investment strategies depends on firm reputation, extending reputation, legitimacy, and VC decision-making research.

**Practical Implication**
In the clean energy setting, the authors argue that reputable VC firms’ ability to legitimate new activity is tempered by extensive use of risk-reduction strategies; given the sector’s large capital requirements, small-scale commitments may be inadequate to accelerate its development. They state that policymakers and clean energy protagonists seeking faster sector development may need to identify alternative funding sources or find ways to encourage greater involvement by the VC community.

**Limitations**
The VC reputation index aggregates firm-level data and does not capture partners’ individual reputations, which could confound firm and individual reputation in other samples even though excluding the two such cases identified here did not change the results. The study does not analyze VC firms’ choices among syndication partners, and the small number of reputable clean energy investors prevents conclusions about the roles of prospective partners’ reputation and sector experience. The observation window ends in 2008 before a major policy-induced change in the sector, and generalizability may be limited because VC firms routinely take risks, make a restricted set of investment decisions, and are knowledge-intensive organizations.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on whether reputable firms’ ability to recognize institutional change produces long-term performance benefits, on how reputation and experience differ under uncertainty versus ambiguity, and on the rationales, benefits, drawbacks, and combined use of syndication, later-stage investment, and lower commitment. They recommend examining confounding between individual and firm reputations, as well as the sector-specific experience and reputation of prospective syndication partners. They also propose using the post-2008 institutional shock as a natural-experiment opportunity to study new clean energy investment models and subsequent investment trajectories, and testing the identified mechanisms in less knowledge-intensive contexts.
