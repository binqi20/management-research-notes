# Turnkey or Tailored? Relational Pluralism, Institutional Complexity, and the Organizational Adoption of More or Less Customized Practices

**Abstract**
We examine how the organizational adoption of new practices is influenced by relational pluralism, i.e., an organization’s multiple ties to actors inside and outside its industry. We theorize that institutional mechanisms of practice diffusion underlying relational networks and filtered by organizational characteristics influence the adoption of practices that are more customized (tailored) or less customized (turnkey). We hypothesize, first, that organizations participating in extra-industry professional networks will, through normative conformity, be more likely to adopt turnkey practices; second, that the normative pressure of professional networks will interact with the mimeticism of industry peers such that organizations will be more likely to adopt tailored practices; and third, that organizational filters will affect adoption of all practices. Using unique survey data from 161 Fortune 500 organizations, supplemented by archival and qualitative data, we focus on two corporate social responsibility practice variants. We find significant support for our first two hypotheses and mixed support for our third: Organizational infrastructure and identity significantly affect practice adoption, albeit in different ways, but only marginal support for leadership and elite organizational status. Our results point to how a complex web of relational ties affects the organizational adoption of practice variants that differ in their degree of customization.

**Research Question**
How do organizations’ ties to extra-industry professional networks and industry peers, together with organizational filters, influence whether firms adopt less customized turnkey practices or more customized tailored practices?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: Organizational adoption of a turnkey practice will be positively affected by participation in an extra-industry network that exposes the organization to the practice.
- H2: Organizational adoption of a tailored practice will be positively affected by the interaction between participation in an extra-industry professional network that normatively sanctions the practice and the practice’s intra-industry prevalence, which legitimates customized adaptation.
- H3: Organizational adoption of a CSR practice will be positively influenced by organizational infrastructure, governance and leadership involvement, organizational identity, and the organization’s elite standing in its field.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Participation in an extra-industry CSR practice network; intra-industry diffusion of the same practice; their interaction; and organizational infrastructure, governance and leadership involvement, organizational identity, and elite field position.
- DV(s): Binary adoption of any employee volunteering program (EVP), a turnkey day of service (DOS) program, and a tailored skills-based volunteering (SBV) program.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: The interaction between practice-network participation and intra-industry practice diffusion for tailored SBV adoption.

Professional networks expose firms to normatively sanctioned practices and ready-made templates, which the paper argues is sufficient for adoption of the less customized DOS practice. Tailored SBV adoption instead depends on relational pluralism: normative pressure from the professional network combines with mimetic legitimation from industry peers to support customized adaptation. Organizational filters shape firms’ receptivity to these pressures, with infrastructure and identity playing different roles across practice variants.

**Data & Measures**
- Data and design: A 2008 survey of senior executives responsible for CSR at 161 Fortune 500 firms (33% response rate), supplemented by public archives, interviews with 12 expert informants, a focus group with 12 community-involvement directors, and CSR-conference observations.
- Outcomes: Three dummy variables coded adoption as 1 and nonadoption as 0 for any EVP, turnkey DOS, and tailored SBV.
- Relational predictors: Industry diffusion was the percentage of firms in the focal firm’s two-digit SIC industry adopting the same practice and was mean-centered. Practice-network participation indicated attendance at one or both CECP and BCCCC annual conferences; H2 used its interaction with industry diffusion.
- Institutional filters: Infrastructure summed four binary items on written CSR processes and standards (Cronbach’s alpha = 0.72); governance and leadership involvement indicated public CSR-event participation by at least 30% of the top two management levels; identity was GRI reporting; and elite standing was Fortune 100 membership in 2008.
- Analysis: Separate associational logit models in STATA 12 predicted EVP, DOS, and SBV adoption, with controls for explicit business benefit, organizational age, and whether EVP practices were based only in the United States.

**Key Findings**
- H1 was supported: Participation in the CSR practice network positively predicted turnkey DOS adoption (b = 0.78, p < .05), while industry diffusion was not significant.
- H2 was supported: The practice-network-by-industry-diffusion interaction positively predicted tailored SBV adoption (b = 0.16, p < .05). The corresponding interaction was not significant for any EVP or for DOS.
- H3 received partial support. Infrastructure positively predicted all three outcomes (EVP b = 0.55, p < .05; DOS b = 0.38, p < .05; SBV b = 0.61, p < .01). Identity positively predicted EVP (b = 1.19, p < .05) and SBV (b = 0.93, p < .05), but not DOS. Governance and leadership involvement positively predicted any EVP (b = 2.76, p < .01), was only marginal for DOS (b = 0.79, p < .10), and was not significant for SBV. Elite field position was marginally negative for EVP (b = -1.20, p < .10) and nonsignificant for DOS and SBV, so it did not support H3’s predicted positive direction.

**Theoretical Contribution**
By distinguishing turnkey from tailored practice variants, the study shows that relational ties do not operate uniformly: professional-network participation predicts turnkey adoption, whereas tailored adoption depends on the joint normative and mimetic pressures created by relational pluralism. It integrates institutional, network, and innovation accounts by showing how organizational embeddedness in practice and industry fields, together with organizational filters, produces heterogeneous responses to institutional complexity.

**Practical Implication**
CSR practitioners should consider both internal organizational capabilities and external relational influences when deciding whether to adopt emerging CSR practices. The paper specifically highlights managers’ active involvement in CSR professional conferences and communities of practice as relevant to EVP adoption and to decisions about when to devote organizational resources to a new practice.

**Limitations**
The study examines 161 Fortune 500 firms, so adoption patterns may differ for smaller, younger, or less prestigious firms. Elite standing is measured through Fortune 100 membership based on revenue, and the authors note that more fine-grained subjective or qualitative status measures may reveal different effects. The 2008 cross-sectional design does not examine temporal waves or the leading and lagging effects of relational ties and institutional filters.

**Future Research**
The authors call for time-series research on temporal waves of practice diffusion and the leading or lagging effects of relational ties and institutional filters. They also propose distinguishing leaders who act as knowledgeable practice champions from symbolic figureheads and examining how internal and external audiences construe organizational identity in relation to practice adoption. Further work should test smaller, younger, or less prestigious firms and other organizational fields, using more fine-grained measures of elite status.
