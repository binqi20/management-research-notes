# Do Ties Really Bind? The Effect of Knowledge and Commercialization Networks on Opposition to Standards

**Abstract**
We examine how the multiplicity of interorganizational relationships affects strategic behavior by studying the influence of two such relationships—knowledge linkages and commercialization ties—on the voting behavior of firms in a technological standards-setting committee. We find that, while centrally positioned firms in the knowledge network exhibit lower opposition to the standard, centrally positioned firms in the commercialization network exhibit higher opposition to the standard. Thus, the influence of network position on coordination is contingent upon the type of interorganizational tie. Furthermore, when we consider these relationships jointly, knowledge centrality moderates the opposing effect of commercialization centrality, such that the commercialization centrality effect increases with decreasing levels of knowledge centrality. In other words, firms most likely to delay the standard are peripheral in the knowledge network yet central in the commercialization network, which suggests that they have the most to lose from changes to current technology.

**Research Question**
How do firms’ positions in knowledge and commercialization networks independently and jointly influence their opposition to a coordinated technological standard?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1: The more central a focal firm is within the knowledge network of member firms, the lower its opposition to the standard.

H2: The more central a focal firm is within the commercialization network of member firms, the higher its opposition to the standard.

H3: Knowledge network centrality negatively moderates the effect of commercialization network centrality on opposition to the coordinated standard; the more peripheral the firm’s knowledge network position, the greater the positive effect of commercialization centrality on opposition.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Knowledge network centrality; commercialization network centrality
- DV(s): Firm’s level of opposition to the coordinated standard, expressed through its ballot vote
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Knowledge network centrality negatively moderates the effect of commercialization network centrality on opposition

Knowledge network centrality indicates that a firm’s knowledge is foundational to other committee members and therefore likely to be reflected in the emerging standard, reducing its opposition. Commercialization network centrality captures a firm’s ability to appropriate rents from complementary ties built around current technologies; a system-wide interoperability standard can devalue those investments, increasing opposition. Consequently, firms peripheral in the knowledge network but central in the commercialization network have the most to lose and are most likely to delay the standard.

**Data & Measures**
The study combines INCITS archival records with alliance announcements from Factiva, NBER patent-citation data, Derwent patent data, and firm data from Compustat, Hoover’s, and Corptech. The dependent variable is a firm’s ballot vote, coded 0 for yes, 1 for abstain, 2 for yes with comments or conditions, and 3 for no, so higher values indicate greater opposition.

Knowledge network centrality is the weighted degree centrality of a firm in a five-year moving network of knowledge-generating alliances; an alternative measure uses in-degree centrality in a five-year patent cross-citation network. Commercialization network centrality is weighted degree centrality in a five-year moving network of alliances involving existing technologies, including interoperability, marketing, licensing, and production agreements. The authors estimate ordered logistic regressions at the firm-vote level with one-year-lagged predictors, firm and year fixed effects, and ballot fixed effects in the fully specified models; the design is observational rather than experimental.

**Key Findings**
H1 was supported: knowledge network centrality was negatively associated with opposition, and a one-unit increase decreased the odds of not voting yes by 0.41 times. H2 was supported: commercialization network centrality was positively associated with opposition, and a one-unit increase increased the odds of not voting yes by 1.85 times.

H3 was supported: the interaction between knowledge and commercialization network centrality was negative and significant, so the positive commercialization-centrality effect increased as knowledge centrality decreased. The likelihood of not voting yes was approximately three times higher for a firm central in the commercialization network and peripheral in the knowledge network than for a firm with the reverse positions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study shows that network-position effects depend on tie function: knowledge and commercialization centrality have opposing, interacting, and nonadditive effects on firms’ standards-setting conduct. Pooling the two alliance types yields no discernible centrality effect because their influences counterbalance, demonstrating why relational pluralism and alliance network multiplicity must be modeled explicitly. It further shows that central network positions can either promote or impede technological coordination depending on the network, industry context, and strategic behavior examined.

**Practical Implication**
In voluntary technology standards committees, firms peripheral in the knowledge network but central in the commercialization network are most likely to cast opposing votes because they have the most to lose from changes to current technology. Their opposition can delay the standard and allow them to continue exploiting current network positions.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that the findings may be particular to a systemic industry in which substantial technological competition occurs within a standards forum, limiting their scope. Opposition to a standard is also a context-specific behavioral dependent variable, so the findings should not be assumed to generalize to other outcomes or settings.

**Future Research**
Future research should incorporate other forms of relational pluralism, including interpersonal networks formed through mobility or common institutional affiliations, and assess differentiation within and commonality across tie classifications. The authors also call for extensions to other industries and context-specific dependent variables, including studies of diverging votes or changing committee participation among longstanding alliance partners.
