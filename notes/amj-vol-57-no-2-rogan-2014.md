# Executive Departures Without Client Losses: The Role of Multiplex Ties in Exchange Partner Retention

**Abstract**
To reduce vulnerability to exchange relationship loss when executives leave, firms often form multiple ties to the same exchange partners. Despite the assumed importance of interorganizational multiplexity for relationship retention, theory and evidence of its effect are lacking. Analysis of a longitudinal sample of client ties of advertising firms confirms that, in general, multiplexity improves retention. However, only relationships that span intraorganizational units with convergent interests reduce the positive effect of advertising agency executive departures on client tie loss. The findings highlight the need to consider the implications of intraorganizational structure for theories of interorganizational relationship retention, and suggest an additional rationale for the persistence of the holding company structure in professional services firms despite limited returns to scale.

**Research Question**
How do within-agency and across-agency multiplex ties affect agency-client tie dissolution, and when do across-agency multiplex ties reduce the positive effect of executive departures on client loss? Specifically, how do the distribution of control and the convergence of interests among intraorganizational units condition relationship retention?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1: The likelihood of agency-client tie dissolution decreases the more the tie is embedded in within-agency multiplex ties.
H2: The likelihood of agency-client tie dissolution decreases the more the tie is embedded in across-agency multiplex ties.
H3: The positive effect of executive departures on tie dissolution decreases the more the tie is embedded in across-agency multiplex ties.
H4: The negative interaction effect of across-agency multiplex ties and executive departures on tie dissolution increases with the proportion of non-competitive ties.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Proportion of agency executives departing; within-agency multiplex tie density; across-agency multiplex tie density; proportion of non-competitive across-agency ties
- DV(s): Annual dissolution of an agency-client account tie
- Mediators: Not measured; enforcement of behavioral norms and learning across ties are the theorized mechanisms
- Moderators: Across-agency multiplex ties moderate the executive departure-tie dissolution relationship; the proportion of non-competitive ties conditions that interaction

Multiplex ties are theorized to improve retention through reputational enforcement of behavioral norms and through learning and information transfer across ties. Within-agency multiplex ties combine convergent interests with concentrated control, so they can stabilize ties generally but do not insulate the relationship from executive departures. Across-agency ties distribute control, reducing individual executives' influence and bargaining power; when those ties connect non-competing agencies, convergent interests also permit learning across units. Ties spanning competing agencies lack this convergence, limiting their additional stabilizing effect when executives depart.

**Data & Measures**
The study uses archival data on client relationships following 10 advertising-firm mergers completed in 2000, yielding 108 agencies. Mergers came from SDC Platinum; agency characteristics, executives, and client relationships came from REDBOOKS; and client information came from Bureau Van Dijk's Amadeus, Osiris, and Orbis databases. The final analysis contains 2,313 relationships observed over 2000-2003, or 4,117 dyad-years. Tie dissolution equals one in the first year an agency no longer lists the client account in REDBOOKS. Executive departures equal the number of previously listed executives absent from the current-year listing divided by the prior executive count. Within-agency multiplexity is the number of accounts an agency has with a client relative to its total accounts, excluding the focal tie; across-agency multiplexity is the density of possible ties to the same client across agencies in the parent firm. The proportion of non-competitive ties is the share of across-agency multiplex ties involving agencies in the same network. The multiplexity measures are logged, interaction components are mean-centered, and hypotheses are tested with discrete-time complementary log-log hazard models, merger fixed effects, and dyad-clustered robust standard errors. The postmerger design reduces the relative influence of general client dissatisfaction but is associational rather than a causal-identification design; robustness checks address client-industry heterogeneity and selection into merger.

**Key Findings**
Executive departures are positively and significantly related to tie dissolution. H1 is supported: within-agency multiplex ties have a negative, significant association with dissolution (Model 4: b = -0.11, p < .05). H2 and H3 are only conditionally supported: their constituent effects are significant in Models 2 and 3, respectively, but lose significance in Model 4 because across-agency multiplex ties reduce dissolution, including the increase associated with executive departures, only when the ties connect non-competing agencies. H4 is supported by a negative, significant three-way interaction (b = -73.96, p < .05); when executive departures and non-competitive ties are high, a one-standard-deviation increase in across-agency multiplex ties decreases the predicted probability of tie dissolution by 80%, whereas the effect is negligible when non-competitive ties are low. The within-agency multiplexity-executive departures interaction is not significant. Most robustness analyses reproduce the main pattern, but the agency-level aggregation did not reproduce moderation by the proportion of non-competitive ties. The proposed alternative in which multiplexity reduces client loss by preventing executive departures is rejected as a mediation explanation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study relaxes the assumption that firms in multiplex relationships are unitary actors, showing that the distribution of control across intraorganizational units and the convergence or divergence of those units' interests determine whether multiplexity stabilizes exchange. It conceptualizes relationships spanning units as multiplex hybrids between dyads and triads: non-competitive hybrids combine distributed control with convergent interests, whereas competitive hybrids behave more like multiplex triads. The findings extend research on executive mobility by identifying firm-level multiplex bonds that reduce dependence on particular individuals and offer a relational-retention rationale for holding-company structures in professional services.

**Practical Implication**
For advertising firms, multiple client ties spread across non-competing agencies can reduce vulnerability to client-account loss when executives leave, because control is distributed while unit interests remain convergent. The paper extends this implication only to firms with comparable multiunit structures, including investment-banking divisions, consulting-firm country offices, and hospital networks, where control over a client relationship is distributed rather than concentrated in one unit.

**Limitations**
The data do not identify which executives served particular clients or where departing executives moved, so the study cannot observe whether clients followed them or distinguish fully between solo and collective departures. It examines only advertising-firm-side multiplexity in a postmerger sample; client-side multiplexity remains untested, and acquisition selection is not entirely exogenous despite the reported selection correction. The arguments assume relationships are maintained for economic value and examine ownership ties joining units that share market ties to a client, leaving affective commitments and other multiplex tie types outside the study.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for studies that track executives' destinations and whether their clients follow, and that compare the retention effects of multiplexity under solo versus collective executive departures. It also proposes empirical investigation of client-side multiplexity. Further research could examine which multiplex ties generate affective commitments, how those commitments affect retention, and whether multiplexity composed of ties such as alliances rather than ownership or market ties produces similar stability.
