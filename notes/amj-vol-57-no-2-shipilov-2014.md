# Relational Pluralism Within and Between Organizations

**Abstract**
Relational pluralism exists when actors maintain multiple kinds of relationships with one another and develop multiple identities as a result. The outcomes of relational pluralism can include greater flexibility in building network ties, more stable exchange relationships, and the ability to adopt tailored innovations. We develop a typology of relational pluralism and examine both the positive and negative features of relational pluralism inside and across organizations. We conclude with a discussion of future directions for research.

**Research Question**
How can relational pluralism, understood through actors' multiple identities and heterogeneous network ties, be classified, and what opportunities and constraints does it create within and between organizations?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core construct: Relational pluralism involves multiple kinds of relationships and multiple identities.
- Typology dimensions: Single versus multiple identities and single versus multiple kinds of ties.
- Enabling outcomes: Flexibility in network tie formation, stable exchange relationships, and tailored innovation adoption.
- Constraining conditions: Identity incoherence, external audience dependence, and the coordination of multiple relationships.

Multiple relationship types and multiple identities give actors additional bases for action and tie formation. Multiple identities can increase flexibility once an actor is intelligible to relevant audiences, whereas incoherent identities under audience dependence can produce an illegitimacy discount. Because relational pluralism operates within and between organizations, its opportunities and constraints also depend on how ties and identities are coordinated across levels.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates identity theory and social network theory by organizing research along two dimensions: whether actors have single or multiple identities and whether they maintain single or multiple kinds of ties. It frames relational pluralism as an inherently multi-level phenomenon and uses the typology to connect its enabling and constraining consequences within and between organizations.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations, relational pluralism can provide flexibility in building network ties, support more stable exchange relationships, and enable the adoption of tailored innovations. It can also constrain an organization when external audiences have difficulty classifying it and the organization depends on those audiences for resources.

**Future Research**
Future studies should examine multiple ties and multiple identities simultaneously, assess how each shapes the other, and investigate how they interact in affecting performance. The authors call for multi-level analyses of how interpersonal and interorganizational relationships develop into one another and how relational pluralism at one level affects another. They also identify organizational coordination of information and resource exchange, temporal multiplexity, relationship dynamics, and change across two-mode networks as research priorities.
