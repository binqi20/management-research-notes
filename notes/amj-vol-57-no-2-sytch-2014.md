# Friends and Foes: The Dynamics of Dual Social Structures

**Abstract**
This paper investigates the evolutionary dynamics of a dual social structure encompassing collaboration and conflict among corporate actors. We apply and advance structural balance theory to examine the formation of balanced and unbalanced dyadic and triadic structures, and to explore how these dynamics aggregate to shape the emergence of a global network. Our findings are threefold. First, we find that existing collaborative or conflictual relationships between two companies engender future relationships of the same type, but crowd out relationships of the different type. This results in (a) an increased likelihood of the formation of balanced (uniplex) relationships that combine multiple ties of either collaboration or conflict, and (b) a reduced likelihood of the formation of unbalanced (multiplex) relationships that combine collaboration and conflict between the same two firms. Second, we find that network formation is driven not by a pull toward balanced triads, but rather by a pull away from unbalanced triads. Third, we find that the observed micro-level dynamics of dyads and triads affect the structural segregation of the global network into two separate collaborative and conflictual segments of firms. Our empirical analyses used data on strategic partnerships and patent infringement and antitrust lawsuits in biotechnology and pharmaceuticals from 1996 to 2006.

**Research Question**
How do collaborative and conflictual relationships among corporate actors coevolve to produce balanced and unbalanced dyadic and triadic structures, and how do these micro-level formation dynamics aggregate to shape the global industry-wide network?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1a: The number of current collaborative relationships between two firms will be positively related to a new collaborative relationship and negatively related to a new conflictual relationship.
- H1b: The number of current conflictual relationships between two firms will be positively related to a new conflictual relationship and negatively related to a new collaborative relationship.
- H2a: The number of third parties to which both firms hold collaborative ties will be positively related to a new collaborative relationship between the firms.
- H2b: The number of third parties to which one firm holds a collaborative tie and the other a conflictual tie will be positively related to a new conflictual relationship between the firms.
- H2c: The number of third parties to which both firms hold conflictual ties will be positively related to a new collaborative relationship between the firms.
- H3a: The number of third parties to which both firms hold collaborative ties will be negatively related to a new conflictual relationship between the firms.
- H3b: The number of third parties to which one firm holds a collaborative tie and the other a conflictual tie will be negatively related to a new collaborative relationship between the firms.
- H3c: The number of third parties to which both firms hold conflictual ties will be negatively related to a new conflictual relationship between the firms.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Numbers of current collaborative and conflictual ties within a firm dyad; numbers of collaborative, conflictual, and mixed third parties shared by the dyad.
- DV(s): Formation of a new collaborative tie and formation of a new conflictual tie in the following year.
- Mediators: Not directly measured. The paper theorizes familiarity, trust, learning, transaction-cost reduction, and inertia for repeated collaboration; escalation, attribution bias, negative affect, dependence uncertainty, and legal constraints for repeated conflict and crowding out; and cognitive alignment or tension, third-party mediation, tertius gaudens intervention, and coalition formation for triads.
- Moderators: None hypothesized in the main model; an interaction between current collaborative and conflictual ties was tested to account for multiplex relationships and was nonsignificant.

Structural balance implies that same-type relationships perpetuate while collaboration and conflict crowd one another out, increasing balanced uniplex dyads and reducing unbalanced multiplex dyads. At the triadic level, common third-party relationships were expected both to pull firms toward balanced triads and away from unbalanced triads, through cognitive alignment or tension and third-party intervention. The supported dyadic perpetuation and crowding-out dynamics, together with avoidance of unbalanced triads, aggregate into segregation of the global network into collaborative and conflictual segments.

**Data & Measures**
The study combines partnership records from MERIT-CATI and BioScan with patent-infringement and antitrust litigation records from Lex Machina and PACER for a population of 2,200 companies in the global pharmaceutical and biotechnology industry during 1996-2006. Because partnerships were modeled with a five-year duration window, annual dual networks were analyzed from 2000 to 2006; the full risk set contained 760,250 dyad-years, and the rare-events logit estimation used a matched sample of 7,908 dyad-years comprising 1,318 realized relationships matched 1:5 to nonmaterialized relationships. Litigation data covered all 93 U.S. federal district courts, although antitrust cases filed only in state courts were not captured.

The collaboration and conflict dependent variables equaled 1 when a dyad entered at least one partnership or initiated at least one lawsuit, respectively, in year t + 1, and 0 otherwise. Current collaborative ties were counts of joint partnerships; current conflictual ties were counts of active patent-infringement and antitrust lawsuits; and the triadic predictors counted shared third parties linked to both firms by collaborative ties, conflictual ties, or one tie of each type. Models included year fixed effects, dyad-level network-autocorrelation terms, and controls for firm assets, long-term debt, profit, patents, joint patent citations, prior collaboration and conflict experience, and past dyadic ties. Complementary analyses compared observed motif formation with 1,000 configuration-model random networks and used agent-based simulations to assess global network segregation.

**Key Findings**
- H1a was supported. A one-standard-deviation increase in current collaborative relationships raised the probability of a new collaborative relationship by 27.1% and reduced the probability of a new conflictual relationship by 13.9%.
- H1b was supported. A one-standard-deviation increase in current conflictual relationships raised the probability of a new conflictual relationship by 14.9% and reduced the probability of a new collaborative relationship by 10.5%.
- H2a, H2b, and H2c were not supported: collaborative third parties and conflictual third parties had no effect on new collaboration, and mixed third parties had no effect on new conflict. The results therefore did not show a pull toward balanced triads.
- H3a was supported in the rare-events logit analysis: collaborative third parties reduced the likelihood of a new conflictual tie by 16.3%. The computational motif comparison was consistent (z = -3.36, p < .001).
- H3b was supported in the rare-events logit analysis: mixed third parties reduced the likelihood of a new collaborative tie by 9.8%. The computational motif comparison was consistent (z = -8.06, p < .001).
- H3c was supported in the rare-events logit analysis: conflictual third parties reduced the likelihood of a new conflictual tie by 15.8%. In the computational motif comparison, however, formation of the all-conflictual unbalanced triad was statistically indistinguishable from the random case.

The real network was segregated into collaborative and conflictual segments (S = -0.520). The agent-based scenario using the empirically estimated perpetuation, crowding-out, and unbalanced-triad effects provided the best fit (E(S) = -0.507, z = -0.359), while a scenario with a weaker pull away from unbalanced triads also fit well; this indicated that dyadic perpetuation and crowding out were more consequential for segregation than the triadic effect.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends structural balance theory from the stability of interpersonal cognitive structures to the formation of balanced and unbalanced structures among organizations. It conceptualizes collaboration and conflict as interdependent elements of one social space and links their dyadic and triadic coevolution to an emergent global network property, showing how balanced dyads and avoidance of unbalanced dyads can produce structural segregation. It also restores agency to common third parties by distinguishing their role as actors that can mediate or inhibit tie formation from their treatment as objects in the original balance formulation.

**Practical Implication**
An apparent gap in collaborative connectivity can mask a fully fledged conflictual relationship, so examining collaborative ties alone can provide an incomplete picture of interorganizational social structure. In the paper's biotechnology and pharmaceutical setting, considering partnership and litigation ties jointly reveals how firms become separated into collaborative and conflictual network segments.

**Limitations**
The authors cannot directly evaluate the generalizability of the findings to a broader range of social systems. The setting isolates extreme, emotionally charged patent-infringement and antitrust conflicts that are highly consequential and share a locus of managerial control with collaborative relationships, so variation in the nature of conflict across other settings may alter the documented dynamics. The short observation period and limited within-dyad variance also prevented dyad fixed-effects estimation, although dyad random-effects models produced consistent results.

**Future Research**
Future work could distinguish brokerage by a mediator between conflicting parties from brokerage that spans two otherwise unconnected parties, and further evaluate the agency of common third parties in forming triadic structures. It could test effects on global network properties beyond segregation, such as small-world or core-periphery structure, and model reciprocal effects from the global system or institutional environment to micro-level tie formation. The theory could also be examined in broader social systems and across relationships where conflict and collaboration are more closely intertwined, including early interactions between venture-capital firms and targets or between acquirers and hostile-takeover targets.
