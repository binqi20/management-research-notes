# Lunch Breaks Unpacked: The Role of Autonomy as a Moderator of Recovery during Lunch

**Abstract**
Work recovery research has focused mainly on how after-work break activities help employees replenish their resources and reduce fatigue. Given that employees spend a considerable amount of time at work, understanding how they can replenish their resources during the workday is critical. Drawing on ego depletion (Muraven & Baumeister, 2000) and self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985), we employed multi-source experience sampling methods to test the effects of a critical boundary condition, employee lunch break autonomy, on the relation between lunch break activities and end-of-workday fatigue. Although specific energy-relevant activities had a main effect on end-of-workday fatigue, each of these was moderated by the degree of autonomous choice associated with the break. Specifically, for activities that supported the psychological needs of relatedness and competence (i.e., social and work activities, respectively), as lunch break autonomy increased, effects switched from increasing fatigue to reducing fatigue. To the extent that lunch break activities involved relaxation, however, lunch break autonomy was only important when levels of relaxation were low. We conclude that lunch break autonomy plays a complex and pivotal role in conferring the potential energetic benefits of lunch break activities. Contributions to theory and practice are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do daily social, work, and relaxing activities during lunch breaks relate to end-of-workday fatigue, and how does employees' daily lunch break autonomy moderate these within-person relations?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1a (SDT): Higher levels of social activities during the lunch break will result in lower end-of-workday fatigue.
- H1b (EDT): Higher levels of social activities during the lunch break will result in higher end-of-workday fatigue.
- H2: Higher levels of lunch break autonomy will result in lower end-of-workday fatigue.
- H3: Lunch break autonomy will moderate the effect of social activities on end-of-workday fatigue, such that social activities result in more fatigue when autonomy is low and less fatigue when autonomy is high.
- H4: Higher levels of work activities during the lunch break will result in higher end-of-workday fatigue.
- H5: Lunch break autonomy will moderate the effect of work activities on end-of-workday fatigue, such that work activities result in more fatigue when autonomy is low and less fatigue when autonomy is high.
- H6: Higher levels of relaxing activities during the lunch break will result in lower end-of-workday fatigue.
- H7: Lunch break autonomy will moderate the effect of relaxing activities on end-of-workday fatigue, such that the effect is stronger when autonomy is lower and weaker when autonomy is higher.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Daily social, work, and relaxing lunch break activities.
- DV(s): Coworker-rated end-of-workday fatigue.
- Mediators: None tested; regulatory resource depletion or recovery and satisfaction of relatedness and competence needs were theorized but not measured.
- Moderators: Daily lunch break autonomy.

Ego depletion theory predicts that social interaction and continued work require effortful self-regulation and consume limited energy, whereas relaxation permits regulatory resources to recover. Self-determination theory predicts that social and work activities can satisfy relatedness and competence needs and restore energy when those activities are autonomously chosen. Integrating the theories, the paper argues that autonomy determines whether social and work activities deplete or restore energy, while low autonomy combined with little relaxation is especially depleting.

**Data & Measures**
Employees reported their lunch break activities and autonomy immediately after lunch, and coworkers rated focal employees' fatigue near the end of each workday for 10 workdays. The final multilevel sample comprised 78 administrative employees and 444 matched daily responses. Relaxing activity used three Work Recovery Experience Questionnaire items; work activity used three modified break-activity items; social activity used two adapted items; and lunch break autonomy used three Work Recovery Experience Questionnaire items, all on 7-point scales. Coworker-rated fatigue used the PANAS items "tired" and "sluggish." The authors estimated multilevel random-coefficient models with restricted maximum likelihood, person-centered Level 1 predictors, and controls for day, sine, cosine, and lagged fatigue; the observational design provides temporally ordered associations rather than experimental causal identification.

**Key Findings**
H1b, H4, and H6 were supported, whereas H1a was not: social activity (B = .17, beta = .23) and work activity (B = .12, beta = .18) predicted higher fatigue, while relaxing activity predicted lower fatigue (B = -.11, beta = -.15; all p < .05 in Step 2). H2 was not supported because lunch break autonomy had no significant main effect on fatigue.

H3 and H5 were supported: the social activity by autonomy (B = -.20, beta = -.14) and work activity by autonomy (B = -.21, beta = -.14) interactions were significant (p < .05). Social activity significantly increased fatigue at autonomy scores at or below .13 and reduced fatigue at scores at or above 1.80; work activity increased fatigue at scores at or below -.06 and reduced fatigue at scores at or above 1.89.

H7 was supported: the relaxing activity by autonomy interaction was positive (B = .16, beta = .12, p < .05), so relaxation's negative association with fatigue was significant at autonomy scores at or below .04 and weakened as autonomy increased. Low autonomy combined with low relaxing activity was especially fatiguing; high autonomy did not add to the benefit of high relaxation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study integrates ego depletion theory and self-determination theory in work recovery research, showing that autonomy reconciles their competing predictions by changing whether social and work lunch activities deplete or restore energy. It also advances a dynamic, within-person view of autonomy and extends recovery research to lunch breaks as consequential within-workday recovery episodes.

**Practical Implication**
Employees should attend to how they use lunch breaks: work and social activities may produce poorer immediate recovery, whereas relaxing activities can aid recovery. Managers and organizations should avoid pressuring employees to work during breaks or to join lunch activities they do not freely choose, because autonomy over personal break time can offset the fatigue associated with work, social activity, or insufficient relaxation.

**Limitations**
Coworker ratings captured externally observable fatigue and may have missed internal aspects, although supplemental self- and coworker-rated fatigue correlated at r = .30. The study theorized resource depletion, resource restoration, and psychological need fulfillment but did not measure those mechanisms directly. Autonomy was measured for the lunch break as a whole rather than for each activity, and the design did not cover other work breaks, after-work time, or weekends; the social measure also did not identify interaction partners or interaction content.

**Future Research**
Future studies should measure regulatory resources, need fulfillment, and how resources are depleted or restored, and could examine conservation of resources theory as an alternative account. Research should track recovery across other work breaks, after-work periods, weekends, and longer-term outcomes, including how one break shapes later activity choices. The paper also calls for greater specificity about social interaction partners and content and for research on nutritional choices and nutrient consumption during lunch.
