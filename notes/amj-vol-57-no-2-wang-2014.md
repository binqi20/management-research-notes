# Knowledge Networks, Collaboration Networks, and Exploratory Innovation

**Abstract**
Innovation in firms is doubly embedded: in a social network of collaborations between researchers, and in a knowledge network composed of linkages between knowledge elements. The two networks are decoupled. Their structural features are distinct and influence researchers’ exploratory innovation differently. Using the patent data of a leading U.S. microprocessor manufacturer, we constructed the firm’s collaboration and knowledge networks, and examined the effects of two structural features in the two networks—structural holes and degree centrality—on researchers’ exploratory innovation. Our findings show that a researcher with knowledge elements rich in structural holes in the knowledge network tends to explore fewer new knowledge elements from outside the firm, while structural holes in the collaboration network increase exploratory innovation. The average degree centrality of a researcher’s knowledge elements in the knowledge network has an inverted-U-shaped relationship with his or her exploratory innovation, while degree centrality in the collaboration network has a negative effect. This study suggests that knowledge and social networks influence where researchers search for discoveries.

**Research Question**
How do structural holes and degree centrality in a firm’s knowledge network and researcher collaboration network differently affect a researcher’s exploration of knowledge elements new to the firm’s extant stock of knowledge?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: A researcher whose knowledge elements have more structural holes in a firm’s knowledge network tends to explore fewer knowledge elements new to the firm’s extant stock of knowledge.
- H2: The degree centrality of a researcher’s knowledge elements in a firm’s knowledge network has an inverted-U-shaped relationship with the number of explored knowledge elements new to the firm’s extant stock of knowledge.
- H3: A researcher with more structural holes in a firm’s collaboration network among researchers tends to explore more knowledge elements new to the firm’s extant stock of knowledge.
- H4: A researcher with higher degree centrality in a firm’s collaboration network tends to explore fewer knowledge elements new to the firm’s extant stock of knowledge.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Mean structural holes and mean degree centrality of a researcher’s knowledge elements in the firm’s knowledge network; the researcher’s structural holes and degree centrality in the firm’s collaboration network.
- DV(s): Number of knowledge elements new to the firm’s extant stock of knowledge that the researcher explored during 1996-2000.
- Mediators: Additional analyses identify an indirect positive effect of knowledge-network structural holes through the number of new patents, alongside the direct negative effect; the other proposed pathways are not tested as mediation models.
- Moderators: Not tested.

Knowledge-network structural holes indicate unexploited combinatorial opportunities among extant knowledge elements; exploiting those opportunities draws limited attention, resources, and motivation away from exploring knowledge elements outside the firm and can create a competency trap. Additional analyses separate a direct negative effect on exploration from an indirect positive effect mediated through the number of new patents, with the overall effect remaining negative. Increasing knowledge-element degree centrality initially raises perceived combinatorial potential and search efficiency, but high centrality brings diminishing returns as combinatorial value is exhausted. In the collaboration network, structural holes provide timely information and autonomy that facilitate exploration, whereas degree-central researchers benefit from current opportunities, are immersed in prevailing paradigms, and have status interests tied to extant knowledge elements.

**Data & Measures**
The study uses USPTO patent data from one leading U.S. microprocessor manufacturer and follows 844 researchers who patented during 1991-1995 and remained active during 1996-2000. The authors constructed a 1976-1995 knowledge network in which nodes were industrial subclasses and ties indicated their joint use in patents, and a 1991-1995 collaboration network in which researchers were tied through patent co-authorship. The dependent variable was the number of industrial subclasses in a researcher’s 1996-2000 patents that had not appeared in the firm’s 1976-1995 patent stock. Knowledge-network structural holes and degree centrality were calculated for each knowledge element and averaged across the researcher’s knowledge portfolio; collaboration-network structural holes used Burt’s constraint-based measure, and collaboration degree centrality counted collaboration partners. Negative binomial regressions modeled the overdispersed count outcome with researcher, knowledge, network, geography, and observation-period controls; the longitudinal design is observational.

**Key Findings**
All four hypotheses were supported in the primary Model 3. H1 was supported: knowledge-network structural holes had a negative effect (b = -1.30, p < .001), and a one-unit increase multiplied the exploration rate by 0.27. H2 was supported: knowledge-network degree centrality had a positive first-order coefficient (b = 0.07, p < .001) and a negative squared coefficient (b = -0.001, p < .05), producing an inverted-U relationship with an inflection point around 39. H3 was supported: collaboration-network structural holes had a positive effect (b = 0.56, p < .05), and a one-unit increase multiplied the exploration rate by 1.75. H4 was supported: collaboration-network degree centrality had a negative effect (b = -0.05, p < .01), and a one-unit increase multiplied the exploration rate by 0.95. In sensitivity analyses, omitting three observation-period controls made the H3 coefficient nonsignificant; using the number of patents containing a new knowledge element as the outcome preserved the other directions but made knowledge centrality linear and positive rather than significantly inverted-U-shaped.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a multilevel account of knowledge embeddedness by showing that researchers are embedded both in a collaboration network of researchers and in a network of knowledge elements, and that these two networks are decoupled. It demonstrates that structural holes and degree centrality have different meanings and effects across the two networks, qualifying accounts that treat social networks primarily as conduits for heterogeneous knowledge. By theorizing knowledge-based search, it connects the combinatorial structure of knowledge to exploratory innovation and explains how knowledge-network structural holes can produce a competency trap in which exploitation displaces exploration.

**Practical Implication**
For firms big and old enough to accumulate a stock of knowledge and separate the two networks, the findings indicate that knowledge and collaboration networks provide distinct signals about where researchers search for discoveries. The paper does not prescribe a specific managerial intervention.

**Limitations**
Patent data do not capture tacit knowledge or trade secrets, so the mapped knowledge network may be incomplete. The longitudinal design uses one earlier and one later period rather than panel observations, lacks researchers’ functional affiliations, and examines a single firm whose characteristics may affect the estimates. The outcome counts new knowledge elements without weighting their relative importance, and the arguments require a firm large and old enough for its knowledge and collaboration networks to separate.

**Future Research**
Future studies should examine how researchers acquire knowledge elements that are rich in structural holes or central, including the roles of socioeconomic background and social networks, and whether knowledge-network positions shape interactions with other researchers. The authors call for firm-level studies using industry-wide knowledge and collaboration networks, tests of network decoupling and other inventive outcomes such as idea quality, and panel designs that capture change over multiple periods. They also propose multi-firm research on how administrative structures, control systems, cultures, leadership, and strategies condition the effects, together with research on significant new knowledge elements and the large-and-old-firm scope condition.
