# With or Without You: When Does Managerial Exit Matter for the Dissolution of Dyadic Market Ties?

**Abstract**
This paper investigates the relationship between managerial exit from advertising agencies and advertisers and the dissolution of dyadic market ties. We examine how the amount and locus (advertiser vs. agency) of managerial exit and the hierarchical level from which exit occurs (executives vs. exchange managers) influence the likelihood that dyadic market ties will dissolve. We theorize that managerial exit disrupts dyadic market ties through its effect on three mechanisms of social capital: investments in relationship-specific assets; formal control over market ties; and the intensity of interaction with exchange partners. We consider how market tie fragility, the financial performance of advertisers, and the duration of market ties condition the effects of managerial exit on market tie dissolution. In short, we theorize about which managerial roles matter more to market tie dissolution, why they matter and when they matter. Using longitudinal data on 232 dyadic ties between advertisers and New York City advertising agencies, we find that the number of executives and exchange managers who exit on both sides of market ties affects tie dissolution. However, these effects are neither symmetrical between exchange partners nor uniform across the hierarchical levels from which exit occurs, and vary with the strength of market ties.

**Research Question**
From which firm—advertisers or agencies—is managerial exit more detrimental to dyadic market ties, from which role—exchange managers or executives—is exit more likely to disrupt ties, and under what conditions are these exits more or less disruptive?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. The number of executives exiting from advertisers will have a stronger effect on the likelihood of dyadic market tie dissolution than will the number of executives exiting from agencies.
H2. The number of exchange managers exiting from agencies will have a stronger effect on the likelihood of dyadic market tie dissolution than will the number of exchange managers exiting from advertisers.
H3. In advertisers, the number of executives who exit will have a stronger effect on the likelihood of dyadic market tie dissolution than the number of exchange managers who exit.
H4. In agencies, the number of exchange managers who exit will have a stronger effect on the likelihood of dyadic market tie dissolution than the number of executives who exit.
H5. In advertisers, the effect of the number of executives and exchange managers who exit on dyadic market tie dissolution will be greater the poorer the advertiser's financial performance.
H6a. In agencies, the effect of the number of executives and exchange managers who exit on dyadic market tie dissolution will decrease the longer the duration of market ties.
H6b. In advertisers, the effect of the number of executives and exchange managers who exit on dyadic market tie dissolution will decrease the longer the duration of market ties.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Number of executives exiting (advertisers; agencies) and number of exchange managers exiting (advertiser marketing managers; agency account services managers; agency creative managers), aggregated to the firm level and lagged (t−1).
- DV(s): Dissolution of the dyadic advertiser–agency market tie (coded 1 in the year the tie dissolved, 0 while intact).
- Mediators: None estimated. The three social-capital mechanisms (relationship-specific assets, formal control over ties, intensity of interaction) are argued theoretically, not tested as statistical mediators.
- Moderators: Advertiser financial performance (advertiser revenue growth) and market tie duration.

Managers and executives occupying roles related to market ties invest role-based social capital—relationship-specific assets, formal control, and the timing and intensity of interaction—that keeps advertiser–agency ties embedded and continuous. When managers exit, that role-based social capital is lost and ties become more likely to dissolve. Because roles differ in what they contribute and buyers (advertisers) hold more control than sellers (agencies) over forming and breaking ties, exit effects are theorized to differ by side of the market, by hierarchical level, and with tie strength (advertiser performance and tie duration).

**Data & Measures**
Data were hand-collected annually (1986–1998) from two public directories: The Standard Directory of Advertising Agencies ("Agency Red Book") and The Standard Directory of Advertisers ("Advertiser Red Book"). DV (dissolution): a dyad is coded 1 when an advertiser and agency are listed as exchange partners in year t but not in year t+1. IVs: counts of executive and exchange-manager exits, where an individual is listed as employed in year t but not t+1, aggregated to the firm level; executives are those holding Chairman/CEO/President titles (advertisers) or Chairman/CEO/President/Chief Creative Officer titles (agencies), and exchange managers are marketing managers (advertisers) and account services or creative managers (agencies). Moderators: advertiser performance = year-to-year change in the log of advertiser gross revenue; tie duration = number of continuous years the advertiser and agency reported working together. Controls include advertiser/agency size, agency mortality, agency reputation, advertiser industry, market orientation, and year effects. Models are logistic regressions estimated via generalized estimating equations (GEE, SAS GENMOD) on pooled cross-sectional time-series data; the design is observational/associational, with no causal-identification strategy claimed.

**Key Findings**
Main effects (Model 2): each advertiser executive who exits increases the likelihood of tie dissolution by roughly 75% (exp(0.56)) and each advertiser marketing manager by roughly 23% (exp(0.21)); for agencies, only creative-manager exits have a positive, significant main effect (about 8%, exp(0.08) per exit), while agency executive and account-services exits are not significant. H1 receives strong support—advertiser executive exits are positive and significant while agency executive exits are negative and non-significant. H2 is not supported and runs contrary to prediction: advertiser marketing-manager exits have a greater effect than either agency creative-manager (p < .05) or account-services-manager (p < .01) exits. H3 is supported—within advertisers, the executive-exit coefficient is significantly larger than the marketing-manager coefficient (p < .05). H4 is supported—within agencies, creative-manager exits have a significantly greater effect than executive exits (p < .05), and account-services vs. executive exits are marginally greater (p < .10). H5 is supported—the advertiser-exit × advertiser-growth interactions are negative and significant for both executives and marketing managers (exit is most disruptive when advertiser revenues are declining, negligible when the advertiser is growing). H6a receives partial support—the agency executive-exit × tie-duration interaction is negative and significant (Model 5), the creative-manager × duration interaction is only marginally significant, and the account-services interaction is non-significant. H6b is not supported—the advertiser executive- and marketing-manager × tie-duration interactions are not significant.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study develops a more balanced, role- and side-differentiated theory of how managerial mobility disrupts inter-organizational market ties, moving beyond the assumption that exit effects are symmetric across buyers and sellers. It shows that the loss of role-based social capital operates through different mechanisms depending on whether exit occurs among advertiser or agency executives versus exchange managers, and it specifies conditions (advertiser performance, tie duration/fragility) that amplify or dampen those effects. In doing so it contributes to research on the dynamics of interfirm relationships and on the organizational consequences of managerial mobility.

**Practical Implication**
Identifying the conditions under which market ties are more or less likely to end can help managers in buyer (advertiser) and seller (agency) firms improve firm performance and enhance chances of survival. The results suggest which departures most threaten client–service-firm relationships—advertiser executive exits and agency creative-manager exits—and when they matter most, namely when advertiser performance is poor and when market ties are new.

**Limitations**
The authors study dyadic market ties in only one setting—advertising, a professional service marked by complex interactions and buyer–seller informational asymmetries—so it is unclear whether the findings generalize to other professional-service contexts or to settings where ties are less strategic or sustained by physical or financial rather than social capital. They also cannot identify the extent to which specific executives and exchange managers are directly involved in the observed dyads, which prevents measuring the investments particular individuals make or lose and thus testing the individual-actor mechanism. Finally, the data do not reveal which individual account services managers attend to particular buyers' accounts, possibly weakening the account-services results.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for pairing individual exchange managers with specific market ties to gain richer insight into how investments in particular types of social capital magnify or diminish the disruptive effects of managerial exit. It also urges disentangling the relative influence of different types of social capital and the conditions under which disruptions to social capital affect ties, extending the framework of mechanisms to other contexts to understand when market ties fail, and investigating why creative managers strongly affect specific dyadic relationships but not a firm's overall portfolio of relationships.
