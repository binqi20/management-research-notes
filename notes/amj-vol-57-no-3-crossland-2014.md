# CEO Career Variety: Effects on Firm-Level Strategic and Social Novelty

**Abstract**
We introduce the concept of “CEO career variety”—defined as the array of distinct professional and institutional experiences an executive has had prior to becoming CEO. Using a longitudinal sample of Fortune 250 CEOs, we hypothesize, and find strong evidence for the assertion, that CEO career variety is positively associated with firm-level strategic novelty—manifested in strategic dynamism (period-on-period change) and strategic distinctiveness (deviance from industry central tendencies). We also find mixed evidence that CEO career variety is positively associated with social novelty—manifested in top management team turnover and heterogeneity.

**Research Question**
Does a CEO's career variety—the array of distinct professional and institutional experiences accumulated before becoming CEO—shape firm-level strategic novelty (strategic dynamism and strategic distinctiveness) and social novelty (top management team membership change and heterogeneity)?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Hypothesis 1. CEO career variety will be positively associated with firm-level strategic dynamism.
Hypothesis 2. CEO career variety will be positively associated with firm-level strategic distinctiveness.
Hypothesis 3. CEO career variety will be positively associated with TMT membership change.
Hypothesis 4. CEO career variety will be positively associated with TMT heterogeneity.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO career variety (breadth of distinct pre-CEO professional and institutional experiences).
- DV(s): Strategic novelty — strategic dynamism (resource reallocation; corporate strategic change) and strategic distinctiveness; Social novelty — TMT membership change and TMT heterogeneity (age, tenure, education, gender).
- Mediators: None estimated. The authors note social novelty could be theorized as a mediator of strategic novelty (or vice versa) but instead posit all four outcomes as co-equal consequences of career variety.
- Moderators: None.

Adopting upper echelons logic, the paper argues that career variety reflects two personal attributes: an ingrained dispositional preference for change and experimentation, and accumulated cognitive breadth (awareness of multiple paradigms and exemplars). High-variety CEOs are therefore predisposed to generate and favor novel, distinctive options and to perceive a wider range of options as feasible, directing their firms toward strategic and social novelty; low-variety CEOs, preferring stability and incrementalism, keep firms closer to industry norms.

**Data & Measures**
Career histories were hand-collected from multiple archival sources (e.g., Marquis Who's Who, Dun & Bradstreet, Standard & Poor's Register, BoardEx, corporate sites and press releases); strategic-choice variables came from Compustat and TMTs from firms' annual 10-K reports. The focal independent variable, CEO career variety, is the sum of distinct industry sectors, distinct firms, and distinct functional areas the individual had worked in prior to becoming CEO, divided by pre-CEO career length (inter-rater ICC1 = .91; Cronbach's alpha = .76; EFA supported convergent/discriminant validity). Strategic dynamism was operationalized as resource reallocation (year-on-year absolute change across six Winsorized, logged, standardized strategic-choice variables: advertising, R&D, overhead efficiency, capital intensity, plant/equipment newness, and financial leverage) and as corporate strategic change (year-on-year change in an entropy diversification measure). Strategic distinctiveness was the standardized absolute difference of the same six variables from the industry mean (logged and summed). TMT membership change was the annual sum of additions and deletions divided by prior-year TMT size; TMT heterogeneity was captured for age and tenure (coefficient of variation) and education and gender (Herfindahl-Blau index). Models included a comprehensive control set (instrumented pre-entry condition, CEO age/education/MBA/experience, incentive compensation, CEO ownership, duality, outsider, previous CEO dismissal, firm departures, board independence, institutional blockholding, firm size/age/performance, industry dynamism, and calendar-year, tenure-year, and industry dummies). Estimation used population-averaged GEEs (Gaussian, identity link, AR(1)) with robust standard errors; the design is associational, and a two-stage residual model addressed potential endogeneity in board selection of CEOs.

**Key Findings**
Strategic novelty received strong support. Hypothesis 1 was supported: career variety was positively related to resource reallocation (β = 3.43, p < .05) and to corporate strategic change (β = 2.88, p < .01). Hypothesis 2 was supported: career variety was positively related to strategic distinctiveness (β = 2.91, p < .05). Social novelty received mixed support. Hypothesis 3 was supported: career variety was positively related to TMT membership change (β = 0.42, p < .01). Hypothesis 4 was not supported for any of the four TMT heterogeneity measures (age, tenure, education, gender), where the career-variety effect was non-significant. Practically, moving from a CEO one SD below to one SD above the mean in career variety corresponded to an increase of about 37% of one SD in resource reallocation (roughly a $34 million annual change in advertising and $149 million in SG&A for the median firm) and about one additional TMT addition or removal per year. A limited post-hoc analysis suggested that strategic novelty brings about subsequent performance volatility but is not related to the subsequent performance level.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces and validates "CEO career variety" as a distinct behavioral construct — reliably measurable and not redundant with openness to experience, risk propensity, age, or education — that reflects both dispositional preferences for change and accumulated cognitive breadth. It extends upper echelons theory by showing that the breadth of an executive's pre-CEO experiences is systematically associated with firm-level strategic novelty and, in part, social novelty, and it responds to calls for research on the antecedents of TMT composition.

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest that boards of directors might benefit from explicitly considering a candidate's career variety when making CEO selection decisions: if a firm's strategy has become too predictable, conventional, or inertial, hiring a high-variety CEO might help promote rapid, large-scale change and greater strategic distinctiveness, whereas if a firm's strategy has been too erratic and volatile, hiring a low-variety CEO might contribute to a more consistent, steady approach. They caution that high-variety CEOs may seek novelty for its own sake, shaking up strategies and TMTs whether or not the firm needs it.

**Limitations**
The study does not demonstrate the underlying psychological content of career variety, relying on prior research to argue what the construct reflects; survey and/or experimental methods would be needed to confirm and clarify what career variety actually indicates about a CEO. The measure is archivally derived, the analysis is restricted to the first five years of each CEO's tenure, and the focus is strictly on CEOs.

**Future Research**
The authors point to confirming the psychological content of career variety through survey/experimental work; examining whether accumulated career-variety experiences reinforce novelty-seeking behaviors; exploring implications for CEO tenure length and succession (e.g., whether high-variety CEOs have shorter tenures, depart even when performance is satisfactory, or are especially likely to be multi-time CEOs); refining or developing more fine-grained measures of prior experiences; studying the effects of career variety during later phases of CEO tenure; and extending the construct to employees at other organizational levels (e.g., high-variety sales representatives, R&D scientists, or country managers, and links to individual creativity, deviance, and supervisory styles). They also note it would be interesting to explore whether high-variety CEOs attract and retain other high-variety individuals to their TMTs, and to examine the prescriptive and performance implications of career variety.
