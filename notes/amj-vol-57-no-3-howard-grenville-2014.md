**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: climate change is so pervasive that its causes and consequences show up at every level of analysis of interest to organizational scholars, and responses to it will fundamentally reshape many of the phenomena, interactions, and relationships central to management scholarship.
- Dual role of organizations: the rise of organizations and industrialized production set us on this path, yet organizations are equally critical to mitigating and adapting to climate change, engaging a suite of approaches under the broad banners of adaptation and mitigation.
- Implication 1 — Reshaping value chains: climate change will reshape supply networks, production arrangements, and the provision of energy and water, prompting industrial symbiosis, circular-economy models, and production closer to the point of consumption.
- Implication 2 — Organizational resilience and adaptation: high, non-linear uncertainty favors ways of organizing that foreground resilience and responsiveness rather than scale or growth, making proactive adaptability a possible "new normal."
- Implication 3 — Shifts in work and life: resource-efficient cities and the decentralization and de-synchronization of activity will alter where and how individuals live, work, and move, reshaping employment practices and how employees identify with organizations.
- Implication 4 — Societal shifts: infrastructure change and less predictable exposure to extreme events place adaptation burdens on society, raising questions of governance, distributional responsibility, climate migration, and social mobilization.

The editorial offers a primer on the science and policy of climate change and then argues that, because climate impacts will impinge on value chains, organizational resilience, individual work patterns, and broader governance systems, the topic poses pressing questions across the typical levels of analysis and range of methodologies that management scholars employ. It frames organizations as both drivers of and responders to climate change, and uses four illustrative domains of implication to open avenues for research and engagement.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial establishes climate change as a pervasive, multi-level phenomenon whose causes and consequences appear at every level of analysis of interest to organizational scholars, arguing that it will fundamentally reshape the phenomena, interactions, and relationships that are of central concern to management scholars. By pairing a brief primer on the science and policy of climate change with four illustrative domains of organizational implication — value chains, organizational resilience and adaptation, work and life, and societal shifts — it positions the issue as ripe for study across the typical levels of analysis and within the range of methodologies that management scholars employ.

**Practical Implication**
Addressing management and organizational scholars, the editorial calls on them to use their expertise to better understand the challenges climate change poses to organizations, individuals, and societies, and to study how business, society, and public entities mobilize for and navigate these challenges. It notes that private, public, and not-for-profit organizations will need to engage a suite of approaches under the broad banners of adaptation and mitigation to cope with the implications of climate change.

**Limitations**
The authors frame the piece as a brief primer intended to offer insight into the science and policy of climate change and to outline potential implications for organizations and organizational scholars, rather than as a comprehensive treatment. They note that the four highlighted implications are neither exhaustive nor exclusive, but are meant to illustrate a wide range of questions ripe for study.

**Future Research**
Across four domains, the editorial maps research directions it explicitly invites. On value chains, it asks how governance, coordination, and risk-mitigation arrangements can anticipate and respond, and how shifts such as sourcing "waste" material as an input alter interorganizational relationships, the boundaries of the firm, and organizational identities. On resilience and adaptation, it calls for developing theory on what adaptation and resilience look like under the assumption of significant disruption to "business as usual." On work and life, it invites study of whether motivation, commitment, identification, or pro-social behaviors are differently manifested, and how altered patterns of mobility, co-presence, and communication change collaboration, creativity, or productivity, including in the developing world. On societal shifts, it asks what new models of social mobilization and change might be occasioned by efforts to respond to climate change, and to what degree a response demands shifts in cultural or institutional values or logics.
