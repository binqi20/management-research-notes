# A Social–Structural Perspective on Employee–Organization Relationships and Team Creativity

**Abstract**
We develop a social–structural perspective on the relationship between employee–organization relationships (EORs) and team creativity. We argue that the mutual investment EOR approach, in which employers expect high levels of employee contributions and offer extensive inducements, will be associated with higher team creativity relative to other EOR approaches. We also advance the argument that this relationship will be mediated by team member work-related communication density and that the mediated relationship will be stronger when team members’ tasks are complex. We find support for the model in a two-wave study of 1,807 employees in 229 teams in 55 Chinese high-technology organizations. We discuss the implications for future employment relationship research and practice.

**Research Question**
How, and under what conditions, do employee–organization relationship (EOR) forms relate to team creativity? The authors theorize the EOR–creativity link at the work-team level, positing team members' work-related communication density as the mediating mechanism and task complexity as a boundary condition.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. The relationship between expected contributions and team members' work-related communication density will be positive and significant when offered inducements are high, but attenuated when offered inducements are low.
H2. Team members' work-related communication density will relate positively to team creativity.
H3. The relationship between EORs and team creativity will be mediated by team members' work-related communication density, such that the mediated effect will be strongest for the mutual investment EOR approach.
H4. The relationship between team members' work-related communication density and team creativity will be moderated by task complexity, such that the positive relationship will be stronger when task complexity is high.
H5. The strength of the mediated relationship between EORs and team creativity (through work-related communication density) will depend on task complexity; the indirect and total effects of EORs (specifically, mutual investment) on team creativity will be stronger when task complexity is high.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee–organization relationship (EOR) form, operationalized through offered inducements and expected contributions (mutual investment = high on both continua); expected contributions is the focal predictor in the path model.
- DV(s): Team creativity (supervisor-rated).
- Mediators: Work-related communication density (team-level aggregate of members' ego-network density).
- Moderators: Offered inducements (first-stage moderator of the expected-contributions → communication-density path); task complexity (second-stage moderator of the communication-density → team-creativity path).

Drawing on Nahapiet and Ghoshal's (1998) social capital theory, the authors argue that offered inducements supply the opportunity and capability to build dense work-related communication networks, while expected contributions supply the motivation and value expectancy to do so; only the mutual investment form satisfies all four preconditions for dense networks. Dense communication networks in turn enable the knowledge transfer and combination that generate team creativity, but this social capital is effectively mobilized into creativity mainly under high task complexity. The result is a first- and second-stage moderated mediation model linking EOR forms to team creativity via communication density.

**Data & Measures**
Data source: a two-wave onsite survey of high-technology organizations in an eastern province of China, with firms drawn from a government-accredited list; the final analytic sample is 55 organizations, 229 teams, and 1,807 employees. EOR (offered inducements and expected contributions): supervisor-rated Time 1 items adapted from Hom et al. (2009) and Wang et al. (2003), with a good-fitting second-order factor structure (offered inducements α = .90; expected contributions α = .92), and mutual investment defined as high on both continua. Work-related communication density (mediator): team members' Time 1 ego-centered networks (up to five alters), binary-coded ties, Scott's (2000) density formula, aggregated to the team level. Task complexity (second-stage moderator): seven items from Dean and Snell (1991), supervisor-rated at Time 1 (α = .80). Team creativity (DV): a six-item supervisor rating at Time 2 (four items from Farmer et al. 2003, two from Leenders et al. 2003; α = .86). Controls include team size, average team tenure, communication network size, percentage female, average education, outside-network percentage, perceived supervisor support (a social-exchange proxy), and team performance. Estimation used clustered regression with a White correction (clustered on organization) and nested-equations path analysis (Edwards & Lambert, 2007) with a 1,000-sample bootstrap for indirect and total effects; the design is associational (two-wave), and the authors state they cannot firmly establish causality.

**Key Findings**
H1 supported: the expected-contributions × offered-inducements interaction on communication density was significant (b = .16, p < .01); the expected-contributions → communication-density slope was positive and significant under high offered inducements (b_high OI = .45, p < .01) but not under low (b_low OI = .13, n.s.). The authors note the observed pattern departed somewhat from the predicted form in that the lowest density occurred in the overinvestment type. H2 not supported: work-related communication density was not significantly related to team creativity overall (b = .11, n.s.). H3 supported: the mediated (indirect) effect of expected contributions on team creativity via communication density was significant under high offered inducements (indirect = .05, p < .05; total = .26, p < .05) but not under low, consistent with mediation being strongest under mutual investment. H4 supported: the communication-density → team-creativity link was moderated by task complexity — positive under high complexity (b = .29, p < .01), not under low (b = -.05, n.s.); this crossing explains the null H2 main effect. H5 supported: the conditional indirect and total effects were positive and significant only when both offered inducements and task complexity were high (indirect = .13, p < .01; total = .36, p < .01), and slope-difference tests showed this high–high combination differed significantly from the others, though its difference from the high-inducement/low-complexity slope was marginal (t = 1.91, p = .06).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study reorients the EOR literature beyond its dominant social-exchange mechanism by advancing a social–structural (social capital) account in which organizational decisions about offered inducements and expected contributions shape the density of work-related communication networks—and thereby team creativity—over and above social exchange. It integrates the EOR, social capital, and team creativity literatures into a single moderated-mediation model and identifies task complexity as the key contextual condition under which the value latent in dense communication networks is realized as team creativity.

**Practical Implication**
For organizational decision makers, the mutual investment EOR—pairing high offered inducements with high expected contributions—is the form most conducive to the dense work-related communication networks that support team creativity. The authors caution, however, that in relatively simple task environments decision makers may not fully realize these creativity benefits; the payoff is concentrated in teams performing highly complex tasks.

**Limitations**
The authors cannot firmly establish causality; despite the two-wave design, the model should be read as suggesting rather than confirming the presumed causal sequence. Unmeasured variables could relate to both EOR and creativity (though several confounds were controlled), and the cognitive processes presumed to link EOR to communication-seeking were not measured. The communication-density measure relied on fixed-format egocentric network data (a maximum of five alters) aggregated to the team level, and its team-mean reliability (ICC(2) = .46) fell below the conventional .70 benchmark.

**Future Research**
The authors call for study of other social network types (e.g., advice or friendship ties) and possible curvilinear network–creativity relationships, and for concurrently assessing intra-team and extra-team networks under different employment forms. They also point to examining the nature and stability of ties (including instrumental/affective and kinship-like ties in the Chinese context), the cognitive processes linking EOR to communication, whether financial incentives enhance or mitigate the EOR–creativity effect, and whether cultural diversity or team members' cultural identity moderates the EOR–creativity relationship.
