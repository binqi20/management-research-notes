# Do Interviewers Sell Themselves Short? The Effects of Selling Orientation on Interviewers' Judgments

**Abstract**
Drawing on alternative perspectives about the automaticity of dispositional judgments, we examine whether the motivation to attract the other (i.e., selling orientation) in interpersonal first meetings (e.g., job interviews) helps or hinders the accuracy and validity of dispositional judgments. In a laboratory study (Study 1), we found that selling orientation reduced the accuracy of interviewers’ judgments about applicants’ core self-evaluations. Then, we investigated the real-world implications of selling orientation in a field study (Study 2) with two different samples (Samples A and B) and found that a selling orientation negatively influenced the predictive validity of interviewers’ judgments. Specifically, when selling orientation was low, interviewers’ judgments accurately predicted which applicants would be most (and least) successful as newcomers in the organization (in terms of citizenship, performance, and fit). However, when selling orientation was high, interviewers’ judgments no longer predicted applicant outcomes. Together, these results suggest that making dispositional judgments in interpersonal first meetings is an effortful process that is hindered by focusing on other goals (e.g., selling). We discuss the practical and theoretical implications of these findings.

**Research Question**
Does an interviewer's motivation to attract the applicant (a "selling orientation") during an interpersonal first meeting such as a job interview help or hinder the accuracy and predictive validity of that interviewer's dispositional judgments about the applicant — specifically, judgments of the applicant's core self-evaluations?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
The paper advances two competing (alternative) hypotheses drawn from opposing perspectives on judgment automaticity:
H1 (automaticity prediction): When interviewers adopt a stronger selling orientation during an interview, their judgments regarding interviewees' core self-evaluations will be more accurate.
H2 (motivated-cognition prediction): When interviewers adopt a stronger selling orientation during an interview, their judgments regarding interviewees' core self-evaluations will be less accurate.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Applicant core self-evaluations (Study 1: applicants' pre-interview self-ratings, plus a friend/family contact composite; Study 2: interviewers' ratings of applicant core self-evaluations)
- DV(s): Study 1 — interviewers' post-interview ratings of applicants' core self-evaluations, with accuracy operationalized as agreement with applicants' own self-ratings. Study 2 — newcomer success: school advocacy and number of job offers (Sample A); organizational citizenship behavior, value congruence, and principal-rated organizational contributions (Sample B)
- Moderator: Interviewer selling orientation (Study 1: manipulated high vs. low; Study 2: measured with a two-item scale)
- Mediators: None specified

A selling orientation is theorized as a motivation that directs the interviewer's limited attentional resources toward attracting the applicant and away from the process of forming dispositional judgments. Two accounts make opposite predictions: if such judgments are automatic (best made with little explicit attention), distraction by selling should improve accuracy; if they require effortful, controlled processing, diverting attention should degrade accuracy and predictive validity. Empirically, selling orientation operates as a moderator that weakens the link between an applicant's true disposition and the interviewer's rating (Study 1) and between the interviewer's rating and the applicant's later newcomer outcomes (Study 2).

**Data & Measures**
Study 1 (lab experiment): 64 participants recruited from a university research pool were randomly assigned to interviewer vs. applicant roles and to high vs. low selling-orientation conditions, then completed a 15-minute mock interview for a customer-service-manager role (materials adapted from Van Iddekinge, Raymark, & Roth, 2005). Core self-evaluations were measured with Judge et al.'s (2003) 12-item CSES: applicant self-ratings collected one week before the interview, interviewer ratings collected afterward, and friend/family contact ratings used to build a robustness "true-score" composite. Selling orientation was manipulated through role instructions and checked with a two-item interviewer-rated measure (alpha = .85). Analysis: OLS regression with an applicant-self-rating x selling-orientation interaction; random assignment supports causal inference. Study 2 (field): two samples of professional interviewers rated applicant CSES (adapted Judge et al. 2003 12-item scale) and reported selling orientation with the same two-item measure. Sample A (MBA admissions, 10 interviewers): DVs were school advocacy (2 items) and count of full-time job offers. Sample B (international teacher placement, 35 interviewers): DVs were organizational citizenship/helping behavior (Van Dyne & LePine, 1998), value congruence (Cable & DeRue, 2002), and principal-rated organizational contributions. Analysis: multilevel random-intercept regression with applicants nested in interviewers, and multilevel Poisson regression for the job-offer count, each testing an interviewer-CSES-rating x selling-orientation interaction. The Study 2 field design is associational (no manipulation).

**Key Findings**
Results supported H2 (the motivated-cognition prediction) and rejected H1 (the automaticity prediction) across all three samples. Study 1: a significant negative interaction between applicants' self-ratings of core self-evaluations and selling orientation on interviewers' ratings (b = -.66, t = -2.12, p < .05, R-squared = .25); when selling orientation was low, interviewer ratings tracked applicants' self-rated core self-evaluations (b = .46, t = 2.81, p < .01), but when it was high there was no significant relationship (b = -.19, t = -.74, n.s.). The pattern held using the applicant-plus-contact composite "true score" (interaction b = -.76, t = -2.36, p < .05). Study 2 Sample A: selling orientation negatively moderated the predictive validity of interviewer core-self-evaluation judgments for school advocacy (gamma = -.04, p < .05) and job offers (gamma = -.17, p < .05) — low selling meant interviewer ratings positively predicted outcomes, high selling meant no relationship. Study 2 Sample B: significant negative moderation for citizenship behavior (gamma = -.06, p < .05) and value congruence (gamma = -.07, p < .05); for organizational contributions the interaction was negative but not significant by a 95% standard (gamma = -.04, p < .06), which the authors report as not supported unless a one-tailed test is applied. Across studies, a stronger selling orientation consistently reduced the accuracy and predictive validity of interviewers' dispositional judgments.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes two contributions. First, it brings competing motivations (judgment vs. selling) into the study of organizational interviews, broadening "selling orientation" from an organization-induced recruitment stance or a sales-specific behavior into an individual-level motivation to attract another person in any interpersonal first meeting. Second, it probes the boundaries of judgment automaticity during naturally occurring social interactions where real outcomes are at stake, showing that forming accurate dispositional judgments in first meetings is not an effortless automatic process but requires dedicated cognitive attention — evidence favoring a motivated-social-cognition view over a pure automaticity view, and an organizational demonstration of inattentional blindness in social judgment.

**Practical Implication**
To maximize the utility of selection and placement interviews, the authors argue that firms should formally separate the applicant-evaluation process from the applicant-attraction (selling) process — either by having evaluation and selling conducted by different people or at different stages of the hiring process. They further suggest that interviewers should assess applicants' core self-evaluations and that firms should incorporate those judgments into their hiring decisions, since interviewers who are least distracted by selling make the most predictive judgments about which applicants will succeed.

**Limitations**
The studies do not test judgment automaticity directly. Study 1 relied on a mock-interview setting with non-professional (novice) interviewers and used a self/contact composite as the core-self-evaluation "true score," which may not perfectly reflect applicants' dispositions. The research examined only one trait (core self-evaluations); interview structure was measured with a single item and selling orientation with a two-item scale, so sub-facets and specific enactments of selling were not captured. The field evidence came from only 45 interviewers across just two organizations (Sample A had only 10 interviewers), and the studies cannot establish when or why a selling orientation is triggered.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on whether the effect generalizes to other dispositional traits and other future work behaviors; on the components and enactments of selling orientation (e.g., thinking about selling vs. specific selling behaviors); on when and why a selling orientation is triggered in real interactions; on larger and more varied samples of organizations that would permit examining the organizational expectations and incentives shaping interviewers' propensity to sell versus evaluate; and on extending the theory to other interpersonal first meetings, such as dating.
