# “Defining What We Do—All Over Again”: Occupational Identity, Technological Change, and the Librarian/Internet-Search Relationship

**Abstract**
Although a growing literature explores occupational identity, or the overlap between “who we are” and “what we do,” this literature has not fully considered how occupational identity may interact with technological change. In this paper, we explore this interaction, asking how an occupation’s identity shapes and is shaped by its interactions with a new technology. We focus, specifically, on the relationship between librarians and Internet search. Drawing on an analysis of 22 years of articles from library journals, we demonstrate how and why librarians initially discounted Internet search and differentiated themselves from it. We argue that these responses were associated with a “paradox of expertise,” by which librarians missed innovation opportunities around one of the most important information technologies in history precisely, and ironically, because of their deep knowledge of non-Internet searching. Later, however, we demonstrate how librarians engaged with this same technology, drawing upon it to redefine their occupational identity. Our findings demonstrate how occupational identity conditions the interpretation of a technology, while also showing how these interpretations can change with ongoing interactions. We also illustrate how occupational identity itself can change in response to new technology. Finally, we elaborate upon why expert insiders may not actually be best positioned to pursue emerging technologies.

**Research Question**
How does an occupation’s identity — the overlap between “who we are” and “what we do” — shape, and how is it shaped by, its interactions with a new technology? The authors pursue this through the mutually shaping relationship between librarians (as an occupation) and Internet search.

Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Focal construct: Occupational identity — the conjunction of “who we are” and “what we do” for an occupation (here, librarianship), defined relationally and treated as dynamic rather than static.
- Focal technology: Internet search, whose core function (information search) overlaps librarians’ identity-central task, making it an unusually direct “foil” for the occupation.
- Core mechanism — “paradox of expertise”: librarians’ deep mastery of (non-Internet) searching led them to judge Internet search against their own criterion (a search returns the single “correct” answer), dismiss it as a flawed niche, and miss innovation opportunities around it.
- Process (four eras / discursive responses): “Masters of Search” (describe-and-question / dismiss) → “Masters of Interpretation” (differentiate) → “Teachers of Search” (engage) → “Connectors of People and Information” (leverage).
- Co-shaping loop: occupational identity refracts how the technology is interpreted; using and interacting with the technology reshapes the identity; the changed identity then refracts the technology differently — a cycle that repeats and, in later eras, enables librarian-led innovation.
- Boundary conditions for the paradox: it is apt to arise when (1) the technology’s task overlaps a task closely tied to the occupation’s identity, (2) the technology performs that task in a way inconsistent with members’ understanding of how it should be done, and (3) the occupation has a strong, stable identity.

The paper models occupational identity as a lens (“like a lens that bears the imprint of the light it refracts”): identity conditions the interpretation and adoption of a technology, but sustained interaction with that technology then alters the identity, which in turn changes subsequent interpretations and innovation activity. Because librarians’ mastery of searching was central to who they were, an overlapping technology first triggered dismissal and differentiation (the paradox of expertise) and only later — as search improved and diffused — appropriation and leveraging, through a series of small steps that each opened space for the next.

**Data & Measures**
Interpretive, process-oriented qualitative design. Data corpus: 199 articles (final dataset) drawn from American Libraries (the ALA’s official journal) and Library Journal, the two most widely read U.S. library-field journals, whose authors and audience are librarians. Articles were retrieved via EBSCO’s Academic Source Premiere using the keywords “Internet,” “search,” and “web” for 1980–2010, excluding advertisements, book reviews, and Internet-filtering-only pieces. Each author independently read every article and coded its overall “discursive response,” yielding four response types (describe-and-question, differentiate, engage, leverage). Iterative grounded coding then moved from detailed codes to first-order categories (Table 1), second-order themes, and three aggregate dimensions (understanding of search, search tasks, and innovation activities); the prevalence of the four responses was traced over time (Figure 2) to bracket four eras. No quantitative measures or hypothesis tests; the output is a grounded process model (Figure 4).

**Key Findings**
The study surfaces a grounded process model of occupational-identity change rather than hypothesis tests. Initially (“Masters of Search,” through the mid-1990s), librarians discounted Internet search as a flawed, disorganized niche that violated their notion of a successful search (the single correct answer) and saw no identity threat — a paradox of expertise led them to miss innovation opportunities even though they had the technical capability to build search tools. As search technology improved and diffused, librarians’ identity shifted through three further eras — “Masters of Interpretation” (differentiate from search), “Teachers of Search” (engage with users and search firms), and “Connectors of People and Information” (leverage search into new work) — progressively ceding searching to Google and reorienting around user-focused connecting. The response mix tracked Internet diffusion: “differentiation” dominated when fewer than half of the U.S. population used the Internet, whereas “leveraging” dominated once the majority did. Throughout, occupational identity both shaped interpretations of the technology and was reshaped by interactions with it, and the redefined identity ultimately enabled librarian-led innovations (e.g., LibraryThing, Reference Extract, link resolvers, and data mining of library records).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions. First, it shows that occupational identity serves as an interpretive lens for a new technology, and that these interpretations shape both adoption decisions and ongoing interactions — linking technology interpretation to adoption and tracing interpretation over time (answering Leonardi and Barley’s call for longitudinal work). Second, it introduces the concept of a “paradox of expertise”: an occupation’s mastery of a task can, ironically, lead its members to dismiss innovation opportunities around that same task, adding to explanations (beyond cannibalization or lack of capability) for why insiders fail to innovate. Third, it demonstrates that technology can reshape occupational identity — identity defined not only by contrast but by appropriation — with implications for the literatures on occupations, professions, professionalization, and de-professionalization.

**Practical Implication**
Staying within the paper’s stated scope (occupations whose core tasks overlap an emerging technology), the case suggests that deep occupational expertise can become a liability, blinding members to innovation opportunities around a technology that mimics their core task. The authors speculate — explicitly tentatively — that occupations may be better equipped to avoid this paradox if they regularly refresh their ranks with new entrants (who are more open to outside innovations) and if they have a history of frequent identity shifts, so that the fluidity of occupational identity becomes an opportunity rather than a liability.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their model rests on a single case — librarians and Internet search — which bounds its transferability. Internet search itself expanded enormously over the period, a pattern that may have shaped librarians’ approaches and their success at narrowly focused innovation. Two features of the librarian case may not generalize: librarians had the technical skill to engage with and even shape search technology, and their practice and skills were not bound up in a single, replaceable technology, leaving them “room to maneuver.” The analysis is also confined to occupational identity, setting aside the multiple identities individuals hold simultaneously.

**Future Research**
The authors highlight two directions they find particularly intriguing: examining occupational-identity/technology dynamics outside the information-technology context (e.g., materials, transportation, or biotechnologies), and studying multiple identities — how individuals’ several simultaneous identities interact with one another as they engage technological change. They also point to studying occupations with less skill or less room to maneuver, and to how technology may drive de-professionalization, as avenues opened by their boundary conditions.
