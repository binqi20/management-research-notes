# Human Capital Flows: Using Context-Emergent Turnover (CET) Theory to Explore the Process by Which Turnover, Hiring, and Job Demands Affect Patient Satisfaction

**Abstract**
The dynamic systems view of voluntary turnover rates advocated in context-emergent turnover theory is used to explore how and why human capital flows impact unit performance over time. We examine hiring rates and employee transfer rates as distinct system components that work alongside voluntary turnover rates to affect job demands, and ultimately patient satisfaction. Our work explores this dynamic system of interrelated constructs, and explains and compares their mutual causality over time. The sample examined consists of 12 nursing units in a large hospital over 72 monthly observations, with patient satisfaction as the measure of unit performance.

**Research Question**
How and why do unit-level human capital flows — voluntary turnover, hiring, and employee transfers (in and out) — affect unit performance (patient satisfaction) over time? The study asks how changes in job demands mediate these effects, and how the relative magnitude and duration of each flow's effect unfold within a dynamic, mutually causal, and co-evolving system.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. The negative effects of (a) voluntary turnover rates and (b) transfer-out rates on patient satisfaction are partially mediated by job demands.
H2. The positive effects of (a) hiring rates and (b) transfer-in rates on patient satisfaction are partially mediated by job demands.
H3a. The immediate effect of voluntary turnover rates on job demands is more strongly negative than the immediate effect of transfer-out rates on job demands.
H3b. The immediate effect of voluntary turnover rates on patient satisfaction is more strongly negative than the immediate effect of transfer-out rates on patient satisfaction.
H3c. The immediate effect of transfer-in rates on patient satisfaction is more strongly positive than the immediate effect of hiring rates on patient satisfaction.
H4a. The negative effect of voluntary turnover rates on patient satisfaction has a longer duration than the negative effect of transfer-out rates on patient satisfaction.
H4b. The positive effect of hiring rates on patient satisfaction has a longer duration than the positive effect of transfer-in rates on patient satisfaction.
H5. The effect of voluntary turnover rates on patient satisfaction has a longer duration than the effect of voluntary turnover rates on job demands.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Unit-level human capital flows — voluntary turnover rates, hiring rates, transfer-out rates, transfer-in rates
- DV(s): Patient satisfaction (the measure of unit performance)
- Mediator(s): Job demands (required output relative to staffing; work intensity per nurse)
- Moderator(s): None formally tested; the study instead models the timing and duration of effects through a dynamic PVAR system

In CET theory's dynamic-systems view, human capital flows change a unit's stock of human capital, which shifts job demands — the amount of work faced by remaining nurses. Outflows (voluntary turnover and transfers-out) raise job demands, whereas inflows (hiring and transfers-in) can lower them, and job demands in turn drive patient satisfaction because nurses have more or less time for patient care. Because managers can counteract these flows (adjusting patient loads, timing transfers, accelerating hiring) and the system is mutually causal and co-evolving, the different flows are expected to differ in both the magnitude and the duration of their reverberations through the system.

**Data & Measures**
Data are 838 unit-month observations from 12 nursing units of one Midwestern U.S. university hospital system, observed 2003-2008; individual nurse career-path records (hire, turnover, transfer events) were aggregated into monthly unit-level rates. Patient satisfaction (DV): six nursing-focused items from the HCAHPS survey administered after each stay, aggregated to monthly unit level (ICC(1) = 0.12, ICC(2) = 0.94). Voluntary turnover rate: nurses voluntarily leaving the organization in a month / nurses at month start. Hiring rate: new external nurse hires / nurses at month start. Transfer-out and transfer-in rates: nurses transferred out of / into a unit / nurses at month start. The four flow-rate variables were transformed via the inverse hyperbolic sine. Job demands (mediator): monthly-adjusted patient-days / nurses employed that month. Prior staffing level (control): nurses employed at month start. Estimation: fixed effects panel regression with product-of-coefficients (Sobel) mediation tests for short-term effects, and panel vector autoregression (PVAR) via system GMM (forward de-meaning / Helmert procedure, impulse response functions, 1,000 Monte Carlo simulations for 90% confidence intervals, variance decompositions) for dynamic long-term effects. The design is observational panel data; "mutual causality" is inferred from the dynamic-systems PVAR model, not from randomization.

**Key Findings**
Short-term mediation (fixed effects, Table 3): voluntary turnover raised job demands (b = 13.30, p < .001) and so did transfer-out (b = 7.89, p < .001); hiring lowered job demands (b = -6.53, p < .001); transfer-in was not significant (b = 0.18, n.s.); job demands lowered patient satisfaction (b = -0.72, p < .01). Indirect effects on patient satisfaction through job demands were significant for voluntary turnover (a x b = -9.58, p < .05) and transfer-out (a x b = -5.68, p < .05), supporting H1a and H1b, and for hiring (a x b = 4.70, p < .05), supporting H2a; the transfer-in indirect effect was not significant (a x b = -0.13, n.s.), so H2b was not supported. Relative-magnitude comparisons (Chow tests) were in the predicted direction but had overlapping confidence intervals and non-significant z-values (z = 1.39, 0.36, 0.85), so H3a, H3b, and H3c were not supported. Dynamic duration (PVAR / impulse responses): voluntary turnover's negative effect on patient satisfaction emerged about month 3 and persisted through the 12-month window, whereas transfer-out's effect was immediate (month 0) but dissipated and was non-significant thereafter — supporting H4a; hiring's positive effect on patient satisfaction was significant from month 2 through month 12, whereas transfer-in was significant only in months 1, 3, and 5 — supporting H4b. Voluntary turnover's effects on both patient satisfaction and job demands persisted throughout the 12 months, so the satisfaction effect did not outlast the job-demands effect and H5 was not supported. The system accounted for about 24% of the variance in patient satisfaction over time.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study tests and extends context-emergent turnover (CET) theory. It validates the quantity dimension of collective turnover as a driver of unit performance; situates voluntary turnover within a broader, mutually causal system of human capital flows by distinctly modeling hiring and transfers (in and out); adds temporal precision by showing that different flows differ in the duration of their effects; and identifies job demands as the previously omitted causal mechanism linking quantity changes in human capital to unit outcomes. Methodologically, it introduces panel vector autoregression (PVAR) to management research as a way to model dynamic, co-evolving human-capital systems.

**Practical Implication**
For managers, unit-level voluntary turnover is negatively related to patient satisfaction while hiring is positively related to it, and much of each effect runs through job demands. Managers can therefore mitigate turnover's effects by managing job demands — using transfers and hiring, ideally before or concurrent with expected turnover, and by holding some slack human capital resources so remaining nurses' workloads do not spike. Hiring ahead of anticipated human capital degradation (and, for some outcomes, favoring external hiring over transfers-in despite higher cost) can reduce performance declines, a decision that is likely more important for highly complex or team-oriented tasks.

**Limitations**
The study could not incorporate individual differences or the quality of the human capital flowing in and out; 76% of the variance in patient satisfaction remained unexplained. Data came from a single hospital and a single job (nurse), which aids internal comparability but limits generalizability across industries and contexts. The 2003-2008 window excluded events such as the post-2008 downturn, and month-long observation units may be too coarse to capture the full process (weeks might reveal stronger, finer-grained effects). The small number of units (12) creates power limitations that may mask relationships, and standard significance testing could not establish whether the observed duration differences (e.g., 12 months vs. one month) were statistically different.

**Future Research**
Future research should quantify the quality of the human capital resource flowing in and out, not just its quantity, including measuring firm-specific knowledge and the social integration of interdependent work units. Research is also needed on how different contexts and industries — with different levels of workflow controllability, replacement timelines, opportunities to use transfers, and standardization of procedures — alter the relationships among these constructs.
