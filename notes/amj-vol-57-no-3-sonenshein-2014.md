# How Organizations Foster the Creative Use of Resources

**Abstract**
Using a multi-year qualitative study, I explain how employees at a fast-growing retail organization used creative resourcing—that is, the manipulation and recombination of objects in novel and useful ways to solve problems. I induce two core organizational processes (autonomous resourcing and directed resourcing) that explain how organizations foster ongoing creative activities in response to different perceived resource endowments. In doing so, I add clarity to a mixed literature that argues on the one hand that limited resources foster creativity, and on the other, that abundant resources foster creativity. Instead, I reorient the questions that scholars ask by shifting the conversation away from variance-explanation models and towards understanding organizational processes, specifically around how employees use resources in dynamic ways and how managers enable them to do so. My study unpacks how the link between resources and creativity is rooted deeply in the actions of managers and employees embedded in organizations over time. In elaborating theory around these actions, I contribute to scholarly and practitioner understanding around how organizations foster creativity in a variety of resource environments.

**Research Question**
How do resources shape creative activities inside organizations over time? The paper asks how managers foster ongoing creative activity as an organization's perceived resource endowment changes, shifting attention from the quantity of resources to the actions through which managers and employees turn objects into resources.

**Mechanism Process**
- Aggregate dimensions (grounded data structure): perceived resource endowment, resourcing identity, regulating objects, creative resourcing, problem solving.
- Creative resourcing forms: manipulating (altering a single object) and recombining (altering the relationships among a set of objects) in novel and useful ways to solve problems.
- Autonomous resourcing (perceived limited resources; family era): managers foster a "resourcing identity" through cultivating ownership schemas and cultivating creator schemas, after which creative resourcing proceeds with minimal ongoing managerial control, yielding original solutions.
- Directed resourcing (perceived abundant resources; investor era): managers "regulate objects" by withholding fixed objects (e.g., planograms) and provisioning dynamic objects (e.g., trend alerts, stories, broad guidelines), exerting more control while sustaining creative resourcing, yielding heterogeneous solutions.
- Transition: problem solving under autonomous resourcing improves managers' perceived resource endowment (via resource-conservation, endogenous-resourcefulness, and knowledge-based mechanisms), prompting a shift toward directed resourcing.

The study elaborates a process model in which the link between resources and creativity runs through the actions and interpretations of managers and employees rather than through resource quantity. Under a perceived limited endowment, managers shape employees' identities as creative owners, and employees enact that identity by manipulating and recombining available objects. Under a perceived abundant endowment, managers instead regulate the flow of objects—withholding those employees would read as fixed and provisioning those they would read as dynamic—so that creativity persists but is more guided. Both processes are recursive: solving problems reinforces the identity or generates new objects to resource, and can itself raise the perceived resource endowment.

**Data & Measures**
Single-site, longitudinal inductive case study of "BoutiqueCo" (pseudonym), a fast-growing U.S. specialty retailer transforming from a family business into a large public chain. Data: 60 interviews (19 home-office employees and regional managers, including most of the top management team, plus 41 store employees), 62.5 hours of (mostly participant) observation, and 151 documents. Analysis followed a grounded-theory approach (Charmaz; Glaser & Strauss) using the Gioia methodology: open/in-vivo coding, then second-order/axial coding into five aggregate theoretical dimensions, with memo writing, constant comparison, triangulation across data sources, member checking, and peer debriefing, managed in NVivo 8.0. The design is interpretive and theory-elaborating; no causal identification is claimed.

**Key Findings**
The study induces two organizational processes through which managers foster creative resourcing, contingent on their perceived resource endowment. Under autonomous resourcing (perceived limited resources, family era), managers foster creativity indirectly by shaping employees' resourcing identity through ownership and creator schemas; employees then creatively resource—manipulating and recombining objects—with minimal managerial control, producing original solutions "not imaginable by corporate managers." Under directed resourcing (perceived abundant resources, investor era), managers exert more control by regulating objects—withholding fixed objects (planograms) and provisioning dynamic objects (trend alerts, stories, broad guidelines)—which sustains creative resourcing but yields more heterogeneous (somewhat less fully original) solutions. Problem solving under autonomous resourcing can improve the perceived resource endowment, moving the organization toward directed resourcing. The overarching finding is that both limited and abundant resource endowments can foster creativity; what matters is how managers and employees interpret and act on those endowments, not the quantity of resources per se.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper elaborates theory that integrates creativity and resourcing research, reorienting creativity scholarship away from variance-explanation models (resource quantity → creative output) toward organizational processes and the situated actions of managers and employees over time. Adopting a resourcing lens, it treats resources not as fixed entities but as objects individuals act on, and shows that managerial action—fostering a resourcing identity (autonomous resourcing) and regulating objects (directed resourcing)—shapes how creative resourcing unfolds as an organization grows. To the resourcing literature it adds creativity as a foundational skill for resourcing and shows that resourcing is not only a bottom-up employee process but is also permitted and guided by managers.

**Practical Implication**
The paper offers two practical implications for managers. First, rather than focusing on the amount of resources available, managers can focus on the actions they and their employees take in response to their perceived resource endowment, choosing between autonomous resourcing (less ongoing managerial involvement and control, but more potential for original solutions) and directed resourcing (greater control, but dependent on a repertoire of objects to provision). Second, managers can spark creative activity by fostering a resourcing identity and by regulating—withholding and provisioning—objects, adding new levers for influencing employee creativity beyond personal characteristics, intrinsic motivation, and leader–member exchange.

**Limitations**
The study is a single-site case study of one retailer, chosen as an extreme case to elaborate theory by compressing the time frame of organizational growth. The author notes that relying on a key informant risks a partial interpretation of the organization, a risk mitigated by drawing on multiple data sources. Several claims—notably the shift from autonomous to directed resourcing and the suggested tradeoff whereby directed resourcing may favor usefulness over novelty—are described as only suggestive, tentative, or speculative and are flagged as needing further research.

**Future Research**
The paper explicitly invites further work on how managers learn to resource in more helpful ways, and on a closer dialogue between resourcing scholars and more psychologically oriented scholars who study the antecedents of creative acts. It also calls for more research to substantiate the tentative tradeoff between novelty and usefulness across autonomous versus directed resourcing, and for further exploration of how the usefulness of creative solutions is contextually dependent on an organization's lifecycle stage.
