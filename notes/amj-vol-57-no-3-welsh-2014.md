# Conscience without Cognition: The Effects of Subconscious Priming on Ethical Behavior

**Abstract**
Research in the field of behavioral ethics has traditionally viewed ethical decision making as rational and deliberate. However, some recent research has proposed a dual process model of ethical decision making that has both conscious and subconscious components (Reynolds, 2006). We extend current theory by using subconscious ethical and unethical priming to test the effects of subconscious processes on ethical behavior through an automatic process of schema activation and implicit association. Studies 1 and 2 extend self-concept maintenance theory (Mazar, Amir, & Ariely, 2008) by exploring the mediated process through which subconscious ethical and unethical primes trigger the activation of moral standards, thereby influencing categorization and subsequent responses to morally ambiguous situations. Study 3 demonstrates that both subconscious ethical and unethical priming reduce dishonesty even when participants are unmonitored and are given difficult performance goals that previously have been shown to lead to unethical behavior.

**Research Question**
Can subconscious, automatic processes—not just conscious, deliberate reasoning—shape ethical behavior? Specifically, does subconscious ethical and unethical priming activate moral standards and thereby increase ethical categorization and reduce unethical behavior, even under conflicting conscious objectives such as high performance goals and external monitoring?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1a: Priming subconscious ethical and unethical content will activate moral standards.
H1b: Priming subconscious ethical and unethical content will increase the likelihood that morally ambiguous situations will be categorized as unethical.
H1c: Activation of moral standards will mediate the relationship between priming subconscious ethical and unethical content and the categorization of morally ambiguous situations.
H2a: People will behave more unethically when they have high performance goals than when they have "do your best" (DYB) goals.
H2b: Priming subconscious ethical and unethical content will reduce unethical behavior when people are given high performance goals.
H3a: People will behave more unethically when they are not monitored than when they are monitored.
H3b: Priming subconscious ethical and unethical content will reduce unethical behavior when people are unmonitored.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Subconscious priming (ethical / unethical / neutral) via a sentence-unscrambling task; performance goals (high vs. "do your best"); external monitoring (monitored vs. unmonitored); (Study 2) conscious activation of moral standards via a recall task.
- DV(s): Activation of moral standards (moral sensitivity and moral identity, Study 1); categorization of a morally ambiguous scenario as unethical (Study 1); reported likelihood of unethical behavior (Study 2); actual cheating via over-reported matrix performance (Study 3).
- Mediators: Activation of moral standards (mediates priming → ethical categorization).
- Moderators: Performance goals and external monitoring (condition when priming reduces unethical behavior).

Integrating Reynolds' (2006) neurocognitive dual-process model with self-concept maintenance theory, the authors argue that subconscious stimuli containing moral content are implicitly matched to moral schemas through the automatic "X-system," activating an individual's moral standards. Activated standards raise the propensity to categorize morally ambiguous situations as unethical and to align behavior with self-concept, thereby reducing unethical conduct. Because the mechanism runs through internal psychological processes, subconscious priming can counteract conscious pressures—high performance goals that divert attention from moral standards—especially when external monitoring is absent.

**Data & Measures**
Study 1 (lab, final n = 103 undergraduates): subconscious priming manipulated with a 20-sentence unscrambling task (12 ethics-related sentences in the ethical condition; 8 in the unethical condition); categorization coded dichotomously from whether participants raised any ethics issue in Butterfield et al.'s (2000) mystery-shopper dilemma; activation of standards measured as moral sensitivity (two items, α = .74) and Aquino and Reed's (2002) 10-item moral identity scale (α = .75); a funnel-debrief awareness check excluded participants who detected the prime; mediation tested with Preacher and Hayes' (2008) bootstrapped multiple-mediation (1,000 resamples, bias-corrected 95% CIs). Study 2 (online mTurk, final n = 291 adults; 3 subconscious-prime × 2 conscious-recall design): DV was self-reported likelihood of unethical behavior on a seven-point scale using Gino, Norton, and Ariely's (2010) toxic-pesticide scenario; an additional 168 participants compared the unscrambling prime against a Ten Commandments recall prime. Study 3 (lab, final n = 312 business students; 3 prime × 2 goal × 2 monitoring design): DV was actual cheating measured as over-reported correct answers on Mazar et al.'s (2008) 20-matrix addition task, analyzed with a three-way ANOVA. Random assignment was used across the laboratory and online experiments, and the authors state the laboratory design let them "infer causality" between subconscious priming and ethics-related outcomes.

**Key Findings**
All hypotheses were supported. Study 1: H1a supported—ethical and unethical priming raised moral sensitivity (5.30 and 4.92 vs. 4.00 neutral) and moral identity (5.73 and 5.65 vs. 5.23 neutral); H1b supported—ethics-issue categorization was 58% (ethical) and 56% (unethical) vs. 28% (neutral); H1c supported—activation of moral standards fully mediated the priming→categorization effect (significant bootstrapped indirect effects; the direct effect dropped from significant to non-significant). Study 2: participants whose standards were activated (subconsciously, consciously, or both) reported significantly less unethical behavior than the neutral/neutral control (3.59 vs. 2.61–2.84); subconscious priming reduced unethical behavior under neutral recall but was attenuated when combined with conscious recall (a ceiling on activation), and the subconscious prime was less consciously detected than the Ten Commandments prime. Study 3: H2a supported—high goals nearly doubled over-reporting (mean .61 vs. .35 DYB; F = 5.15, p < .05); H2b supported—ethical/unethical priming reduced cheating under high goals (priming main effect F = 4.21, p < .05); H3a supported—monitoring reduced cheating (F = 5.61, p < .05); H3b supported—when unmonitored, over-reporting was more than double under neutral (1.02) vs. ethical (.35) and unethical (.45) priming (priming × monitoring interaction F = 3.84, p < .05), while priming had little effect when participants were monitored; the three-way interaction was non-significant.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends self-concept maintenance theory (Mazar et al., 2008) by integrating it with Reynolds' (2006) neurocognitive dual-process model to show that subconscious primes—not only conscious moral reminders—can activate existing moral standards and reduce unethical behavior. It advances four points: it tests the mediating mechanism linking subconscious processes to ethical behavior; shows ethical and unethical priming operate similarly by triggering schemas people already possess rather than changing their values; provides a first demonstrated mechanism for reducing goal-induced unethicality; and shows subconscious priming is most effective when individuals are unmonitored.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations, the authors suggest that subconsciously priming ethical behavior—through the images, symbols, stories, and slogans that comprise organizational culture (e.g., a fire-alarm poster calling attention to unethical conduct on campus, or In-N-Out's discreet Bible-verse notations)—may activate employees' existing moral standards and reduce unethical behavior. This may matter because today's organizations have fewer resources to monitor employees, and priming reduced dishonesty even for unmonitored individuals strongly committed to difficult performance goals. Priming works by activating standards employees already hold rather than by altering their moral values.

**Limitations**
The mediated process was tested in Studies 1 and 2 but not in Study 3. The studies could not test how long the effects of subconscious priming persist over time. Subconscious ethical priming also requires that individuals already possess the appropriate moral schemas, so effects may be attenuated in contexts lacking widely accepted norms for ethical behavior.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how long subconscious priming effects last; on the effectiveness of other forms of subconscious primes (images, symbols, words, and sounds across a variety of contexts); on the effects of subconscious priming on unethical behaviors of various types and magnitudes; on individual-level characteristics (e.g., people who rely more on intuition than cognition); and on links between subconscious priming and other organizational theories, including whether priming increases prosocial behaviors such as organizational citizenship behaviors (altruism, civic virtue, and voice).
