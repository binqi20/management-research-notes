# It's Not Fair … Or Is It? The Role of Justice and Leadership in Explaining Work Stressor–Job Performance Relationships

**Abstract**
We develop and test a theoretical model of multilevel moderated mediation in which organizational justice serves as an intervening mechanism that explains associations among two dimensions of work stressors (challenges and hindrances) and five dimensions of job performance (task performance, helping, voice, counterproductive behavior, and creativity) over and above the intervening role of strain. We also consider how leadership influences the intervening role of justice in the stressor–job performance relationships by virtue of the effect it has on how stressors are interpreted with regard to fairness. Results of a study of 339 employees and their supervisors provide support for this model across dimensions of performance. Somewhat unexpectedly, the moderating effect of leadership is found to be contingent on the type of leadership and the type of stressors. Transactional leaders reduce the negative effect of hindrance stressors on job performance because they weaken the negative link between hindrance stressors and justice perceptions. Alternatively, transformational leaders enhance the positive effect of challenge stressors on job performance because they foster a positive link between challenge stressors and justice perceptions. We discuss how this intriguing pattern of moderated mediation could be explained by using theory and research on regulatory focus.

**Research Question**
How and why do challenge and hindrance stressors differentially relate to employee job performance? Specifically, does organizational justice operate as an intervening mechanism linking the two stressor dimensions to five job-performance dimensions over and above strain, and how do unit-level transformational and transactional leadership moderate the stressor–justice relationships?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1a. Organizational justice perceptions mediate the negative relationship between hindrance stressors and job performance.
H1b. Organizational justice perceptions mediate the positive relationship between challenge stressors and job performance.
H2a. Transactional leadership moderates the (negative) hindrance stressor–justice relationship such that it is less negative when transactional leadership is high.
H2b. Transactional leadership moderates the negative indirect effect of hindrance stressors on job performance via justice such that it is less negative when transactional leadership is high.
H3a. Transactional leadership moderates the (positive) challenge stressor–justice relationship such that it is more positive when transactional leadership is high.
H3b. Transactional leadership moderates the positive indirect effect of challenge stressors on job performance via justice such that it is more positive when transactional leadership is high.
H4a. Transformational leadership moderates the (negative) hindrance stressor–justice relationship such that it is less negative when transformational leadership is high.
H4b. Transformational leadership moderates the negative indirect effect of hindrance stressors on job performance via justice such that it is less negative when transformational leadership is high.
H5a. Transformational leadership moderates the (positive) challenge stressor–justice relationship such that it is more positive when transformational leadership is high.
H5b. Transformational leadership moderates the indirect effect of challenge stressors on job performance via justice such that it is more positive when transformational leadership is high.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Challenge stressors; hindrance stressors (individual level, L1)
- DV(s): Job performance — task performance, helping, voice, counterproductive behavior, creativity (supervisor-rated, L1)
- Mediators: Organizational justice perceptions (focal mechanism); strain / exhaustion (competing mediator modeled simultaneously)
- Moderators: Transactional leadership; transformational leadership (unit level, L2)

Grounded in social exchange theory (Blau, 1964) and Folger and Cropanzano's (1998) fairness theory, the model argues that employees appraise stressful demands against the reciprocity norms of their exchange relationship with the organization. Hindrance stressors imply contributions with little prospect of return, violating reciprocity, so they are perceived as unfair and prompt employees to withhold performance contributions. Challenge stressors carry the promise of future benefits and growth, are consistent with reciprocity, and are therefore perceived as fair and reciprocated with performance. This justice pathway is proposed to operate over and above the conventional strain/energy-depletion pathway. Leaders, as sense-givers and resource allocators, shape how stressors are interpreted with regard to fairness: transactional (contingent-reward) behavior frames demands as "part of the deal," buffering the hindrance–justice link, whereas transformational behavior fosters a meaning-and-growth framing that strengthens the challenge–justice link.

**Data & Measures**
Data: 339 full-time white-collar employees and their 88 supervisors from automobile-manufacturing and pharmaceutical firms in eastern China; surveys translated and back-translated into Chinese. Two-wave, two-source lagged design (employee survey first; supervisor performance ratings about three weeks later).

Measures (employee-reported unless noted):
- Challenge stressors: 6 items (workload, time pressure, task complexity, responsibility); frequency-rated 1–5.
- Hindrance stressors: 7 items (role ambiguity, role/interpersonal conflict, politics, hassles, red tape); frequency-rated 1–5.
- Strain: 4 exhaustion items from the Maslach Burnout Inventory (Maslach et al., 1996).
- Organizational justice: Colquitt's (2001) 19-item measure (distributive, procedural, interpersonal, informational), modeled as a second-order overall-justice factor.
- Transformational / transactional leadership: MLQ Form 5X (Bass & Avolio, 1995) — transformational = 20 items across four dimensions; transactional = 4-item contingent-rewards subscale; aggregated to unit-level means (L2).
- Job performance (supervisor-rated): task performance (4 items; Williams & Anderson, 1991), helping (4) and voice (4) (Van Dyne & LePine, 1998), counterproductive behavior (5; Bennett & Robinson, 2000), and creative behavior (5; George & Zhou, 2001; Scott & Bruce, 1994).

Analysis: multilevel SEM (MSEM) in Mplus 6.11 with level-1 stressors group-mean centered and level-2 leadership grand-mean centered; job tenure and industry controlled on all endogenous variables; bias-corrected 95% CIs for indirect effects from 20,000 Monte Carlo re-samples. The design is cross-sectional/associational — the authors do not claim causal identification.

**Key Findings**
Justice pathway (H1). Hindrance stressors were negatively related to organizational justice (γ = −.57, p < .05); challenge stressors were unrelated to justice (γ = .10, p > .05). Justice predicted all five performance dimensions (task β = .37; helping β = .32; voice β = .30; creative β = .32; counterproductive β = −.19; all p < .05). **H1a supported** — hindrance stressors' average indirect effects via justice were significant for all five outcomes (task ρ = −.21; helping −.18; voice −.17; creative −.18; counterproductive +.11; all p < .05). **H1b not supported** — challenge stressors' indirect effects via justice were nonsignificant (ρ ≈ .02–.04; all p > .05).

Strain pathway (comparison). Both stressors related to strain (hindrance γ = .50; challenge γ = .17; both p < .05), but in the full model strain predicted only counterproductive behavior (β = .12, p < .05); strain-mediated indirect effects were significant only for counterproductive behavior.

Leadership moderation. **H2a supported** — transactional leadership moderated the hindrance–justice link (γ = .32, p < .05; simple slope −.24 when high vs −.78 when low). **H2b supported** — the hindrance→justice→performance indirect effects differed by transactional leadership for all five outcomes (e.g., task diff = .24, p < .05). **H3a and H3b not supported** (transactional × challenge: γ = −.28, p > .05; indirect-effect differences ns). **H4a and H4b not supported** (transformational × hindrance: γ = −.22, p > .05; indirect-effect differences ns). **H5a supported** — transformational leadership moderated the challenge–justice link (γ = .32, p < .05; simple slope .28 when high vs .03 when low). **H5b supported** — the challenge→justice→performance indirect effects differed by transformational leadership for all five outcomes (e.g., task diff = .24, p < .05).

Summary — supported: H1a, H2a, H2b, H5a, H5b; not supported: H1b, H3a, H3b, H4a, H4b. The unexpected asymmetry (transactional leadership matters for hindrance stressors, transformational leadership for challenge stressors) suggests a contingency rather than a simple augmentation view of leadership; results also held for a single overall-performance latent factor.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study positions organizational justice as a new intervening mechanism — beyond the conventional strain/energy-depletion account — that explains how challenge and hindrance stressors translate into job performance, integrating the stress, organizational-justice, and leadership literatures through social exchange theory. It shows that leadership's role in the stress process is contingent on stressor type: transactional leadership is more influential in buffering the negative effect of hindrance stressors on justice, whereas transformational leadership is more effective in enhancing the positive effect of challenge stressors on justice. This asymmetric, contingency pattern reframes the augmentation view of transformational–transactional leadership, and the authors advance regulatory-focus theory as a post hoc explanation for it.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue organizations should take steps to minimize hindrance stressors — for example, using employee task forces to identify and resolve them — since justice's encompassing effects may make such initiatives more valuable than previously thought. They suggest stress management can be improved by shaping how employees judge the fairness of demands, leveraging "justice rules" and more transparent, informative communication about why demands exist and how coping is acknowledged and rewarded. Finally, because leaders shape stressor–justice appraisals, they argue for leadership training and development that fosters both transformational and transactional behaviors and helps leaders identify challenge versus hindrance stressors and apply the fitting style.

**Limitations**
The relationships are cross-sectional, so alternative pathways and feedback loops cannot be ruled out, though the multi-source, multilevel, lagged, moderated design mitigates this concern. Employees provided ratings of the independent variables, moderators, and mediators, raising a common-source-variance concern, though the outcomes were rated by a second source (supervisors). The study did not include a direct measure of the social-exchange process presumed to transmit stressor effects to justice. The broad, averaged conceptualization of justice is somewhat atypical, complicating direct comparison with prior dimension-specific research and potentially masking source-specific justice effects.

**Future Research**
The authors call for longitudinal and experimental designs to further rule out alternative pathways, and for measures from additional sources to further address common-source concerns. They suggest extending the mediational chain to include a direct measure of social exchange and examining the "micro-mediations" within the model. They encourage studying features of stressful demands that evoke different fairness perceptions and contextual factors such as leadership that shape them, better understanding the offsetting moderating effects (the compatibility of work demands and leader behaviors), and testing the regulatory-focus explanation directly. They also point to testing the mediating role of narrower, specific justice dimensions and to examining how strain/energy-depletion processes drive counterproductive behavior.
