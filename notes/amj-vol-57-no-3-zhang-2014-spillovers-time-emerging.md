# FDI Spillovers Over Time in an Emerging Market: The Roles of Entry Tenure and Barriers to Imitation

**Abstract**
In this study, we examine how foreign direct investment (FDI) spillovers to domestic firms in an emerging market occur over time. From the organizational learning perspective, we propose that, as entry tenure of foreign firms in an industry increases, domestic firms can learn from the foreign firms over time and improve their productivity. We further build upon the competitor imitation argument to propose that this effect will be stronger when barriers to imitation faced by the domestic firms are lower. Based upon a comprehensive panel dataset on manufacturing firms in China in 1998–2007, our findings strongly support these arguments. We find that entry tenure of foreign firms in an industry has a positive relationship with the productivity of individual domestic firms in the same industry, albeit at a diminishing rate. We also find that this positive relationship is stronger when the foreign firms have lower export intensity, lower intangible asset intensity, and have followed a more rhythmic (i.e., less irregular) entry pattern—situations characterizing lower barriers to imitation.

**Research Question**
How do FDI spillovers to domestic firms in an emerging market occur over time, and how do barriers to imitation—shaped by attributes of the foreign firms—affect the role of time in these spillovers?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. The entry tenure of foreign firms in an industry has a positive relationship with the productivity of an individual domestic firm in the same industry.
H2. That positive relationship becomes weaker (i.e., increases at a diminishing rate) as the foreign firms' entry tenure continues to increase.
H3. The positive relationship is stronger when the foreign firms have lower export intensity.
H4. The positive relationship is stronger when the foreign firms have lower intangible asset intensity.
H5. The positive relationship is stronger if the foreign firms have followed a more rhythmic (i.e., less irregular) pattern of entering the industry.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Entry tenure of foreign firms in an industry (asset-weighted mean number of years since foreign firms were founded in China).
- DV(s): Productivity of individual domestic firms (log output from a Cobb–Douglas production function).
- Mediators: None formally tested. The four theorized spillover channels—demonstration effect, employee turnover, local business linkages, and competitive pressure—are argued to require time but are not measured as mediators.
- Moderators: Foreign firms' export intensity; foreign firms' intangible asset intensity; rhythm of foreign firms' entry pattern. A squared entry-tenure term captures the diminishing (curvilinear) effect.

From an organizational-learning view, as foreign firms' entry tenure in an industry lengthens, domestic firms have more time to observe, imitate, and assimilate the foreign firms' advanced technologies and management practices, raising their productivity—but at a diminishing rate, because obvious learning opportunities gradually dry up and remaining foreign advantages become tacit and idiosyncratic. Drawing on the competitor-imitation/barriers-to-imitation argument, the speed of this learning ("imitation from a distance") depends on how imitable the foreign firms are: lower export intensity, lower intangible-asset intensity, and a more rhythmic entry pattern reduce information asymmetry and causal ambiguity, thereby strengthening the tenure–productivity relationship.

**Data & Measures**
Data source: the Chinese National Bureau of Statistics (CNBS) Annual Industrial Survey Database (1998–2007), covering all state-owned firms and larger non-state-owned firms (annual sales of RMB 5 million or above) in manufacturing. DV: domestic firm productivity measured as log output—firm annual sales deflated to year-2000 constant prices—within a log-linear Cobb–Douglas production function that also includes log capital and log labor inputs. IV: industry-level entry tenure of foreign firms = asset-weighted mean of individual foreign firms' years since founding in China. Moderators: export intensity = foreign firms' export sales divided by total sales; intangible asset intensity = foreign firms' intangible assets divided by total assets; rhythm of entry pattern = kurtosis of the entry-tenure distribution (recoded ×−1 so higher values indicate more rhythmic entry). Estimation: firm-fixed-effect ("within") transformation with year, industry, and region dummies and one-year-lagged predictors; standard errors clustered at the firm level; an inverse Mills ratio included to correct survival bias. Identification is associational (a fixed-effects panel design), not a natural or randomized experiment.

**Key Findings**
All five hypotheses are supported in the main firm-fixed-effect model (Table 2, Model 3). H1 supported: entry tenure of foreign firms is positively related to domestic firm productivity (b = 0.016, p < .001). H2 supported: the squared entry-tenure term is negative (b = −0.002, p < .001), so the positive effect diminishes as tenure grows (the slope flattens but does not turn negative); as entry tenure rose from 1 to 10 years, a domestic firm's output increased by about 14.4%. H3 supported: entry tenure × export intensity is negative (b = −0.006, p < .001)—the relationship is stronger when foreign export intensity is lower. H4 supported: entry tenure × intangible asset intensity is negative (b = −0.032, p < .001)—stronger when foreign intangible-asset intensity is lower. H5 supported: entry tenure × rhythm is positive (b = 6.95E-04, p < .001)—stronger when foreign firms entered more rhythmically. Robustness: results hold using domestic firm survival (Gompertz model) as an alternative outcome (H1–H4 consistent; H5 not supported there), a post-1998 left-censoring subsample, within-/outside-region and country-origin splits, alternative ownership cut-offs, and random subsamples; with longer lags H1–H2 stay robust, H3 is the most robust moderator and H5 the least (none of the three moderators holds at a 5-year lag).

**Theoretical Contribution**
This is, to the authors' knowledge, the first empirical study to explicitly examine the role of time—the inter-temporal dynamics—in FDI spillovers, shifting the literature from a static "snapshot" view of FDI presence to a dynamic view centered on foreign firms' entry tenure. By integrating the organizational-learning perspective with the competitor-imitation/barriers-to-imitation argument, it shows that domestic firms' learning "from a distance" accrues over time but only up to a limit. It also repositions foreign firms—previously treated as "black boxes" and studied mainly through domestic firms' absorptive capacity—as knowledge-source firms whose attributes (export intensity, intangible-asset intensity, entry-pattern rhythm) govern the speed at which spillovers occur.

**Practical Implication**
The findings speak to MNC managers, domestic managers, and policy makers in emerging markets. For MNC managers, the results clarify how, and under what conditions, host-country domestic firms learn from them over time and when the MNCs are more or less vulnerable to domestic imitation. For domestic managers in emerging markets, the benefits of FDI spillovers take time to materialize (roughly a 10–12-year window before additional learning gains flatten) and are greatest when the "targets" for learning are foreign firms with a local-market focus and high tangible-asset intensity. For policy makers in emerging markets, bringing in FDI at a more rhythmic pace helps domestic firms observe and learn from entrants, and pressuring foreign firms to export may delay domestic firms' learning.

**Limitations**
The authors note that they captured the role of time only through foreign firms' entry tenure, which gives an overall picture but not more specific temporal dimensions (e.g., time elapsed since particular FDI milestone events). They also examined only three foreign-firm attributes that shape barriers to imitation, leaving other potential sources of such barriers unexamined. Finally, the study is confined to FDI spillovers in China, so whether the findings generalize to other emerging markets remains unanswered.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research that (a) examines more specific dimensions of time, such as time elapsed since milestone events—for example, a major MNC's significant investment in an industry, or the point at which foreign firms begin purchasing from local suppliers; (b) investigates other sources of barriers to imitation, such as foreign firms' strategic alliances with domestic firms, through which knowledge may be passed on to domestic non-partner firms; and (c) replicates the study with firms in other emerging markets (e.g., Romania, Kenya, Ecuador) to test whether the core ideas generalize beyond China.
