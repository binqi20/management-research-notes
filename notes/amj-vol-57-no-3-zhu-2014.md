# How Directors' Prior Experience with Other Demographically Similar CEOs Affects Their Appointments onto Corporate Boards and the Consequences for CEO Compensation

**Abstract**
In recent years, new director appointments have increasingly posed a dilemma for corporate leaders: while CEOs prefer individuals who have similar backgrounds to them, they face increased pressure to appoint new directors who have a different demographic profile. We suggest that CEOs may resolve this dilemma by appointing new directors who have prior experiences working with other demographically similar CEOs. We then explain why this tendency is stronger when new directors are demographically more different from CEOs. Moreover, we posit that new directors’ prior experiences with other similar CEOs will reduce the negative effect of their demographic differences from the CEO on CEO compensation. Longitudinal analysis of Fortune 500 companies’ new director appointments and subsequent CEO compensation provided support for our theoretical expectations. This study identifies an important new role that interlock ties to other CEOs can play in corporate governance and leadership. In particular, we suggest that such ties are a means by which CEOs evaluate whether a new director will support their leadership and decision making. In explaining the role of directors’ ties to other CEOs in influencing director appointments and CEO compensation, this study also highlights the important influence of triads on CEO-director dyadic relations.

**Research Question**
How do CEOs resolve the dilemma of appointing demographically dissimilar new directors, under external pressure to do so, despite their preference for similar individuals who are likely to support their leadership and decision making? The study further asks how such appointments, working through directors' prior interlock ties to other similar CEOs, affect subsequent CEO compensation.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. The focal CEO's power will be positively associated with a new director's prior experience with other CEOs who are demographically similar to the focal CEO.
H2. The demographic difference between a new director and the focal CEO will strengthen the positive relationship between the focal CEO's power and the new director's prior experience with other CEOs who are demographically similar to the focal CEO.
H3. New directors' demographic difference from the CEO will reduce the CEO's compensation to a smaller degree when new directors have more prior experience with other CEOs who are demographically similar to the focal CEO.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Focal CEO's power (H1); new directors' demographic difference from the CEO (main predictor of lower CEO compensation, H3)
- DV(s): A new director's prior experience with other demographically similar CEOs (appointment analysis, H1/H2); CEO compensation in year t+1 (compensation analysis, H3)
- Mediators: None empirically tested; the argued intervening construct is the focal CEO's perception of a new director's social acceptability
- Moderators: New director's demographic difference from the CEO (moderates CEO power -> prior experience, H2); new directors' prior experience with other similar CEOs (moderates demographic difference -> CEO compensation, H3)

Drawing on social comparison theory, the paper argues that a focal CEO estimates a new director's social acceptability (the likelihood that the director will support the CEO's leadership and evaluate the CEO's decisions fairly) by observing whether the director has previously served with other CEOs who are demographically similar to the focal CEO, treating those similar CEOs as a reference group. Powerful CEOs are better able to appoint such directors, and they give these prior ties more weight when a director is demographically more different from them. Because prior contact with similar or different others can also reduce reliance on stereotypes, a dissimilar director's prior work with other similar CEOs mitigates the director's less favorable view of the focal CEO, thereby weakening the otherwise negative effect of CEO-director demographic difference on CEO compensation.

**Data & Measures**
- Sample and sources: 275 companies drawn from the 1995 Fortune 500, yielding 1,114 new director appointments and 613 subsequent CEO compensation decisions over 1995-2006. Board membership from the Compact Disclosure database (all U.S. SEC-filing public companies); CEO compensation from COMPUSTAT ExecuComp; executive characteristics mainly from Capital IQ, supplemented by the Social Register, Marquis' Who's Who, proxy statements, and annual reports; additional data from COMPUSTAT, CRSP, Thomson Financials, and RiskMetrics.
- New director's prior experience with other similar CEOs (DV1): the director's other Fortune board appointments in the prior three years, scored for CEO similarity on seven characteristics (gender, ethnicity, age, highest degree earned, Ivy League background, functional background, industry of employment) and combined into a single index via discrete principal components analysis.
- CEO compensation (DV2): logarithm of the focal CEO's total compensation (salary, bonus, options valued by the Black-Scholes method, and grants) in year t+1.
- CEO power (IV): index of four indicators - CEO tenure, number of board appointments held, CEO ownership, and whether the CEO is board chair - combined via discrete PCA.
- New director's demographic difference from the CEO (moderator): coded 1 for a difference on each of the seven characteristics and combined via discrete PCA.
- Identification and design: observational/associational. A Heckman two-stage sample selection model (inverse Mills ratio from a first-stage model on all 6,135 new Fortune directors) addresses selection because prior experience is observable only for directors with earlier Fortune appointments; estimation uses FGLS with firm-wise heteroscedasticity, extensive firm-, CEO-, and director-level controls, and industry and year dummies.

**Key Findings**
All three hypotheses were supported (one-tailed tests for hypothesized variables).
- H1 supported: CEO power is positively associated with a new director's prior experience with other similar CEOs (β = 0.09, p < .05; Table 2).
- H2 supported: the CEO power × new director's demographic difference interaction is positive (β = 0.11, p < .05; Table 2, Model 3), indicating that powerful CEOs rely more on these prior ties when the new director is demographically more different from them.
- H3 supported: the interaction between new directors' demographic difference and their prior experience with other similar CEOs on CEO compensation is positive (β = 5.69, p < .05; Table 4, Model 2); the negative effect of CEO-director demographic difference on CEO compensation is significantly weakened and can disappear entirely when prior experience with other similar CEOs is relatively high.
- Robustness: results held under OLS with clustered robust standard errors, two alternative measures of prior experience, alternative aggregations of demographic difference, and a three-level (director/CEO/firm) multilevel analysis.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study identifies a new role for directors' interlock ties in corporate governance: beyond serving as information conduits or resource-dependence mechanisms, a director's prior ties to other demographically similar CEOs act as a social cue that the focal CEO uses to gauge the director's social acceptability. By extending social comparison theory to director selection and showing how a director's ties to third-party CEOs (triads) shape the dyadic CEO-director relationship and its consequences, it redirects behavioral governance research from dyad-level factors to triadic social structure. It further contributes to CEO compensation research by identifying a mechanism through which powerful CEOs can secure compensation despite externally demanded increases in CEO-director dissimilarity.

**Practical Implication**
The authors caution that when a CEO is powerful within the firm, it is especially important for the public and shareholders to attend to a broad range of new directors' characteristics, including their prior experience with other similar CEOs. Shareholders and governance reform advocates in particular should be wary when a powerful CEO appears to conform to demands for greater formal board independence or CEO-director dissimilarity, because such appointments can reflect impression management rather than a substantive improvement in board monitoring and control.

**Limitations**
The study examines only two consequences of directors' prior ties to other similar CEOs, namely their appointment onto boards and subsequent CEO compensation, and does not examine other consequences of such third-party ties. The data cannot completely rule out that a small number of directors' prior experiences with other CEOs were perceived as negative, though the authors argue this makes the test conservative. They also cannot fully exclude alternative indicators a CEO might use to infer a new director's social acceptability.

**Future Research**
The authors call for examining how new directors' prior ties to other similar CEOs influence board control over other major decisions such as CEO dismissal, CEO succession, acquisitions, and diversification, and whether such ties affect the board's advice-and-counsel function. They suggest adopting more refined, longitudinal measures of a director's prior experience, and applying the social comparison perspective to other strategic decisions, such as the choice of alliance partners.
