# Collective Organizational Engagement: Linking Motivational Antecedents, Strategic Implementation, and Firm Performance

**Abstract**
We present a comprehensive theory of collective organizational engagement, integrating engagement theory with the resource management model. We propose that engagement can be considered an organization-level construct influenced by motivationally focused organizational practices that represent firm-level resources. Specifically, we evaluate three distinct organizational practices as resources—motivating work design, human resource management practices, and CEO transformational leadership—that can facilitate perceptions that members of the organization are as a whole physically, cognitively, and emotionally invested at work. Our theory is grounded in the notion that, when used jointly, these organizational resources maximize each of the three underlying psychological conditions necessary for full engagement; namely, psychological meaningfulness, safety, and availability. The resource management model also underscores the value of top management team members implementing and monitoring progress on the firm's strategy as a means to enhance the effects of organizational resources on collective organizational engagement. We empirically test this theory in a sample of 83 firms, and provide evidence that collective organizational engagement mediates the relationship between the three organizational resources and firm performance. Furthermore, we find that strategic implementation positively moderates the relationship between the three organizational resources and collective organizational engagement. Implications for theory, research, and practice are discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether employee engagement can operate as a shared firm-level motivational capability, which organizational practices foster that shared engagement, and how executives' strategic implementation affects the link between those resources and collective organizational engagement.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Motivating work design, HRM investments and expectation-enhancing practices, and CEO transformational leadership.
- DV(s): Firm performance, measured by return on assets.
- Mediators: Collective organizational engagement.
- Moderators: Strategic implementation by the top management team.

The paper argues that motivating work design, HRM practices, and CEO transformational leadership jointly support psychological meaningfulness, safety, and availability, producing shared perceptions that organizational members are collectively engaged. Collective organizational engagement then functions as an organization-level capability that links these resources to firm performance. Strategic implementation strengthens the resource-to-engagement relationships by aligning and monitoring organizational effort around the firm's objectives.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends engagement theory by conceptualizing engagement as a firm-level collective construct rather than only an individual-level state. It contributes to resource management theory by specifying collective organizational engagement as a motivational capability through which bundled organizational resources create firm value, and by showing that TMT strategic implementation is a boundary condition that strengthens this capability-building process.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that leaders can improve firm performance by deliberately creating shared engagement across the organization rather than relying on isolated engagement initiatives. It points to enriched entry-level work design, fair and developmental HRM practices, transformational CEO leadership, and active TMT monitoring of strategy as mutually reinforcing levers.

**Limitations**
The authors note that much of the research design is cross-sectional, so causal inference rests primarily on theory rather than a fully longitudinal empirical design. They also note that the study focuses on one level of analysis, relies heavily on survey measures for constructs other than firm performance, and uses small- to medium-sized credit unions, which may limit generalizability to larger firms.

**Future Research**
The authors call for longitudinal research to test causal relationships and examine whether collective organizational engagement is dynamic over time. They also suggest multilevel research on whether collective engagement influences individual or group motivational states and further work on the complexity of strategic implementation.
