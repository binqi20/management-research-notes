# How Early Implementations Influence Later Adoptions of Innovation: Social Positioning and Skill Reproduction in the Diffusion of Robotic Surgery

**Abstract**
We report on a multilevel study investigating the diffusion of robotic surgery in the Italian health care system between 1999 and 2010. A combination of qualitative and quantitative methods allowed us to link organization-level processes associated with the adoption and implementation of the innovation to its diffusion at the population level. Our findings advance the understanding of how early experiences with the implementation of an innovation influence later adoptions, and they draw attention to how the search for social gains drives some peripheral actors to pioneer an innovation and engage in practices of discursive persuasion and skill reproduction that, in turn, constitute them to be "exemplary users." These practices eventually trigger and support the isomorphic diffusion of the innovation, even in the presence of persistent uncertainty about its technical or economic benefits.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how early adopters' implementation experiences influence later adoption decisions during the early diffusion of an innovation. It focuses on why some actors adopt robotic surgery under high technical and economic uncertainty, how their social positions shape those motivations, and how their later dissemination practices affect field-level diffusion.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key actors: Central hospitals, peripheral hospitals, surgeons, hospital managers, patients, professional associations, and technology distributors.
- Motivational drivers: Central actors adopted to preserve organizational or reputational imperatives, while peripheral actors adopted to gain social standing and professional prominence.
- Process / phases: Phase I involved pioneering adoptions under uncertainty; Phase II involved imitation of apparently successful exemplary users; Phase III involved coercive patient pressure, bandwagon effects, and renewed social-gain motives in peripheral regions.
- Dissemination practices: Exemplary users used peer-oriented and public-oriented narratives, codification, mediated exposure, schooling, migration, and proctoring.
- Emergent outcomes: These practices enabled skill reproduction, reassured later adopters about implementation feasibility, and supported isomorphic diffusion despite unresolved uncertainty about technical and economic benefits.

The process is that peripheral early adopters treated robotic surgery as a chance to improve their field position, then constituted themselves as exemplary users by publicizing positive experiences and transferring implementation know-how. Their discursive persuasion helped frame the innovation as viable and desirable, while skill reproduction reduced later adopters' uncertainty about how to implement it. As adoption accumulated, patient demand, media visibility, and competitive fear shifted diffusion toward coercive and bandwagon pressures.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends diffusion theory by showing that early diffusion depends not only on how many actors have adopted, but also on how earlier adopters implemented the innovation and disseminated those experiences. It links social position to adoption motives, arguing that central and peripheral actors may both adopt early but for different reasons: central actors protect their position, whereas peripheral actors seek social gains. It also adds micro-level mechanisms, especially discursive persuasion and skill reproduction, to institutional accounts of isomorphic diffusion.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that managers evaluating complex innovations should examine not only adoption counts or promotional narratives, but also the implementation experience and skill-transfer infrastructure behind those adoptions. For hospitals and policymakers, the study warns that technologies can diffuse rapidly under social and competitive pressure even when technical and economic benefits remain uncertain. It also shows that proctoring, schooling, and codified protocols can materially affect whether late adopters believe they can implement a complex technology.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the single-case design prevents direct comparison with other fields, so claims about which contextual conditions generalize remain speculative. The medical setting may heighten reputational dynamics because technological innovation is central to the status of hospitals and surgeons. The observability and complexity of robotic surgery may also amplify the importance of mediated exposure and skill reproduction relative to less visible or easier-to-implement innovations.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether similar social-positioning and exemplary-user dynamics occur for innovations that matter less for actors' reputations or provide clearer direct economic gains. Studies could examine whether early adopters disclose implementation knowledge differently when doing so risks helping direct competitors. The authors also encourage research on how knowledge exchanges between early and late adopters, including the mechanisms and kinds of knowledge transferred, shape diffusion patterns.
