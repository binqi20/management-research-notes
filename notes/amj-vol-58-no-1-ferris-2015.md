# Ostracism, Self-Esteem, and Job Performance: When Do We Self-Verify and When Do We Self-Enhance?

**Abstract**
Self-esteem level has been positioned as a key mediating mechanism accounting for the effects of ostracism on behaviors, invoking the notion that individuals seek to verify their self-perceptions by behaving in a way that is consistent with those self-perceptions. However, evidence supporting the relation of ostracism and self-esteem level to behavioral outcomes has been mixed. We argue that such mixed effects arise because individuals may engage in behaviors alternately to verify their self-perceptions (suggesting a relation between self-esteem level and behavioral outcomes) or to self-enhance (suggesting no relation between self-esteem level and behavioral outcomes). Within this framing, the question becomes: When do we self-verify and when do we self-enhance? To that end, we position contingent self-esteem—or the extent to which individuals base their self-worth on outcomes in a particular domain—as a determining factor in whether we self-verify or self-enhance, and present a moderated mediation model to account for varying relations between ostracism and job performance. Our predictions regarding self-verification and self-enhancement motivation are fully supported across two field samples using multi-wave, multi-source study designs. Theoretical and practical implications for self-verification and self-enhancement motivation, as well as negative interpersonal behaviors at work, are discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when ostracized employees self-verify through behavior consistent with lowered self-esteem and when they self-enhance by maintaining strong job performance. It examines whether the importance of performance to self-esteem determines when self-esteem level mediates the relationship between workplace ostracism and job performance outcomes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Workplace ostracism.
- DV(s): In-role behaviors, individually directed organizational citizenship behaviors, organizationally directed organizational citizenship behaviors, organizational deviance, and interpersonal deviance.
- Mediators: Self-esteem level.
- Moderators: Importance of performance to self-esteem.

The central mechanism is that workplace ostracism lowers self-esteem, and lower self-esteem can lead employees to act in ways that verify negative self-perceptions through poorer performance or more deviance. The paper argues that this self-verification path is strongest when employees' self-worth is not contingent on workplace performance. When performance is important to self-esteem, employees are motivated to self-enhance in that domain, so self-esteem level no longer predicts poorer job performance in the same way.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to ostracism research by explaining why prior findings on ostracism, self-esteem, and performance were mixed: the indirect effect depends on contingent self-esteem. It also contributes to motivation theory by positioning contingent self-esteem as a boundary condition separating self-verification from self-enhancement. More broadly, it offers a moderated mediation model that can extend to other negative interpersonal behaviors carrying rejection and self-worth threats.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that organizations should understand workplace ostracism as a threat to employees' self-esteem and job behavior, not only as an interpersonal discomfort. Interventions that support employee self-esteem may reduce performance costs of ostracism, but the authors caution that developing high performance-contingent self-esteem may have downsides for well-being.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the samples were convenience samples, so generalizability to the broader working population should be treated cautiously. They also state that the field data are cross-sectional for some theoretical paths, limiting causal inference. A further limitation is that performance could influence ostracism or self-esteem, and the available data are not well suited to test those reciprocal possibilities.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on whether relationship-contingent self-esteem moderates ostracism's effect on self-esteem. They also suggest examining other moderators of ostracism's direct performance effects, including task interdependence and situational interventions that reduce ambiguity or improve affect after ostracism. Future studies could also integrate behavioral and well-being outcomes to test when contingent self-esteem buffers performance damage while worsening well-being.
