# Professional Image Maintenance: How Women Navigate Pregnancy in the Workplace

**Abstract**
Women now constitute a significant portion of the workforce, making the effects of pregnancy on professional image (others’ perceptions of competence and character at work) more salient. While opinions regarding how pregnant women should manage others’ impressions and the consequences of doing so abound (Noveck, 2012) research to substantiate or disconfirm these opinions has lagged. In this paper, we present three studies that develop and test a model of social identity-based impression management (SIM) techniques used by pregnant workers. In Study 1 (n ⴝ 35), we utilized qualitative methods to identify the motives and strategies used by pregnant women to manage their professional images. In the second study, we collected two samples (n ⴝ 199 and n ⴝ 133) to develop and validate two scales based on the motives and strategies identified in Study 1. In Study 3 (n ⴝ 200), we employed a time-lagged design to examine how SIM motives and strategies affect important workplace outcomes: perceived discrimination, burnout, and returning to one’s job after maternity leave. Our findings demonstrate both positive and negative outcomes of the motives and strategies women use to manage their images at work when pregnant.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how pregnancy affects working women's professional experiences and how pregnant workers use social identity-based impression management to maintain professional image. It also asks how SIM motives and strategies relate to perceived discrimination, burnout, and returning to one's job after maternity leave.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / motives: Legitimacy preservation and devaluation reduction.
- Key constructs / strategies: Image maintenance and decategorization.
- Process / sequence: Qualitative identification of motives and strategies, scale development and validation, then time-lagged testing of links to workplace outcomes.
- Emergent outcomes: Perceived discrimination, burnout, and return to one's job.
- Boundary conditions / controls: Pregnancy stage, number of pregnancies at the organization, pregnancy risk, workplace gender composition, self-image at work, self-verification striving, and prior burnout.

The paper argues that pregnancy can make a new and potentially devalued social identity salient at work, threatening women's prior professional image. Pregnant workers respond through approach-oriented image maintenance, such as maintaining pace, avoiding accommodations, going the extra mile, or shortening leave, and through avoidance-oriented decategorization, such as passing or downplaying pregnancy. In Study 3, legitimacy preservation predicted image maintenance, which was associated with lower perceived discrimination, lower burnout, and greater likelihood of returning to the job; devaluation reduction predicted decategorization but was directly associated with higher perceived discrimination and burnout rather than being improved by avoidance strategies.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends professional image construction and SIM theory by showing that changing social identities can produce non-loss-focused image maintenance rather than the gain-focused positive distinctiveness emphasized in work on stable identities. It also develops and validates measures of SIM during pregnancy and integrates self-regulation theory to distinguish approach-related image maintenance from avoidance-related decategorization. The results show that approach-oriented SIMp strategies can have beneficial outcomes, whereas avoidance motives may be more consequential than avoidance behaviors.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that organizations should focus less on whether pregnant workers' priorities shift and more on reducing the devaluation concerns that make pregnancy feel professionally risky. Supportive supervisors and workplaces may reduce the perceived need for avoidance strategies and help pregnant workers maintain professional image without burnout or perceived discrimination. The paper also indicates that image maintenance behaviors can be linked to retention after maternity leave.

**Limitations**
The authors note possible common-method bias because the data came from the same source, although outcomes were collected in a separate time-lagged survey. They also state that the time lag may not have been long enough to observe meaningful changes in some SIMp constructs and that causality cannot be definitively established. The sample was largely U.S.-based, limiting claims about international generalizability.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on individual-level traits, additional mediators, and robust within-person designs across pregnancy stages and numbers of pregnancies. They also recommend testing whether the findings generalize to other new social identities, including chronic illness and other temporary or permanent identity changes. Future work should examine international differences in stereotypes of pregnant workers and the outcomes of gain-focused versus non-loss-focused SIM motives.
