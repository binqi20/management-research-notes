# Investor Perceptions of Financial Misconduct: The Heterogeneous Contamination of Bystander Firms

**Abstract**
We suggest that, when one firm reveals financial misconduct, others in the industry suffer lower valuations, but do so heterogeneously. To understand this heterogeneity, we conceptualize such contamination as a generalization–instantiation process: investors generalize the culpability to the industry category and perceive the instantiation of generalized culpability within the industry bystander firms. This theoretical separation allows us to hypothesize the factors that affect the degree to which both of these elements of the contamination process occurs. Specifically, we predict that characteristics of the misconduct firm or event—factors that lend to investors’ familiarity with the misconduct firms, or that prompt attributions of blame for the misconduct—affect the potency of the generalization of culpability to the industry, while characteristics of the industry bystander firms—investors’ familiarity with such firms, or factors that lend to investors’ perceptions that they have strong governance—affect the firms’ vulnerability to being perceived as instantiating the generalized culpability. We tested our hypotheses on a sample of 725 firms across 84 financial misconduct events, and the results of our event analyses broadly support our predictions. Our study thus has implications for future research on the social view of financial markets, organizational misconduct, and corporate governance.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether financial misconduct by one firm lowers investors' valuations of other firms in the same industry, and why this contamination varies across misconduct events and bystander firms. It focuses on how investors generalize culpability to an industry category and then perceive that generalized culpability as instantiated in particular bystander firms.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Revelation of financial misconduct by a perpetrator firm; perpetrator familiarity; employee firing; government probe; bystander familiarity; bystander board vigilance; bystander CEO duality.
- DV(s): Cumulative abnormal returns of bystander firms around restatement announcements.
- Mediators: Not directly tested; the theorized process is investors' generalization of culpability and perceived instantiation of that culpability in bystander firms.
- Moderators: Perpetrator firm size, employee firing, government probe, bystander firm size, board independence, and CEO duality.

The paper argues that investors use industry categories to make sense of financial misconduct under uncertainty. When a restatement reveals misconduct, investors generalize culpability from the perpetrator to the industry, lowering valuations for industry bystanders. The strength of that category-level generalization depends on perpetrator and event characteristics, while the degree to which particular bystander firms are penalized depends on their familiarity and governance signals.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the social view of financial markets by showing that investor valuation processes involve categorical social judgments, not only firm-specific financial information. It extends research on organizational misconduct by separating contamination into generalization and instantiation, which clarifies why innocent third parties suffer heterogeneous market penalties. It also contributes to corporate governance research by treating governance structures as signals that shape investor perceptions during misconduct events.

**Practical Implication**
The study implies that firms may suffer valuation losses from peer misconduct even when they are not directly involved. It suggests that investor-facing governance signals and the visibility of both perpetrator and bystander firms can affect the magnitude of contamination after financial restatement announcements.

**Limitations**
The paper's empirical context is accounting-related financial restatements in 2004, shortly after SOX-related governance changes, so the findings may not generalize unchanged to other misconduct forms or regulatory periods. The design identifies market reactions around events but infers the investor cognition behind generalization and instantiation from observable valuation patterns. The paper also notes that board vigilance operated opposite to the authors' prediction, indicating context-specific ambiguity in how investors read governance signals.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on whether similar generalization-instantiation processes operate for other forms of organizational misconduct and for other stakeholder audiences. They also encourage work on positive or ambiguous events, such as innovation, CEO charisma, mergers, acquisitions, and diversification, to test whether investor generalization works similarly outside negative judgments. Further research is needed on how contamination becomes stigmatization and how different investor types may perceive perpetrator, event, and bystander characteristics differently.
