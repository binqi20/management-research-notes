# I Need Time! Exploring Pathways to Compliance under Institutional Complexity

**Abstract**
In this study, we examined responses to institutional complexity by analyzing when and how organizations respond to a coercive institutional demand from a powerful constituent when other important constituents do not accept the demand as legitimate. We experimentally manipulated institutional complexity and gauged the time to compliance of 100 childcare managers in the Netherlands, and then asked them to describe and explain their anticipated responses to multiple pressures. We found that institutional complexity leads decision makers to delay compliance, but usually not passively: decision makers used the time before compliance to attempt to reduce institutional complexity by neutralizing opposing pressures, challenging the coercive pressure, adapting the practice to suit opponents and their own personal beliefs, and/or waiting to see how the situation would unfold as multiple parties influenced one another. We found two factors influenced decision makers' choice of responses: (1) their interpretation of institutional complexity and (2) their personal beliefs toward the practice itself. Our findings contribute to an emerging understanding of how decision makers interpret and respond to institutional complexity, and thus complement recent studies in the institutional complexity literature.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and how organizational decision makers respond to coercive institutional demands when important constituents contest the demand's legitimacy. It focuses on whether institutional complexity delays intended compliance and what pathways to compliance decision makers plan before complying.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key pressures: A coercive government demand to adopt a new childcare practice, combined with varying support or opposition from parents and employees.
- Timing outcome: Delay of intended compliance with the mandated digital wall.
- Response pathways: Early adoption, delay, accommodative responses such as neutralizing opposition and partial compliance, resistant responses such as fighting the practice and stimulating opposition, and exit.
- Interpretive conditions: Decision makers' interpretations of institutional complexity and their personal beliefs toward the mandated practice.
- Field process: Time before compliance is used either as a buffer against uncertainty or as a resource for action to reduce complexity.

The central process is that conflicting constituent pressures make the path to legitimacy ambiguous, so decision makers delay compliance rather than simply obeying the most coercive actor. During the delay, they may wait for others to clarify the situation or actively try to reshape pressures by influencing parents, employees, government actors, or the practice itself. Personal beliefs shape which responses they choose, especially whether they move toward accommodative or resistant pathways.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional complexity research by showing that coercive pressure does not automatically produce fast compliance when other constituents contest the practice. It adds microfoundations to institutional theory by explaining how decision makers interpret conflicting pressures, use time strategically, and engage in sensemaking and institutional work before compliance. It also extends work on institutionalization pace by showing that force-driven adoption can be slowed by influence-based efforts to construct a clearer path to legitimacy.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that regulators and powerful constituents should not assume that coercive mandates will be implemented quickly when target organizations face opposition from other salient constituents. Managers can use implementation windows to negotiate with stakeholders, experiment with partial adaptations, and reduce uncertainty before full compliance. The paper also suggests that delays may be active attempts to preserve legitimacy rather than mere resistance or passivity.

**Limitations**
The study uses hypothetical vignettes and anticipated responses rather than observed organizational implementation behavior. Its empirical setting is the Dutch childcare sector, where coercive regulation, parents, employees, and managers' pedagogical beliefs create a specific form of institutional complexity. The authors caution that generalizability is strongest for similar contexts where actors cannot ignore coercive pressure but also depend on important constituents who may oppose compliance.

**Future Research**
Future research should identify contingencies affecting responses to institutional pressures under complexity, including when actors can safely ignore pressures, when fields sustain differentiated meanings, and when complexity resolves into shared understandings. Further work could examine how personal beliefs condition flexibility or inflexibility in responses to contested practices. The paper also argues that experimental approaches can help investigate the microfoundations of institutional theory and institutionalization processes.
