# Family- versus Lone-Founder-Controlled Public Corporations: Social Identity Theory and Boards of Directors

**Abstract**
We distinguish between “family companies,” involving multiple family members as owners and/or managers, and “lone-founder companies,” involving only the founder and no other family members. We apply social identity theory and organizational identification to elucidate the differences between these two types of firms, and how their differing organizational identities reflect unique desires for control and influence. We then consider how these differences are reflected in a firm’s board structure (i.e., directorship interlocks, director experiences, and director tenures). Specifically, we predict that family firms are more likely to interlock with other family firms, select directors with more experience in family firms, and keep directors on their boards longer. Correspondingly, we posit that family firms are less likely to interlock with lone-founder firms or to select directors with experience in lone-founder-controlled firms. Lone-founder firms follow a similar pattern. We also consider the consequences of family and lone-founder ownership and board structures on the investment behavior of three classes of institutional investors. We test our hypotheses with a sample of publicly traded corporations between 1991 and 2006, and report strong support for most of our predictions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how family-controlled and lone-founder-controlled public corporations differ in organizational identity and how those identities shape board structure, outside director experience, director tenure, and institutional investor ownership. It also asks whether treating family and lone-founder firms as a single category obscures meaningful governance differences.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm control type, director prior family-firm experience, director prior lone-founder-firm experience, director prior regular-public-firm experience, and outside-director board experience profiles.
- DV(s): Interlocks to family, lone-founder, and regular public firms; outside director prior experience by firm type; outside director exit; and dedicated, transient, and quasi-indexed institutional ownership.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators.
- Moderators: Matching between firm identity type and outside director prior experience is modeled for director exit.

The mechanism is that family and lone-founder firms have distinct organizational identities even though both controlling owners seek to preserve control. Family firms' familial identity motivates ties to other family firms and directors with family-firm experience, while lone-founder firms' individualistic and entrepreneurial identity motivates ties to other lone-founder firms and directors with lone-founder experience. These identity-consistent board structures reduce perceived threats to owner control, lengthen matched directors' tenure, and signal different governance and shareholder orientations to dedicated institutional investors.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to corporate governance research by separating family-controlled firms from lone-founder-controlled firms rather than treating both as a single family-firm category. It extends social identity theory and organizational identification into board research by showing how owner and director identities shape interlocks, director selection, and director exit. It also links director identity to institutional investor behavior, arguing that outside directors' family- or lone-founder-firm experience signals a firm's openness to outside investor influence and shareholder-value priorities.

**Practical Implication**
For boards and controlling owners, the findings imply that director selection is not merely a matter of generic independence or expertise; prior experience in similar ownership contexts can affect trust, control preservation, and director retention. For institutional investors, director experience in family firms versus lone-founder firms may serve as a governance signal because outside-director experience indicates a firm's governance and shareholder orientation. The results also imply that researchers and practitioners should avoid pooling family and lone-founder firms when assessing board structure and investor reactions.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the sample is limited to publicly traded companies, so the findings are not generalizable to private firms. They also note that identifying family and lone-founder ownership is difficult and may not capture every qualifying firm, especially family firms with informal family involvement or late-stage generational complexity. The study only observes directors' experience within the sample firms and does not directly measure organizational identity, relying instead on theoretically grounded ownership and experience classifications.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other governance mechanisms through which family and lone-founder firms preserve control, including leadership structure, CEO duality, and the types of directors they recruit. The paper also calls for research on generational transitions in family firms and how board configurations differ across first-, second-, and third-generation family control. Additional work could directly measure organizational identity and test whether directors with family- or lone-founder-firm experience behave differently in monitoring, advising, and strategic decision making.
