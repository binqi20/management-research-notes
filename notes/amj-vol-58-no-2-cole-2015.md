# Lessons from a Martial Arts Dojo: A Prolonged Process Model of High-Context Communication

**Abstract**
Rather than treat the context of communication as an exogenously determined factor, I investigate the extent to which communicators actively utilize context as a vehicle of communication. Focusing on three cases of “physicality norm” violations observed during a five-year ethnographic study of a Japanese martial arts dojo, I document the use of specific context management and content management practices on the part of the instructors to reconcile the situations in which they found themselves. The prolonged process model of high-context communication that I develop shows how communicators manipulate the two key dimensions of the contexting model of communication—the message content (via continuum staggering and continuum straddling), and the shared understandings that constitute the context in which messages are being delivered and interpreted (via context reliance, i.e., time giving, and context nonreliance, i.e., tearing and reprogramming)—often over extended periods of time.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether communicators actively use context to communicate messages, and how they mix context and content to convey meaning to targets. It examines these questions through norm violations in a Japanese martial arts dojo where high-context communication depends on shared understandings, hierarchy, and face-saving interaction.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: high-context communication, low-context communication, shared understandings, tacit common ground, physicality norms, group boundaries, group bonds, and speech norms.
- Cases: the visitor, Ringo, and Einstein each violate physicality norms in different ways, creating opportunities to observe how instructors and senior members guide targets toward norm compliance.
- Context management practices: context reliance through time giving, and context nonreliance through tearing or reprogramming shared understandings.
- Content management practices: continuum staggering shifts from implicit to more explicit messages over time, while continuum straddling uses implicit and explicit messages simultaneously for different audiences.
- Process outcome: communicators begin with less interactive, face-saving guidance and escalate only when time giving and implicit cues do not resolve the violation.

The process model shows high-context communication as prolonged and staged rather than as a one-shot choice between implicit and explicit speech. Communicators rely on shared understandings first, monitor whether the target self-corrects, and then adjust message content or the context itself when the target fails to interpret the intended meaning. The central process is not direct causal control of behavior but guided self-realization and norm restoration with minimal loss of face.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to cross-cultural communication theory by showing that context is not merely an exogenous national or individual characteristic; communicators can actively use, tear, and reprogram context as part of communication. It develops a prolonged process model that links context management practices with content management practices across time. It also argues that micro-level contexts such as dojo norms, hierarchy, and ongoing relationships can shape communication more directly than broad national stereotypes.

**Practical Implication**
Managers working across high-context and low-context communication styles should attend to indirect guidance, silence, altered routines, and contextual changes as possible communicative acts rather than treating only explicit statements as meaningful. The paper suggests that attentiveness to workplace dissatisfaction and interpersonal friction can help managers respond before problems become visible conflicts. It also warns low-context communicators that direct messages from high-context communicators may come only after earlier indirect efforts have failed.

**Limitations**
The paper explicitly notes that its single-organization design raises questions about generalizability across other organizations. Its evidence comes from one Japanese martial arts dojo and three physicality norm violation cases, so the model should be treated as theory-building rather than as a statistically generalizable test. The setting is also not a conventional firm, although the author argues that the observed processes parallel phenomena in organizational and managerial contexts.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on whether low-context communicators understand high-context messages but intentionally avoid acknowledging that understanding, for example to evade responsibility or signal defiance. It also suggests studying whether expatriate experiences in high-context locales improve managers' later ability to manage employees after returning home. Future work could also test whether continuum staggering and continuum straddling operate with content characteristics other than explicitness.
