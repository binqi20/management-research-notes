# A Confucian Social Model of Political Appointments among Chinese Private-Firm Entrepreneurs

**Abstract**
In China, many successful private-firm entrepreneurs hold representational appointments in political councils such as the People’s Congress (PC) or People’s Political Consultative Conference (PPCC). By considering Western theories of life career development and Confucian doctrines of personal development, we seek to understand the complex motivations of successful entrepreneurs for joining political councils. We propose a Confucian social model of role transition to explain the pursuit and attainment of political appointment in China. We hypothesize that the “proself” and “prosocial” motives for seeking political connections will, respectively, attenuate or strengthen the relationship between private-firm entrepreneurs’ business success and their intention and attainment in seeking political appointment. We conducted a cross-sectional survey of 166 private-firm chief executive officers (CEOs) and chairs, and a longitudinal archival study of 1,323 Chinese publicly listed private firms from 2006 to 2012. The findings support our hypotheses on the moderating effect of the proself and prosocial motives. The study offers insights into how Confucian cultural values may explain the dynamic between business success and political appointments among private-firm entrepreneurs in China.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why successful Chinese private-firm entrepreneurs increasingly pursue PC or PPCC political appointments despite declining economic returns to political connections. It examines whether proself and prosocial motives condition the relationship between business success and entrepreneurs' desire for, and attainment of, political appointment.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Entrepreneurial or firm success.
- DV(s): Desire to seek political appointment in Study 1; attained PC or PPCC political appointment in Study 2.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Proself motive and prosocial motive.

The proposed Confucian social model argues that business success gives entrepreneurs discretion to act on salient motives. When proself motives dominate, successful entrepreneurs are less likely to pursue costly political appointments because marginal economic benefits are limited. When prosocial motives dominate, successful entrepreneurs are more likely to pursue appointments as legitimate channels for influencing policy, addressing social problems, and serving the broader society.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends political-connection research beyond the dominant economic lens by showing that personal motives, especially prosocial motives rooted in Confucian role-transition doctrine, help explain political appointment-seeking. It also links Western life-career development theories with an Eastern Confucian account of movement from self-orientation to social orientation, offering a cross-cultural model of how entrepreneurs define life goals after business success.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that political appointment among successful Chinese private-firm entrepreneurs should not be interpreted only as self-interested rent seeking. Policymakers selecting PC or PPCC members may need to distinguish entrepreneurs motivated by public contribution from those primarily seeking private or firm-level economic benefits.

**Limitations**
The authors note that political appointment is only one possible channel for expressing prosocial motives, and its meaning may vary by cultural and institutional context. Study 1 may suffer from sample-selection bias, with women underrepresented and possible generational differences among entrepreneurs. The authors also caution that CSR disclosure may become institutionalized over time and may then be a weaker proxy for prosocial motive.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine other channels through which entrepreneurs pursue prosocial life goals, including social entrepreneurship and other social outcomes. It should test the proself and prosocial motive scales in other cultural contexts, compare them with related psychological measures, and develop better objective indicators of prosocial motive. Further work should also compare political appointments with other forms of political connection and investigate inauthentic prosocial motives.
