# The Dark Side of Socialization: A Longitudinal Investigation of Newcomer Alcohol Use

**Abstract**
Organizational veterans and external stakeholders such as clients often play an important and informal role in newcomer socialization, influencing newcomer cognition and behavior and providing learning opportunities and social support that facilitate employee adjustment and performance enhancement. However, from a sensemaking perspective, newcomers may also draw insight from stakeholder behavior in order to better understand how to best meet job-related objectives and expectations. These understandings may manifest as performance-related motives, leading to the adoption of risky behaviors that can be detrimental to newcomer health and organizational effectiveness. Using multisource, multilevel, and longitudinal data, we demonstrate that the alcohol use norms of both organizational veterans and clients are significantly associated with the performance drinking motives of newcomers in sales and client service teams, suggesting that veteran and client norms signal to newcomers that drinking alcohol is an effective and legitimate means to improve job performance. In addition, we demonstrate that performance drinking motives mediate the positive relationship between veteran and client alcohol use norms and newcomers’ frequency of work-based heavy drinking. Finally, we find that the frequency of work-based heavy drinking is positively related to newcomer alcohol misuse and mediates the positive relationship between performance drinking motives and newcomer alcohol misuse.

**Research Question**
How do veteran peers' and clients' alcohol use norms shape newcomers' performance drinking motives, work-based heavy drinking, and later alcohol misuse? The paper asks whether informal socialization can produce a "dark side" in which newcomers infer that risky behaviors are legitimate and effective ways to meet job-related objectives.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Veteran peers' alcohol use norms and clients' alcohol use norms.
- DV(s): Newcomers' frequency of work-based heavy drinking and alcohol misuse.
- Mediators: Performance drinking motives mediate the relationships between alcohol use norms and work-based heavy drinking; work-based heavy drinking mediates the relationship between performance drinking motives and alcohol misuse.
- Moderators: Not modeled as moderators.

The mechanism is a cross-level sensemaking process. Newcomers face uncertainty about how to achieve performance goals, observe veteran peers' and clients' descriptive alcohol-use norms, and form accounts that drinking is an effective and legitimate way to improve job performance. These accounts appear as performance drinking motives, which are associated with more frequent heavy drinking with clients; work-based heavy drinking is then associated with subsequent alcohol misuse. The evidence supports the indirect paths from veteran and client norms to work-based heavy drinking through performance motives, but it does not support the full hypothesized indirect paths from norms to alcohol misuse.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends newcomer socialization research by showing that informal socialization can generate dysfunctional outcomes, not only adjustment and performance gains. It contributes to sensemaking theory by treating motives as an operational manifestation of newcomers' accounts and by showing that both internal stakeholders and external clients provide inputs to those accounts. It also adds to workplace alcohol research by theorizing how permissive drinking cultures emerge through norm-based sensemaking rather than only documenting their effects.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should attend to informal socialization processes, especially in client-facing roles where newcomers may infer that risky practices are required for performance. The findings imply that laissez-faire reliance on peer and client norms can impose long-run health and organizational costs even when the behavior seems performance-enhancing in the short run. Organizations may need to clarify acceptable means of relationship building and guide newcomers away from risky client-service routines.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that its measures of workplace alcohol-use norms may not capture all relevant sources or forms of normative influence, including injunctive norms and norms from other newcomers or competitors. Work-based heavy drinking is measured with a single self-report item, and performance drinking motives and work-based heavy drinking are measured at the same wave, so the causal ordering between motives and drinking cannot be fully ruled out. The sample is limited to sales and client service employees in China, where business drinking is culturally salient, so generalizability to other occupations and national contexts requires replication.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine other sources and forms of workplace drinking norms, including injunctive norms and normative cues from newcomers, competitors, and other socialization agents. The authors call for experience-sampling designs, cross-lagged panel designs, and experimental designs to better capture drinking behavior over time and clarify reciprocal relationships between motives and behavior. They also encourage research on boundary conditions such as job demands, alcohol tolerance, age, first-job status, newcomer turnover, and replication in other cultural settings.
