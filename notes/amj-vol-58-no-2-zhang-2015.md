# Paradoxical Leader Behaviors in People Management: Antecedents and Consequences

**Abstract**
As organizational environments become increasingly dynamic, complex, and competitive, leaders are likely to face intensified contradictory, or seemingly paradoxical, demands. We develop the construct of “paradoxical leader behavior” in people management, which refers to seemingly competing, yet interrelated, behaviors to meet structural and follower demands simultaneously and over time. In Study 1, we develop a measure of paradoxical leader behavior in people management using five samples from China. Confirmatory factor analyses support a multidimensional measure of paradoxical leader behavior with five dimensions: (1) combining self-centeredness with other-centeredness; (2) maintaining both distance and closeness; (3) treating subordinates uniformly, while allowing individualization; (4) enforcing work requirements, while allowing flexibility; and (5) maintaining decision control, while allowing autonomy. In Study 2, we examine the antecedents and consequences of paradoxical leader behavior in people management with a field sample of 76 supervisors and 516 subordinates from 6 firms. We find that the extent to which supervisors engage in holistic thinking and have integrative complexity is positively related to their paradoxical behavior in managing people, which, in turn, is associated with increased proficiency, adaptivity, and proactivity among subordinates.

**Research Question**
How can paradoxical leader behavior in people management be conceptualized and measured, and what cognitive and contextual antecedents explain its occurrence? The paper also asks whether paradoxical leader behavior is associated with subordinate proficiency, adaptivity, and proactivity.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Holistic thinking, integrative complexity, organic organizational structure, and paradoxical leader behavior in people management.
- DV(s): Paradoxical leader behavior in the antecedent model; subordinate task proficiency, adaptive behavior, and proactive behavior in the consequence model.
- Mediators: Paradoxical leader behavior is the focal linking construct between leader cognition and subordinate work-role performance, but the paper does not estimate a formal indirect-effects mediation model.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is that leaders with a both-and cognitive basis can accept, connect, and integrate contradictory structural and follower demands over time. Paradoxical leader behavior then provides both bounded and discretionary work environments: followers understand work roles and standards while retaining autonomy, flexibility, dignity, and individualized consideration. This combination is theorized to model openness to contradictions and support proficiency, adaptation, and proactive change behavior. The field evidence supports holistic thinking and integrative complexity as antecedents, rejects organic structure as a significant antecedent in the controlled model, and links paradoxical leader behavior to all three subordinate outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends organizational paradox research from macro strategic tensions to supervisory people management, arguing that leaders face paradoxes in structural and follower demands. It contributes a five-dimensional measure of paradoxical leader behavior and shows construct and predictive validity beyond established leadership constructs. It also bridges Eastern yin-yang philosophy with Western paradox and leadership literatures to theorize both-and leader behavior.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that leaders should be trained not only to choose situationally among behavioral alternatives, but also to handle competing demands through long-term paradoxical thinking and action. For international and complex organizational contexts, a paradox-based lens may help managers respond to uncertainty without reducing every tension to an either-or choice.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting is China, so the authors caution that findings may reflect Chinese-specific ways of integrating opposites and should be tested in Western and cross-cultural contexts. Study 2 uses a small firm-level sample and supervisor-rated organizational structure, which may explain the unsupported organizational-structure finding. The validation samples also include evening students from one university and professional, college-educated workers, and some Study 1 relationships may be vulnerable to common method effects.

**Future Research**
Future research should test paradoxical leader behavior in Western and cross-cultural contexts, examine whether different cultures display different paradoxical behaviors, and extend the construct beyond people management to stakeholder, globalization, and strategic leadership domains. Researchers could also examine downsides such as leader cognitive strain and subordinate discomfort when followers hold black-white views, and expand the nomological network to include open-mindedness, learning orientation, uncertainty avoidance, long-term orientation, environmental uncertainty, work characteristics, and group-level outcomes.
