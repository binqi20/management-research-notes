# Hot Buttons and Time Sinks: The Effects of Electronic Communication During Nonwork Time on Emotions and Work-Nonwork Conflict

**Abstract**
As advances in communication technologies have made organizations more easily connected to their workforce outside of normal work hours, there is increased concern that employees may experience heightened work-nonwork conflict when away from the office. The current study investigates the effects of electronic communication received during nonwork time using an experience sampling methodology to examine within-person relationships among elements of electronic communication (affective tone, time required), emotional responses (anger, happiness), and work-to-nonwork conflict in a sample of 341 working adults surveyed over a seven-day period. Hierarchical linear modeling results suggested that both affective tone and time required were associated with anger, but only affective tone was associated with happiness. Further, anger was associated with work-to-nonwork conflict and mediated the effects of affective tone and time required on work-to-nonwork conflict. Results also revealed cross-level moderating effects of abusive supervision and communication sender together, as well as segmentation preference. Implications of these findings for future theorizing and research on electronic communication during nonwork time are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do the affective tone and time required by work-related electronic communications received during nonwork time shape employees' anger, happiness, and work-to-nonwork conflict? The paper also asks whether abusive supervision, communication sender, and segmentation preference condition these within-person relationships.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Electronic communication affective tone and electronic communication time required during nonwork time.
- DV(s): Work-to-nonwork conflict.
- Mediators: Anger; happiness was modeled as an emotional response but did not mediate work-to-nonwork conflict in the reported results.
- Moderators: Abusive supervision, communication sender, and segmentation preference.

The paper treats electronic communications during nonwork time as affective work events that employees appraise as favorable or unfavorable. Negative affective tone and greater time demands are associated with anger, while affective tone is also associated with happiness; the reported indirect effects on work-to-nonwork conflict operate through anger, not happiness. Relational context matters because abusive supervision and supervisor-sent messages shape appraisals of affective tone, while segmentation preference makes time-demanding intrusions into nonwork time more conflictual.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends work-nonwork and electronic communication research by shifting attention from overall communication volume to event-level properties of individual messages. It integrates affective events theory, appraisal theory, electronic communication emotion theory, positive-negative asymmetry, relational schemas, and boundary theory to explain why anger mediates the effects of affective tone and time required on work-to-nonwork conflict. It also shows that abusive supervision, sender status, and segmentation preference are boundary conditions for these processes.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should be careful about affective tone, timing, and effort demands when sending electronic communications after normal work hours. The findings suggest value in training employees to communicate clearly and constructively after hours, limiting unnecessary time demands, and recognizing that employees with stronger segmentation preferences may experience time-consuming after-hours messages as especially intrusive. The paper also implies that supervisor behavior is consequential because abusive supervisory relationships can intensify anger responses to negatively toned messages.

**Limitations**
The study used convenience and snowball sampling rather than random population sampling, so the authors caution against overgeneralizing to a defined population. The daily measures were collected concurrently, which prevents the design from fully eliminating alternative causal orderings such as anger shaping perceived tone. The focal within-person measures of affective tone and time required were single-item self-reports, leaving room for reliability limits, retrieval errors, and common method concerns.

**Future Research**
Future work should test the model in more controlled settings or a single organization and use time-separated measures of emotions and work-nonwork conflict across the end of the workday, evening communications, and the next morning. Research could examine additional message features such as salutations, closings, capitalization, response timing, emoticons, and time of day. The authors also call for research on other relational and receiver factors, including leader-member exchange, career ambition, empowerment, daily work-at-home expectations, and perceptual accuracy in affective tone interpretation.
