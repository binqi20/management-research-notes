# Sanctioning in the Wild: Rational Calculus and Retributive Instincts in Gourmet Cuisine

**Abstract**
Why do we sanction norm violations? Despite near universal agreement on the role of sanctions for maintaining norms of cooperation, scholars hotly dispute whether individuals sanction based on a rational calculus or because of strong retributive instincts. In this paper we report on a mixed-method field study examining sanctioning behavior. Our goal is to extend theories of sanctioning by evaluating the conditions under which individuals are more likely to administer a sanction in response to a norm violation. To guide the development of our hypotheses, we engage in a qualitative examination of sanctioning decisions in the context of gourmet cuisine. We then test our predictions in a field experiment involving more than 500 gourmet chefs in Italy. Our results suggest that individuals follow retributive instincts, but they also engage in cost–benefit calculations. Indeed, we find that the two logics of sanctioning jointly influence participation in social exchange. Recognizing their own tendency to sanction at a cost, individuals avoid circumstances that could trigger the need for costly sanctions.

**Research Question**
Why do individuals sanction norm violations in real-world social exchange, and under what conditions are they more likely to administer sanctions based on rational calculus versus retributive instincts?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Reputation of the norm violator, public scrutiny/frequency of review, competition with the violator through physical proximity and similarity of positioning, and severity of the norm violation.
- DV(s): Propensity to sanction a norm violation; in the cooperation analysis, propensity to transfer culinary knowledge.
- Mediating relationship: Propensity to sanction is analyzed as a link between scenario treatments and willingness to transfer knowledge.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The paper argues that sanctioning combines weak-reciprocity and strong-reciprocity processes. Chefs calculate the social cost of sanctioning by anticipating whether peers will see the sanction as legitimate, so they sanction less when a high-reputation violator may receive the benefit of the doubt and more when physical proximity increases the stakes. At the same time, severe misuse of recipes and signature dishes triggers a retributive response that makes sanctioning feel obligatory. Anticipating this costly obligation, chefs sometimes avoid knowledge exchange before any violation occurs.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the strong- versus weak-reciprocity debate by showing that both logics predict sanctioning behavior in a field setting. It also extends legitimacy research by showing that legitimacy loss can arise not only from violating a norm but also from sanctioning a norm violation when observers cannot reconstruct the prior offense. Finally, it contributes to work on norm-based intellectual property systems by examining how sanctions maintain norms governing transferred culinary knowledge.

**Practical Implication**
Communities that rely on informal norms need ways to make violations and legitimate sanctions interpretable to peers; otherwise, potential enforcers may avoid sanctioning or avoid exchange altogether. In professional communities where reputation is valuable, actors may withhold knowledge not because they reject cooperation but because they anticipate the social and emotional costs of enforcing cooperation later.

**Limitations**
The study measures intended action rather than observed sanctioning behavior, so actual behavior may differ from respondents' scenario-based responses. The models explaining propensity to sanction also have limited explanatory power, suggesting missing variables and the need to study individual characteristics. The authors note that the social legitimacy mechanism may be especially salient in status-based industries and professional elites.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how network structures, agglomeration, and dense relationships influence the propensity to sanction in field settings. The paper also calls for research on individual attributes that explain who becomes willing to police norm violations, especially because a decomposition analysis suggests that individual-level characteristics explain substantial variance in sanctioning behavior.
