# Same Page, Different Books: Extending Representational Gaps Theory to Enhance Performance in Multiteam Systems

**Abstract**
Multiteam systems are increasingly used by organizations, but are difficult to coordinate effectively. Building from theory on representational gaps, we explain why coordination between teams in multiteam systems can be hindered by inconsistencies that exist between them regarding the definition of shared problems. We argue that frame-of-reference training, an intervention that is theorized to reduce representational gaps, can facilitate coordination and subsequent performance in multiteam systems. We studied the effects of frame-of-reference training on multiteam system coordination and performance in 249 multiteam systems comprised of specialized teams. Supporting our expectations, we found that: (a) frame-of-reference training had a positive effect on multiteam system performance by enhancing between-team coordination; (b) within-team coordination improved the relationship between frame-of-reference training and between-team coordination, ultimately improving multiteam system performance; and (c) the patterns by which within-team coordination leveraged our intervention depended on teams' specific functions. Our findings extend representational gaps theory, highlight the interdependencies between team-level and system-level coordination, and demonstrate the utility of frame-of-reference training in a new setting.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what enhances between-team coordination and subsequent multiteam system performance, and when these effects are most likely to occur. It tests whether frame-of-reference training improves coordination and performance through an intervention theorized to reduce representational gaps, and whether within-team coordination in point and support teams changes the effect of that intervention.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Frame-of-reference training.
- DV(s): Multiteam system performance.
- Mediators: Between-team coordination.
- Moderators: Within-team coordination in point teams and within-team coordination in support teams.

The mechanism is that frame-of-reference training provides standardized criteria and a common language for interpreting task information, an intervention theorized to reduce representational gaps between specialized teams. This improves between-team coordination, which integrates point-team, support-team, and leadership-team actions toward system-level goals. Within-team coordination changes how strongly the training translates into between-team coordination: point-team coordination enhances the intervention's benefits, while support-team coordination shows a compensatory pattern.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends representational gaps theory from specialized members within teams to specialized component teams within multiteam systems. It also extends information processing theory by arguing that reducing uncertainty from multiple sources can interact, rather than merely add, when shaping between-team coordination and system performance. The study further clarifies how team-level processes can affect system-level coordination in multiteam systems.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of multiteam systems can use frame-of-reference training to create standardized confidence ratings and a common language for translating functionally specific judgments across team boundaries. The paper also cautions that intervention design should account for component-team functions, because point and support teams condition the training-performance pathway differently.

**Limitations**
The study uses behavioral indicators and does not directly measure psychological constructs such as shared cognition, representational gaps, or the specific cognitive and motivational mechanisms changed by training. The context is a USAF simulation with working professionals and career-relevant stakes, so extrapolation to non-military, non-simulated, or more uncertain environments requires caution. Several effects are statistically significant but small.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine the specific cognitive and motivational mechanisms altered by frame-of-reference training in multiteam systems. It should also test alternate training designs, such as different duration, feedback intensity, target audience, or levels of representational gaps. The authors further call for research on other interventions that enhance between-team coordination and on contexts with different multiteam system types, sizes, goals, cultures, and uncertainty levels.
