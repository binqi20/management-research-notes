# Which Problems to Solve? Online Knowledge Sharing and Attention Allocation in Organizations

**Abstract**
Why do individuals allocate attention to specific problems in organizations? Viewing online knowledge sharing as a matching process between knowledge providers and problems, we examine attention allocation in the context of an online community within which knowledge providers respond to problems posted by other organization members. We argue that knowledge providers are more likely to allocate attention to solving problems that more closely match their expertise, but that decisions to allocate attention are also influenced by problem characteristics such as length, breadth, and novelty, as well as by problem crowding. Analyzing 1,251 realized matches and 12,510 nonrealized matches among knowledge providers and problems posted over a 32month period on an online discussion forum within a global engineering firm, we find evidence to support our claim that attention allocation is driven by the features of a particular provider–problem match, thereby shifting the discourse from knowledge provider–seeker relationships to knowledge provider–problem matches. The implications for theories of knowledge sharing, matching processes, and managerial attention are discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why organization members allocate attention to some online knowledge-sharing problems rather than others. It specifically shifts the explanation from provider-seeker relationships to provider-problem matches in an intraorganizational online discussion forum.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Provider-problem expertise matching; problem length; problem breadth; problem novelty; problem crowding.
- DV(s): Whether a knowledge provider allocates attention to a focal problem by posting a response.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Provider-problem expertise matching moderates the relationships between attention allocation and problem length, breadth, novelty, and competing problems.

The mechanism is an attention-allocation process in which knowledge providers compare the expected benefits and costs of responding to specific problems. Closer expertise matching increases the expected value of responding and lowers cognitive costs, making providers more likely to respond. Problem length, breadth, and novelty attract attention up to a point but can become costly when cognitive load is high; problem crowding increases forum salience, with expertise matching amplifying providers' willingness to attend to the focal problem.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes by theorizing attention allocation as a matching process between providers and problems inside organizations. It extends online knowledge-sharing research beyond interpersonal provider-seeker ties by showing that expertise fit, problem characteristics, and problem crowding shape which problems receive attention. It also brings matching theory into an intraorganizational setting where the match is between an actor and an issue rather than between two actors.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that organizations using online discussion forums should not assume that making problems visible is enough for knowledge sharing. The study highlights provider-problem expertise fit, problem presentation, and competing problems as attention-allocation conditions that matter for managerial attention, while stopping short of a detailed forum-design prescription.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a single professional services organization, so the generalizability of the patterns to smaller firms, less dispersed organizations, or settings with stronger participation pressure is uncertain. The study measures attention allocation mainly as whether a provider posted a response; its supplementary response-length analysis is suggestive but cannot fully capture time, effort, or response quality.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine attention intensity with richer measures than response length and study the quality or helpfulness of responses, including peer-rated online contributions. Further work could also investigate why knowledge seekers post some problems to online forums, which individuals are more likely to post problems, and which problem types are more likely to be posted.
