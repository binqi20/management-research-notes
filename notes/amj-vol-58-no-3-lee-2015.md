# Discrimination in Selection Decisions: Integrating Stereotype Fit and Interdependence Theories

**Abstract**
We integrate stereotype fit and interdependence theories to propose a model that explains how and why decision makers discriminate in selection decisions. Our model suggests that decision makers draw on stereotypes about members of different social groups to infer the degree to which candidates possess the specific ability required for the task. Decision makers perceive candidates that have a greater ability required for the task as less (more) instrumental to their personal outcomes if they expect to compete (cooperate) with the candidate, and they discriminate in favor of candidates that are perceived as more instrumental to them. We tested our theory in the context of racial (Studies 1–3) and age (Study 4) discrimination in selection decisions with all-male samples and found evidence consistent with our predictions. By explaining when and why decision makers discriminate in favor of, but also against, members of their own social group, this research may help to explain the mixed support for the dominant view that decision makers exhibit favoritism toward candidates that belong to the same social group. In addition, our research demonstrates the importance of considering the largely overlooked role of interdependent relationships within the organization in order to understand discrimination in organizations.

**Research Question**
How do stereotype-based inferences about task fit and expected competition or cooperation with a candidate jointly shape discrimination in selection decisions? The paper asks when decision makers favor candidates from their own social group and when self-interested interdependence leads them to prefer candidates from a different social group.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Candidate social group membership-task requirement stereotype fit; expected decision-maker-candidate interdependence as competition versus cooperation.
- DV(s): Selection preference or selection decision for candidates differing by race or age.
- Mediators: Perceived instrumentality of the candidate to the decision maker's personal outcomes.
- Moderators: Task requirement, interdependence type, and the social group contrast used to operationalize stereotype fit.

Decision makers use stereotypes to infer whether a candidate has the ability required for the focal task. When the decision maker expects competition, a candidate stereotyped as more able becomes a more threatening future competitor and is perceived as less instrumental; when cooperation is expected, the same candidate becomes a more useful future collaborator and is perceived as more instrumental. Selection preference follows this perceived instrumentality, producing discrimination for or against the stereotypically stronger candidate depending on interdependence.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper qualifies the dominant in-group favoritism account of discrimination in selection decisions by specifying conditions under which decision makers discriminate against members of their own social group. It contributes by integrating stereotype fit theory with interdependence theory, showing that task requirements and expected intra-organizational relationships jointly determine whether stereotypes produce favorable or unfavorable treatment.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not treat discrimination risk as only a matter of generalized prejudice or similarity attraction. The paper suggests that selection systems should account for whether evaluators expect to compete or cooperate with the candidate, and that accountability practices may reduce self-interested reliance on stereotypes in hiring and assignment decisions.

**Limitations**
The studies used all-male decision makers and candidates, and Studies 1-3 focused on White-Asian racial comparisons, so generalization to other gender and race combinations requires caution. The experiments also focused on choices between equally qualified candidates; stereotype-based effects may be weaker when candidates clearly differ in qualifications or when decision-maker-candidate interdependence is weak.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the model with other gender, race, religion, and sexual-orientation combinations, and examine individual differences in self-interest, prejudice, and organizational orientation. The authors also call for large-scale passive observational studies of hiring committees and for research on how the strength of interdependence affects the model's explanatory power.
