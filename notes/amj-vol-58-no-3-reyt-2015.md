# Seeing the Forest for the Trees: Exploratory Learning, Mobile Technology, and Knowledge Workers’ Role Integration Behaviors

**Abstract**
Role integration is the new workplace reality for many employees. The prevalence of mobile technologies (e.g., laptops, smartphones, tablets) that are increasingly wearable and nearly always “on” makes it difficult to keep role boundaries separate and distinct. We draw upon boundary theory and construal level theory to hypothesize that role integration behaviors shift people from thinking concretely to thinking more abstractly about their work. The results of an archival study of Enron executives’ emails, two experiments, and a multi-wave field study of knowledge workers provide evidence of positive associations between role integration behaviors, higher construal level, and more exploratory learning activities.

**Research Question**
How does role integration, enabled by mobile technologies and blurred boundaries across work and nonwork roles, shape knowledge workers’ construal level and exploratory learning behavior? The paper asks whether role integration behaviors move workers toward more abstract work representations and whether that abstraction explains greater exploratory learning.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Role integration behaviors, including temporal role integration in Enron email patterns, experimentally primed role integration versus segmentation, and spatial role integration through mobile-device-enabled work.
- DV(s): Work-based construal level, exploratory learning intentions, distant search behavior, and exploratory learning activities.
- Mediators: Work-based construal level mediates the relationship between role integration and exploratory learning in the field study; exploratory learning intentions mediate the construal-level effect on distant search in Study 3.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism starts from boundary theory: role integration makes multiple role demands simultaneously salient and weakens temporal, spatial, and social boundaries. Because people have bounded cognitive resources, these overlapping demands push workers toward higher-level, more abstract construals that emphasize central goals and transferable meanings rather than local details. Higher construal then supports exploratory learning by helping workers generate, compare, and search across more distant alternatives. Across the studies, the authors treat role integration as a behaviorally grounded antecedent of abstract work cognition, which in turn predicts exploratory learning.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends boundary theory by identifying construal level as a cognitive mechanism through which role integration affects work behavior, moving beyond prior emphasis on stress, satisfaction, and work-family conflict. It extends construal level theory by applying abstraction to social roles rather than only to discrete objects, events, or temporal distance. It also contributes to organizational learning research by opening the cognitive black box of exploratory learning and linking individual role integration to distant search and exploratory activity.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that mobile-technology-enabled role integration can shape how abstractly knowledge workers think, not only how available they are. The paper suggests that role integration may be useful for employees whose work benefits from exploratory learning, innovation, and broader search, but may be costly where effectiveness depends on focus, detail-orientation, and execution. It also implies that managers can shape construal through work design, technology expectations, time horizons, and reward systems.

**Limitations**
Study 1 is archival and cannot establish causal direction, and its email-based measures are noisy proxies for role integration and construal level. Study 4 reduces common-method concerns through a multi-wave design, but its dependent variable is self-reported exploratory learning. The authors also limit the model to cumulative micro-role integration and leave open whether momentary role integration, macro-role transitions, role-integration preferences, or non-mobile forms of integration operate similarly.

**Future Research**
Future research should identify when the benefits of role integration for exploratory learning outweigh its costs for attention, stress, and focused performance. The authors call for more dynamic work on momentary versus cumulative integration, comparisons across temporal, spatial, and social boundaries, and tests of whether mobile technology is a boundary condition or one example of a broader integration process. They also suggest examining role preferences, macro-role transitions, and managerial tools for monitoring or shaping employees’ role integration.
