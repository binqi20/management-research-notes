# When the Leader Follows: Avoiding Dethronement through Imitation

**Abstract**
When is imitation of follower actions an effective competitive strategy for a leader? Building on prior work in competitive dynamics from the Austrian School perspective, we propose that imitation can be an effective means of staying ahead, even in the absence of mimetic social pressures. This is because the leader’s imitation of follower actions represents equilibrating moves to maintain the status quo in reaction to the disequilibrating actions that the follower undertakes to catch up with the leader. Furthermore, reduction of difference in competitive positioning between leader and follower serves the same purpose, and both imitation strategies are complementary. These effects of “action imitation” and “positioning imitation,” we argue, are moderated by the degree of environmental uncertainty, by the extent of the leader’s initial advantage, and by the difference between leader and follower capabilities. Our theoretical arguments are supported by an analysis of data on head-to-head boat races from the America’s Cup World Series. By developing mechanisms that take endogenous and exogenous contingencies of competitive interactions into account, this paper advances competitive dynamics as a predictive theory of performance outcomes.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether, and under what conditions, a leader’s imitation of follower actions can be an effective competitive strategy for avoiding dethronement. It focuses on how action imitation and positioning imitation shape the likelihood that the leader remains ahead under uncertainty, initial advantage, and follower capability differences.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader action imitation of the follower, leader positioning imitation of the follower, and their interaction.
- DV(s): Leadership defended, measured as whether the boat leading at the start of the upwind leg remains first at the windward mark.
- Mediators: Not modeled as formal mediators; the theoretical process is equilibration through matched competitive trajectories and reduced exposure differences.
- Moderators: Environmental uncertainty, leader initial advantage, and follower capability advantage.

The core mechanism is that follower actions create disequilibrium and opportunities to catch the leader, while leader imitation can equilibrate those moves and preserve the status quo. Action imitation aligns the leader’s tactical trajectory with the follower’s moves, and positioning imitation reduces longer-term differences in exposure to environmental shifts. The results support positive direct effects of both imitation strategies and their complementarity, but the moderation results are mixed: environmental uncertainty strengthens action imitation and weakens positioning imitation, initial advantage strengthens both, and follower capability advantage weakens action imitation but not positioning imitation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper redirects competitive dynamics research from the familiar question of followers imitating leaders to the less examined possibility that leaders imitate followers to defend leadership. It uses the Austrian School view of competition as movement toward and away from equilibrium to explain imitation as an equilibrating response to follower disruption. It also advances competitive dynamics as a predictive theory by specifying endogenous and exogenous boundary conditions around imitation: environmental uncertainty, initial advantage, and capability differences.

**Practical Implication**
For market leaders, the findings imply that selective imitation of follower moves can be a defensive strategy, especially when the environment is uncertain and the leader has a strong head start. The paper also warns that imitation has costs: if followers possess superior capabilities, leader imitation can release information that helps them exploit those capabilities and catch up. Managers therefore need to weigh the defensive benefits of matching follower moves against reputational concerns and the risk of strengthening a capable challenger.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting provides fine-grained data from match racing, but the authors state that generalizability to business settings still needs validation. The study examines head-to-head competition, so the theory also needs testing in settings with more than two rivals. The authors further note the difficulty of separating imitation from shared reactions to environmental shocks, and they discuss possible sailing-specific aerodynamics and right-of-way concerns.

**Future Research**
Future work should test the theory in business contexts with multiple rivals and more complex competitive interdependence. Researchers should use more fine-grained approaches to distinguish imitation from common responses to environmental shocks. The paper also points to settings where firms receive low-lag information about exogenous conditions, such as oil prices, foreign exchange rates, stock prices, commodity prices, airlines, and trading, as useful contexts for studying uncertainty and imitation.
