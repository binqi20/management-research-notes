# The Dynamic Nature of Collective Turnover and Unit Performance: The Impact of Time, Quality, and Replacements

**Abstract**
This study builds from context-emergent turnover theory to examine the dynamic properties of turnover rates, including: (a) the changing quality and quantity of the human capital resources that depart, (b) the changing turnover dispersion (i.e., how distributed turnover events are over time), and (c) the changing quality and quantity of replacement hires. We examine these properties using data drawn from a sample of retail employees nested within stores of a prominent U.S. retail chain over five quarters, and show that the turnover rate (level) is conceptually and empirically distinct from turnover rate change, and that the two interact with each other to influence changes in unit performance. We also find that the relationship between turnover rate change and change in unit performance is moderated by both the quality of those who leave as well as turnover dispersion. Overall, we contribute to turnover rate, staffing, and human capital resource literatures by testing core context-emergent turnover theory propositions to show when, why, and how turnover rate change and replacement hires, as part of a holistic human capital resource system, influence unit performance.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how turnover rate change affects unit performance over time, above and beyond static turnover rates. It also asks when this relationship is strengthened or weakened by turnover quality, turnover dispersion, and the quality and quantity of replacement hires in a human capital resource flow system.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Turnover rate and turnover rate change; changes in turnover quality, turnover dispersion, replacement hire quality, and replacement hire quantity.
- DV(s): Change in unit performance, measured as adjusted controllable unit profit.
- Mediators: Not directly tested; the theory emphasizes workload, workflow, routines, and psychological emergence such as climate and cohesion.
- Moderators: Baseline turnover rate, quality of turnover, turnover dispersion, replacement hire quality, and quantity of replacement hires.

The paper theorizes turnover as part of a dynamic human capital resource flow system rather than as an isolated rate. Increasing turnover rate change is expected to disrupt unit workload, coordination, routines, and psychological processes, thereby lowering unit performance. The results support the distinct negative effect of turnover rate change, show that losses are stronger when departing employees are higher quality, and show that more dispersed turnover weakens the negative effect. The predicted moderating benefits of replacement quality are not supported under two-tailed tests, and replacement quantity moderates in the opposite direction from the prediction.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends collective turnover research by distinguishing static turnover rates from turnover rate change and showing that both matter for unit performance. It provides an empirical test of context-emergent turnover theory by modeling turnover quantity, turnover quality, turnover dispersion, and replacement hires within one longitudinal human capital resource system. It also shows that the timing and quality of turnover events add explanatory value that static turnover-rate models miss.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should monitor changes in turnover rates, not only average turnover levels, because small changes can be especially consequential in units with lower baseline turnover. The findings imply that firms should pay particular attention to retaining higher-quality employees and to avoiding clustered turnover events. Staffing forecasts should integrate turnover timing and replacement patterns, while also avoiding overstaffing that may erode profit.

**Limitations**
The study uses one Fortune 100 U.S. retail chain with high turnover and relatively low job complexity, so the findings may not generalize to lower-turnover, higher-complexity, or non-retail contexts. The sample covers five quarters and may be affected by seasonality, and the study cannot fully rule out omitted managerial ability despite including a replacement manager quality control. Replacement quality and turnover dispersion are measured with specific operationalizations, and the sample consists exclusively of new hires.

**Future Research**
Future research should directly measure the workload, workflow, routine, climate, and cohesion processes that the paper theorizes but does not observe. Additional studies should test turnover quality and dispersion in other industries, lower-turnover settings, and more complex jobs. Research should also refine turnover dispersion measures and examine the timing of replacement hires relative to turnover events.
