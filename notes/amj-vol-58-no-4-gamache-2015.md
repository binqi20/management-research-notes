# Motivated to Acquire? The Impact of CEO Regulatory Focus on Firm Acquisitions

**Abstract**
Regulatory focus theory proposes that decision making and goal pursuit occur via either a promotion focus (a sensitivity to gains and a desire for advancement and growth) or a prevention focus (a sensitivity to losses and a desire for stability and security). Recent theorizing in strategic management research suggests that there may be important firm-level outcomes influenced by the regulatory focus of top executives. We expand research on regulatory focus theory by testing whether chief executive officers’ (CEOs’) regulatory focus impacts the proclivity of firms to undertake acquisitions. Furthermore, regulatory focus theory suggests that the effects of people’s promotion and prevention foci are magnified when their regulatory focus is congruent with salient situational characteristics, a phenomenon known as regulatory fit. As a test of this idea, we demonstrate how the effects of CEO promotion and prevention foci are differentially impacted by one such characteristic, namely incentive compensation. Our findings indicate that CEO regulatory focus impacts both the quantity and scale of acquisitions undertaken by a firm. We also find some support for our arguments that these relationships are moderated by stock option pay.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether CEOs' promotion and prevention regulatory focus shape firms' proclivity to undertake acquisitions. It also asks whether stock option pay moderates these regulatory focus-acquisition relationships through regulatory fit.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO promotion focus and CEO prevention focus, measured through LIWC content analysis of letters to shareholders.
- DV(s): Number of completed majority acquisitions and logged total annual value of completed majority acquisitions.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Stock options granted to the CEO, tested as a moderator of the CEO regulatory focus-acquisition relationships.

The mechanism is that promotion-focused CEOs attend more to accomplishment, growth, opportunity search, and the gains available from acquisitions, making them more inclined toward more and larger acquisitions. Prevention-focused CEOs attend more to security, responsibility, possible losses, and due diligence, making them less inclined toward more and larger acquisitions. Stock option pay creates a gain-oriented compensation context that can attenuate prevention-focused CEOs' conservative acquisition tendencies, although the paper does not find that it further amplifies promotion-focused CEOs' acquisition activity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends regulatory focus theory from individual and non-executive leadership outcomes to firm-level strategic actions by showing that CEO regulatory focus predicts acquisition quantity and scale. It contributes to strategic leadership research by treating regulatory focus as a motivation-based CEO attribute distinct from self-concept traits such as hubris or narcissism. It also contributes to executive compensation and acquisition research by showing that stock option pay interacts with CEO prevention focus, qualifying simple incentive-compensation accounts of acquisition risk taking.

**Practical Implication**
CEOs can use awareness of their own regulatory focus to counterbalance tendencies that might push them toward overly aggressive or overly cautious acquisition behavior. Boards may need to provide more cautious oversight for highly promotion-focused CEOs and more encouragement for highly prevention-focused CEOs when risk taking is warranted. The findings also suggest that stock option pay can be a tool for encouraging acquisition risk taking among prevention-focused CEOs, but not necessarily for further increasing the acquisition activity of already promotion-focused CEOs.

**Limitations**
The paper's measure of CEO regulatory focus depends on an unobtrusive content analysis of annual-report letters rather than direct observation of CEO cognition or acquisition decision processes. The authors discuss concerns that shareholder letters may involve public relations staff, although they cite prior evidence and supplemental analyses to support the measure's validity. The empirical scope is acquisitions and stock option pay, so the findings do not by themselves establish whether regulatory focus similarly affects other strategic actions or other compensation designs.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether CEO regulatory focus affects acquisition performance and whether promotion- and prevention-focused CEOs pursue different types of acquisitions. The authors also call for studies of other strategic outcomes, including research and development, greenfield investment, capital investment, international expansion, divestitures, and conformity to industry practices. Additional work could test other moderators, such as top management team or board regulatory focus, industry dynamism, munificence, and other combinations of CEO traits and compensation elements.
