# Network Defense: Pruning, Grafting, and Closing to Prevent Leakage of Strategic Knowledge to Rivals

**Abstract**
We explore how firms protect themselves from the risks of knowledge spillover to indirectly connected rivals in a network of interorganizational ties. We argue that the safeguards to limit opportunistic behavior by directly linked firms in a dyad, which have been the focus of extant research, are insufficient to overcome extra-dyadic leakage risks. Instead, firms terminate or avoid ties that expose their knowledge to indirectly linked rivals (“pruning” and “grafting”) and embed themselves in dense networks (“closing”) to prevent strategic knowledge spillover. Through a longitudinal study of German board interlocks during 1990–2003, we find that firms are more likely to prune, graft, and close their networks as they accumulate strategic knowledge and as the firms to which they are interlocked increasingly generate indirect ties to competitors, even when controlling for dyadic safeguards discussed by prior research. We capture strategic knowledge by tracking firms’ experience in the former Warsaw Pact countries from immediately after the sudden fall of communism in 1990 until 2003. The study introduces indirect links to rivals as a source of knowledge spillover in networks, shows how firms deal with extra-dyadic risks, and provides a defensive explanation for the evolution of network composition and structure.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how firms protect strategic knowledge from leaking to indirectly connected rivals through interorganizational networks. It examines whether firms with more strategic experience respond to indirect competitor links by terminating existing board interlocks, avoiding new risky interlocks, and increasing ego-network closure.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Indirect competitor links generated by existing or potential board-interlock partners; focal firms' Eastern European experience as accumulated strategic knowledge.
- DV(s): Board tie termination, board tie formation, and ego-network closure.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Focal firms' accumulated strategic experience in Eastern Europe moderates the relationship between indirect competitor links and pruning, grafting, and closing.

The central mechanism is defensive network adaptation. Indirect links to rivals create extra-dyadic leakage risk because partners can transmit strategically sensitive knowledge beyond the focal dyad. As firms accumulate strategically valuable experience, the cost of leakage rises, making them more likely to prune existing risky ties, graft by forming safer new ties, and close their networks to increase monitoring and circumscribe information flows.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to network and strategy research by identifying indirect ties to rivals as a distinct source of knowledge-spillover risk, not merely a channel for learning or intelligence. It shows that dyadic safeguards such as legal protections, trust, and embeddedness may be insufficient for extra-dyadic leakage risks. It also offers a defensive explanation for network evolution: firms may close structural holes and alter network composition to preserve strategic knowledge rather than only to access resources.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that firms should assess not only direct partners but also the second-order competitor paths those partners create. In board networks and other interorganizational settings, firms may need to manage tie termination, tie formation, and network closure as knowledge-protection tools when strategic knowledge becomes competitively sensitive.

**Limitations**
The authors do not directly observe firms' motives for dissolving or forming board ties, nor the exact strength and content of knowledge exchanged through those ties, so the design cannot conclusively prove the proposed knowledge-leakage mechanism. The study is also limited by its focus on German board interlocks, projection of a two-mode director-firm network into an interfirm network, and available measures of Eastern European investment experience.

**Future Research**
The paper suggests studying whether direct and indirect links to rivals create different leakage risks in other networks, such as alliances, where direct competitor ties are more common. It also calls for research on different path lengths to rivals, the possible intelligence benefits of indirect competitor links, and heterogeneity in the size and mode of investments that shape perceived leakage threats.
