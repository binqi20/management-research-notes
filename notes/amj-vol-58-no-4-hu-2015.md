# Making a Difference in the Teamwork: Linking Team Prosocial Motivation to Team Processes and Effectiveness

**Abstract**
Although the importance of team motivation has been increasingly emphasized, few studies have focused on prosocial motivation. Integrating theories on team effectiveness with prosocial motivation, we propose a theoretical model that links team prosocial motivation to team effectiveness as mediated by team processes. Team process is captured through the task-driven process of team cooperation and the affect-based team viability, and team effectiveness is operationalized as team performance, team organizational citizenship behavior (OCB), and team voluntary turnover. The model is tested in Study 1, a field study with three-source data collected from 310 members of 67 work teams over four time periods, and Study 2, a laboratory experiment with 124 four-person teams in which team prosocial motivation is manipulated. In Studies 1 and 2, we find support for indirect effects of team prosocial motivation on team performance and team OCB through the mediating role of team cooperation. Team voluntary turnover is indirectly affected by team prosocial motivation through team viability. Furthermore, in both studies the indirect effects of team prosocial motivation on team performance and team OCB through team cooperation and on team voluntary turnover through team viability are stronger when the nature of the teams’ work requires greater task interdependence.

**Research Question**
How does team prosocial motivation relate to team effectiveness, and through which team processes are these effects realized? The paper also asks when these indirect relationships are stronger or weaker, focusing on task interdependence as the team-context condition.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Team prosocial motivation.
- DV(s): Team performance, team organizational citizenship behavior, and team voluntary turnover.
- Mediators: Team cooperation and team viability.
- Moderators: Task interdependence, modeled as strengthening the first-stage relationship from team prosocial motivation to team processes and the resulting conditional indirect effects.

The mechanism is that teams sharing a desire to benefit others are more willing to cooperate, share ideas, reduce social loafing, and coordinate work toward collective outputs. This task-driven cooperation transmits team prosocial motivation into higher team performance and OCB. In parallel, team prosocial motivation can build stronger emotional ties and team viability, which mainly explains lower team voluntary turnover. Task interdependence strengthens these pathways because highly interdependent work gives prosocially motivated members more opportunity to interact, coordinate, and translate prosocial purpose into team processes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends prosocial motivation from the individual level to the team level by defining team prosocial motivation as a shared collective belief about working to benefit others. It contributes to team effectiveness theory by showing that task-oriented cooperation and affect-based viability are distinct mediating processes linking team motivation to different team outcomes. It also identifies task interdependence as a boundary condition that determines when team prosocial motivation is more likely to improve effectiveness.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should cultivate team prosocial motivation when team outcomes depend on coordinated action, because concern for others can support smoother cooperation, stronger interpersonal ties, higher performance, more OCB, and lower voluntary turnover. The paper suggests combining prosocial motivation with work designs that create real task interdependence; high prosocial motivation without interdependence gives members fewer opportunities to translate their motives into effective teamwork. Interventions such as increasing beneficiary contact, modeling prosocial behavior, and selecting or introducing members with high prosocial motivation may help create a prosocial team culture.

**Limitations**
Study 1 is time-lagged but not experimental, so reverse causality and omitted organizational or team-level factors remain possible. Study 2 improves causal inference but uses temporary undergraduate student teams in a laboratory task, which differs from permanent work teams in organizations. The authors also note some inconsistencies across the studies, especially in whether mediation is full or partial for team OCB and team voluntary turnover.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine additional task-based and affect-based mechanisms, such as whether a prosocial motivation climate creates positive affective tone or work engagement that improves team outcomes. The paper also calls for research on other boundary conditions, including opportunities for team members to contact beneficiaries. Further studies could test whether task processes and affective processes differ systematically in predicting performance, OCB, and turnover across team contexts.
