# Decoding the Adaptability–Rigidity Puzzle: Evidence from Pharmaceutical Incumbents’ Pursuit of Gene Therapy and Monoclonal Antibodies

**Abstract**
The emergence of radical technologies presents a significant challenge to incumbent firms. We study firms’ management of radical technological change by separating their actions into upstream research (the “R” of R&D) and downstream development (the “D” of R&D). We introduce two contingencies to explain when incumbents’ research investments in radical technologies translate into product development and when these upstream investments may get voided by organizational inertia downstream. First, radical technologies can differ in how they conform to incumbents’ existing business models, impacting the extent to which the movement of research outputs toward development will be subject to inertial pressures. Second, incumbents can invest in a radical technology through a variety of modes (internal research, external research contracts, alliances, acquisitions). These modes represent unique combinations of who does research and who is involved in the decision for subsequent development, and, hence, differ in the extent to which they are shielded from inertial pressures. This difference helps explain why incumbents, despite responding to radical technologies, may still be unable to adapt, as well as what types of investments will be more effective in helping firms navigate technological change. Evidence from pharmaceutical incumbents’ pursuit of monoclonal antibodies and gene therapy offers strong support for our arguments.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when incumbent firms' research investments in radical technological regimes translate into downstream product development and when those investments are voided by organizational inertia. It specifically examines how this translation depends on whether the radical technology conforms to the incumbent business model and on whether the firm invests through internal research, research contracts, research alliances, or acquisitions.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Incumbents' research investments in radical technologies through internal research, research contracts, research alliances, and technology acquisitions.
- DV(s): Initiation of drug development, operationalized as whether the incumbent initiates a new preclinical trial for mAbs or GT in a given year.
- Mediators: Not directly modeled; the theorized mechanism is organizational inertia in the movement from research output to development commitment.
- Moderators: Technological regime fit with the prevailing business model, comparing sustaining mAbs with disruptive GT; organizational mode also shapes who participates in development decisions.

The mechanism is that internal research and contract research leave development decisions inside the incumbent organization, where managerial cognition, incentives, resource allocation, and routine rigidity can block movement from research to development when the technology disrupts the existing business model. Research alliances and acquisitions are more structurally separated from the incumbent's mainstream organization and involve external partners or acquired personnel with stronger incentives to commercialize the new technology. This separation helps explain why alliances and acquisitions are more effective for GT, while internal and contract research more readily translate into development for sustaining mAbs.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on incumbent adaptation to radical technological change by separating upstream invention from downstream innovation and showing that the transition between them is a distinct locus of inertia. It adds a technological contingency by distinguishing sustaining and disruptive radical regimes according to business model fit, and an organizational contingency by comparing internal research, contracts, alliances, and acquisitions. The result is a framework that moves beyond the simple adaptability-versus-rigidity contrast and explains when incumbent investments are likely to support adaptation.

**Practical Implication**
Incumbents should not treat investment in a radical technology as equivalent to adaptation, because research activity may fail to become product development when downstream decision processes remain tied to the existing business model. For disruptive technologies, managers may need organizational modes that separate development decisions from incumbent routines and bring in external partners or acquired teams with stronger commercialization incentives. In the pharmaceutical context, alliances and acquisitions can help overcome the inertia that limits internal and contract research in gene therapy.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a single industry, so the boundary conditions of the findings need to be examined in other empirical contexts. The paper observes preclinical trial initiation rather than final commercialization outcomes such as product approval, market share, sales, or profits. The authors also note limits in observing each underlying research project and potential measurement error, especially in classifying acquisitions that involved targets pursuing both mAbs and GT.

**Future Research**
Future research could test the framework in other industries and technology settings to clarify its generalizability and boundary conditions. Studies could examine final commercialization outcomes and project-level pathways rather than only preclinical development initiation. Additional work could use in-depth project histories to trace how specific research investments move through different organizational modes.
