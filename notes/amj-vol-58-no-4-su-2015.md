# Product Diversification and Financial Performance: The Moderating Role of Secondary Stakeholders

**Abstract**
The challenges firms face increase with their product diversification levels because different product markets possess different sociopolitical issues. We argue that secondary stakeholders, as represented by various nonprofit or non-governmental organizations, serve as agents mitigating the external constraints embedded within sociopolitical environments. Firms should therefore maintain relationships with different secondarystakeholder scopes commensurate with their product diversification levels in order to enhance financial performance. Analyzing a sample of U.S. Fortune 500 firms during the period from 1996 to 2003, we found that secondary stakeholders play a positive moderating role in the relationship between product diversification and financial performance. Furthermore, this moderating effect was stronger in the case of unrelated diversification than in related diversification.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what moderating role secondary stakeholders play in the relationship between product diversification and firm financial performance. It also asks whether this moderating effect differs between related and unrelated diversification.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Product diversification, decomposed into total, related, and unrelated diversification.
- DV(s): Firm financial performance, measured primarily as return on assets.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Diversity or scope of secondary stakeholders, measured through the diversity of nonprofit organizations to which a firm donates; the paper also compares related versus unrelated diversification.

The paper argues that diversification exposes firms to more varied sociopolitical environments and external constraints. Secondary stakeholders such as nonprofit and non-governmental organizations can provide legitimacy, sociopolitical knowledge, and reputational support that reduce those external costs. Broad secondary-stakeholder scope is therefore expected to improve the diversification-performance relationship, especially for unrelated diversifiers that face more heterogeneous product markets and stakeholder environments.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends product diversification research by shifting attention from internal coordination costs to the external sociopolitical costs that diversified firms face. It contributes to stakeholder management research by treating secondary stakeholders as heterogeneous and by showing that the scope of nonprofit relationships conditions the financial consequences of corporate strategy. It also links corporate social behavior and corporate strategy by arguing that social engagement matters financially when it fits the firm's product scope.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should align the breadth of secondary-stakeholder relationships with the firm's diversification scope rather than treating corporate donations as a generic social activity. Highly diversified firms, especially unrelated diversifiers, may benefit from broader relationships with nonprofits and NGOs that provide legitimacy, knowledge, and reputational protection. Less diversified firms may not need the same breadth of secondary-stakeholder engagement.

**Limitations**
The authors could not directly assess the latent sociopolitical threats that donations may have mitigated or identify managers' motives for donating to nonprofit organizations. They also lacked exact donation amounts to each nonprofit and could not match specific nonprofits to the industries in which each firm operated. The study focuses on donation diversity and therefore does not capture other forms of secondary-stakeholder engagement.

**Future Research**
Future research could use surveys and interviews to examine how firms control external environments through secondary stakeholders and when stakeholder engagement becomes greenwashing. Studies could use more fine-grained donation data and broader social performance data to connect particular nonprofit relationships with specific product-market environments. Further work could also examine secondary-stakeholder diversity as separation or disparity, including conflicts of interest or power imbalances among nonprofit partners.
