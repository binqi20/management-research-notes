# “You Wouldn’t Like Me When I’m Sleepy”: Leaders’ Sleep, Daily Abusive Supervision, and Work Unit Engagement

**Abstract**
We examine the daily sleep of leaders as an antecedent to daily abusive supervisory behavior and work unit engagement. Drawing from ego depletion theory, our theoretical extension includes a serial mediation model of nightly sleep quantity and quality as predictors of abusive supervision. We argue that poor nightly sleep influences leaders to enact daily abusive behaviors via ego depletion, and these abusive behaviors ultimately result in decreased daily subordinate unit work engagement. We test this model through an experience sampling study spread over 10 workdays with data from both supervisors and their subordinates. Our study supports the role of the indirect effects of sleep quality (but not of sleep quantity) via leader ego depletion and daily abusive supervisor behavior on daily subordinate unit work engagement.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether leaders' nightly sleep quantity and sleep quality explain day-to-day variation in abusive supervisory behavior. It further asks whether leader ego depletion and daily abusive supervisor behavior serially transmit leaders' sleep quality into lower daily subordinate unit work engagement.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Daily leader sleep quantity and daily leader sleep quality.
- DV(s): Daily abusive supervisor behavior and daily unit work engagement.
- Mediators: Daily leader ego depletion as the first-stage mediator; daily abusive supervisor behavior as the second-stage mediator for unit work engagement.
- Moderators: No primary moderator in the hypothesized model; supplementary analyses examined abusive supervision variability, abusive supervision trend, and sleep quantity by sleep quality interactions.

The mechanism is a self-regulation pathway grounded in ego depletion theory. Poor sleep quality is theorized to leave leaders with fewer self-regulatory resources, making them less able to suppress impulses toward hostile verbal or nonverbal behavior. Those daily abusive behaviors then reduce subordinates' willingness to invest physical, emotional, and cognitive energy in work. The results support this serial mediation for sleep quality but not for sleep quantity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends abusive supervision theory by shifting attention from stable abusive supervisors to daily abusive supervisory behavior that varies within the same leader. It connects a nonwork antecedent, leader sleep quality, to leader behavior through ego depletion and then to subordinate unit engagement through a cross-level daily process. The findings also qualify sleep-based self-regulation theory by showing stronger support for sleep quality than sleep quantity in this field setting.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should treat abusive supervision partly as a daily, preventable risk rather than only as a stable leader trait. Leadership training can help supervisors recognize poor sleep as a trigger for abusive behavior and delay sensitive interactions after especially poor sleep. The paper also suggests practical sleep-management strategies, including protecting off-hours from smartphones and work email, because better sleep quality may reduce next-day abusive behavior.

**Limitations**
The study does not manipulate sleep, ego depletion, or abusive supervision, so causal inference rests on theory, temporal separation, and the within-person design rather than random assignment. The authors also note that they did not include an exhaustive set of day-level controls such as workload, stress, health, or well-being. The study focuses on supervisor sleep and does not test subordinate sleep as either an antecedent or consequence.

**Future Research**
Future research should test day-level controls, subordinate sleep, and lagged crossover effects between supervisors and subordinates. Additional studies could examine individual-difference moderators such as trait self-control, agreeableness, and emotional stability. The authors also call for work on other outcomes, longer timeframes, chronic sleep deprivation, and the more detailed causal steps linking abusive supervision to work unit engagement.
