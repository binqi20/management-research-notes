# Passing Probation: Earnings Management by Interim CEOs and Its Effect on Their Promotion Prospects

**Abstract**
Drawing on chief executive officer (CEO) succession research and the impression management literature, we examine earnings management by interim CEOs, its impact on interim CEOs’ promotion prospects, and the moderating effect of governance mechanisms on the relationship between the two. Based on a sample of 145 interim CEO succession events in U.S. public firms from 2004 to 2008, we find that (a) an interim CEO is more likely than a noninterim CEO to engage in earnings management to improve firm earnings performance (“income-increasing earnings management”), (b) the greater the income-increasing earnings management, the more likely it is that the interim CEO will be promoted to the permanent position, and (c) the relationship between earnings management and the likelihood of interim CEO promotion is weakened when effective internal and external governance mechanisms are in place.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether interim CEOs manage earnings upward during temporary succession periods, whether such income-increasing earnings management improves their chances of being promoted to permanent CEO, and whether internal and external governance mechanisms weaken that promotion benefit. It studies interim CEO succession as a setting in which executives have career incentives to create favorable impressions for boards, shareholders, analysts, and other appointment-relevant stakeholders.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Interim CEO status during the interim period; income-increasing earnings management measured through discretionary accruals; governance moderators including board independence, board ownership, board financial or accounting expertise, nonbusy board, and analyst coverage.
- DV(s): Income-increasing earnings management in the DID models; interim CEO promotion to the permanent CEO position in the event-history models.
- Mediators: Stakeholder impression formation is the theorized process, but the paper does not directly estimate a mediator.
- Moderators: Board independence, board ownership, board financial or accounting expertise, nonbusy board, and analyst coverage.

The mechanism is that interim CEOs face an uncertain, temporary leadership contest and therefore have strong incentives to appear capable before stakeholders who influence the permanent appointment decision. Because they may have limited time and authority to produce substantive strategic results, they can use within-GAAP income-increasing earnings management to make reported performance look better. The paper finds that interim CEOs manage earnings upward more than comparable noninterim successors, that higher earnings management predicts a higher likelihood of promotion, and that the promotion effect is weaker when board financial/accounting expertise, board attentiveness, and analyst monitoring make the managed performance signal more likely to be scrutinized.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to CEO succession research by shifting attention from permanent CEO appointments to the interim CEO succession process and by showing how interim CEOs may use managerial tactics to improve promotion prospects. It extends impression management theory by treating income-increasing earnings management as a career-oriented tactic through which executives shape stakeholder evaluations in a leadership contest. It also contributes to corporate governance and earnings management research by showing that governance mechanisms may matter less by directly reducing earnings management and more by weakening the link between earnings management and promotion outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Boards should not treat improved short-term reported performance during an interim CEO period as sufficient evidence of permanent-CEO capability. Firms should maintain stronger succession planning, develop internal CEO pipelines, and define realistic interim-period targets so temporary leaders are not rewarded primarily for symbolic performance. When interim succession is unavoidable, boards can reduce this risk by appointing financially knowledgeable and committed directors and by relying on external monitoring such as analyst coverage.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that interim CEOs may be appointed for strategic objectives other than financial-performance improvement, such as international expansion or product innovation, which the study does not fully observe. The analysis focuses on earnings management as one impression management tactic and cannot capture other tactics, such as symbolic policies, ingratiation, or verbal impression management. The sample is limited to U.S. public firms, so the findings may not generalize to private firms, family firms, emerging economies, or other governance contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether interim CEOs adapt their behavior to firms' specific strategic needs beyond financial-performance presentation. Additional studies could use survey or richer behavioral data to test how multiple impression management tactics work independently or jointly during interim succession. The authors also call for evidence from unlisted firms and emerging economies, where leadership succession and governance may operate differently from U.S. public-firm settings.
