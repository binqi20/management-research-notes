# Cracking but not Breaking: Joint Effects of Faultline Strength and Diversity Climate on Loyal Behavior

**Abstract**
This study examines the joint effects of diversity composition (as manifested in faultline strength) and diversity management (as manifested in diversity climate) on loyal behavior. Using data gathered from a sample of 1,652 managerial employees in 76 work units, we assess the cross-level effects of unit-level relationshipand task-related faultline strength and diversity climate on individual-level loyal behavior of managerial employees. We find a negative relationship between gender faultline strength and loyal behavior, and a positive relationship between diversity climate and loyal behavior. In addition, we find that work unit diversity climate moderates the relationships between the strength of gender and function faultlines and loyal behavior; specifically, a supportive diversity climate reduces the negative consequences associated with relationshiprelated faultlines and increases the positive consequences associated with task-related faultlines. The results highlight the value of simultaneously considering faultlines and diversity climate in understanding and managing workforce diversity.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether different types of work-unit faultlines have different effects on managerial employees' loyal behavior and whether diversity climate constrains or amplifies those effects. It focuses on relationship-related faultlines based on gender and age, task-related faultlines based on function and tenure, and work-unit diversity climate as a cross-level contextual moderator.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Work-unit relationship-related faultline strength, measured through gender and age faultline strength; work-unit task-related faultline strength, measured through function and tenure faultline strength.
- DV(s): Individual-level loyal behavior by managerial employees.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators; the theory invokes social categorization, identity-based subgrouping, information processing, and inclusion as explanatory processes.
- Moderators: Work-unit diversity climate moderates the relationships between faultline strength and loyal behavior.

The paper argues that relationship-related faultlines can activate social categorization, identity-based subgrouping, and intergroup conflict, reducing employees' willingness to engage in loyal behavior for the work unit or organization. Task-related faultlines may instead expose members to distinct knowledge and perspectives, potentially supporting extra effort and task-focused citizenship. Diversity climate shapes whether dormant faultlines become harmful or useful: supportive climates reduce the negative consequences of gender-based relationship faultlines and make function-based task faultlines more likely to support loyal behavior.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends faultline research by separating gender, age, function, and tenure faultline strength rather than treating faultlines as a single aggregate force. It shows that gender faultline strength is negatively associated with loyal behavior, while diversity climate is positively associated with loyal behavior and moderates selected faultline effects. The paper also contributes to diversity climate and organizational citizenship behavior research by showing cross-level links between work-unit climate and individual loyal behavior in a multinational organizational setting.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that organizations seeking loyal behavior from a diverse managerial workforce should focus on building supportive diversity climates rather than only trying to engineer workforce composition. Bias-free HR practices, inclusive training, and senior management attention to diversity values may help make relationship-related faultlines less damaging and task-related differences more useful. The authors caution that trying to solve faultline risks purely through compositional redesign is difficult and risky because it may reduce one faultline while strengthening another.

**Limitations**
The authors note that gender faultline effects may partly reflect the categorical measurement of gender compared with less visibly categorical age, tenure, and functional-background measures. They could not include work-unit tenure, so they cannot assess whether deeper-level faultlines become more salient as work groups spend time together. The study also omits faultlines based on ethnicity, religion, and other attributes, relies on self-reported loyal behavior, and uses Hofstede country controls that cannot capture the full richness of cultural diversity across the 22 countries.

**Future Research**
Future studies should examine how complex arrays of demographic and task attributes shape faultline dynamics and employee behavior over time. The authors call for research on other faultline bases, such as ethnicity and religion, and on how culture, faultlines, and diversity climate jointly affect loyal behavior and other individual or organizational outcomes. They also encourage studies using supervisor or peer ratings of citizenship behavior and broader OCB dimensions beyond loyal behavior.
