# Gender Research in AMJ: AN Overview of Five Decades of Empirical Research and Calls to Action

**Abstract**
Not reported in paper

**Research Question**
The editorial asks what AMJ's five decades of empirical gender research reveal about the field's treatment of gender equality, discrimination, and inclusion, and how management scholars can make future gender research more actionable. It also asks whether progress toward gender equality has plateaued or is entering a renewed phase, and what AMJ can contribute to that conversation.

**Mechanism Process**
- Editorial frame: The article introduces AMJ's thematic issue on gender in management research and positions the journal as a useful site for taking stock of gender research across micro and macro perspectives.
- Retrospective content analysis: The authors review AMJ articles using gender or sex as focal constructs, code theoretical frames, content areas, outcomes, levels of analysis, research settings, and practical implications, and organize trends by decade.
- Theoretical trajectory: They show movement from sex-difference and stereotyping accounts toward broader structural, institutional, relational, and cross-level explanations of gender inequality and inclusion.
- Action agenda: The authors use the retrospective analysis and Catalyst scholar conversations to call for research that studies men's roles, structural barriers, non-Western contexts, barriers beyond organizations, and manager-facing translation.
- Research-practice translation: The editorial argues that gender scholarship needs stronger links to managers, leading-edge organizations, and dissemination channels that convert cumulative knowledge into organizational action.

The process is editorial and agenda-setting rather than a hypothesis-testing causal model. Joshi, Neely, Emrich, Griffiths, and George use the AMJ record to argue that gender research has often identified barriers to women's advancement but has not always converted that knowledge into organizational change. Their core argument is that future research should move beyond individual-level explanations and isolated interventions toward structural, cross-level, globally attentive, and manager-facing scholarship on gender equality and inclusion.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial contributes a field-level synthesis of how gender research in AMJ has changed across five decades. It identifies a decline after the 1980s, a tendency toward theory testing around established gender frames, a shift from barriers and sex differences toward structural and contextual explanations, and an increasing need for more nuanced causal mechanisms. Its main conceptual contribution is to reframe gender research as a grand challenge that requires broader levels of analysis, attention to global and institutional variation, and stronger translation into action.

**Practical Implication**
For scholars, the editorial implies that actionable gender research should study organizational and societal structures rather than placing the burden of change mainly on women. For managers and organizations, it emphasizes accountability, inclusive climates, gender diversity at senior levels, bias-free human resource practices, and translation of research through channels that managers actually use. It also suggests working with leading-edge organizations that are ready to apply evidence-based gender inclusion practices.

**Limitations**
The authors explicitly state that the editorial is not a comprehensive review of gender research. Its retrospective evidence is limited to AMJ articles with gender or sex as focal constructs and to themes visible in that journal corpus. The authors also acknowledge that their vantage point is shaped by Western scholarly traditions and the developed-world context, which limits how fully the editorial can capture barriers faced by girls and women globally.

**Future Research**
The editorial calls for future research that does not treat gender inequality only as a women's issue, including studies of men's roles in work and family domains and men's advocacy for gender inclusion. It also calls for research on structural barriers at the job, work group, organizational, institutional, and societal levels, especially in emerging markets and less developed contexts where overt barriers may remain salient. Finally, it encourages work on how management scholars can map managers' needs, communicate research in accessible language, and convert cumulative gender research into organizational action.
