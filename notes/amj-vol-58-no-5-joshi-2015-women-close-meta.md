# When Can Women Close the Gap? A Meta-Analytic Test of Sex Differences in Performance and Rewards

**Abstract**
Drawing on macro and micro domains in gender research, we meta-analytically test whether occupation-, industry-, and job-level factors mitigate or exacerbate differences in performance evaluations (k 5 93; n 5 95,882) and rewards (k 5 97; n 5 378,850) between men and women. Based on studies conducted across a variety of work settings and spanning nearly 30 years, we found that the sex differences in rewards (d 5 .56) (including salary, bonuses, and promotions) were 14 times larger than sex differences in performance evaluations (d 5 .04), and that differences in performance evaluations did not explain reward differences between men and women. The percentage of men in an occupation and the complexity of jobs performed by employees enhanced the male– female gap in performance and rewards. In highly prestigious occupations, women performed equally, but were rewarded significantly lower than men. Only a higher representation of female executives at the industry level enabled women to reverse the gender gap in rewards and performance evaluations. Our configurational analysis also revealed that some occupation-, industry-, and job-level attributes of the work context jointly contributed to differences in rewards and performance evaluations.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when women can close the gap in workplace performance evaluations and organizational rewards. It tests whether occupation-, industry-, and job-level attributes mitigate or exacerbate sex differences in performance evaluations, salary, bonuses, and promotions.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Proportion of men in an occupational category, occupational prestige, proportion of women at executive or senior managerial levels within an industry, and job complexity.
- DV(s): Sex differences in performance evaluations and organizational rewards.
- Mediators: Performance evaluation differences as a potential explanation for reward differences.
- Moderators: Occupation-, industry-, and job-level work-context attributes; configurations of these attributes in the QCA.

The paper argues that gender inequality is shaped by cross-level work contexts rather than only by individual human capital or overt discrimination. Male-dominated occupations and complex jobs strengthen gendered status cues, ambiguous evaluation criteria, and discretionary reward allocation, while prestigious occupations can operate as hierarchy-enhancing settings in which women perform equally but receive lower rewards. Industries with more women in executive or senior managerial roles provide a contrasting context, because greater representation and scrutiny can reduce or reverse sex differences in both performance evaluations and rewards.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study bridges macro institutional and structural perspectives on gender inequality with micro sociopsychological perspectives on gender bias. Its central contribution is showing that sex differences in rewards are much larger than sex differences in performance evaluations and are not explained by performance gaps, making reward allocation a distinct site of gender inequality. It also adds a configurational account of how occupation, industry, and job attributes jointly produce settings where men or women receive higher evaluations and rewards.

**Practical Implication**
The paper recommends that diversity management focus on accountability structures in performance management and compensation, job designs that reduce inequitable discretion in complex work, and industry-wide mentoring networks for women. It argues that scrutiny by neutral observers or advisors can help move calibration and reward-allocation discussions away from stereotypes and toward performance-relevant information.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the meta-regression analyses used smaller samples than the overall meta-analysis because some primary studies lacked information needed to code external context variables. They could not test a full model with all key and control variables simultaneously, incorporate consistent organization-level variables such as culture or HR policies, include fine-grained human-capital controls, or isolate selection effects into jobs and occupations. They also acknowledge possible file-drawer bias for null or politically uncomfortable gender findings.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on how high-involvement performance management and compensation practices affect gender inequality in male-dominated versus gender-integrated settings. It also proposes work on whether promotion practices that avoid zero-sum choices reduce hierarchy-enhancing reward allocation, how job complexity shapes social comparison and scrutiny, and how organizational attributes operate within broader occupational or industry contexts. Future meta-analyses could compare selection-biased and selection-corrected estimates and extend the argument to contextual performance, creativity, turnover, and specific reward types.
