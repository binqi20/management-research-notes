# Leadership Over-Emergence in Self-Managing Teams: The Role of Gender and Countervailing Biases

**Abstract**
We examine leadership over-emergence, defined as instances when the level of one’s leadership emergence is higher than the level of one’s leadership effectiveness, in a sample of intact self-managing teams who worked together for a period of seven months. We draw from Gender Role Theory and Expectancy Violation Theory to examine the role of gender in predicting leadership over-emergence. Building on arguments from Gender Role Theory, we find that all else equal, men over-emerge as leaders. However, Expectancy Violation Theory suggests that women are likely to benefit from a countervailing bias. Specifically, women are attributed with higher levels of leadership emergence than men when they engage in agentic leadership behaviors—even if the level of the behaviors exhibited by men is exactly the same. Because the impact of these behaviors on leadership effectiveness is not contingent on gender, however, women who engage in more task behaviors and boundary spanning behaviors in self-managing teams also over-emerge as leaders. We discuss the implications of this study for Gender Role Theory and Expectancy Violation Theory, along with practical implications for managing the problem of leadership over-emergence in work groups.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when leadership emergence exceeds leadership effectiveness in self-managing teams and whether gender helps explain that mismatch. It specifically examines whether men over-emerge because gender stereotypes favor men as leaders, and whether women over-emerge when they perform agentic task and boundary-spanning behaviors that violate gendered expectations.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Gender; task-related behaviors; boundary spanning behaviors; social behaviors.
- DV(s): Leadership over-emergence, operationalized as the relative effect size difference of predictors on leadership emergence versus leadership effectiveness.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators; the theory explains over-emergence through perceptual bias, gender role expectations, expectancy violations, and recategorization.
- Moderators: Gender moderates the relationship between task-related and boundary spanning behaviors and leadership emergence versus effectiveness; the hypothesized gender moderation for social behaviors was not supported.

The mechanism is a perceptual-bias process in which leadership emergence and leadership effectiveness are related but distinct judgments. Gender Role Theory predicts that men receive more emergence credit than effectiveness credit because male gender roles are more congruent with leader roles. Expectancy Violation Theory predicts a countervailing bias: women who engage in agentic task and boundary spanning behaviors receive more emergence credit than equivalent men, while those behaviors do not produce a corresponding gender-contingent difference in effectiveness. Social behaviors increased effectiveness but did not produce the predicted male over-emergence effect.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces leadership over-emergence as a distinct problem in self-managing teams: the person seen as most leaderlike may not be the person judged most effective. It contributes to Gender Role Theory by showing that men over-emerge under baseline conditions, while also showing that agentic women can receive a countervailing emergence advantage. It contributes to Expectancy Violation Theory by supporting the counter-stereotypic benefit for agentic women but not the predicted gender-neutral benefit for men who perform communal social behaviors.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that organizations relying on self-managing teams should not assume informal leadership emergence identifies the most effective leader. Teams may need 360-degree feedback, after-action reviews, or rotational leadership schemes to surface discrepancies between emergence and effectiveness. For individuals, especially women seeking to emerge as leaders in small groups, task and boundary-spanning behaviors may increase emergence, although the authors emphasize that this is not a structural solution to gender stereotypes.

**Limitations**
Leadership emergence was measured through subjective perceptions, which may be more vulnerable to bias than objective indicators such as coded video behavior. Gender was also confounded with minority status because the MBA sample contained more men than women. The study did not measure likeability or other indicators of social backlash, and it did not examine other possible predictors such as cognitive ability or prior leadership experience.

**Future Research**
Future research should develop more objective and direct measures of leadership over-emergence and examine whether agentic and social behaviors matter differently across stages of group development. The authors also call for research on leadership under-emergence, male- versus female-dominated group composition, and whether other-advocating versus self-oriented agentic behavior produces different over-emergence patterns. Additional work should test whether agentic women who over-emerge are liked less than men who perform the same behaviors.
