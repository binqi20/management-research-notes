# Folding Under Pressure or Rising to the Occasion? Perceived Time Pressure and the Moderating Role of Team Temporal Leadership

**Abstract**
“Team temporal leadership” orients teams toward managing the time-related aspects of their work. We examine how perceived time pressure affects team processes and subsequent performance under weak versus strong team temporal leadership. The results of our field study of 111 project teams show that the mediated relationship between perceived time pressure and team performance is non-linear. Moreover, this non-linear mediated relationship is moderated by team temporal leadership such that, under strong team temporal leadership, the indirect effect of perceived time pressure on team performance is mostly positive, while, under conditions of weak team temporal leadership, the indirect effect is positive at low levels of perceived time pressure and negative at intermediate to high levels. Implications for current and future time pressure research are also discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when perceived time pressure helps rather than harms project team performance. It specifically examines whether team processes mediate the nonlinear relationship between perceived time pressure and team performance, and whether team temporal leadership changes that mediated relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived time pressure, modeled with linear and squared terms.
- DV(s): Team performance, assessed by whether the project output met client expectations, was high quality, and was delivered on time and within budget.
- Mediators: Team processes, captured through transition, action, and interpersonal processes.
- Moderators: Team temporal leadership.

The mechanism is that time pressure changes team members' engagement in interdependent task-management processes. At low to intermediate levels, time pressure can activate teams to plan, coordinate, monitor progress, and manage interpersonal demands; at higher levels, especially under weak temporal leadership, teams may abandon those processes and focus narrowly on task completion. Strong team temporal leadership shifts this pattern by directing attention to deadlines, sequencing, milestones, and temporal resources, so the indirect effect of time pressure through team processes is positive rather than harmful.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances time-pressure theory by showing that team-level perceived time pressure has nonlinear effects rather than a uniformly positive or negative relationship with performance. It integrates time-pressure research with temporal leadership theory by identifying team temporal leadership as a condition that shapes whether time pressure is translated into effective team processes. It also broadens team-process theory by treating transition, action, and interpersonal processes as the pathway through which temporal context affects project performance.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should direct teams' attention to temporal constraints early enough for teams to respond with planning, coordination, and prioritization rather than panic. The findings suggest that temporal leadership behaviors such as setting interim milestones, synchronizing tasks, pacing work, and allocating time for contingencies can help teams continue using effective processes under deadline pressure. Organizations should also build teams' temporal-management capability before deadlines become acute.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on perceived rather than actual time pressure, so the findings may not transfer directly to settings where objective time constraints are experimentally manipulated. The field design also leaves some possibility of reverse causality, including the possibility that temporal leadership changes perceived time pressure rather than only shaping responses to it. The authors note that the software development context may explain why strong temporal leadership allowed teams to benefit even under high time pressure.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether actual time pressure has similar effects on team processes as a function of team temporal leadership.
