# Strategic Responses to FDI in Emerging Markets: Are Core Members More Responsive than Peripheral Members of Business Groups?

**Abstract**
With increasing foreign direct investment (FDI) into emerging markets, local firms must make critical strategic decisions in order to remain competitive. When faced with multinational enterprises (MNEs) announcing FDI into their industry, local firms can expand operations and challenge the MNE head on, or refrain from responding directly, effectively ceding market share to the MNE investor. We propose that local firms’ responses are shaped by their affiliation with, and position in, a business group. Using data on investment announcements by MNEs and local firms in India from 1995 to 2010, we find that firms that are affiliated with business groups tend to be more sensitive to MNE investment announcements than are stand-alone firms. More professionally managed group affiliate firms are more likely to respond to MNE threats. Furthermore, firms that are in the group’s identity domain—that is, those holding more prominent positions within their group (especially as measured by centrality in the group’s directorship network)—appear more likely to respond to MNE investment announcements. Overall, our results highlight the importance of business group affiliation in examining FDI spillovers in emerging markets.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether local emerging-market firms respond to MNE investment announcements by announcing expansions of their own, and whether that response differs between stand-alone firms and business group affiliates. It further asks whether a business group's professionalization, directorship-network density, and a member firm's prominence within the group shape its responsiveness to FDI threats.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): MNE investment announcements in a firm's industry-year; business group affiliation; group professionalization; group density; promoter ownership; control-cash flow rights wedge; group affiliate binary centrality.
- DV(s): Local firm expansion announcements, measured as whether the local firm announced one or more projects in the same industry-year; robustness models also examine the number of local investment projects.
- Mediators: Awareness, motivation, and capability are the theorized competitive-dynamics processes, but no mediator is directly estimated.
- Moderators: Business group affiliation, group professionalization, group density, promoter ownership, control-cash flow rights wedge, and group affiliate centrality moderate the relationship between MNE investment announcements and local expansion.

The mechanism is a competitive-response process in which MNE investment creates a strategic threat, and local firms differ in their awareness of the threat, motivation to respond, and capability to expand. Business group affiliation can supply resources and coordination, but group heterogeneity matters: professionally managed groups and central member firms are more likely to support expansion responses, while dense interlocks and larger control-cash flow wedges can dampen response. The empirical results support stronger responsiveness among group affiliates, professionally managed groups, and central group members, but do not support promoter ownership and run counter to the predicted positive role of group density.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to FDI spillover research by shifting attention from longer-run productivity outcomes to local firms' immediate strategic responses to MNE investment announcements. It links spillover research with competitive dynamics and business group theory, showing that the governance and relational structure of local firms helps explain heterogeneous responses to FDI in emerging markets. It also extends the identity-domain concept to business groups by arguing and showing that core group members, especially those central in directorship networks, are more responsive than peripheral members.

**Practical Implication**
For managers of emerging-market firms, the findings imply that response to foreign entry depends not only on firm-level resources but also on the firm's position in a wider business group. Business group leaders should recognize that professional management and the allocation of attention and resources to core member firms can shape whether affiliates respond assertively to MNE threats. Policy makers seeking to understand FDI effects should expect heterogeneous local responses rather than assuming a uniform productivity or crowding-out effect.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the data do not allow them to identify entry deterrence scenarios in which aggressive pre-positioning by incumbent firms deters MNE investment altogether. Their dependent variable captures announced expansion projects, so it may miss other strategic responses, although robustness tests using R&D and marketing expenses did not show evidence of those alternatives. The setting is Indian public limited companies during 1995-2010, so the results may not generalize to private firms, other emerging economies, or later institutional periods without further evidence.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on whether increasingly capable emerging-market firms respond to MNE entry at home by making strategic moves in other markets where the MNE operates. It also suggests examining how relational embeddedness within business groups shapes such global strategic interaction. Future work could further investigate entry deterrence and compare how different emerging-market governance structures condition local firm responses to FDI.
