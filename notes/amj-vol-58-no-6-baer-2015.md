# Uneasy Lies the Head that Bears the Trust: The Effects of Feeling Trusted on Emotional Exhaustion

**Abstract**
The construct of feeling trusted reflects the perception that another party is willing to accept vulnerability to one’s actions. Although this construct has received far less attention than trusting, the consensus is that believing their supervisors trust them has benefits for employees’ job performance. Our study challenges that consensus by arguing that feeling trusted can be exhausting for employees. Drawing on Stevan Hobfoll’s conservation of resources theory, we develop a model in which feeling trusted fills an employee with pride—a benefit for exhaustion and performance—while also increasing perceived workload and concerns about reputation maintenance—burdens for exhaustion and performance. We test our model in a field study using a sample of public transit bus drivers in London, England. Our results suggest that feeling trusted is a double-edged sword for job performance, bringing with it both benefits and burdens. Given that recommendations for managers generally encourage placing trust in employees, these results have important practical implications.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether feeling trusted by a supervisor is uniformly beneficial or whether it can also deplete employees. It specifically examines how feeling trusted affects emotional exhaustion and job performance through pride, perceived workload, and reputation maintenance concerns.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Feeling trusted by a supervisor.
- DV(s): Emotional exhaustion and supervisor-rated job performance.
- Mediators: Pride, perceived workload, reputation maintenance concerns, and emotional exhaustion in the serial paths to job performance.
- Moderators: Pride moderates the indirect effects through perceived workload and reputation maintenance concerns; the reputation maintenance moderation was supported, while the perceived workload moderation was not.

The mechanism is a conservation-of-resources process in which feeling trusted creates both resource gains and resource drains. Feeling trusted increases pride, which reduces emotional exhaustion and indirectly supports job performance. It also increases perceived workload and reputation maintenance concerns, which heighten emotional exhaustion and indirectly reduce job performance. Pride further amplifies the exhausting effect of reputation maintenance concerns because a valued positive image becomes more threatening to lose.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study challenges the prevailing assumption that feeling trusted is simply beneficial for employees and shows that it can operate as a double-edged sword. It extends trust research by using conservation of resources theory to distinguish resource gains, resource losses, and resource threats in the experience of being trusted. It also contributes to conservation of resources theory by simultaneously operationalizing gains, losses, and threats and showing that pride can intensify, not only buffer, stress processes.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that managers should recognize that trusted employees may still become emotionally exhausted, especially when trust brings more responsibilities or pressure to protect a positive image. Managers can accentuate the positive meaning of trust, redistribute or remove other tasks when trusted employees receive extra responsibilities, and reassure employees that their reputation does not rise or fall with every stretch assignment. Social support is also presented as a way to reduce emotional exhaustion.

**Limitations**
The design used two sources and three waves, but pride, perceived workload, reputation maintenance concerns, and emotional exhaustion were measured in the same wave, so those links may still contain common-method inflation. The data established temporal precedence but did not measure all variables at all time periods, limiting causal authority compared with a panel design. The sample was predominantly male and had a final complete-data sample of around 200 participants, so larger replications would strengthen generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine additional mediators linking feeling trusted to performance, including psychological empowerment, idiosyncrasy credits, and different forms of managerial risk-taking behavior such as delegating tasks, promoting ideas, or requesting favors. The authors also call for research on outcomes beyond traditional job performance, including creativity and adaptive performance. Further work should test whether the effects of feeling trusted vary by job control, work-unit dispersion of trust, and individual differences such as conscientiousness and prosocial motives.
