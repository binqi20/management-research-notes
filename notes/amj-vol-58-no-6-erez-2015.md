# Inherently Relational: Interactions between Peers’ and Individuals’ Personalities Impact Reward Giving and Appraisal of Individual Performance

**Abstract**
Introverted individuals may experience and evaluate their dyadic work relationships differently than extraverts. In two studies, we investigated the interaction effect of an individual’s and observing peer’s personality traits on performance evaluations and reward giving. Results from Study 1 showed that introverted (but not extraverted) peers consistently evaluated extraverted and disagreeable (but not introverted and agreeable) individuals’ performance as lower. Study 2 replicated these findings with regard to performance evaluation and reward giving using an experimental design that manipulated actor personality and held objective performance constant. The results also showed that introverts’ trait sensitivity and negative person impressions mediated these relationships. Overall, our results support an information utilization model of interpersonal dyadic evaluation, wherein introverts are more sensitive to interpersonal personality traits than are their extraverted counterparts, incorporating interpersonal traits in person impressions and subsequent evaluations and reward distributions. Our paper concludes with implications for dyadic workplace interactions, personality, and sources of emergent dyadic influences on performance evaluation.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether an observing peer's personality changes how that peer evaluates and rewards coworkers with different interpersonal traits. Specifically, it tests whether introverted peers are more likely than extraverted peers to penalize extraverted and disagreeable actors in performance evaluations, reward allocations, and promotion-related judgments.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Actor interpersonal traits, especially extraversion and agreeableness/disagreeableness; observing peer extraversion/introversion.
- DV(s): Peer performance evaluations, peer reward decisions, and promotion recommendations.
- Mediators: Trait sensitivity and negative person impressions in Study 2.
- Moderators: Observing peer extraversion/introversion moderates the effects of actor extraversion and actor agreeableness/disagreeableness.

The proposed mechanism is an information-utilization process in dyadic evaluation. Drawing on the Realistic Accuracy Model of personality judgment, the authors argue that introverted peers are more sensitive to interpersonal trait cues and more likely to use those cues when forming general person impressions. Those impressions then shape ratings and rewards: introverted peers evaluate and reward extraverted or disagreeable actors more negatively, even when the experiment holds objective task performance constant. The results support this pattern in both the field dyadic team study and the virtual-team experiment, with trait sensitivity and person impressions mediating parts of the Study 2 effects.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to dyadic workplace-interaction theory by showing that peer evaluations are not only properties of actors or raters but can emerge from actor-by-peer personality combinations. It extends personality research by applying introversion-linked sensitivity to interpersonal traits in work settings. It also contributes to performance appraisal theory by identifying a dyadic source of rating variance that may be hidden when evaluations are modeled only as rater bias or ratee performance.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations using peer evaluations, peer bonuses, or 360-degree feedback should monitor whether actor-peer personality combinations systematically shape ratings and rewards. The findings suggest that highly extraverted or disagreeable employees may need feedback about how their trait-relevant behaviors affect introverted peers' experiences of dyadic work encounters. Managers can also design team interactions and workspaces to reduce overwhelming interpersonal exposure for introverted employees.

**Limitations**
The authors note that hidden psychological processes are difficult to observe directly, and Study 2 holds task output constant but cannot fully resolve whether introverted peers are biased or are evaluating broader interpersonal performance more holistically. The studies focus on dyads, so full-team processes could buffer or alter the effects. The experiment also uses salient personality manipulations, limited interaction time, and an introversion manipulation that may partly signal shyness or social anxiety.

**Future Research**
Future work should test whether introverted peers make more accurate judgments of interpersonal performance or whether accurate personality judgments spill into biased performance ratings. Research could examine larger team contexts, three-way combinations of actor extraversion and agreeableness with peer introversion, and longer interactions where trait-consistent behaviors emerge without explicit personality descriptions. Additional work could also identify threshold levels at which introversion or extraversion begins to change dyadic evaluation patterns.
