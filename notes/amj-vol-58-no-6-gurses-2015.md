# Entrepreneurship in Regulated Markets: Framing Contests and Collective Action to Introduce Pay TV in the U.S.

**Abstract**
In their endeavor to establish new products and services, entrepreneurs can face strong resistance from market incumbents whose resources and market position they threaten. This paper looks at the battles between entrepreneurs and market incumbents in a regulated market where various institutional actors (e.g., regulators, courts) have the power to protect the incumbents by hindering the entrepreneurs. Our comparison of one failed and one successful attempt to introduce pay TV in the U.S. reveals how entrepreneurs can first enter a regulated market without facing resistance, and then introduce a new frame to legitimize their product or service despite growing resistance from incumbents. Our framework highlights framing as a strategy and framing contests as a mechanism through which entrepreneurs and incumbents can battle to enable or disable institutional change. As part of this process, we also uncover how entrepreneurs evolve from self-serving actors with no field-level intentions to powerful groups that create a ripple effect in their environment by moving their target of influence from private to institutional actors. Our work constitutes a step toward a more “realistic” tale of institutional change.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how entrepreneurs and incumbents advance or protect their interests and shape a regulated market in their favor. It examines why over-the-air pay TV failed while pay cable TV succeeded, despite both facing incumbent resistance and regulatory scrutiny in the U.S. broadcasting market.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key actors / contestants: Pay TV entrepreneurs, broadcasting and theater incumbents, the FCC, courts, legislators, executive-branch actors, public-interest groups, schools, churches, and consumers.
- Process / phases: Initial entry through alignment with incumbents and the regulator's public-interest frame; incumbent resistance that frames the new service against public interest; entrant creation and diffusion of an alternative public-interest frame; collective action that moves from private groups to public and institutional actors; institutional pressure on the regulator and eventual frame consensus.
- Practices: Frame alignment, new frame creation, frame multiplicity, frame consensus, industry association activity, public campaigns, coalition building, lobbying, and indirect pressure on regulators.
- Emergent outcomes: OTA pay TV remained constrained and eventually disappeared, whereas pay cable TV built support, benefited from deregulation, and became institutionalized.

The process centers on framing contests over what counts as the public interest in a regulated market. Incumbents can protect their position by persuading regulators that an entrant's technology threatens public welfare. Entrepreneurs are more likely to succeed when they avoid direct dependence on the regulator early, mobilize groups that benefit from the new technology, diffuse a broader public-interest frame, and use collective action to create pressure across public and institutional arenas.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to entrepreneurship and institutional entrepreneurship research by showing how entrepreneurs in regulated markets can shape rather than simply accept their institutional environment. It develops framing as both a market-entry strategy and a mechanism through which entrants and incumbents battle over institutional change. It also extends work on framing contests by tracing how frames coevolve over time among entrepreneurs, incumbents, regulators, courts, legislators, and public groups.

**Practical Implication**
Entrepreneurs entering regulated markets should not rely only on direct petitions to regulators, especially when incumbents have strong institutional ties. The study suggests that entrants can build legitimacy by aligning initially with dominant public-interest frames, then mobilizing beneficiaries and coalition partners who can help diffuse a new frame. Incumbents and regulators should also recognize that public-interest claims may be strategic attempts to preserve or contest market positions.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that the broader political context of deregulation in the 1970s and 1980s may have contributed to the success of pay cable TV, even though it does not fully explain the case. The theory is built from two historical cases in one U.S. regulated industry, so its transferability is strongest for settings where regulation is tied to public-interest interpretations. The archival design reconstructs long-term processes from historical documents and retrospective interviews rather than observing actors in real time.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether similar public-interest framing contests occur in other regulated markets such as automotive, biotechnology, food, healthcare, telecommunications, or VOIP. Additional work could also study how incumbents and entrants learn from prior framing contests and adapt their strategies across technologies and regulatory regimes.
