# You Don’t Forget Your Roots: The Influence of CEO Social Class Background on Strategic Risk Taking

**Abstract**
Social class is increasingly recognized as a powerful force in people’s lives. Yet despite the long and extensive stream of research on the upper echelons of organizations, we know little about how executives’ formative childhood experiences with social class influence their strategic choices. In this study, we investigate the influence of chief executive officers’ (CEOs’) perceived social class origins on firm risk taking. We also explore the moderating influences of other important career experiences, in the form of elite education and diverse functional backgrounds. Our theory and findings highlight that an executive’s social class origins have a lasting and varying impact on his or her preferences, affecting his or her tendency to take risks. By examining this novel managerial characteristic, we offer important implications for theorizing about social class and upper echelons.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether CEOs' perceived childhood social class origins shape firm-level strategic risk taking after they have reached the corporate elite. It further asks whether later career experiences, specifically elite education and general management functional background, strengthen or weaken the influence of those social class imprints.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO perceived social class origins, modeled as upper social class origins and lower social class origins relative to middle social class origins.
- DV(s): Strategic risk taking, measured as a composite of R&D expenditures, capital expenditures, and long-term debt at a one-year lag.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators.
- Moderators: Elite education and general management functional background.

The proposed mechanism is an imprinting process linking childhood social class experiences to later executive risk preferences. Upper-class origins provide safety nets, perceived control, and opportunity orientation, while lower-class origins can make middle-class status maintenance less central and increase willingness to accept downside risk; both are theorized to produce more strategic risk taking than middle-class origins. Elite education reduces risk taking among lower-class-origin CEOs in the main endogeneity-corrected models, while general management functional background amplifies the risk-taking tendency for CEOs from both upper and lower social class origins by broadening networks, experience, and perceived strategic options.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to upper echelons theory by directly testing CEO social class background as an early-life managerial characteristic that predicts firm strategy, rather than treating elite education as a proxy for class origin. It integrates imprinting theory with social class theorizing to argue that childhood access to resources and societal rank can persist into executive decision making. It also contributes to research on executive risk taking by showing that social class origins and later career experiences jointly condition firm-level strategic risk preferences.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that executives' formative social class experiences may shape their comfort with risky resource commitments. Elite education and broad general management careers do not have uniform effects across executives; their implications depend partly on the CEO's class-origin imprint. The paper cautions against treating visible credentials alone as capturing the formative backgrounds shaping strategic behavior.

**Limitations**
The authors identify the North American setting as a limitation and note that social class structures may operate differently in other institutional contexts. They also focus on CEOs because of data availability, leaving open how the social class backgrounds of other TMT members or the diversity of social class backgrounds within the TMT may affect strategy. Finally, although the study uses direct measures of perceived social class background and endogeneity-corrected models, it measures strategic risk taking from firm-level archival outcomes rather than observing executive decision processes directly.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other firm-level outcomes shaped by CEO social class origins, including employment programs, cooperative strategies, strategic alliances, and responses to compensation incentives. The authors call for work on how upward mobility affects executives' identity, organizational connection, and interactions with coworkers, leaders, and subordinates. They also encourage more proximal studies of executive decision making through scenario analysis, computer simulation, policy capturing, or samples of smaller firms and executive MBA populations.
