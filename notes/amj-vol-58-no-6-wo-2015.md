# What Drives Trickle-Down Effects? A Test of Multiple Mediation Processes

**Abstract**
Organizational research has established the existence of trickle-down effects, wherein the perceptions, attitudes, or behavior of one person in an organization affects those of another person at a lower level. Although current research has explained the phenomenon using several different theoretical perspectives, prior studies have not explicitly tested the theorized mechanisms. This paper develops and tests a model that incorporates three theories of trickle-down effects for interactional justice perceptions: social exchange theory, social learning theory, and displaced aggression. Using crosssectional data from 200 supervisor–subordinate dyads in Study 1 and longitudinal data from 270 supervisor–subordinate dyads in Study 2, we test this multiple mediator model. The results demonstrate that the two aspects of interactional justice perceptions— interpersonal and informational justice perceptions—trickle down from supervisors to subordinates through two different mechanisms. Specifically, displaced aggression accounts for the trickle-down effects of interpersonal justice perceptions and social exchange accounts for the trickle-down effects of informational justice perceptions. We discuss the implications of the findings for research on trickle-down effects and organizational justice.

**Research Question**
The paper asks which theoretical mechanisms explain trickle-down effects in interactional justice perceptions from managers to supervisors to subordinates. It specifically examines whether interpersonal and informational justice perceptions trickle down through the same or different processes: social exchange, social learning, and displaced aggression.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Supervisors' perceptions of their own interpersonal justice experiences and informational justice experiences from their managers.
- DV(s): Subordinates' perceptions of interpersonal justice and informational justice from their supervisors.
- Mediators: Study 1 tests perceived organizational support, role model influence, and anger; Study 2 tests felt obligation toward the organization, interactional efficacy, and anger/irritation.
- Moderators: No moderator is central to the hypothesized mediation model.

The mechanism is a cross-level trickle-down process in which supervisors' treatment by managers shapes how supervisors treat subordinates. The two studies show different processes for the two justice facets: interpersonal justice trickles down through anger or anger/irritation, consistent with displaced aggression, while informational justice trickles down through perceived organizational support or felt obligation, consistent with social exchange. The studies do not find support for social learning as the mediating process for either justice facet.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to trickle-down and organizational justice research by directly testing, rather than merely invoking, three major theoretical explanations for trickle-down effects in a single multiple-mediation framework. It shows that the mechanism depends on the justice facet: interpersonal justice follows a more affective displaced-aggression route, whereas informational justice follows a more cognitive social-exchange route. It also challenges the common use of social learning theory as a default explanation for trickle-down effects by finding no support for role model influence or interactional efficacy as mediators.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that supervisors are not only transmitters of higher-level treatment but may also serve as buffers who can stop negative treatment from cascading downward. Because interpersonal justice trickles down through anger and irritation, organizations may reduce harmful spillovers by helping supervisors manage emotional responses to unfair treatment. The separate informational-justice pathway through felt obligation also shows that different forms of justice may cascade through different mechanisms.

**Limitations**
The authors note that they test three major trickle-down theories but do not exhaust all possible justice theories, such as relational, deontic, or needs-based accounts. They also caution that the evidence speaks directly only to interpersonal and informational justice, not every kind of trickle-down phenomenon. Additional limitations include potential unfairness in how the Study 2 mediators were operationalized, relatively fair samples that may understate displaced aggression, and the possibility that other operationalizations of the mechanisms could yield additional insight.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on additional mediating mechanisms from other justice theories, including self-identity, moral identity, and needs for autonomy or control. It also suggests examining whether the same affective and cognitive mechanisms generalize to other kinds of trickle-down effects beyond interpersonal and informational justice. Future studies should use more parallel mediator operationalizations and samples that include both fair and unfair environments.
