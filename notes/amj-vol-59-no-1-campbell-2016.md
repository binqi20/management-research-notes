# Fuzzy Logic and the Market: A Configurational Approach to Investor Perceptions of Acquisition Announcements

**Abstract**
Prior research on mergers and acquisitions (M&As) has substantially advanced our understanding of how isolated acquirerand deal-specific factors affect abnormal stock returns. However, investors are likely to perceive and evaluate M&As holistically—that is, as complex configurations (i.e., gestalts) of characteristics, rather than as a list of independent factors. And, yet, extant M&A literature has not addressed why and how configurations of factors elicit positive or negative reactions. Overlooking the interdependent nature of factors known to influence acquisition success has limited our understanding of both M&As and investor judgment. Taking an inductive approach to addressing this important issue, the present study relies on fuzzy set methodology. Our results provide compelling evidence that investor perceptions of M&A announcements are not only configurational in nature but also characterized by equifinality (or the presence of multiple paths to success) and asymmetric causality (i.e., configurations that represent bad deals are not simply a mirror image of good deals, but differ fundamentally). By constructing a typology of “good” and “bad” deals as perceived by market participants, we develop a mid-range theory of M&A stock market performance. As such, this study offers novel theoretical and empirical insights to scholars as well as implications for practitioners.

**Research Question**
The paper asks which configurations of acquirer- and deal-specific factors elicit positive investor reactions to M&A announcements and which configurations fail to elicit positive reactions. It examines investor judgment as holistic, configurational sensemaking rather than as the additive evaluation of independent deal attributes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Configurations of nine calibrated conditions: acquirer performance, acquirer leverage, acquirer experience, industry relatedness, acquisition premium, cash payment percentage, friendliness, geographic proximity, and acquirer-target size similarity.
- DV(s): Investor reaction to acquisition announcements, operationalized as short-window cumulative abnormal returns and analyzed as positive market reaction versus absence of positive reaction.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is configurational: investors facing bounded rationality, information asymmetry, and high-velocity decision contexts use salient signals about opportunity, motivation, and ability to form holistic perceptions of an announced acquisition. Fuzzy set analysis shows equifinality, with multiple configurations producing positive reactions, and asymmetric causality, with poorly received deals differing qualitatively from well-received deals rather than simply reversing the same factors.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a mid-range configurational theory of M&A stock market performance by treating acquisition announcements as complex signals evaluated as gestalts. It contributes to M&A research by showing that individual deal factors such as relatedness, financing, premium, leverage, and experience have contingent meanings within configurations, and it contributes to behavioral strategy by theorizing investors as boundedly rational market participants rather than purely reductionist evaluators.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that managers should consider how acquisition attributes will be read together before announcing a deal. Cash financing is especially consequential because investors reacted positively to configurations with cash financing and negatively to configurations combining high premiums with no cash financing, particularly when other factors suggested integration difficulty, hubris, turnaround risk, or overextension.

**Limitations**
The study explains only part of the variation in positive and negative M&A announcement reactions, and the authors note that the overall solution coverage is limited. The design uses short-term cumulative abnormal returns, so it captures investors' perceived value of strategy formulation rather than long-term acquisition implementation performance. The sample is limited to acquisitions by North American acquirers in developed financial and regulatory settings.

**Future Research**
Future research should refine the configurational framework by identifying additional deal prototypes, examining finer time periods, and testing boundary conditions such as changing credit-market environments. The authors also call for work on whether these configurations generalize to emerging-market contexts, whether deal communication wording and tone shape investor reactions, and how stock-financed deals can elicit positive reactions when cash financing is absent.
