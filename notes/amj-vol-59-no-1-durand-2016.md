# Classical Deviation: Organizational and Individual Status as Antecedents of Conformity

**Abstract**
Beside making organizations look like their peers through the adoption of similar attributes ("alignment"), this paper highlights the fact that conformity also enables organizations to stand out by exhibiting highly salient attributes key to their field or industry ("conventionality"). Building on the conformity and status literatures, and using the case of major U.S. symphony orchestras and the changes in their concert programming between 1879 and 1969, we hypothesize and find that middle-status organizations are more aligned and middle-status individual leaders make more conventional choices than their lowand high-status peers. In addition, the extent to which middle-status leaders adopt conventional programming is shown to be moderated by the status of the organization and by its level of alignment. Thus, this paper offers a novel theory and operationalization of organizational conformity, and contributes to the literature on status effects, and, more broadly, to the understanding of the key issues of distinctiveness and conformity.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how organizational and individual status shape two distinct forms of organizational conformity: alignment with peer repertoires and conventionality through emphasis on salient canonical attributes. It examines whether middle-status organizations and middle-status individual leaders conform differently because they face different exposure to penalties and rewards.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Orchestra status and musical director status, including linear and quadratic status terms.
- DV(s): Repertoire alignment and repertoire conventionality in orchestra programming.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Orchestra status and orchestra alignment moderate the director-status effect on conventionality.

The mechanism separates penalty avoidance from quality-comparison rewards. Alignment lets organizations blend in with peers and reduce sanctions for misalignment, so middle-status organizations have the strongest incentive to align. Conventionality lets individual leaders stand out on salient, comparable repertoire choices and claim quality recognition, so middle-status musical directors have the strongest incentive to adopt conventional programming; this effect is stronger when orchestra status or low alignment creates more room for reward capture.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper decomposes conformity into alignment and conventionality, showing that organizations can conform by blending in or by standing out on highly salient field attributes. It refines middle-status conformity theory by linking organizational status more strongly to alignment and individual leader status more strongly to conventionality, thereby integrating status effects across organizational and individual levels. It also challenges the assumption that misalignment necessarily equals deviance, because misaligned actors may still be conventional.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations and leaders in status-ordered fields, conformity pressure is not a single strategic constraint. The study shows that organizations may reduce penalties by maintaining broad alignment with peer norms, while individual leaders may seek recognition by concentrating effort on salient, quality-comparable choices. Interpreting conformity therefore requires distinguishing between blending in with peers and emphasizing canonical features that invite quality comparison.

**Limitations**
The study is based on major U.S. symphony orchestras between 1879 and 1969, a setting with strong programming norms, elite cultural audiences, and unusually visible individual artistic leaders. The authors note that their analysis does not explain norm dynamics and considers conformity only with a contemporaneous norm. Generalization should be tested in fields with different audience structures, technological change, or competing institutional logics.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether audience heterogeneity changes the relationship between status, alignment, and conventionality. Other individual or organizational characteristics besides status may also shape responses to negative sanctions and positive rewards. Research on faster-changing normative environments could examine discrepancies between historical norms and contemporary imperatives for conformity.
