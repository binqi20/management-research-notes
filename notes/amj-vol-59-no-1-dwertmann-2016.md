# Status Matters: The Asymmetric Effects of Supervisor–Subordinate Disability Incongruence and Climate for Inclusion

**Abstract**
Growing workforce diversity increases the likelihood that supervisors and subordinates will differ along demographic lines, a situation that has important implications for their relationship quality and individual outcomes. In a sample of 1,253 employees from 54 work units, we investigate the effects of differences in disability status between supervisors and subordinates on leader–member exchange (LMX) quality and subsequent performance ratings, and find that incongruence in general is related to lower LMX quality and lower performance. In addition, we propose and find an asymmetrical effect of disability incongruence, such that LMX quality is worse in dyads in which the supervisor has a disability than in dyads in which the subordinate has a disability. Furthermore, we investigate the moderating role of unit-level climate for inclusion on this relationship and find support for a buffering effect of inclusive climates on the negative incongruence-LMX relationship for scenarios in which the supervisor, but not the subordinate, has a disability. We build relevant theory for the relational demography, disability, LMX, and organizational climate literatures by predicting these effects on the basis of status mechanisms. These findings have important practical implications, as they provide companies with a feasible way to manage their diverse workforce.

**Research Question**
How does supervisor-subordinate disability incongruence affect leader-member exchange quality and subsequent subordinate performance? Does unit-level climate for inclusion buffer the negative LMX effects of disability incongruence, and does this buffering differ depending on whether the supervisor or subordinate has the disability?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Supervisor-subordinate disability congruence scenario: neither has a disability, both have a disability, subordinate has a disability, or supervisor has a disability.
- DV(s): Leader-member exchange quality and subordinate performance ratings.
- Mediators: Leader-member exchange quality mediates the link between disability incongruence and performance.
- Moderators: Unit-level climate for inclusion moderates the disability incongruence-LMX relationship.

The paper argues that disability incongruence weakens LMX through relational demography, similarity-attraction, social identity, stigma, and status mechanisms. Incongruence is expected to be especially damaging when the supervisor has a disability because subordinates may experience stigma-by-association and a status-based discrepancy between the supervisory role and disability stereotypes. Climate for inclusion operates as a unit-level norm that can reduce stereotyping and social categorization, but the paper finds this buffer only when the supervisor, not the subordinate, has a disability.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends relational demography and disability research by showing that disability incongruence matters in both directions and that the supervisor-with-disability scenario is especially consequential for LMX. It contributes to LMX and organizational climate research by demonstrating a cross-level moderation effect: inclusive unit climates can partially buffer negative dyadic effects, but asymmetrically. The study also foregrounds status as a mechanism that shapes when demographic incongruence is especially damaging.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should treat positive LMX relationships across disability-status differences as a central inclusion and performance concern. The findings suggest that inclusive climates, equitable HR practices, disability-supportive leadership, and manager accountability for developing diverse subordinates can reduce the relational costs of disability incongruence. The practical emphasis is strongest for contexts in which supervisors with disabilities may otherwise face stigma-by-association dynamics from subordinates.

**Limitations**
The data come from one German federal agency with strong disability rights norms and unusually high representation of employees with disabilities, which may limit generalizability across countries, sectors, and more competitive organizational cultures. Disability congruence was reported by subordinates, so the measure captures perceived rather than independently verified supervisor disability status. The study also uses subjective performance ratings, although the authors justify them as aligned with the organization's promotion and benefit system.

**Future Research**
Future studies should test whether combining higher hierarchical status with membership in a devalued demographic group consistently produces negative relational outcomes or whether status sometimes buffers them. Research should examine disability type and severity, because physical, intellectual, and mental or psychological disabilities may generate different stereotypes and LMX patterns. Further work should also investigate why climate for inclusion affects supervisors and subordinates asymmetrically and identify antecedents of inclusive climates.
