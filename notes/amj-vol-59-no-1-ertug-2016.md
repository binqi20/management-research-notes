# The Art of Representation: How Audience-specific Reputations Affect Success in the Contemporary Art Field

**Abstract**
We study the effects of actors’ audience-specific reputations on their levels of success with different audiences in the same field. Extending recent work that has emphasized the presence of multiple audiences with different concerns, we demonstrate that considering audience specificity leads to an improved understanding of reputation effects. Using data on emerging artists in the field of contemporary art from 2001 to 2010, we investigate the manner in which artists’ audience-specific reputations affect their subsequent success with two distinct audiences: museums and galleries. Our findings suggest that audience-specific reputations have systematically different effects with respect to success with museums and galleries. Our findings also illuminate the extent to which audience-specific reputations are relevant for emerging research on the contingent effects of reputation. In particular, our findings support our predictions that audiences differ from one another in terms of the extent to which other signals (specifically, status and interaction with other audiences) enhance or reduce the value of audience-specific reputations. Our study thus advances theory by providing empirical evidence for the value of incorporating audience-specific reputations into the general study of reputation.

**Research Question**
How do actors' audience-specific reputations affect their success with different audiences in the same field, and how do status, cross-audience interaction, and audience accountability condition these effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Audience-specific reputation, measured as award winning for artistic quality and magazine-cover appearance for commercial viability.
- DV(s): Artist success with museum audiences and gallery audiences, measured as annual museum or gallery exhibitions.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Prior exhibitions at high-status audience members, prior exhibitions with non-focal audiences, and audience accountability.

The paper argues that reputation is valuable when it reduces uncertainty for a focal audience whose concerns match the attribute signaled by that reputation. Museums are more accountable to diverse stakeholders and care more about artistic quality, so a reputation for artistic quality is more valuable for museum success and is more strongly reinforced by high-status museum affiliations. Interaction with a non-focal audience can signal lack of fit, especially for high-status museums, reducing the value of an artist's audience-specific reputation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances reputation theory by showing that reputation effects are audience-specific rather than uniformly actor-level. It also contributes to research on reputation-status interplay and inter-audience spillovers by identifying audience accountability as a boundary condition that shapes whether other signals enhance or discount the value of reputation.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that people working across multiple client or audience groups should not assume that a positive reputation with one audience will transfer equally to another. They may need to build separate reputations around the attributes valued by each audience and attend to whether the focal audience must justify its decisions to diverse stakeholders.

**Limitations**
The paper reports no statistical support for the differential effect of magazine-cover appearances on gallery versus museum success, which the authors attribute to noise in measuring commercial viability. It also examines only two additional signals, status and interaction with other audiences, and focuses on the contemporary art field, limiting generalizability to other settings.

**Future Research**
Future research could use better measures of commercial viability, such as auction appearances or sales ratios, where systematic data are available. It could also examine additional signals or intangible assets and test the framework in other multi-audience settings, including academic careers, biotechnology scientists, doctor-managers, and contexts with clearer audience hierarchies.
