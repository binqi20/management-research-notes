# Ready, AIM, Acquire: Impression Offsetting and Acquisitions

**Abstract**
Drawing on expectancy violation theory, we explore the effects of anticipatory impression management in the context of acquisitions. We introduce impression offsetting, an anticipatory impression management technique organizational leaders employ when they expect a focal event will negatively violate the expectations of external stakeholders. Accordingly, in these situations, organizational leaders will announce the focal event contemporaneously with positive, but unrelated information. We predict impression offsetting will generally occur in the context of acquisitions, but also more frequently for specific acquiring firms and acquisitions that are more likely to lead to an expectancy violation. We also posit that offsetting will effectively inhibit observers' perceptions of events as negative expectancy violations by positively influencing shareholder reactions to acquisition announcements. Consistent with our hypotheses, in a sample of publicly traded acquisition targets, we find evidence for impression offsetting, in which characteristics of both acquirers and their announced acquisitions predict its frequency of use. We also find evidence that impression offsetting is efficacious; on average, it reduces the negative market reaction to acquisition announcements by over 40%, which translates into approximately $246 million in market capitalization.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether acquiring firms use anticipatory impression management to offset the negative expectancy violations associated with acquisition announcements. It also asks whether impression offsetting is more frequent when acquirer or target characteristics make an acquisition more likely to be viewed negatively, and whether offsetting improves shareholder reactions.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Impression offsetting; prior acquisition activity; high reputation; target unrelatedness; unprofitable target.
- DV(s): Count of positive unrelated press releases around acquisition announcements; cumulative abnormal returns around acquisition announcements.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators.
- Moderators: Not modeled as moderators.

The mechanism is that managers anticipate that acquisitions may violate investors' expectations that firms should enhance shareholder value. They therefore release positive but unrelated information contemporaneously with the acquisition announcement, forcing investors to process multiple pieces of news at once and reducing the perceived negativity of the acquisition event. The authors further argue that riskier acquisitions, serial acquirers, and high-reputation acquirers create stronger predictive expectancy violations, increasing the likelihood of offsetting.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces impression offsetting as a distinct anticipatory impression management tactic, separate from strategic noise and big bath accounting. It extends expectancy violation theory to explain when firms proactively manage stakeholder perceptions before or during potentially negative events. It also contributes by quantifying the market benefits of an impression management tactic in the acquisition context.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that managers can reduce negative market reactions to acquisition announcements by timing positive unrelated disclosures around the focal event. For investors and analysts, the results suggest that press releases near acquisition announcements should be interpreted cautiously because contemporaneous positive news may bias the observed market reaction. Acquisition researchers should also account for confounding positive announcements when estimating market reactions.

**Limitations**
The authors could not directly observe managerial intent, so they infer agency from deviations between event-window press releases and baseline press-release behavior. The study focuses on public-company acquisitions with available press-release and market data, limiting direct generalization to private deals or nonmarket stakeholder reactions. The authors also note that the tactic is inexpensive and effective, but the study does not explain why many firms still do not use it.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine CEO characteristics, reputation, or tenure as predictors of impression offsetting. Researchers could also study positive expectancy violations, the interaction between anticipatory and reactive impression management, and the timing of negative announcements. Additional work could re-examine acquisition market reactions after accounting for systematic positive-news offsets.
