# Magnification and Correction of the Acolyte Effect: Initial Benefits and Ex Post Settling up in NFL Coaching Careers

**Abstract**
What are the long-term consequences of initially beneficial high-reputation workplace ties? Under uncertainty, acolytes (i.e., subordinates with work connections to high-reputation industry leaders) are likely to benefit in terms of signaling fitness for promotion in the external job market. Analysis of promotion outcomes of coaches in the National Football League over 31 years showed that the acolyte effect was reduced for individuals for whom uncertainty was lowest (acolytes with considerable industry experience or high centrality in the co-worker industry network). There was no support for either a knowledge-transfer or an intrinsic-quality explanation for why acolytes initially gained advantage. Rather, evidence supported the idea that ties to high-reputation leaders were somewhat randomly distributed so that acolytes faced ex post settling-up consequences after their promotions: i.e., fewer further promotions or lateral moves, and more demotions. Thus, acolytes initially benefited from loose linkages between their unobservable quality and signals offered by their industry-leader ties, but also suffered as the unreliability of social network signals became evident. The results suggest that a competitive job market may exhibit selfcorrection over time. We offer countervailing theory and evidence to the prevailing view that high-reputation third-party endorsements perpetuate a rich-get-richer social structure that is resistant to performance outcomes.

**Research Question**
What are the long-term career consequences of initially beneficial high-reputation workplace ties? The paper asks which acolytes benefit most from high-reputation leader ties, whether the mechanism is knowledge transfer, intrinsic quality, or unreliable signaling, and whether acolytes continue to outperform after promotion.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Acolyte status, defined as having worked on the same management team as a high-reputation NFL head coach.
- DV(s): Promotion and post-promotion outcome, where post-promotion outcomes distinguish further promotion or lateral movement from demotion.
- Mediators: Not directly tested; the proposed explanatory mechanisms are signaling under uncertainty, perceived knowledge transfer, intrinsic-quality inference, and ex post settling up.
- Moderators: Evaluative certainty, measured through co-worker network centrality, NFL coaching experience, and number of teams worked for; knowledge match between acolyte and high-reputation leader expertise.

The paper argues that acolyte ties operate as social signals when evaluators lack other information about a coach's quality. These ties initially magnify promotion chances, especially for less central and less experienced coaches, but the evidence does not support a knowledge-transfer or intrinsic-quality explanation. Instead, acolyte status appears to be an unreliable signal that later produces settling-up effects as promoted acolytes experience fewer favorable post-promotion outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends social network and signaling theory by theorizing both the initial benefits and later penalties of high-reputation third-party ties. It challenges rich-get-richer accounts of reputational endorsement by showing that, in a highly competitive labor market, misleading social signals can be corrected over time. It also introduces the acolyte as a theoretical construct for explaining how actors and evaluators simplify complex social-network information in career markets.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that organizations should not treat high-reputation leader ties as straightforward evidence of candidate quality. For individuals, ties to prestigious leaders can help open promotion opportunities but may also create inflated expectations and later career risk if the signal is not backed by observable performance.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the NFL is an unusually competitive, interlinked industry with quantitative performance indicators and stochastic season outcomes, which may limit generalizability. They also could not link coaches to specific performance outcomes as directly as sports studies of players can, and the archival design cannot directly observe the cognitive and emotional processes behind evaluator reactions, envy, or halo effects.

**Future Research**
Future research could test acolyte effects in other settings, including academia and entertainment, where high-reputation endorsements may matter but performance is harder to observe. It could also examine alternatives to ex post settling up, such as acolytes resting on their laurels, losing aspiration after proximity to stars ends, or being punished by others' envy. Experimental work could isolate the halo effects and boundary conditions of network endorsements more directly.
