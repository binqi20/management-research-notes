# The Online Shadow of Offline Signals: Which Sellers Get Contacted in Online B2B Marketplaces?

**Abstract**
This article extends the understanding of what impels buyers to contact particular sellers in online business-to-business (B2B) marketplaces, which are typically characterized by sparse social structures and concomitant limitations in observing social cues. Integrating an institutional perspective with signaling theory, our core argument is that offline seller characteristics that are visible online—in particular, geographic location and legal status—convey credible signals of seller behavior because they provide buyers with information on sellers’ local institutional quality and the institutionally induced obligations and controls acting on sellers. Using unique data from a large, Italian, online B2B marketplace between the fourth quarter of 1999 and July 2001, we find that both sellers’ local institutional quality and their legal statuses affect a buyer’s likelihood of contacting a seller. Moreover, consistent with the idea that a buyer’s own local institutional quality generates a relevant reference point against which sellers are evaluated, we find that a buyer is progressively more likely to contact sellers the higher their local institutional quality relative to the buyer. Jointly, our findings imply that, in online B2B marketplaces, signals conveyed by sellers’ geographic locations and legal statuses may constitute substantive sources of competitive heterogeneity and market segmentation.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what impels buyers to contact particular sellers in online B2B marketplaces when social structures are sparse and buyers have limited social cues. It specifically examines whether visible offline seller characteristics, especially geographic location and legal status, shape buyer-seller contact initiation online.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Seller local institutional quality; seller legal status; seller local institutional quality relative to buyer; seller legal status relative to buyer.
- DV(s): Whether a buyer submits a request for quotation to a seller.
- Mediators: Not formally modeled as mediators.
- Moderators: Buyer local institutional quality and buyer legal status as reference points for evaluating seller signals.

The proposed mechanism is that visible offline characteristics signal seller reliability in an anonymous online marketplace. Geographic location signals local institutional quality, especially judicial efficiency and the absence of corruption or organized crime, while legal status signals the strength of institutionally induced obligations and controls. Buyers use these signals to infer trading and value appropriation risks, and the effectiveness of institutional-quality signals depends partly on the buyer's own local institutional quality as a reference point.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates an institutional perspective with signaling theory to show how offline institution-based signals can shape online buyer-seller contact dynamics. It extends signaling research by showing that signal effectiveness can vary across signal recipients because buyers interpret seller institutional quality relative to buyer-specific reference points. It also contributes to research on online market segmentation by showing that sellers can become differentiated before pricing information is disclosed.

**Practical Implication**
For sellers in online B2B marketplaces, offline characteristics that are visible online may affect whether buyers even initiate contact. Sellers located in higher-quality institutional environments or operating under stronger legal-status obligations may enjoy advantages in attracting requests for quotation. Marketplace operators and sellers should recognize that online profile fields such as location and legal form may function as substantive signals, not merely descriptive metadata.

**Limitations**
The authors note that data limitations prevent complete coverage of company size and order types, although supplementary analyses did not suggest seller size confounded the main results. The study focuses on an Italian marketplace from 1999-2001, so formal institutional variation is largely ruled out by design, but this also limits direct evidence about cross-national or state-level formal institutional signals. The relative legal status test is limited by coarse categorical measurement.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how transaction-specific characteristics such as order quantity moderate institution-based signaling effects. Studies could also test formal institutional signals in online B2B markets that cross regional, state, or national borders, and examine other offline seller characteristics such as industry association membership or prominent partner affiliations. The authors also encourage research on how buyers combine multiple signals, including brand, reputation, certification, price, geographic location, and legal status.
