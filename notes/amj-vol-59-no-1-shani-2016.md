# Persona Non Grata? Determinants and Consequences of Social Distancing from Journalists Who Engage in Negative Coverage of Firm Leadership

**Abstract**
We consider how social and psychological connections among CEOs explain the propensity for corporate leaders to distance themselves socially from journalists who engage in negative reporting about firm leadership at other companies, and we examine the consequences for the valence of journalists’ subsequent coverage. Our theoretical framework suggests that journalists who have engaged in negative coverage of a firm’s leadership and strategy are especially likely to experience distancing from other leaders who (a) have friendship ties to the firm’s CEO, (b) are demographically similar to the CEO on salient dimensions, or (c) are socially identified with the CEO as a fellow member of the corporate elite. Our theory and findings ultimately suggest that, due to the multiple sources of social identification between CEOs, journalists who engage in negative coverage of firm leadership tend to experience social distancing from multiple CEOs, and that such distancing has a powerful influence on the valence of journalists’ subsequent reporting about firm leadership and strategy across all the firms they cover. We also extend our theoretical framework to suggest how the effect of social distancing on the valence of journalists’ coverage is moderated by the early and late stages of the journalists’ career.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when CEOs socially distance themselves from journalists who have written negative coverage of other firms' leadership and strategy, and how that distancing affects the valence of journalists' later coverage. It also asks whether journalists' career stage changes their vulnerability to this social influence.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Journalists' prior negative statements about another firm's leadership or strategy, interacted with friendship ties between CEOs, demographic similarity between CEOs, and focal CEOs' social identification with the corporate elite.
- DV(s): CEO social distancing from journalists; subsequent negative and positive statements by journalists about firm leadership and strategy.
- Mediators: Supplemental analyses indicate that CEOs' negative affect and distrust toward the journalist mediate the social-connection effects on distancing.
- Moderators: Journalists' early, middle, and late career stage moderate the relationship between social distancing and subsequent coverage valence.

The mechanism is that CEOs socially identify with friends, demographically similar CEOs, and fellow members of the corporate elite, so negative coverage of those leaders elicits negative affect and distrust toward the journalist. Distancing withholds access, information, and status from journalists, creating negative reinforcement that encourages more positive and less negative subsequent coverage of firm leadership. The effect is strongest for middle-career journalists, who are theorized to be less protected by early-career idealism or late-career return to professional ideals.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends corporate governance and strategic leadership research by theorizing social distancing as a collective form of negative social influence over information intermediaries. It shifts attention from individual CEO influence tactics to elite-level patterns in which multiple CEOs' social and psychological connections jointly shape journalists' behavior toward corporate leaders as a group. It also adds a career-stage account of why information intermediaries vary in their susceptibility to elite social influence.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that journalists' access to CEOs can be socially conditioned by prior critical reporting about other leaders, which may reduce the objectivity and negativity of subsequent coverage. For stakeholders who rely on media as a governance mechanism, social distancing can weaken external pressure for needed changes in leadership, governance, and strategy. The authors suggest that greater awareness and publicity around distancing behavior could deter CEOs from using this form of influence.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on large public U.S. companies and journalists at major U.S. news and business publications, so the findings may not generalize to other national media systems, private firms, or other information-intermediary contexts. The design uses panel surveys and archival content analysis, but the core distancing construct depends on reported willingness and responsiveness in CEO-journalist interactions. The evidence concerns social distancing around coverage of firm leadership and strategy, not all forms of media criticism or stakeholder influence.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether the theory generalizes to other information intermediaries, such as sell-side security analysts, or to auditor-client relationships. Studies could examine whether similar patterns of distancing arise in other elite communities where group members are affected by a third party's behavior. Further work could also investigate how other journalist attributes shape vulnerability to CEO influence and whether distancing affects coverage of less-connected or minority leaders differently.
