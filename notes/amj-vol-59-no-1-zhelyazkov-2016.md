# After the Break-Up: The Relational and Reputational Consequences of Withdrawals from Venture Capital Syndicates

**Abstract**
Organizational theorists are increasingly interested in the antecedents of terminating interorganizational relationships, but have paid little attention to the disruptive consequences of such terminations on future tie formations. To redress this imbalance, the present study focuses on how venture capital (VC) firms' withdrawals from VC syndicates are associated with their subsequent syndication in the period from 1985 to 2008. We argue that withdrawals disrupt the relationships of the withdrawing VC firms with the coinvestors and reduce the likelihood of them entering into subsequent exchange (relational consequences). Furthermore, public information on the withdrawals can undermine the withdrawing VC firm's reputation for reliability, making it a less desirable exchange partner overall (global reputational consequences). Finally, we find that abandoned coinvestors can spread negative private information about the withdrawing firm, reducing its chances of syndication with their other network contacts (local reputational consequences). We also show that the global and local reputational consequences attenuate each other, due to redundancy in the content of the information each provides. We discuss the implications of our theory for the research on network dynamics and reputation.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how a VC firm's withdrawals from prior syndicates shape its likelihood of forming subsequent syndication ties. It distinguishes relational consequences for abandoned coinvestors, global reputational consequences from public withdrawal histories, and local reputational consequences transmitted through abandoned coinvestors' network contacts.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Direct withdrawals against the focal firm; overall withdrawal rate; focal firm's social overlap with the alter's abandoned coinvestors; focal firm's social overlap with the alter's non-abandoned coinvestors.
- DV(s): Indicator for whether two VC firms jointly invested in a first-round syndicate in the focal year.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators.
- Moderators: Social overlap with abandoned coinvestors and non-abandoned coinvestors moderates the effect of overall withdrawal rate.

The mechanism is that withdrawal can make abandoned coinvestors reassess a VC firm's reliability and damage interpersonal and interorganizational relationships, reducing repeat syndication. Public withdrawal histories can signal unreliability to the broader VC market, while abandoned coinvestors can transmit negative private information to their own contacts. Contrary to the authors' amplification hypothesis, global and local negative information attenuate each other, suggesting redundancy in the information available to prospective partners.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to network dynamics research by showing that past ties do not always cast a positive shadow over future tie formation; withdrawals can disrupt repeat ties and relationships with structurally proximate actors. It extends reputation research by distinguishing global public reputation from local private reputational information and by showing that social ties shape audience reactions to the same behavioral record. It also provides evidence for network-based ex post punishment of behavior perceived as violating collaboration norms.

**Practical Implication**
For VC firms, withdrawal decisions can carry future syndication costs beyond the abandoned deal, especially through damaged relationships and negative information flows. The findings imply that prospective syndication partners attend not only to technical resources and network position but also to a partner's reliability record. Firms facing liquidity-driven withdrawals may suffer less reputational damage, suggesting that audiences interpret withdrawals partly through attribution processes.

**Limitations**
The authors note that withdrawal decisions are not randomly assigned and may be driven by unobserved factors that also affect future syndication. Archival data cannot fully distinguish deliberate, pre-negotiated, forced, liquidity-driven, or relationship-breakdown withdrawals. The VC setting also involves small, cohesive firms, so private information transmission may be stronger than in large, divisionalized organizations.

**Future Research**
Future research could use qualitative or survey evidence to examine withdrawal circumstances, attribution processes, and relationship dynamics before and after syndicate exits. Additional work could test whether the theory generalizes to strategic alliances, investment banking syndicates, or institutional contexts where collaborators can be forcibly removed rather than voluntarily withdraw. Research could also examine how internal cohesion within organizations affects their ability to transmit reputational information.
