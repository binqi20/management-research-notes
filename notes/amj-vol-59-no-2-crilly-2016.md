# The Grammar of Decoupling: A Cognitive-Linguistic Perspective on Firms’ Sustainability Claims and Stakeholders’ Interpretation

**Abstract**
Can firms deceive their stakeholders, by failing to deliver on their commitments to undertake sustainability practices without being detected? Extant theory posits that, due to information asymmetry, stakeholders struggle to comprehend the actual change in firms’ practices. In contrast, we advance a cognitive-linguistic perspective to explain why stakeholders are sometimes misled. Accordingly, we propose that firms’ deception does not appear in the content of their communication, but in its linguistic properties, which derive from how managers cognitively construe the sustainability challenge. Thus, firms cover the same points of content in their reports, but firms that practice what they preach use more complex styles of language than do firms that decouple their action from their statements. Moreover, we theorize that generalist stakeholders and stakeholders with conflicts of interest are unable to detect these linguistic nuances, whereas specialist stakeholders can. We find evidence for this cognitive-linguistic perspective in a textual analysis of grammatical structure in 261 interviews conducted in a large field study of 12 multinational corporations and their stakeholders. This lens advances our understanding of how firms deceive and how stakeholders can detect such deception and opens a new and promising avenue for research on firm–stakeholder relations.

**Research Question**
Can stakeholders detect firms that espouse sustainability policies but do not consistently implement them, and which stakeholders are persuaded by these decoupled claims? The paper asks why stakeholders are sometimes misled despite access to firm communications and external metrics.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Implementor firms versus decoupler firms; sustainability claims; grammatical complexity; stakeholder specialism; resource relations and conflicts of interest.
- Firm communication process: Implementors use more exclusive language and past-oriented empirical claims, while decouplers make broader inclusive and future-oriented claims despite communicating similar content.
- Stakeholder interpretation process: Specialist stakeholders use non-negotiable standards and past empirical evidence; generalist and resource-receiving stakeholders rely more on peripheral cues, shared identity, and future-oriented confidence.
- Emergent outcomes: Stakeholders generally rate implementors more highly, but resource-receiving stakeholders evaluate firms positively regardless of implementation, whereas specialists are better able to distinguish decouplers.

The central process is interpretive rather than simply informational. Decoupling is difficult to detect because truthful and less truthful firms communicate similar content, but differ in grammatical structure. Stakeholders' own cognitive and motivational positions shape whether they attend to those linguistic cues and whether they evaluate sustainability performance from observed implementation or from relationship-based confidence.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends decoupling theory by arguing that the problem is not only information asymmetry between firms and stakeholders. It shows that grammatical structure can reveal differences in managers' cognitive construal of sustainability commitments and in stakeholders' interpretive work. It also adds a stakeholder-heterogeneity account, distinguishing perceptive specialist stakeholders from resource-dependent or generalist stakeholders who may miss or discount evidence of decoupling.

**Practical Implication**
Stakeholders should not rely only on the content of sustainability claims or the availability of metrics, because firms with different implementation records may cover similar topics in their reports. Specialist stakeholders and evaluators may be better positioned to assess implementation when they define non-negotiable standards and compare firms using concrete past evidence. Firms and monitors should also recognize that resource relationships can make stakeholder evaluations more favorable without necessarily improving implementation.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a small number of firms, and the authors encourage replication with larger samples and different contexts. The authors do not claim that executives in decoupler firms are deliberately deceptive; their language may reflect confusion about how to achieve sustainability commitments.

**Future Research**
Future research could apply cognitive-linguistic analysis to other corporate communications, such as executives' communications with financial markets or stock repurchase announcements. Additional work could examine how stakeholders interpret sustainability metrics and how rhetoric shapes the presentation and sensemaking of data. The authors also call for research on stakeholder heterogeneity, especially how potent stakeholders and perceptive stakeholders share information and coordinate influence.
