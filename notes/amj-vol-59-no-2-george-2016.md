# Bringing Africa In: Promising Directions for Management Research

**Abstract**
Not reported in paper

**Research Question**
The editorial asks why Africa should be brought into mainstream management research and theories. It aims to show how African contexts can both influence existing theories and generate new theory in management research.

**Mechanism Process**
- Contextual premise: Africa combines fast growth, demographic change, institutional voids, resource constraints, weak infrastructure, informal markets, and sociopolitical heterogeneity.
- Literature overview: The authors review management scholarship on Africa and group the existing literature into navigating institutional voids, developing capabilities, and enabling opportunities.
- Theory-extension logic: African contexts expose boundary conditions in theories developed mainly in resource-abundant Western and Eastern developed economies.
- Theory-generation logic: Underresearched African phenomena can generate new frameworks for leadership and governance, natural-resource management, resilience, entrepreneurship, labor mobility, and managing across cultures and countries.
- Audience and purpose: The editorial invites management scholars to adopt Africa as a research context and to produce evidence useful to business executives and policy makers.

The process is editorial and agenda-setting rather than causal. George and colleagues use Africa's institutional, economic, demographic, cultural, and ecological diversity to argue that management research can learn from contexts where firms must design around voids, build capabilities under resource constraints, and operate in informal or fragile institutions. The article turns a thematic overview of prior work into a research agenda for extending existing theory and building new theory from African phenomena.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial contributes by reframing Africa as a theoretically generative context rather than only a setting for social-development concerns. It identifies how management theories about institutions, resources, governance, entrepreneurship, leadership, CSR, informal markets, and cross-cultural management can be tested, extended, or reworked through African evidence. It also organizes the existing literature into three broad categories and links them to future research domains.

**Practical Implication**
For researchers, the editorial recommends studying Africa not as a peripheral application site but as a context where management problems are pressing, varied, and theoretically revealing. For business executives and policy makers, it points to questions about navigating weak institutions, building managerial and employee capabilities, improving governance, managing natural resources, strengthening resilience, supporting entrepreneurship, and adapting across cultural and national boundaries.

**Limitations**
The editorial is a narrative and thematic overview rather than an empirical study that tests propositions or adjudicates among competing explanations. Its review is limited to the Social Sciences Citation Index search strategy, articles published between 1990 and 2015, and a January 16, 2016 search date.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on leadership and governance in African contexts, including how organizations can respond to fragile state governance and conflict. They also call for research on managing human and natural resources, organizational and social resilience, institutional capacity, labor mobility, entrepreneurship, and managing across cultures and countries. Across these areas, they urge scholars to examine both how African phenomena modify existing management theories and how they can generate new theories.
