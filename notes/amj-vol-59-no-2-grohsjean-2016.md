# Coming Back to Edmonton: Competing with Former Employers and Colleagues

**Abstract**
Drawing on human and social capital theory, research on employee mobility has discussed the benefits and drawbacks of hiring employees from rival firms. To explain the performance implications of employee mobility, the literature has focused on what moving individuals can do, but has ignored what they are willing to do. However, to fully understand what individuals will actually do at the new firm, we need to understand both. We argue that what individuals are willing to do depends on their collective and relational identity. When competing against a former employer, individuals experience a conflict in their collective identity as they identify with both organizations but can only increase the welfare of one. To reduce this conflict, individuals strengthen their identification with the new organization and deidentify with the former by competing harder against the former organization. At the relational level, individuals can still identify with their ex-colleagues without harming the welfare of the new organization by competing harder with non-former colleagues but behaving less competitively toward former colleagues. We analyze data from the National Hockey League and find strong support for our hypotheses.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how individuals behave when they have to compete against their former employer and former colleagues. It reframes employee mobility by asking not only what mobile employees can do through transferred human and social capital, but also what they are willing to do when prior collective and relational identities remain salient.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Facing a former team; facing a former teammate; supplementary splits for facing the first NHL team, non-first former teams, and different circumstances of leaving a team.
- DV(s): Number of checks a focal player delivered against a single opposing player in one game.
- Mediators: Theorized identity processes rather than directly measured mediators: collective identity conflict, identification with the new organization, deidentification with the former organization, and relational identification with former colleagues.
- Moderators: Tenure with the former team and time since leaving the former team; time as teammates and time since players stopped being teammates.

The proposed mechanism is cross-level. At the collective level, competing against a former organization creates identity conflict because the individual identifies with both organizations but can increase the welfare of only one; more competitive behavior toward the former organization helps strengthen identification with the new organization and deidentify with the former one. At the relational level, former coworker or friend relationships can remain inclusive, so individuals can support the new organization while sparing former colleagues by directing competitive behavior toward non-former colleagues. The NHL analyses support this contrast: players check former teams more, but check former teammates less.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to human capital theory and employee mobility research by adding willingness and identity-based motivation to the usual focus on transferable human and social capital. It also contributes to theories of identity and identification by showing that collective and relational identities need not converge after mobility; leaving an organization may instead trigger divergence, producing different behavior toward former organizations and former colleagues.

**Practical Implication**
The study implies that firms hiring from competitors may gain not only human and social capital but also heightened effort when new employees compete against former employers. It also warns that employees may be less competitive toward former colleagues, which makes team hiring, employee bonding, staffing timing, and non-compete clauses relevant to how firms manage mobility from rivals.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting is a single professional sports industry where head-to-head competition is visible, decisions are fast, and behavior is constrained by explicit rules. The authors also note that identification is continuous but their independent variables are not continuous measures of identification, and that focusing on competitive behavior captures only part of how movers may change their behavior.

**Future Research**
Future research could measure identification more continuously and examine whether identity transitions change helpful behavior as well as competitive behavior. It could also test the framework in broader managerial settings, such as sales, professional services, advertising, or product competition, where former employers and former colleagues remain salient but behavior is less rule-bound than in ice hockey.
