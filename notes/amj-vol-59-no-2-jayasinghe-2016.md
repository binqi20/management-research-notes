# The Operational and Signaling Benefits of Voluntary Labor Code Adoption: Reconceptualizing the Scope of Human Resource Management in Emerging Economies

**Abstract**
Labor codes have been voluntarily adopted and used by manufacturers in emerging economies for the past two decades as a means of ensuring minimally acceptable or core labor standards for workers. However, far too little is known of the potential benefits from the voluntary adoption of labor codes to the manufacturer, and prior human resource management research has been virtually silent on the business implications of their use for emerging economy manufacturers participating in global supply chains. Drawing on previous work across multiple disciplines and proposing a framework that extends human resource management theory more explicitly and rigorously to the context of emerging economy manufacturing, I theorize and demonstrate that the voluntary adoption of a labor code can constitute an effective human resource investment in emerging economies in improving establishment-level employee outcomes and operational and financial performance. The hypotheses are tested using longitudinal data on a sample of apparel manufacturing plants in Sri Lanka. Implications of this study include providing insight into how to expand the scope and relevance of human resource management theory to better understand research and practice in emerging economies.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether voluntary labor code adoption can be understood as an effective human resource investment for emerging-economy manufacturers and whether it improves employee outcomes, operational performance, and financial performance. It further asks whether financial gains arise through internal operational improvements, external signaling, or both.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Voluntary adoption of the Garments without Guilt labor code by apparel manufacturing plants.
- DV(s): Employee outcomes, operational performance, and financial performance, measured as absenteeism, voluntary turnover, efficiency, productivity, defect rate, on-time delivery, sales volume, and average selling price.
- Mediators: Absenteeism, voluntary turnover, and operational performance outcomes, especially on-time delivery.
- Moderators: Not directly modeled; the theoretical scope condition is emerging-economy manufacturing, where core labor standards are not reliably assured by law or existing HRM investments.

The mechanism is that voluntary labor code adoption improves basic labor standards through changes in work practices, amenities, and certification, which can reduce employee withdrawal and improve operational performance. The paper also theorizes a signaling pathway: certification makes otherwise unobservable labor standards visible to retailers and end-consumers, allowing adopters to differentiate themselves and increase sales volume. Empirically, GwG adoption is associated with lower absenteeism, higher productivity, higher on-time delivery through reduced absenteeism, and higher sales volume, with signaling providing the clearest explanation for sales-volume gains.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper expands strategic HRM theory by arguing that, in emerging economies, effective HR investments may begin with meeting core labor standards rather than with resource-intensive systems aimed at discretionary employee contributions. It integrates contingency and human capital investment perspectives with certified management standards and CSR arguments to show that HR investments can affect performance through both internal employee-operational processes and external signaling. The contribution is a contextualized account of HRM in emerging-economy manufacturing that broadens the relevant set of HR investments beyond high-performance work systems.

**Practical Implication**
For emerging-economy manufacturers, voluntary labor code adoption may be a commercially valuable HR investment rather than merely a compliance or social-responsibility cost. The findings suggest that improving basic labor standards can reduce absenteeism, support on-time delivery and productivity, and help firms win higher sales volume through credible signaling to external stakeholders. Managers should treat labor-code adoption as potentially complementary to, and possibly foundational for, more resource-intensive HR systems.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that signaling is inferred from the direct effect of code adoption on sales volume rather than measured through retailers' redistribution of orders from non-adopters to adopters. The study is based on one industry and one country, so the results may depend on apparel manufacturing, Sri Lankan institutions, retailer composition, and local labor regulation. The analysis also does not fully account for adoption costs, potential endogeneity from managerial discretion in adopting the code, or the dynamic relationship between labor-code adoption and resource-intensive HR systems.

**Future Research**
Future research should measure signaling mechanisms more directly, including whether retailers shift orders from non-adopters to adopters. The paper calls for longitudinal, multi-industry and multi-country studies to test whether labor-code effectiveness varies with labor regulation, culture, and retailer composition. It also recommends research on the antecedents of voluntary labor code adoption, its costs, and its complementarity with resource-intensive HR systems.
