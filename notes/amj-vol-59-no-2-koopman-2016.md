# Integrating the Bright and Dark Sides of OCB: A Daily Investigation of the Benefits and Costs of Helping Others

**Abstract**
Although the general picture in the organizational citizenship behavior (OCB) literature is that OCB has positive consequences for employees and organizations, an emerging stream of work has begun to examine the potential negative consequences of OCB for actors. Drawing from the cognitive-affective processing system framework and conservation of resources theory, we present an integrative model that simultaneously examines the benefits and costs of daily OCB for actors. Utilizing an experience sampling methodology through which 82 employees were surveyed for 10 workdays, we find that daily OCB is associated with positive affect, but it also interferes with perceptions of work goal progress. Positive affect and work goal progress in turn mediate the effects of OCB on daily well-being. Moreover, employees’ trait regulatory focus influences the strength of the daily relationships between OCB and its positive and negative outcomes. We conclude by discussing theoretical and practical implications of our multilevel model.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether daily organizational citizenship behavior is good or bad for the actors who perform it, and more precisely for whom it is beneficial or detrimental and why. It examines whether daily OCB has positive and negative implications for emotional exhaustion, job satisfaction, and affective commitment through two paths: positive affect for job satisfaction and affective commitment, and work goal progress for all three well-being indicators, with promotion and prevention focus shaping these daily relationships.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Daily interpersonal organizational citizenship behavior.
- DV(s): Daily well-being indicators: emotional exhaustion, job satisfaction, and affective commitment.
- Mediators: Positive affect as a resource-generating mechanism; perceived work goal progress as a resource-consuming mechanism.
- Moderators: Trait promotion focus moderates the daily OCB-positive affect relationship; trait prevention focus moderates the daily OCB-work goal progress relationship.

The mechanism is dual rather than uniformly positive or negative. Drawing on CAPS and COR, the authors argue that helping others can generate psychological resources and positive affect, improving job satisfaction and affective commitment, while also consuming scarce work time and reducing perceived progress toward work goals, thereby increasing exhaustion and lowering satisfaction and commitment. Promotion-focused employees are more attuned to resource gains from OCB, whereas prevention-focused employees are more sensitive to the resource losses and goal-progress costs of OCB.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the OCB literature by integrating the bright-side and dark-side streams in one daily, actor-focused model. It extends COR by modeling resource generation and resource consumption simultaneously in a dynamic within-person design, and extends CAPS by specifying how daily helping episodes activate affective and goal-progress processes. It also positions regulatory focus as a cross-level boundary condition explaining why the same helping behavior can have different well-being implications for different employees.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that encouraging high levels of citizenship behavior may improve mood and commitment but can also drain actors by interfering with goal progress. Organizations that depend on helping should monitor exhaustion, avoid treating OCB as costless, and support practices that replenish resources, such as reflection on the prosocial impact of helping. The findings also suggest that employees' regulatory focus matters: promotion focus may amplify the benefits of OCB, while prevention focus may make the costs more salient.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study relies on self-reports, although they separated measures across time, controlled for related constructs, and centered daily variables within individuals. They also did not disentangle all possible mediators, such as recipient gratitude, pride, contentment, guilt, or recipient indifference, and the lagged next-day analyses were underpowered. The study examined interpersonally focused OCB only, with relatively mild helping items, and did not test whether organizationally focused or change-focused OCB would operate similarly.

**Future Research**
Future research could collect observer ratings of daily OCB and use designs better able to capture next-day spirals in OCB, well-being, and subsequent helping. The authors call for work on specific OCB event features, including complexity, novelty, routine character, and time or resource intensity. They also encourage research on additional individual boundary conditions, broader well-being outcomes such as physiological and behavioral consequences, and downstream effects such as home helping, workplace reciprocation, or deviant behavior after OCB.
