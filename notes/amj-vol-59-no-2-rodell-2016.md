# Perceptions of Employee Volunteering: Is It “Credited” or “Stigmatized” by Colleagues?

**Abstract**
As research begins to accumulate on employee volunteering, it appears that this behavior is largely beneficial to employee performance and commitment. It is less clear, however, how employee volunteering is perceived by others in the workplace. Do colleagues award volunteering “credit” (e.g., associating it with being concerned about others) or do they “stigmatize” it (e.g., associating it with being distracted from work)? Moreover, do those evaluations predict how colleagues actually treat employees who volunteer more often? Adopting a reputation perspective, we draw from theories of person perception and attribution to explore these research questions. The results of a field study reveal that colleagues gave credit to employee volunteering when they attributed it to intrinsic reasons and stigmatized employee volunteering when they attributed it to impression management reasons. Ultimately, through the awarded credits, volunteering was rewarded by supervisors (with the allocation of more resources) and coworkers (with the provision of more helping behavior) when it was attributed to intrinsic motives—a relationship that was amplified when stigmas were low and mitigated when stigmas were high. The results of a laboratory experiment further confirmed that volunteering was both credited and stigmatized, distinguishing it from citizenship behavior, which was credited but not stigmatized.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how colleagues perceive employee volunteering and whether those perceptions affect how supervisors and coworkers treat employees who volunteer more often. It focuses on whether volunteering is credited, stigmatized, or both, and on how attributed motives shape those reputational evaluations.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee volunteering frequency; experimentally manipulated high versus low volunteering in Study 2.
- DV(s): Volunteering credits, volunteering stigmas, and supportive reactions from colleagues, including coworker helping and supervisor resource allocation.
- Mediators: Volunteering reputation, separated into credits and stigmas.
- Moderators: Intrinsic attributions, impression management attributions, and the credit-by-stigma interaction.

The proposed mechanism is reputational. Colleagues observe employee volunteering, infer motives, and translate those inferences into positive credits or negative stigmas. Intrinsic attributions amplify the positive volunteering-credit relationship, while impression management attributions amplify stigmatization. Credits then increase supportive reactions, but their benefits are weaker when stigmas are also high.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to employee volunteering research by shifting attention from the volunteer's attitudes and behavior to colleagues' interpretations and treatment of the volunteer. It also extends reputation research by treating reputation as attribute-specific and potentially ambivalent: the same non-work behavior can produce both credits and stigmas depending on perceived motives.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that employee volunteering can affect interpersonal treatment at work, not only volunteer wellbeing or organizational image. Volunteer programs may be more beneficial when employees' participation is perceived as intrinsically motivated and less beneficial when it appears impression-management oriented, because stigma can weaken the rewards associated with positive credits.

**Limitations**
The field study contains some same-source, same-time colleague reports for credits, stigmas, attributions, and supportive reactions, leaving possible common method and causal-order concerns despite temporal and source separation where possible. The paper studies volunteering frequency rather than the initial decision to volunteer, persistence of volunteering, specific volunteer causes, or the timing of volunteering during versus outside work hours.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how different company supports for volunteering shape colleague attributions and whether formal volunteer programs reduce or intensify reputational evaluations. It could also study how intrinsic and impression-management attributions coexist, how specific volunteer causes or schedules affect stigma, and whether volunteering direction and persistence produce different workplace reactions than volunteering frequency.
