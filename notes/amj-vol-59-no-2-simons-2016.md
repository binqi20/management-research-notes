# There’s No Beer without a Smoke: Community Cohesion and Neighboring Communities’ Effects on Organizational Resistance to Antismoking Regulations in the Dutch Hospitality Industry

**Abstract**
This study highlights the importance of communities in explaining organizational resistance to institutional pressures. Examining the active resistance of small bars to smoking regulations in 427 Dutch municipalities (communities), we argue that the likelihood of organizational resistance to institutional pressure from a powerful actor is affected by the social cohesion of the focal community. In addition, we propose a contiguity effect that emphasizes the broader social context of the community—its neighboring communities—as a source for support or information about appropriate ways to resist such pressures. By incorporating community attributes to account for organizations’ heterogeneous responses to institutional pressure, the study advances current institutional scholarship and demonstrates empirically how such a theory can help explain the success of relatively weak organizational actors’ resistance in the face of strong institutional pressures by the state—that is, as a result of their embeddedness in a community.

**Research Question**
How do community cohesion and resistance in neighboring communities shape small bars’ active resistance to institutional pressure from a powerful state actor? The paper asks why resistance to the Dutch smoking ban varied across municipalities even though small bars faced the same national regulation.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Community residential stability, community kinship, and resistance in neighboring communities.
- DV(s): Number of bars in a municipality resisting the smoking ban, measured through SSB foundation membership.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Residential stability and kinship weaken the effect of neighboring communities’ resistance.

The mechanism links community embeddedness to organizational resistance. Residential stability creates a latent relational infrastructure that can be mobilized when the smoking ban threatens valued local bars, while kinship generates belonging, mutual obligation, and willingness to defend the community’s social function. Resistance in neighboring communities supplies signals, information, and coordination opportunities, but cohesive communities have less permeable boundaries and are therefore less influenced by neighboring resistance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional theory by showing that community-level social cohesion helps explain heterogeneous organizational resistance to the same institutional pressure. It brings community back into the study of institutional processes by showing that resistance is socially embedded rather than only a narrow strategic response by individual organizations. It also develops a spatial account of resistance by showing that neighboring communities matter, but that their influence depends on the focal community’s cohesion.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that regulators and managers should attend to local community characteristics when anticipating organizational responses to new rules. Even small and resource-constrained organizations may resist powerful actors when they serve an important community function and can draw on cohesive local support.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the theory mainly applies to small and relatively powerless organizations facing pressure from a powerful and legitimate actor. They also note that the arguments are most applicable when the institutional pressure threatens the normative framework of the community. Although the time structure and qualitative evidence make reverse causality unlikely, the study cannot fully rule out omitted historical community factors that influence both cohesion and resistance.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine longer time periods to capture the dynamics of organizational resistance to regulatory pressures at the community level. The authors also call for more fine-grained measures of local social endowments, including histories of community activism, and for studies of other communities such as virtual or open-source communities that share collective identity and may face powerful adversaries.
