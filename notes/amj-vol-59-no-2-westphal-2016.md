# Psyched-up to Suck-up: Self-regulated Cognition, Interpersonal Influence, and Recommendations for Board Appointments in the Corporate Elite

**Abstract**
This study reveals how self-regulated cognition in advance of social interaction can resolve the so-called “ingratiator’s dilemma,” which is that the most attractive targets of social influence—those who have relatively high social status—tend to be the most difficult to ingratiate successfully. Our theoretical argument suggests that in anticipation of social interaction with a relatively high-status colleague, corporate directors tend to reflect on characteristics shared with that colleague, while avoiding thoughts about characteristics not shared. We explain how this pattern of cognition induces genuine positive feelings and admiration for the colleague, such that their interpersonal behaviors are less likely to appear as insincere attempts to curry favor, and thus more likely to engender influence. We further suggest that this pattern of cognition is especially valuable when interacting with relatively high-status colleagues who are demographically dissimilar from the focal actor. Finally, our theoretical framework indicates how this self-regulated cognition facilitates advancement in the corporate elite by positively moderating the effects of one’s interpersonal behavior on the likelihood that colleagues will recommend him or her for a board appointment. We test our hypotheses with a unique dataset that includes survey data from a large sample of directors collected before and after meetings.

**Research Question**
How can corporate directors overcome the ingratiator's dilemma when trying to influence relatively high-status colleagues? The paper asks whether anticipating interaction with a high-status alter leads directors to reflect on shared characteristics and avoid reflection on nonshared characteristics, and whether this self-regulated cognition makes expressions of liking and admiration more effective in securing board-appointment recommendations.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Anticipation of social interaction with a relatively high-status alter; reflection on characteristics held in common with the alter; low reflection on characteristics not shared with the alter; expressions of liking and admiration.
- DV(s): Director cognitions and whether the alter recommends the focal director for a board appointment.
- Mediators: Perceived liking and admiration by the alter, tested in supplemental mediation analyses.
- Moderators: Alter's relative social status and demographic dissimilarity between focal director and alter.

The mechanism is that anticipating interaction with a high-status colleague prompts directors to focus cognitively on shared personal and social characteristics while avoiding nonshared characteristics. This pattern induces more genuine positive affect and admiration, making later expressions of liking and admiration appear less like strategic flattery. The influence benefit is especially pronounced when the target is both high-status and demographically dissimilar, because such targets are attractive sources of opportunity but also more vigilant for insincere ingratiation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to social influence research by identifying a cognitive route through which actors can resolve the ingratiator's dilemma rather than merely using more subtle influence tactics. It extends self-regulation research by theorizing self-regulated social cognition: cognition about another person that regulates feelings or attitudes before interaction. It also contributes to corporate governance research by showing how micro-level interpersonal psychology helps directors convert elite ties into recommendations for board appointments.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that directors' interpersonal influence depends not only on what they say or do in meetings but also on how they prepare cognitively before interacting with powerful colleagues. For leaders in demographically diverse elite settings, reflecting on shared characteristics may increase sincere liking and reduce the risk that compliments or admiration are read as opportunistic. The study also suggests that influence behaviors can shape access to board seats and other elite opportunities beyond formal governance performance.

**Limitations**
The paper does not present a formal limitations section, but the empirical setting bounds the claims to corporate directors at large and mid-sized public U.S. firms. The design relies heavily on survey reports around director interactions, although the authors use inter-rater checks, archival controls, selection models, and robustness analyses. The theory focuses on self-regulated cognition about shared personal and social characteristics, leaving other forms of anticipatory cognition less directly tested.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on whether the mechanism generalizes to firm leaders beyond corporate directors, lower-level managers, and professionals outside the corporate sphere. Future work could examine whether influence-target characteristics such as personality traits moderate the effects of self-regulated cognition. The paper also suggests studying other kinds of anticipatory cognition, such as reflection on organizational characteristics or strategy, before interactions with important constituents.
