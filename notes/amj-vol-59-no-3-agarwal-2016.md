# What Do I Take With Me? The Mediating Effect of Spin-out Team Size and Tenure on the Founder–Firm Performance Relationship

**Abstract**
We extend the knowledge-based perspective to consider the impact of spin-out founders on knowledge transfer to new ventures. We argue that existing theory largely ignores the founder’s role as a team catalyst who mobilizes a team and transmits the team’s knowledge to a new venture. We address this gap by building theory on the role of a spin-out founder as a facilitator of co-mobility and whose impact on firm outcomes is mediated by the size and organizational experience of the recruited team. The support for our hypotheses, through use of linked employee–employer U.S. Census data from the legal services industry, has theoretical and practical implications for the knowledge-based view and human resource strategies for both existing and entrepreneurial firms.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how spin-out founders affect knowledge transfer and firm performance by mobilizing teams, rather than only by carrying their own human capital into a new venture. It examines whether founder performance shapes spin-out team size and tenure, and whether those team characteristics mediate the effects of founder performance on parent firm performance and spin-out survival.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Founder performance, measured through pre-spin-out founder earnings.
- DV(s): Parent firm performance, measured as revenue per employee after the spin-out event; spin-out performance, measured as next-year failure or survival.
- Mediators: Spin-out team size and average organizational tenure of the departing team.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is a founder-as-catalyst process within the knowledge-based view. Higher-performing founders have better access to coworkers, stronger information about complementary skills, and a more credible signal for persuading others to leave secure employment. Larger teams transfer more distributed and complementary knowledge from the parent to the spin-out, while longer-tenured teams carry routines and organizational experience that especially benefit the new venture. The results support team size as a mediator for both parent and spin-out outcomes, and team tenure as a mediator for spin-out survival but not for parent firm performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper refines the knowledge-based view by showing that team-based knowledge is not always an isolating mechanism when the rival is a spin-out founded by an informed insider. It contributes to employee entrepreneurship research by linking founder characteristics and founding-team characteristics as interdependent rather than independent sources of parent and spin-out performance. It also advances microfoundational theorizing by connecting individual founder performance, team mobilization, and firm-level outcomes in one mediation model.

**Practical Implication**
For established firms, the findings suggest that reducing the harm from employee entrepreneurship may require attention to potential followers, not only to the high-performing founder. Retention efforts aimed at team members may be more cost effective than trying only to deter founders. For potential founders, the ability to attract a larger and more experienced team is an important validation of both the opportunity and the founder’s capacity to exploit it.

**Limitations**
The authors note generalizability concerns because the empirical setting is legal services, where human capital and client relationships are central and physical capital is less important. They cannot directly measure competitive interactions, the specialties or knowledge bases of team members, or the size of the opportunity pursued by the spin-out founder. They also cannot distinguish voluntary from involuntary mobility and cannot completely rule out reverse mediation, although they argue this pathway is logically unlikely in the empirical context.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether the relationships extend to manufacturing and other settings where complementary assets include physical capital and intellectual property. The authors call for work that measures direct parent-spin-out competition, compares parent and spin-out knowledge bases, and examines opportunity quality more directly. They also identify demographic homophily and skill or task diversity within founding teams as important directions for decomposing how team composition affects firm performance.
