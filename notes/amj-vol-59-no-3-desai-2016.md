# The Behavioral Theory of the (Governed) Firm: Corporate Board Influences on Organizations’ Responses to Performance Shortfalls

**Abstract**
The Behavioral Theory of the Firm provides a well-evidenced perspective on organizational decision making that has influenced a wide array of literatures, including the
substantial body of work on organizational change. This literature suggests that organizations are more likely to undertake major changes when their performance declines
below aspirations or targets for acceptable performance, but few studies examine how
multiple groups of organizational decision makers, each with potentially conflicting
interests, might collectively influence this process. To that end, I incorporate theory
regarding corporate boards and their role in organizational decision making. I use this
integration to suggest that boards with particular characteristics may have interests that

**Research Question**
The paper asks what board characteristics moderate the relationship between performance feedback and organizational change, and through what processes those moderating influences occur. It focuses on whether board size, board turnover, and board equity ownership shape how organizations respond when performance falls below aspiration levels.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Performance relative to historical aspiration when below aspiration.
- DV(s): Annual count of service line divestitures.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Board size, board turnover, and board equity ownership.

The mechanism is that performance shortfalls increase both managerial search for change and board monitoring, advising, and resource-provision influence. Boards with divergent interests can force compromises, provide conflicting advice, compel reconsideration, or withhold resources, limiting the extent of organizational change. The results support moderation by board size and board equity ownership, but not by board turnover.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper connects the Behavioral Theory of the Firm with corporate governance research by showing that responses to performance feedback may depend on interactions between managerial and board coalitions rather than a single dominant coalition. It contributes to performance feedback theory by offering board structure as a reason why the direct relationship between poor performance and change can appear mixed or non-significant. It also contributes to board research by showing how boards may limit, rather than promote, aggressive change during performance shortfalls.

**Practical Implication**
For managers and directors, the paper implies that governance structure can materially shape whether performance shortfalls lead to major organizational change. Larger boards or boards with higher equity ownership may require managers to negotiate more extensively over proposed changes, which can reduce or reverse the usual momentum toward problemistic search and change.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that hospital boards may be more involved in operational decisions than boards in many other contexts, so board characteristics may have stronger effects in this setting. The healthcare industry is also highly institutionalized, which may constrain service line divestitures differently from change in other industries. The study cannot directly observe board decision making, so it infers governance processes from archival panel data.

**Future Research**
Future research could replicate the theory in other settings where boards influence different forms of strategic or operational change. Studies could directly observe governance practices to clarify how directors and managers negotiate specific change proposals under performance shortfalls. Further work could distinguish among alternative mechanisms that explain why high board size or equity ownership reverses, rather than only weakens, the expected performance feedback effect.
