# A Two-Phase Longitudinal Model of a Turnover Event: Disruption, Recovery Rates, and Moderators of Collective Performance

**Abstract**
Blending theory on collective turnover and group adaptability, this paper develops a two-phase longitudinal model that explains how and why an individual-level turnover event has effects on collective performance. Phase 1 (disruption) is marked by a sudden and negative change in unit-level performance, while Phase 2 (recovery) entails a gradual increase in unit performance over time. We further propose that the positional distribution (manager or employee) of the turnover event and the amount of interdependence within the collective will influence the consequences of a turnover event. Using a sample of 524 branches of a U.S. bank, the results largely support the hypotheses as a turnover event leads to an immediate and negative change in branch-level performance. Notably, there is an interaction between manager and employee turnover events that significantly decreases performance. In the recovery period, branches recover more quickly after losing an employee than they do after losing a manager. However, the moderating effect of branch-level interdependence depends on the position, and there is no statistically significant recovery from a manager turnover event within our performance window. These results highlight the insights of our two-phase model and the need to study collective turnover dynamics over time and across different positions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how an individual turnover event affects collective performance over time, how long those consequences last, and why disruption and recovery rates differ. It specifically examines whether the position of the leaver and branch-level interdependence shape immediate disruption and subsequent recovery in branch sales performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee turnover event, manager turnover event, employee recovery rate, manager recovery rate, and their interactions.
- DV(s): Branch-level sales performance, measured monthly as percent to sales goal.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Positional distribution of the turnover event and branch-level interdependence.

The mechanism is a two-phase adaptation process linking individual exits to collective outcomes. In Phase 1, turnover disrupts branch routines, coordination, relationships, and role coverage, producing an immediate drop in sales performance. In Phase 2, remaining members adapt by reconfiguring routines, socializing replacements, and absorbing work, allowing recovery after employee turnover but not statistically significant recovery after manager turnover within the 12-month window. Interdependence slows recovery after employee turnover because more coordinated branch systems are harder to reconfigure after membership change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends collective turnover research by shifting attention from aggregate turnover rates to discrete turnover events and their temporal consequences. It integrates context-emergent turnover theory with team adaptability theory to specify disruption and recovery as distinct phases with potentially different moderators. It also advances positional-distribution theorizing by showing that manager and employee turnover are not redundant and can have multiplicative consequences for collective performance.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat turnover costs as dynamic rather than one-time losses, because branch performance can remain depressed for months after a turnover event. The findings imply that manager exits require especially swift higher-level intervention and that employee turnover is more damaging when paired with manager turnover. Branches with high interdependence may need cross-training and proactive coordination support to shorten the recovery period after employee turnover.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the branch banking setting may have lower interdependence than more tightly coupled teams, so generalizability to other workflows requires caution. Interdependence was measured broadly and only once, limiting causal inference about changes in interdependence over time. The 12-month monthly data window obscures intra-month dynamics and cannot show longer-term consequences after sales goals reset.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the two-phase model in settings with higher and lower interdependence, other occupations, and different cultural, organizational, and industry contexts. Studies should use repeated measures of interdependence and other collective states, finer-grained time windows, and longer performance windows. The authors also call for work on leaver and replacement quality, turnover functionality, alternative longitudinal models, and the net financial impact of turnover events.
