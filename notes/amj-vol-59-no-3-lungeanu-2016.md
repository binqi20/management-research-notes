# Venture Capital Ownership as a Contingent Resource: How Owner–Firm Fit Influences IPO Outcomes

**Abstract**
This study seeks to contribute to the literature on corporate ownership and firm performance by advancing an expertise-based perspective that views owners as a contingent resource. Specifically, we propose that heterogeneous prior experiences of corporate owners creates identifiable and evolving differences in owner expertise, and that these differences in expertise, when matched appropriately to firms’ specific and changing strategic needs, will be a source of value over the life cycle of a firm. We draw from the venture capital (VC) context to identify ex ante the meaningful differences in owner expertise, as well as the firmspecific situations in which we believe a fit or misfit would exist between VC owners and these private firms. We test and find support for our predictions regarding the performance benefits of well-matched owners and firms using an extensive longitudinal dataset of the population of U.S. private firms seeking to go public from 1997 to 2004, and their VC owners. We discuss the implications of our approach as they relate to future research opportunities across the corporate governance, strategy, and entrepreneurship literatures.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether, when, and how corporate owners affect firm performance through expertise rather than only through agency alignment, monitoring, or power. In the venture capital context, it examines whether fit between VC owners' evolving expertise and private firms' evolving strategic needs improves successful exit and short-term post-IPO performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Stage fit, industry fit, and time-horizon fit between VC owner expertise and the private firm's strategic needs.
- DV(s): Successful exit, coded as IPO or qualifying acquisition after an IPO attempt; short-term post-IPO performance, measured as the natural logarithm of the ventures' percentage change in stock price at 90 days.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: VC success history in robustness analyses; board membership is examined as a conduit condition for expertise transfer.

The mechanism is an owner-as-contingent-resource process. VC owners accumulate expertise from prior investments, but that expertise becomes valuable only when it fits the portfolio firm's stage of development, industry, and time horizon at relevant financing rounds. Fit creates a channel for advice and experience transfer; misfit can lead owners to apply expertise in the wrong context. The results support positive effects of stage fit and time-horizon fit on both successful exit and short-term post-IPO performance, and partial support for industry fit on successful exit.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper redirects corporate ownership research from differences in owner type, monitoring incentives, or power toward heterogeneity within a class of owners. It advances a contingency perspective in which ownership expertise is not inherently valuable, but becomes a resource-in-use when matched to firms' changing strategic needs. It also links corporate governance, strategy, and entrepreneurship by showing how the portfolio of investors surrounding a firm can shape venture outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
For entrepreneurs and private firms, the findings imply that choosing VC investors should involve assessing fit between investor experience and the firm's current and future strategic needs, not only investor reputation or capital availability. For VC firms, the results suggest that value creation depends on deploying the right experience in the right portfolio-firm context and at the right stage of the investment relationship.

**Limitations**
The authors note that expertise is captured at the VC firm or partner level and that the transfer process between individual VC partners, syndicate partners, and portfolio-firm executives is not directly observed. The study is also specific to VC owners and private firms attempting to go public, a setting chosen because expertise differences and owner involvement are visible. The authors acknowledge that other owner types and firm contexts may reveal additional dimensions of ownership expertise.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine fit or misfit between the expertise of VC partners and portfolio-firm top executives, as well as the conditions under which expertise transfer is more or less effective. The authors also call for work on how owner-firm fit affects the type of exit chosen, such as IPO versus M&A, and on whether the contingent-resource perspective extends to other owners and firms beyond venture capital and IPO settings.
