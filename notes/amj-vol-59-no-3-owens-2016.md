# How Does Leader Humility Influence Team Performance? Exploring the Mechanisms of Contagion and Collective Promotion Focus

**Abstract**
Using data from 607 subjects organized into 161 teams (84 laboratory teams, 77 organizational field teams), we examined how leader humility influences team interaction patterns, emergent states, and team performance. We develop and test a theoretical model, positing that, when leaders behave humbly, followers emulate their humble behaviors, creating a shared interpersonal team process (collective humility). This, in turn, creates a team emergent state focused on progressively striving toward achieving the team’s highest potential (collective promotion focus), which ultimately enhances team performance. We test our model across three studies wherein we manipulate leader humility to test the social contagion hypothesis (Study 1), examine the impact of humility on team processes and performance in a longitudinal team simulation (Study 2), and test the full model in a multistage field study in a health services context (Study 3). Our findings collectively support our theoretical model, demonstrating that leader behavior can spread via social contagion to followers, producing an emergent state that ultimately affects team performance. We contribute to the leadership literature by suggesting the need for leaders to lead by example and showing how a specific set of leader behaviors influence team performance, providing a template for future leadership research on a wide variety of leader behaviors.

**Research Question**
How does leader humility influence team interaction patterns, emergent states, and team performance? The paper asks whether humble leader behavior spreads to followers as collective humility, generates collective promotion focus, and thereby improves team performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader humility; collective humility.
- DV(s): Study 1 outcomes are collective humility and team collective promotion focus; team performance is measured as ending stock price in Study 2 and leader-rated team performance in Study 3.
- Mediators: Collective humility and collective promotion focus.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is a two-stage team process. Humble leaders model admitting limits, appreciating others' strengths, and teachability; followers emulate these behaviors, creating collective humility as a shared interpersonal process. Collective humility then creates opportunity, motivation, and ability for a collective promotion focus. Study 1 tests this contagion path to collective humility and collective promotion focus, while Studies 2 and 3 test whether that emergent state links collective humility to team performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends leadership research by specifying how a nontraditional, character-based leadership construct affects team performance through imitable leader behavior. It introduces collective humility as a team-level interpersonal process and positions collective promotion focus as the motivational emergent state linking humble team interaction to performance. It also contributes to regulatory focus research by identifying leader humility and collective humility as antecedents of collective promotion focus in both laboratory and field settings.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that leaders can influence team performance by modeling behaviors that followers can actually emulate, rather than only articulating values or visions. Teams may benefit when leaders openly acknowledge limits, highlight member strengths, and remain teachable, because these behaviors can normalize constructive interaction and growth-oriented striving. The field-study comparison also suggests that the leader humility pathway is distinct from transformational leadership.

**Limitations**
Collective humility and team collective promotion focus were measured from the same sources at the same time, so their relationship may be inflated by common method variance. The studies were conducted with Western samples, leaving generalizability to Eastern contexts unclear. The authors also note the need to examine boundary conditions across task specialization, hierarchy, humility dispersion, time pressure, task interdependence, and contexts where directive leadership may be more appropriate.

**Future Research**
Future research could replicate the findings with behavioral or extra-team observer measures of collective humility and collective promotion focus. Studies should test the model in Eastern contexts and in team settings with different levels of task specialization, hierarchy, interdependence, and time pressure. The authors also call for work on other antecedents of collective humility and on whether other virtues, such as courage or compassion, spread through similar leader-modeling processes.
