# Managing the Consequences of Organizational Stigmatization: Identity Work in a Social Enterprise

**Abstract**
In this inductive study, we shift the focus of stigma research inside organizational boundaries by examining its relationship with organizational identity. To do so, we draw on the case of Keystone, a social enterprise in the East of England that became stigmatized after it initiated a program of support for a group of migrants in its community. Keystone’s stigmatization precipitated a crisis of organizational identity. We examine how the identity crisis unfolded, focusing on the forms of identity work that Keystone’s leaders enacted in response in order to reframe the meaning that organizational members attached to the stigma. Interestingly, we show not only that the internal effects of stigmatization on identity can be managed, but also that they may facilitate unexpected positive outcomes for organizations.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what effects organizational stigmatization has on organizational identity, how those effects can be managed, and what outcomes follow for the organization. It examines these questions through Keystone, a social enterprise whose support for migrants transferred community stigma onto the organization and triggered an internal identity crisis.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Organizational stigmatization, image crisis, stigma mirroring, secondary stigmatization, empathy with stigmatizers, organizational identity, and identity work.
- Process / phases: Stigma transfer from migrants to Keystone; image crisis among established residents; internal identity crisis through secondary stigmatization and empathy with stigmatizers; identity work through advocacy and valorization; identity affirmation and resource augmentation.
- Practices: Keystone leaders advocated for migrants by reframing migration as part of Thetford's history, economically necessary, and important to public services; they valorized Keystone's social-justice mission by making migrant support a source of organizational pride.
- Emergent outcomes: Keystone deepened rather than reduced its alignment with migrants, strengthened internal clarity and solidarity, and gained visibility and resources from stakeholders who valued its migrant-support work.
- Boundary conditions: The authors frame the model as most transferable to organizations that experience sudden stigma transfer and operate among stakeholders with diverse value commitments.

The process argument is that external stigmatization can be mirrored inside an organization when members experience secondary stigma and identify partly with the stigmatizing community. Leaders can manage this identity crisis through identity work that changes the meaning attached to the stigma: advocacy builds internal support for the stigmatized group, while valorization reframes the organization's stigmatized position as evidence of its core values. Once the organization repairs identity enough to live with a tarnished image among some stakeholders, stigma can also generate positive outcomes by attracting support from other stakeholders who share the organization's values.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to organizational stigma research by shifting attention from external reputation and image effects to the internal consequences of stigmatization for organizational identity. It develops a model of identity management in stigmatized organizations, showing how advocacy and valorization can repair identity crises created by stigma transfer. It also extends stigma theory by explaining how stigmatization can produce positive outcomes, including identity affirmation and resource augmentation, under some conditions.

**Practical Implication**
For leaders of stigmatized organizations, the study suggests that managing external image is insufficient when stigma damages members' understanding of what the organization stands for. Leaders may need to engage directly in internal identity work, especially when frontline members experience stigma personally or sympathize with stigmatizing stakeholders. The Keystone case also suggests that organizations can sometimes convert a stigmatized association into a clearer mission and stronger support from value-aligned stakeholders.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a single qualitative case, so the authors caution that transferability depends on the type and timing of stigmatization. The model is most applicable to sudden stigma transfer and may not apply directly to core-stigmatized organizations that are stigmatized from inception, such as abortion clinics, strip clubs, or arms manufacturers. The authors also note that positive resource outcomes are likely only when salient stakeholders hold diverse values.

**Future Research**
Future research could test the model in other cases of stigma transfer and event stigma to clarify which forms of identity work are effective in different stigmatization contexts. Scholars could also examine how the speed of stigmatization shapes member disidentification, exit, and identity repair. Additional work should investigate when split images across stakeholder groups create resource opportunities rather than compounding identity and legitimacy problems.
