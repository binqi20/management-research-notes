# When in Rome, Look Like Caesar? Investigating the Link between Demand-side Cultural Power Distance and CEO Power

**Abstract**
Agency theory-grounded research on boards of directors and firm legitimacy has historically viewed CEO power as de-legitimating, often taking this fact for granted in theorizing about external assessors’ evaluations of a firm. With few exceptions, this literature has focused exclusively on capital market participants (e.g., investors, securities analysts) as the arbiters of a firm’s legitimacy and has accordingly assumed that legitimate governance arrangements are those derived from the shareholder-oriented prescriptions of agency theory. We extend this line of research in new ways by arguing that customers also externally assess firm legitimacy, and that firms potentially adjust their governance characteristics to meet customers’ norms and expectations. We argue that the cultural-cognitive institutions prevalent in customers’ home countries influence their judgments regarding a firm’s legitimacy, such that firms competing heavily in highpower distance cultures are more likely to have powerful CEOs, with CEO power a source of legitimacy—rather than illegitimacy—among customers. We also argue that the more dependent a firm is on its customers and the more salient cultural power distance is as a demand-side institutional norm, the greater this relationship will be. Data from 151 U.S. semiconductor and pharmaceutical firms over a 10-year period generally support our predictions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether firms exposed to customers in high cultural power distance markets exhibit greater CEO power on their boards. It also asks when this demand-side cultural-cognitive legitimacy relationship is stronger, focusing on customer dependence, variance in demand-side cultural power distance, and industry bargaining power.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Demand-side cultural power distance, calculated as a sales-weighted average of cultural power distance in the countries where the firm sells.
- DV(s): CEO power on the board of directors, measured through CEO duality, board independence reverse-coded, and CEO stock ownership.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Geographic concentration of product markets, variance in demand-side cultural power distance, and industry, contrasting semiconductors with pharmaceuticals.

The mechanism is a demand-side legitimacy process. Customers in high power distance cultures may see a powerful CEO as more culturally comprehensible and legitimate, so firms selling heavily into those markets have reason to align board power structures with customers' cultural-cognitive expectations. The relationship should strengthen when sales are geographically concentrated, when demand-side cultural expectations are less varied, and when firms have less bargaining power over large institutional customers.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper expands board-legitimacy research beyond capital market assessors by treating customers as grantors of firm legitimacy. It extends comparative corporate governance by showing that foreign product-market institutions, not only home-country institutions, can be associated with firm-level governance. It also complicates agency-theory assumptions about CEO power by showing that powerful CEOs may be legitimacy-enhancing in some customer environments.

**Practical Implication**
The findings caution investors, proxy advisors, and regulators against treating low CEO power as universally good governance. For firms competing in foreign product markets where power distance is accepted, CEO-dominated boards may provide demand-side legitimacy benefits. Governance evaluations should therefore consider product-market culture, geographic sales concentration, cultural variance, and industry context.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge possible ambiguity about whether firms adjust CEO power to match markets or target markets that fit existing CEO power. They also note that geographic segment sales data assume that purchasers are located in the reported country, which may not always hold. The study focuses on demand-side legitimacy among large institutional customers, so other stakeholder groups and other customer types remain outside the empirical scope.

**Future Research**
Future research could use more granular customer-location data to assess the cultural-cognitive influence of actual purchasing agents. Additional studies could extend the demand-side legitimacy framework to suppliers, employees, or other salient stakeholders. The authors also call for work on whether the framework generalizes beyond firms that sell primarily to large institutional customers.
