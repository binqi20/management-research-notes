# Local Partnering in Foreign Ventures: Uncertainty, Experiential Learning, and Syndication in Cross-Border Venture Capital Investments

**Abstract**
If partnering with local firms is an intuitive strategy with which to mitigate uncertainty in foreign ventures, then why don’t organizations always partner with local firms, especially in uncertain settings? We address this question by unbundling the effects of uncertainty in foreign ventures at the venture and country levels. We contend that, while both levels increase the need for partnering with local firms in foreign ventures, country-level uncertainty increases the difficulty of partnering with local firms and decreases the likelihood of such partnerships. We also posit that experiential learning helps firms manage the two types of uncertainty, and thereby reduces the need for partnering—yet, experience in the host country makes partnering more feasible and increases the likelihood of such partnerships. To test our hypotheses, we conceptualize the decision to partner with a local firm in a foreign venture as a multilayered decision, and model it accordingly. Using a global sample of venture capital investments made between 1984 and 2011, we find support for the distinct effects of ventureand country-level uncertainty as well as for corresponding levels of experiential learning. These findings have implications for the literature on cross-border venture capital investment and international business in general.

**Research Question**
Why do firms not always partner with local firms in uncertain foreign ventures, despite the intuitive value of local partnering for mitigating uncertainty? The paper asks how venture-level uncertainty, country-level uncertainty, and corresponding forms of international experience shape VC firms' propensity to syndicate cross-border investments with local VC partners.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Venture-level uncertainty, country-level uncertainty, international experience in managing venture-level uncertainty, international experience in managing country-level uncertainty, and international experience in the host country.
- DV(s): VC firms' partnering choice in first-round investments: domestic investment, foreign investment alone, foreign investment with only other foreign VC firms, or foreign investment with at least one local VC firm.
- Mediators: No formal mediator is estimated; the theorized mechanisms are need for local knowledge, reduced knowledge gaps through experience, feasibility of contracting with local partners, local embeddedness, legitimacy, and local networks.
- Moderators: No formal moderator is estimated in the main hypotheses.

The mechanism contrasts the need for local partnering with the feasibility of forming local partnerships. Venture-level uncertainty increases the value of local information and therefore increases local syndication. Country-level uncertainty also creates a need for local knowledge, but weak institutional environments make dependable local partners and enforceable cooperation harder to secure, reducing local partnering. Experience generally reduces the need for partners, but host-country experience increases local embeddedness and makes local partnering more feasible.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to cross-border VC and international business research by unbundling uncertainty into venture-level and country-level sources with different effects on local partnering. It extends organizational learning arguments by showing that experience does not simply reduce reliance on partners: host-country experience can increase local partnering by improving feasibility, legitimacy, and local network access. Methodologically, it treats local partnering as a multilayered market-entry and partner-choice decision rather than a single binary choice.

**Practical Implication**
For firms entering foreign countries, the paper implies that local partnering decisions should distinguish uncertainty about the venture from uncertainty about the host country. Local partners may be most valuable when venture-level uncertainty is high, but country-level institutional weakness can make local cooperation harder and riskier. For policy makers seeking foreign investment, improving formal institutional environments may increase the feasibility of cooperation between foreign investors and local partners.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the VC sector is a professional service industry that relies on intense social relationships and limited physical infrastructure, so generalizability to manufacturing or other organizational populations requires further study. The focal country-level institutional dimension is the regulatory/legal environment, leaving broader institutional configurations and interactions for future work. The authors also acknowledge remaining opportunities to disentangle knowledge-based need and feasibility effects from alternative explanations such as risk sharing.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether the findings generalize beyond international VC to other organizational populations with project- and country-level uncertainty. The paper calls for studies examining a broader set of institutions and their interactions, as well as contextual factors at the country level and dyadic country-pair level. It also points to further work on the complex drivers and interactions shaping partnering decisions in foreign ventures.
