# Creative, Rare, Entitled, and Dishonest: How Commonality of Creativity in One’s Group Decreases an Individual’s Entitlement and Dishonesty

**Abstract**
We examine when and why creative role identity causes entitlement and unethical behaviors, and how this relationship might be reduced. We found that the relationships among creative identity, entitlement, and dishonesty are contingent on the perception of creativity being rare. Four experiments showed that individuals with a creative identity reported higher psychological entitlement and engaged in more unethical behaviors. Additionally, when participants believed that their creativity was rare rather than common, they were more likely to lie for money. Moreover, manipulation of the rarity of creative identity, but not of practical identity, increased psychological entitlement and unethical acts. We tested for the mediating effect of psychological entitlement on dishonesty using both measurement of mediation and experimental causal chain approaches. We further provide evidence from organizations. Responses from a sample of supervisor–subordinate dyads demonstrated that employees reporting strong creative identities who perceived creativity as rare in their work group, rather than common, were rated as engaging in more unethical behaviors by their supervisors. This paper extends prior theory on negative moral consequences of creativity by shedding new light on assumptions regarding the prevalence of creativity and the role psychological entitlement plays.

**Research Question**
When and why does creative role identity lead individuals to feel entitled and engage in dishonest or unethical behavior? The paper asks whether this relationship depends on whether creativity is perceived as rare rather than common in the relevant group.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Creative identity or creative role identity, manipulated or measured across studies.
- DV(s): Psychological entitlement, behavioral dishonesty or theft, deceptive message choice, and supervisor-rated unethical behavior.
- Mediators: Psychological entitlement.
- Moderators: Perceived rarity or prevalence of creativity in the relevant group; comparison identity conditions such as logical or practical identity.

The mechanism is that creative identity can derive value from creativity being viewed as rare, special, and unique. When individuals see their creative identity as rare in their group, that perceived rarity increases psychological entitlement, which then increases willingness to lie, steal, or engage in unethical behaviors. When creativity is framed as common in the group, the entitlement pathway and the link between creative identity and dishonesty are reduced.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends creative role identity theory by showing that creative identity can have negative moral consequences, not only positive links to creative behavior. It distinguishes creative identity from creative ability: prior work emphasized cognitive flexibility and rationalization, whereas this study theorizes entitlement as the pathway from creative identity to dishonesty. It also contributes to identity theory by showing that altering a defining component of an identity, here the rarity of creativity, can change the identity's behavioral implications.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations that encourage creative identities should avoid framing creativity as a rare possession of a few special employees. The authors suggest emphasizing the common, everyday, and accessible nature of creativity so that employees can value creativity without inferring that they deserve special treatment. This is especially relevant for organizations or departments that label some roles as creative and others as noncreative.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the experimental studies may limit generalizability even though they provide control over the psychological mechanism. They also note that the field study is correlational, so causal ordering in that sample cannot be inferred strongly. Another limitation is that supervisors' judgments of unethical behavior may vary with how creative they perceive an employee to be.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the effect with different methods, samples, and manipulations of creative identity. The authors call for work comparing stable creative identity with primed creative identity and examining interactions between creative identity and creative ability. They also suggest studying cultural variation in the meaning of creative role identity and other rare identities or attributes, such as wealth, status, leadership, or hierarchical position.
