# When Justice Promotes Injustice: Why Minority Leaders Experience Bias When They Adhere to Interpersonal Justice Rules

**Abstract**
Accumulated knowledge on organizational justice leaves little reason to doubt that organizational members benefit when leaders adhere to interpersonal justice rules. However, upon considering how justice behaviors influence subordinates’ cognitive processes, we predict that interpersonal justice has a surprising, unintended negative consequence. Supervisors who violate interpersonal justice rules trigger subordinates to search for reasons why their supervisors are threatening them, causing subordinates to be more attuned to supervisors’ individual characteristics and therefore unlikely to use stereotypes when evaluating them. In contrast, supervisors who adhere to interpersonal justice rules allow subordinates to divert attention away from them, leading subordinates’ judgments of their supervisors to be influenced by stereotypes. Consistent with these predictions, using field data we found that minority supervisors faced bias relative to Caucasian supervisors when supervisors adhered to—but not when they violated— interpersonal justice rules. We replicated this effect in an experiment and established that it is explained by an alternating pattern of stereotype application and inhibition: the stereotype that minority supervisors are more deceitful than Caucasians influenced fairness judgments when supervisors adhered to—but not when they violated— interpersonal justice rules. We then conducted exploratory analyses and identified one factor (unit size) that mitigates this troubling pattern.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and why minority supervisors experience bias from subordinates despite adhering to interpersonal justice rules. It tests whether interpersonal justice reduces threat and attention to supervisors' individuating characteristics, thereby allowing stereotypes to influence fairness judgments and subordinate behavior toward minority supervisors.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Supervisor race and supervisor interpersonal justice rule adherence or violation.
- DV(s): Fairness perceptions, helping behavior toward supervisors, and undermining outcomes including blame-throwing, displays of distrust, and recommendation against supervisor bonus eligibility.
- Mediators: Perceptions of supervisor deceit and perceptions of overall fairness.
- Moderators: Interpersonal justice rule adherence or violation; exploratory analyses also examine unit size.

The mechanism is an alternating pattern of stereotype application and inhibition. When supervisors violate interpersonal justice rules, subordinates attend more closely to supervisors as possible sources of threat, which inhibits reliance on racial stereotypes. When supervisors adhere to interpersonal justice rules, subordinates feel less threat and divert attention elsewhere, making fairness judgments more vulnerable to stereotypes that minority supervisors are deceitful. These fairness judgments then translate into less helping and more undermining toward minority supervisors.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the assumption in organizational justice and social exchange perspectives that supervisors who treat subordinates well reliably receive better treatment in return. It adds an attention-based account of when fairness heuristics become vulnerable to stereotyping. It also extends research on racial bias by theorizing upward-directed bias from subordinates toward minority supervisors.

**Practical Implication**
The authors caution that minority supervisors face a lose-lose situation: violating interpersonal justice harms subordinates, but adhering to it can make stereotypes more influential. They suggest structural and situational interventions that increase subordinates' attention to supervisors' individuating characteristics, such as smaller units, proximity, one-on-one meetings, small group caucuses, and relationship-building routines. They also recommend that organizations supplement global fairness ratings with questions about specific justice behaviors.

**Limitations**
The Study 1 field design remains vulnerable to spurious effects and did not include multiple subordinates from each organization, limiting assessment of group and interpersonal dynamics. Study 2 allows causal inference but used an electronically mediated supervisor who was not physically present. The authors also examined Black and Hispanic supervisors together and did not use a within-person design in which individuals had multiple supervisors of different races.

**Future Research**
Future research can examine how bias may snowball through social contagion or be buffered by third parties who speak out against it. Scholars could test whether expectations for unjust behavior vary by supervisor race and whether lower expectations cause employees to perceive more injustice. The authors also call for more work on attention as a mechanism linking interpersonal justice, stereotyping, organizational structure, and other organizational outcomes.
