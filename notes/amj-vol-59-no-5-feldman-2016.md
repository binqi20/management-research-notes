# Dual Directors and the Governance of Corporate Spinoffs

**Abstract**
This paper investigates how “dual directors” enable firms that undertake corporate spinoffs to manage their post-spinoff relationships with the firms they divest, as well as the performance implications of dual directors serving simultaneously on these companies’ boards. While the presence of dual directors is positively associated with the average stock market returns of parent and spinoff firms, their presence is also increasingly positively associated with parent firm performance, but increasingly negatively associated with spinoff firm performance as the share of sales a spinoff firm makes to its parent firm rises. These findings show that, while dual directors give a parent firm power over its spinoff firm, dual directors only exercise that power at the spinoff firm’s expense when that company is highly dependent on its parent firm.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how dual directors enable firms undertaking corporate spinoffs to manage post-spinoff relationships with divested firms. It also asks what implications dual directors have for parent and spinoff firm performance, especially when spinoff firms depend on parent firms for sales.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Presence of one or more dual directors on parent and spinoff firm boards.
- DV(s): Value-weighted parent-spinoff stock returns, parent annual stock returns, and spinoff annual stock returns.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators.
- Moderators: Spinoff firm's sales dependence on the parent firm, measured by share of sales and large share of sales.

The mechanism is that dual directors give parent firms power over spinoff firms by recreating some governance capacity after a shift from hierarchy to market. Drawing on transaction cost economics, the paper argues that this power can coordinate post-spinoff relationships and be value-creating on average. Drawing on resource dependence theory, it argues that when the spinoff firm is highly dependent on the parent firm as a buyer, dual directors have both the ability and incentive to exercise power in ways that benefit the parent firm at the spinoff firm's expense.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops corporate spinoff governance as a distinct domain rather than treating parent and spinoff firms as independent after divestiture. It extends transaction cost economics by showing that governance mechanisms can enable one firm to exercise power over a market counterparty, not only safeguard against opportunism. It also uses resource dependence theory to specify when that power becomes redistributive rather than merely coordinative.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that parent firms can use dual directors to coordinate post-spinoff relationships and improve average parent-spinoff returns. They also warn that dual directors may create distributional concerns when the spinoff firm depends heavily on the parent firm for sales. Thus, the same governance mechanism can appear value-creating on average while redistributing value toward the parent in high-dependence spinoff relationships.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that it analyzes the static relationship between dual directors and performance rather than the dynamic evolution of dual director appointments and departures. It treats dual directors as a governance mechanism used by parent firms, while leaving their individual incentives, characteristics, and motivations largely unexplored. It also raises questions about generalizability beyond corporate spinoffs to other market-governed relationships where firms may appoint representatives to counterparties' boards.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine why dual directors leave parent boards, spinoff boards, or both, and how those departures affect parent and spinoff firms. Additional work could study how dual director characteristics shape corporate governance quality in spinoff firms, including the meaning of director independence in this context. Researchers could also test whether similar power-preserving board appointments appear in other scope-reducing deals, buyer-supplier exchanges, ecosystems, top management teams, or large-shareholder relationships.
