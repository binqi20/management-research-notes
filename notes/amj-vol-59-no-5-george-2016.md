**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Big data and data science can help management scholars produce better answers to existing questions and develop new questions by using greater data scope and data granularity.
- Key constructs: Big data; data science; data scope; data granularity; volume; velocity; variety.
- Data scope: The editorial reframes big data volume as the comprehensiveness of data available for examining a phenomenon, including wider variables, whole populations, and numerous observations.
- Data granularity: The editorial reframes data variety as theoretically proximal measurement, such as sensors, biometrics, messages, or digital traces rather than distal surveys or retrospective reports.
- Starter kit: The essay organizes practical guidance around data collection, data storage, data processing, data analysis, and reporting and visualization.
- Boundary conditions: The authors caution that the field is evolving rapidly and that privacy, data rights, construct validity, causal inference, robustness, and reporting transparency remain central challenges.

The process logic is editorial and methodological rather than empirical. George, Osinga, Lavie, and Scott argue that data science can expand management theory by improving the precision of answers to established questions and opening questions that were previously constrained by data access or analysis limits. They connect this opportunity to concrete methods, including sensors, web scraping, communications monitoring, SQL and NoSQL storage, text mining, variable selection, cross-validation, field experiments, parallelization, sequential updating, and visualization.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial does not develop a new named theory. Its contribution is to translate big data's volume, velocity, and variety into management-research concepts of data scope and data granularity, then explain how those concepts can support theory testing, theory development, refined units of analysis, and clearer attention to mechanisms.

**Practical Implication**
For management scholars, the editorial provides a starter kit for deciding how to collect, store, process, analyze, report, and visualize big data. It also advises authors to describe data sources, preprocessing, merging, model tuning, robustness checks, effect sizes, out-of-sample performance, and visualization choices carefully rather than treating large sample size or predictive validity as sufficient evidence.

**Limitations**
The authors state that data science methods are evolving rapidly and that the editorial can only offer a primer rather than a definitive methods manual. They also note practical and ethical limits around privacy laws, research ethics, data ownership, data reliability, data storage tradeoffs, causal inference, spurious correlations, and context-sensitive measurement.

**Future Research**
The editorial encourages scholars to use data scope and data granularity to revisit established management theories, generate more accurate answers, and pose new questions about mobility, communication, physical space, collaboration, decision making, social inclusion, public services, and organizational design. It also suggests that management research can enter a new phase as researchers adopt more refined units of analysis and use data science to illuminate mechanisms behind observed effects.
