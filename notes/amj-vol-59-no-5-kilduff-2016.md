# Whatever It Takes to Win: Rivalry Increases Unethical Behavior

**Abstract**
This research investigates the link between rivalry and unethical behavior. We propose that people will be more likely to engage in unethical behavior when competing against their rivals than when competing against non-rival competitors. Across an archival study and a series of experiments, we found that rivalry was associated with increased unsporting behavior, use of deception, and willingness to employ unethical negotiation tactics. We also explore the psychological underpinnings of rivalry in order to illuminate how it differs from general competition and why it increases unethical behavior. The data reveal a serial mediation pathway whereby rivalry heightens the psychological stakes of competition (by increasing actors’ contingency of self-worth and status concerns), which leads to the adoption of a stronger performance-approach orientation, which then increases unethical behavior. These findings highlight the importance of rivalry as a widespread, powerful, yet largely unstudied phenomenon with significant organizational implications. They also help to inform when and why unethical behavior occurs within organizations, and demonstrate that the effects of competition are dependent upon relationships and prior interactions between actors.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether actors engage in more unethical behavior when competing against rivals than when competing against non-rival competitors. It also asks what psychological mechanisms distinguish rivalry from general competition and explain its effect on unethical behavior.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Rivalry versus non-rival competition, operationalized through co-located Serie A soccer teams, university-rival counterpart assignment, manipulated rivalry history in a car-dealership negotiation, and recalled personal rivalry.
- DV(s): Yellow and red cards in professional soccer, deception in a decision game, deception in opening negotiation statements, and willingness to use ethically questionable negotiation tactics.
- Mediators: Contingency of self-worth, status concerns, and performance orientation; Study 4 tests serial mediation through psychological stakes and performance orientation.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is that rivalry adds relational and historical psychological stakes to competition beyond the objective stakes of winning. Competing against rivals increases the extent to which outcomes matter for actors' self-worth and relative status, which then encourages a stronger performance-approach orientation focused on outperforming the rival. That orientation makes actors more willing to use deception, unsporting conduct, and other unethical tactics to improve competitive outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to rivalry research by identifying a dark side of rivalry: it can increase unethical behavior, not only motivation and effort. It contributes to unethical behavior research by identifying rivalry as a relational antecedent and by showing that competition's effects depend on prior relationships and histories between actors. It also extends models of competition by separating rivalry from structural competition and specifying psychological stakes and performance orientation as explanatory mechanisms.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that rivalry can motivate effort while also increasing ethical risk. The paper suggests caution with structures that repeatedly pit similar and evenly matched employees against one another or overemphasize outperforming rival companies. It also recommends using objective data, explicit decision criteria, and outside perspectives to reduce the risk that rivalry distorts strategic and moral judgment.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Study 1 is archival, uses an indirect co-location measure of rivalry, and cannot definitively rule out objective stakes or referee judgment as alternative explanations. They also note that yellow and red cards are face-valid but imperfect measures of unethical behavior. Study 4 relies on self-report measures for threat and challenge rather than physiological assessment, and the empirical evidence focuses on individual behavior rather than direct organization-level misconduct.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether rivalry increases organization-level unethical behavior such as accounting fraud. The authors also call for work on different types of unethical behavior, including carryover effects beyond the rivalry domain and behavior less directly tied to performance goals. Additional research could examine rivalry's affective and physiological profiles, the deliberative versus automatic nature of its effects, other consequences of rivalry, and moderators that prevent rivalry from becoming harmful.
