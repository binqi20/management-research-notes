# Beginning’s End: How Founders Psychologically Disengage From Their Organizations

**Abstract**
Exit is a critical part of the entrepreneurial process. At the same time, research indicates that founders are likely to form strong identity connections to the organizations they start. In turn, when founders exit their organizations, the process of psychological disengagement might destabilize their identities. Yet, limited research addresses how founders experience exit or how they manage their identities during this process. Through a qualitative, inductive study of founders of technology-based companies, I developed a theoretical model of founder psychological disengagement that delineates how founder work orientations relate to the disengagement paths that founders follow when leaving one organization and starting another. In elaborating on theory of psychological disengagement, this study has implications for understanding the psychology of founders, how founders exit and begin again, and psychological disengagement more broadly.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how founders manage psychological disengagement from their organizations when they choose to leave them. It also examines how psychological disengagement processes influence how founders begin again.

**Mechanism Process**
- Focal process: Founder psychological disengagement during voluntary successful exit from one organization and movement toward another.
- Work orientations: Self-fortifying founders prioritize their own ideas, personal development, money, learning, and ego-gratification; stewarding founders prioritize people, organizational sustainability, customers, and social contribution.
- Portfolio path: Self-fortifying founders tend to use idea stockpiling and side business developing before physical departure, making anticipatory identification with the next venture more salient than deidentification from the current organization.
- Serial path: Stewarding founders tend to use empowering others and recruiting replacements before leaving, followed by idea searching after departure; deidentification and loss are more salient before anticipatory identification with the next venture.
- Emotional dynamics: The portfolio path is associated with excitement and enthusiasm around the next opportunity, whereas the serial path involves sadness, pain, loss, and later movement toward more positive emotions.

The process model links founders' work orientations to different behavioral tactics, identification dynamics, and emotional experiences during exit. Self-fortifying founders psychologically move forward by building attachment to future ideas before fully leaving the current organization. Stewarding founders psychologically separate by protecting the current organization, making room for successors, and only later searching for the next opportunity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops an experience-based process theory of psychological disengagement that integrates behavior, cognition, and emotion before and after physical exit. It extends identification and role-transition theory by showing how deidentification and anticipatory identification vary in timing and salience. It also contributes to entrepreneurship research by explaining differences between serial and portfolio habitual founders through founder work orientations rather than only formal patterns of ownership or involvement.

**Practical Implication**
Founders may need explicit preparation for the emotional challenges of disengagement, even when exit is financially or strategically successful. The paper suggests that founders, employees, venture capitalists, and other stakeholders should attend to founder work orientations because these orientations shape whether founders prioritize organizational continuity or move attention toward side businesses and future ventures.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on founders, who may have unusually strong identity connections to their organizations, so transferability to employees or other occupational roles is uncertain. It studies founder experience rather than the consequences for the organizations being left. The sample size also limited the paper's ability to examine demographic or organizational predictors of founder work orientations.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether the disengagement paths apply to other roles, occupations, and organizational-exit contexts. Longitudinal studies could examine how founders' self-fortifying and stewarding orientations shift across careers and organizations. Larger-sample research could investigate demographic, investor, cofounder, employee-competence, and organizational conditions associated with different orientations and tactics, and could assess consequences for both exited and newly started organizations.
