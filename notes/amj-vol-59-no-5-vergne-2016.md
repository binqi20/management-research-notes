# How do Firms Adapt? A Fuzzy-Set Analysis of the Role of Cognition and Capabilities in U.S. Defense Firms’ Responses to 9/11

**Abstract**
How do firms adapt? In recent years, this old question has attracted new answers—albeit partial ones. On the one hand, cognition researchers have emphasized managerial attention to environmental change as a key driver of adaptation. On the other hand, dynamic capabilities scholars have underscored the role of asset reconfigurations implemented amid shifting environments. However, the explanatory powers of the two perspectives have not yet been assessed comparatively. This mixed methods study uses fuzzy-set analyses of defense firms’ responses to 9/11 to model the two perspectives as potentially competing or complementary, while our findings suggest that neither dynamic capabilities nor superior cognition must be present for firms to adapt. Instead, we identify four types of adapters (anticipative, responsive, opportunistic, and decisive), as well as the possibility of strategic non-adaptation. These results lead us to reassess the cognition and capabilities literatures and to outline a new, integrative framework to explain adaptation.

**Research Question**
In an industry experiencing an environmental shift, how do managerial attention and asset reconfiguration contribute to firm adaptation or lack of adaptation? The paper asks whether cognition and dynamic capabilities explain adaptation as competing, complementary, necessary, or substitutable conditions.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / causal conditions: Defense dependence, attention timing, attention intensity, reconfiguration scope, and reconfiguration intensity.
- Outcome: Firm adaptation to post-9/11 industry conditions, calibrated from expert assessments.
- Process / paths: Four equifinal adaptation configurations: anticipative, responsive, opportunistic, and decisive.
- Non-adaptation path: Haphazard non-adaptation among low-defense-dependence firms with delayed, low-intensity attention and high-intensity but narrow reconfiguration.
- Boundary conditions: The U.S. defense sector is a research- and technology-intensive, business-to-government setting shaped by Pentagon demand and the post-9/11 environmental shift.

The process argument is conjunctural rather than linear: attention and reconfiguration matter in different combinations, and neither superior cognition nor dynamic capabilities alone is required for adaptation. Anticipative adapters use early, intense attention with relatively lower-intensity reconfiguration; responsive adapters reconfigure substantially after later attention; opportunistic adapters leverage existing assets; and decisive adapters respond with broader and more intense post-shift reconfiguration. Strategic non-adaptation is possible when managers may be aware of change but judge adaptation as a low priority given the firm's revenue profile.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates cognition and capabilities perspectives by comparing their explanatory power in the same empirical setting. It shows that adaptation can occur without superior cognition and without intensified dynamic capabilities, recasting these literatures as sometimes substitutable and sometimes complementary rather than universally necessary. It also contributes a taxonomy of adaptation and non-adaptation paths and argues that adaptation and non-adaptation are asymmetric outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that managers should not treat adaptation as a single best-practice recipe. Depending on dependence on the affected market segment and the timing of managerial attention, firms may adapt through early anticipation, broad reconfiguration, opportunistic redeployment of existing assets, or rapid decisive reconfiguration. It also cautions that large-scale reconfiguration concentrated in a narrow set of business units may not produce adaptation when attention is delayed and industry dependence is low.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the defense industry has distinctive features: it is research- and technology-intensive, dominated by government customers, and shaped by public tenders. They also note heterogeneity among sampled firms, including specialists, related diversifiers, and conglomerates, and acknowledge that some configurations group firms that may differ in corporate scope. The use of letters to shareholders captures top-management attention and may miss middle-manager cognition in industries where resource reallocation can occur without top-management approval.

**Future Research**
Future research could replicate the study in larger samples and add causal conditions such as politics or leadership change. The authors also call for studies of middle-manager cognition, especially in settings where resource reallocation may occur without top-management approval. Additional work could examine how cognition variables drive the choice between internal and external asset reconfiguration.
