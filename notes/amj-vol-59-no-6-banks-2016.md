# Management’s Science–Practice Gap: A Grand Challenge for All Stakeholders

**Abstract**
Despite multiple high-profile calls—across decades and from multiple stakeholders—to address the widening gap between science and practice, the relevance of research conducted in the management domain remains in question. To once again highlight this issue and, more importantly, identify solutions, we explore the grand challenge of the science–practice gap by applying stakeholder theory. Using a grounded theory approach, we conducted a series of interviews (n 5 38) and formed an interactive focus group with academics and practitioners (e.g., executives, entrepreneurs, government officials) in order to develop a set of theoretical models and propositions that extend stakeholder theory. We supplemented our inductive theory building approach with a survey of academics (n 5 828) and practitioners (n 5 939) and a qualitative content analysis to identify 22 grand challenges (i.e., eight shared, eight uniquely academic, and six uniquely practitioner). We discuss the theoretical and practical implications of our findings and illustrate multiple directions for future research to build permanent bonds—not just temporary links—between science and practice.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how management academics and practitioners can overcome collaboration challenges in knowledge creation and transfer, how information asymmetry and goal incongruence raise collaboration costs, and how grand challenges can reduce the science–practice gap. It also asks which grand challenges academics view as accomplished, which future challenges academics and practitioners identify, and how those challenges can be leveraged for collaboration.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: science–practice gap, information asymmetry, goal incongruence, collaboration costs, utility functions, grand challenges, knowledge creation, and knowledge transfer.
- Process / models: information asymmetry and goal incongruence increase collaboration costs; knowledge of utility functions and mutually valued grand challenges reduce those costs; lower collaboration costs enable knowledge creation with collaboration.
- Knowledge transfer: incentives and communication channels shape whether created knowledge is effectively transferred to management stakeholders.
- Empirical basis: grounded theory analysis produced theoretical models and propositions; survey-based qualitative content analysis identified 22 grand challenges, including overlapping academic and practitioner priorities.

The core process is that academics and practitioners often develop knowledge separately because they do not understand one another’s utility functions and do not always share aligned goals. Stakeholder theory is used to argue that reciprocal exchanges, mutual value creation, and attention to shared grand challenges can lower collaboration costs and create more valuable knowledge. The paper treats the science–practice gap as both a knowledge creation problem and a knowledge transfer problem.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends stakeholder theory by applying it to the management science–practice gap and by specifying how information asymmetry, goal incongruence, utility functions, grand challenges, and collaboration costs shape stakeholder collaboration. It contributes theoretical models and propositions for knowledge creation and knowledge transfer, arguing that collaboration between academics and practitioners can create more valuable knowledge by capitalizing on different perspectives and joint work.

**Practical Implication**
The paper argues that management academics, practitioners, business schools, and the Academy of Management should build more durable collaboration structures around shared grand challenges. It recommends practitioner-oriented communication outlets, better use of conferences and executive education, organizational signatories for collaborative research, and business-school reward systems that explicitly value practical impact.

**Limitations**
The authors identify sample-related limits: the academic respondents may not represent all active management researchers, and the practitioner survey respondents were largely mid-level U.S. managers. They also note unclear survey response rates, more practitioners than academics in the qualitative interviews and focus group, and a primarily for-profit framing that should be expanded.

**Future Research**
Future research should use stratified random samples across academic and practitioner stakeholder groups, roles, occupational tenure, and management subfields. The authors also encourage more targeted work on the science–practice gap in domains such as social entrepreneurship and sustainability, plus empirical efforts that use the identified grand challenges as bases for collaborative research.
