# Selective Regulator Decoupling and Organizations’ Strategic Responses

**Abstract**
Organizations often respond to institutional pressures by symbolically adopting policies and procedures but decoupling them from actual practice. Literature has examined why organizations decouple from regulatory pressures. In this study, we argue that decoupling occurs within regulatory agencies and results from a combination of conflicting institutional pressures, complex goals, and internal fragmentation. Further, regulatory decoupling is selective—that is, regulators fail to adequately enforce standards only for one set of organizations. Regulated organizations that benefit from selective regulatory decoupling use nonmarket strategies to maintain their favorable regulatory status, and, in the process, selectively decouple their norms in one organizational activity but not others. As an empirical context, we use the hospital industry in which regulators have to balance conflicting pressures to be tough on fraud while maintaining the community’s access to essential but unprofitable services, such as charity care and medical education. In response, hospital regulators selectively decouple and exhibit leniency in enforcement of mispricing practices toward “beneficent hospitals,” defined as hospitals that provide more charity care and medical education. In turn, beneficent hospitals selectively decouple their service and profit goals by providing unprofitable services to uninsured patients, while mispricing insured patients to earn higher reimbursements.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether regulatory agencies themselves decouple from the rules they are charged with enforcing, and whether this decoupling is selective across regulated organizations. It then asks how hospitals that benefit from selective regulatory leniency respond strategically through nonmarket activity and upcoding behavior.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Hospital beneficence, operationalized as percentage of indigent patients and resident-to-bed ratio; post-2000 exposure to observable regulatory leniency in additional tests.
- DV(s): Regulatory enforcement, measured by prosecution likelihood and log settlement amount; hospital upcoding, measured by DRG Ratio 79, DRG Ratio 144, and DRG Ratio 296.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Post-2000 period, used to test whether upcoding by beneficent hospitals became stronger after hospitals could observe regulator leniency; DRG incentive category distinguishes high-, medium-, and low-incentive coding opportunities.

The mechanism is that regulators face conflicting institutional pressures, complex goals, and fragmented internal structures. In health care, these pressures push regulators to balance fraud enforcement against community access, charity care, and medical education. Hospitals that provide these beneficent services receive more lenient enforcement and use nonmarket strategies to maintain that status. After observing regulatory leniency, beneficent hospitals selectively decouple service ethics from pricing ethics by continuing unprofitable services while increasing upcoding.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends decoupling theory by moving the locus of decoupling from regulated organizations to regulatory agencies themselves. It introduces selective regulator decoupling, in which regulators symbolically enforce rules for favored organizations while enforcing them more rigorously for others. It also links institutional theory and nonmarket strategy by showing how regulated organizations can respond to regulator decoupling by shaping the enforcement climate and selectively decoupling their own norms.

**Practical Implication**
The paper cautions that uniform enforcement can be difficult when regulators face real tradeoffs between fraud control and socially valued service provision. For regulators, reducing upcoding may require stronger fraud-control resources, better insulation from stakeholder pressure, and direct subsidies for organizations that provide socially important but underfunded services. For nonprofit hospitals, the findings show that nonmarket strategies and cost-shifting through upcoding may preserve beneficent services but create ethical and regulatory exposure.

**Limitations**
The enforcement analysis has missing settlement amounts for some settlement-hospital-years, which the authors address with additional tests but cannot fully eliminate as a data limitation. The empirical setting is California nonprofit hospitals and selected Medicare DRG families, so the evidence is strongest for health care reimbursement regulation rather than all regulatory domains.

**Future Research**
The discussion suggests examining selective regulatory decoupling in other domains where regulators balance enforcement against social, economic, or political costs, such as environmental regulation and financial regulation. Future research could also study how regulatory agency structure, stakeholder pressure, and social-norm change alter the boundary between symbolic and actual enforcement. Additional work could examine when subsidies or institutional redesign reduce the need for selective leniency.
