# Mobilization in the Internet Age: Internet Activism and Corporate Response

**Abstract**
Civil society’s inability to hold powerful businesses accountable in authoritarian regimes is a grand challenge in today’s global environment. We propose that the development of Internet activism provides a novel mechanism to pressure for corporate response in those societies. Internet activism is dispersed, fast moving, and interactive, and hence can effectively focus public attention and potentially undermine a company’s public image by generating social comparison. In addition, firms with public image vulnerability may experience magnified pressure from Internet activism, as well as more intense social comparison. We explore this framework in the setting of corporate donations made in the wake of the 2008 earthquake in the Sichuan Province of China, which triggered Internet activism that challenged corporations to contribute to the good of the community. Analysis based on 613 large publicly listed Chinese firms supports our framework.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether and how Internet activism can pressure large corporations to respond to social demands in authoritarian regimes where traditional civil society, organized protest, and press freedom are constrained. It examines this question through Chinese firms' donation responses after the 2008 Sichuan earthquake.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Internet activism pressure through online donation rankings and online articles on corporate donations; unfavorable ranking status; absence from donor rankings.
- DV(s): Speed of corporate donation response, modeled as the hazard rate of donation; supplementary total donation amount.
- Mediators: Not directly modeled as mediators; the theoretical process centers on public attention, social comparison, and corporate public image threat.
- Moderators: Public image vulnerability, including private ownership, real estate industry membership, high reputation, and executives' political appointments.

Internet activism is theorized to focus public attention rapidly, make corporate donation comparisons salient, and threaten firms' public image. Firms that compare unfavorably in online rankings, or that are vulnerable because of reputation, industry culpability, ownership, or political status, are expected to respond faster to preempt or repair image damage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a theory of Internet activism as a mobilizing structure that can pressure corporations in authoritarian contexts despite weak formal civil society channels. It extends social movement and organization research by identifying online rankings, rapid information diffusion, and social comparison as mechanisms through which dispersed Internet users can create corporate image threat and elicit firm response.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that firms operating in tightly controlled societies may still face rapid public pressure through online activism, especially when their public image is vulnerable. Managers should monitor online comparisons and recognize that delayed or inadequate responses to social demands can intensify reputational exposure.

**Limitations**
The authors note that they did not directly capture the interactive features of online media, such as comments or article forwarding, which may mean the study underestimates Internet activism's impact. They also study one form of Internet activism in one post-earthquake setting, limiting direct evidence about other online tactics and contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research can examine how newer online tactics, including microblogging and social-networked communication through platforms such as Twitter, Weibo, and WeChat, affect corporate responses. The paper also points to the need to study Internet activism's constraints, including government monitoring, false information, and the limits of online mobilization in tightly controlled settings.
