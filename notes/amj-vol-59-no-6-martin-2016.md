# Echoes of Our Upbringing: How Growing Up Wealthy or Poor Relates to Narcissism, Leader Behavior, and Leader Effectiveness

**Abstract**
We investigate how parental income during an individual’s upbringing relates to his or her effectiveness as a leader after entering an organization. Drawing on research on the psychological effects of income, social learning theory, and the integrative trait-behavioral model of leadership effectiveness, we propose a negative, serially mediated association between higher parental income and lower future leader effectiveness via high levels of narcissism and, in turn, reduced engagement in behaviors that are viewed as central to the leadership role. We test our model using multisource data collected from active soldiers in the United States Army. Results reveal that parental income exerts indirect effects on leadership effectiveness criteria because (a) parental income is positively related to narcissism as an adult, (b) narcissism relates negatively to engaging in task-, relational-, and change-oriented leadership behaviors, and (c) reduced engagement in these behaviors relates to lower leader effectiveness. Our investigation advances theory by identifying pathways through which parental income relates to the effectiveness of leaders in organizations, and by illuminating the origins of a trait—narcissism—that predicts the behavior and effectiveness of leaders.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how parental income during an individual’s upbringing is associated with later leadership effectiveness after entering an organization. It specifically examines whether higher parental income relates to higher adult narcissism, reduced relational-, task-, and change-oriented leadership behaviors, and lower follower-rated effectiveness and workgroup behavior outcomes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Parental income during upbringing, measured from USMA archival application data.
- DV(s): Follower-rated leader effectiveness, followers’ citizenship behaviors, and followers’ counterproductive behaviors.
- Mediators: Adult narcissism and leaders’ engagement in relational-, task-, and change-oriented leadership behaviors.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The proposed mechanism is serial and cross-level: higher parental income is theorized to foster self-sufficiency and self-focused behavioral learning, which is associated with higher narcissism in adulthood. Higher narcissism is then associated with lower engagement in relational-, task-, and change-oriented leadership behaviors. The serial indirect effects are significant for selected behavior-outcome paths: relational and change behaviors for effectiveness, change behavior for citizenship, and task and change behaviors for counterproductive behavior.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper connects research on parental income and the psychological consequences of income with leadership effectiveness theory. It extends the integrative trait-behavioral model by identifying parental income as a developmental antecedent of narcissism and by showing how that trait is associated with leadership behavior and outcomes. It also brings social learning and self-sufficiency arguments into an organizational leadership setting.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that organizations may benefit from attenuating the entitlement and grandiosity that can accompany some wealthy backgrounds, for example by eliciting compassion or valuing humility. They caution against selecting leaders on parental income, emphasizing instead that the negative pathways through narcissism and subsequent leadership behaviors should be mitigated.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the cross-sectional survey design prevents conclusive causal claims for some reported relationships. They also acknowledge possible omitted variables, same-source ratings for leadership behaviors and effectiveness outcomes, few participants in the highest income bracket, and the use of one strong-norm organization where ongoing leader-follower relationships may make narcissism especially costly.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine other pathways through which parental income relates to leadership, including potentially positive effects through educational opportunities, competencies, or technical abilities. Scholars could also study other leadership behaviors such as servant leadership, test the model in organizations with weaker norms or less frequent interaction, and assess whether narcissistic leaders fare differently in contexts where they are fast-tracked or where self-oriented behavior is viewed more favorably.
