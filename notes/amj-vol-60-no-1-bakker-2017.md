# Pull the Plug or Take the Plunge: Multiple Opportunities and the Speed of Venturing Decisions in the Australian Mining Industry

**Abstract**
Effectively capturing opportunities requires rapid decision making. We investigate the speed of opportunity-evaluation decisions by focusing on firms’ venture termination and venture advancement decisions. Experience, standard operating procedures, and confidence allow firms to make opportunity-evaluation decisions faster; we propose that a firm’s attentional orientation, as reflected in its project portfolio, limits the number of domains in which these speed-enhancing mechanisms can be developed. Hence, firms’ decision speed is likely to vary between different types of decisions. Using unique data on 3,269 mineral exploration ventures in the Australian mining industry, we find that firms with a higher degree of attention toward earlier-stage exploration activities are quicker to abandon potential opportunities in early development but slower to do so later, and that such firms are also slower to advance on potential opportunities at all stages compared to firms that focus their attention differently. Market dynamism moderates these relationships, but only with regard to initial evaluation decisions. Our study extends research on decision speed by showing that firms are not necessarily fast or slow regarding all the decisions they make, and by offering an opportunity-evaluation framework that recognizes that decision makers can—in fact, often do—pursue multiple potential opportunities simultaneously.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what determines the speed at which firms make opportunity-evaluation decisions when they are pursuing multiple potential opportunities at the same time. It focuses on whether a firm's attentional orientation toward earlier-stage exploration makes it faster or slower to terminate or advance ventures at prospecting and developing stages, and whether environmental dynamism changes those relationships.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Orientation toward earlier stages of opportunity advancement, proxied by the firm's venture portfolio; environmental dynamism; interaction between attentional orientation and environmental dynamism.
- DV(s): Decision speed, measured as duration until a mining venture is advanced or terminated at the prospecting stage or developing stage.
- Mediators: Not directly measured; the theorized speed-enhancing mechanisms are domain-specific experience, standard operating procedures, and confidence.
- Moderators: Environmental dynamism, with separate tests for prospecting-stage and developing-stage decisions.

The mechanism is stage- and decision-specific. Firms that focus attention on earlier-stage exploration develop experience, routines, and confidence for early fault detection, so they terminate prospecting ventures faster. The same orientation makes advancement decisions slower because advancement requires assessing upside potential, feasibility, scaling, and later-stage exploitation issues for which exploration-focused firms have weaker domain-specific routines and confidence. Environmental dynamism weakens or intensifies these effects at the initial prospecting stage because dynamic markets make existing knowledge and information less reliable, but the paper does not find significant moderation at the developing stage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends opportunity-evaluation research by moving beyond the assumption that firms evaluate one opportunity at a time. It shows that a firm's internal portfolio of active potential opportunities shapes how quickly it evaluates a focal venture, and it highlights prospecting, developing, and exploiting as analytically distinct stages.

The paper also contributes to decision-speed research by challenging the idea that firms are generally fast or slow decision makers. Decision speed is presented as domain-specific: attention determines where firms develop experience, standard operating procedures, and confidence, so the same firm can be quick for one kind of venturing decision and slow for another.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not assume that general decisiveness transfers automatically across all opportunity decisions. By focusing attention on the decision domains most relevant to the firm's future, managers can build experience, routines, and confidence for faster "pull the plug" or "take the plunge" decisions in those domains.

The findings also imply a tradeoff: a portfolio orientation that helps a firm rapidly terminate weak early-stage prospects may slow advancement decisions and later-stage termination decisions. Managers therefore need to align attention with the kinds of venture decisions where speed matters most.

**Limitations**
The study investigates one industry, mineral mining, so the authors cannot determine how far the findings generalize to other industries, especially creative R&D settings or ventures focused primarily on learning. Few ventures progressed all the way from initial prospect to operational mine during the observation period, which prevented analysis of sequences of multiple decisions across the full opportunity process.

The dependent variable uses yearly duration data, so finer-grained monthly data might reveal more precise decision-speed patterns. The authors also did not analyze decisions to start new exploration ventures, had relatively small advancement-event subsamples, lacked location novelty measures, and did not test whether faster decisions improved firm performance.

**Future Research**
Future research could test the framework in industries with faster venture progression or more dynamic portfolios, and could examine whether earlier decision speed affects later advancement or termination decisions. Studies using finer-grained duration data could clarify the timing of opportunity-evaluation decisions more precisely.

Additional work could examine decisions to start exploration ventures, distinguish new-to-firm from new-to-industry locations, and connect decision speed to performance. The authors specifically suggest examining whether speed itself matters or whether better fit between timing, the venture, and the environment is more important.
