# Moral Symbols: A Necklace of Garlic against Unethical Requests

**Abstract**
This paper investigates whether, by exposing superiors to moral symbols, subordinates can discourage their superiors from asking them to perform unethical acts. Findings from five laboratory studies and one organizational survey study demonstrated that exposure to moral symbols displayed by the subordinates dissuades superiors from both engaging in unethical behaviors themselves and asking their subordinates to engage in unethical behavior. This paper shows that the display of moral symbols leads to two main consequences: (1) the activation of the concept of morality and increases in individuals’ moral awareness to decrease unethical behavior, and (2) eliciting inferences about the moral character of the displayer to lower the likelihood of that person being subjected to unethical directives. Additionally, our findings demonstrate that moral symbols influence ethical decisions without provoking hidden backlash effects against those who display them. In sum, our findings show that followers can serve as a form of social influence to guide their leader’s behavior and reduce the occurrence of unethical acts in the workplace.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether subordinates can reduce superiors' unethical behavior and unethical requests by exposing them to moral symbols. It specifically examines whether moral symbols work through increased moral awareness in the superior and through inferences that the symbol-displaying subordinate has high moral character.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Exposure to moral symbols displayed by subordinates, including a moral quote, a moral-symbol avatar T-shirt, and religious icons in a workspace.
- DV(s): Superiors' own unethical behavior and their likelihood of asking the symbol-displaying subordinate to carry out an unethical directive.
- Mediators: Moral awareness for the general reduction in unethical behavior; perceived high moral character of the displayer for reduced unethical directives toward that subordinate.
- Moderators: Ethical dilemma versus no-dilemma task context in Study 3; otherwise no core individual-difference moderator is theorized.

Moral symbols operate as subtle social influence cues. They make morality more cognitively accessible and increase superiors' moral awareness, which lowers unethical behavior in general. In interpersonal contexts, they also lead superiors to infer that the displayer has high moral character; this makes superiors less willing to direct unethical requests toward that particular subordinate. The studies distinguish this protective effect from simple avoidance by showing that moral symbols did not reduce requests in a no-dilemma condition.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts behavioral ethics and ethical leadership research from a primarily top-down account toward a bottom-up account of how followers can shape leaders' ethical behavior. It contributes a dual-process explanation of moral symbols by separating a general moral-awareness pathway from a displayer-specific perceived-moral-character pathway. It also extends moral identity and social influence research by showing that moral displays can regulate others' ethical behavior, not only the displayer's own behavior.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that employees may have a low-conflict, preemptive way to reduce unethical requests from superiors by displaying morally meaningful symbols. For organizations, subtle moral cues may complement formal ethics programs and codes of conduct. The studies also suggest that such displays did not produce detectable backlash in the tested settings, although the authors caution that broader workplace consequences require further study.

**Limitations**
Several studies were laboratory experiments using controlled scenarios, and the field evidence came from subordinate-supervisor dyads in India, so generalizability to Western field settings remains open. The experimental unethical behaviors were largely pecuniary forms of deception or fraudulent reporting, leaving uncertainty about whether moral symbols would reduce discrimination, harassment, or other workplace misconduct. The authors also note possible collateral damage, because unethical superiors who avoid the moral-symbol displayer might redirect unethical requests toward other employees.

**Future Research**
Future research should test moral symbols in additional field settings, especially Western organizational contexts, and examine a wider range of unethical behaviors. The authors call for work on other forms of moral symbols, morality-related personality moderators, and why perceived subordinate morality deters unethical requests. They also encourage study of authenticity and impression management in moral-symbol displays, including whether strategic or inauthentic displays work differently from naturally occurring ones.
