# Green by Comparison: Deviant and Normative Transmutations of Job Search Envy in a Temporal Context

**Abstract**
We propose a novel temporal-based theory of how a painful social comparative emotion—job search envy—transmutes as deviant or normative job search behaviors (resume fraud or search effort). We theorize that as job searches progress across time or discrete events, temporal-based pressure increases via perceptions that situations are less changeable or more critical, propelling envious job seekers toward deviant rather than normative search behavior. We propose that market-based pressure, deriving from employment opportunity perceptions, further moderates these effects. In a first study of unemployed job seekers, after more search time passes, job search envy relates to deviant search behavior. Market pressure further qualifies this relationship, although contrary to our prediction, lower market pressure exacerbates rather than attenuates the relationship. Study 2, a two-year study of graduate students engaged in internship and full-time job searches, focuses on event-based temporal pressure and mostly replicates the Study 1 findings. It also indicates that under lower event and market pressures, job seekers expend more effort but do not commit resume fraud in response to job search envy. Overall, we conclude that job search envy transmutes differently depending on temporaland market-based contingencies and discuss future research possibilities.

**Research Question**
How does job search envy translate into deviant or normative job-search behavior as temporal pressure and perceived employment opportunities change? The paper asks whether envy leads job seekers toward resume fraud or greater search effort under different clock-based, event-based, and market-based search pressures.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Job search envy.
- DV(s): Resume fraud and job search effort, operationalized partly as employment applications submitted.
- Mediators: Challenge-oriented versus threat-oriented envy lenses are theorized but not directly measured as mediators.
- Moderators: Temporal-based pressure, measured as job search length in Study 1 and internship versus post-graduation search event in Study 2; market-based pressure, measured as perceived employment opportunities; and their interactions.

The proposed mechanism is that upward social comparison during job search produces envy, and the pressure context shapes whether that envy is appraised as a challenge or a threat. Under higher temporal pressure, search situations seem less changeable or more critical, making resume fraud a more likely response. Under lower temporal pressure, especially when opportunities appear plentiful, envy is more likely to be channeled into increased search effort.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces job search envy as a socio-emotional link between social comparisons and job-search behavior. It extends envy and job-search theory by showing that envy can transmute into both deviant and normative behaviors, and that this transmutation depends on clock-based time, event criticality, and perceived employment opportunities. It also adds a temporal lens to social comparison theory by showing that the same comparative emotion can have different behavioral associations at different stages of a search.

**Practical Implication**
Recruiters should recognize that job seekers may be more prone to resume fraud when envy coincides with longer searches or more critical search events, even when job markets appear favorable. Career counselors and placement offices can use this insight to help job seekers acknowledge envy, maintain self-worth, and redirect comparative pressure into legitimate search effort rather than resume embellishment. The supplementary findings also suggest that internship resume screening may require particular care.

**Limitations**
The resume fraud measures are self-reported, so fraud may be underreported or shaped by willingness to disclose unethical behavior. Study 2 had a smaller-than-planned sample and missing data across the five survey waves, making its search-effort findings less robust than the replicated resume-fraud pattern. The paper also theorizes changeability, criticality, and challenge-versus-threat appraisals but does not directly measure these mediating processes in the main studies.

**Future Research**
Future work should directly test the proposed mediating processes, including perceived changeability, event criticality, and challenge-versus-threat appraisal. Researchers should examine objective or detected resume fraud alongside self-reports, while recognizing that detected fraud may miss successful deception. The authors also call for extending the temporal model to other pressured goal-striving contexts and for qualitative work on how job seekers choose, change, or avoid social comparison referents.
