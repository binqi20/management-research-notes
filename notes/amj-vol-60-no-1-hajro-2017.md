# Knowledge Exchange Processes in Multicultural Teams: Linking Organizational Diversity Climates to Teams’ Effectiveness

**Abstract**
We developed a model illuminating team knowledge exchange processes as a key link between organizational diversity climate and the effectiveness of multicultural teams (MCTs). Our analysis is based on 143 in-depth interviews and extensive observations of team interactions that occurred in 48 teams from 11 companies. Our findings revealed that teams that oscillated between assertive and cooperative knowledge exchange processes were more effective. We also found such dual processes were more prevalent in organizations that had an engagement-focused diversity climate characterized by utilization of diversity to inform and enhance work processes based on the assumption that cultural differences give rise to different knowledge, insights, and alternative views. Based on our findings we developed specific propositions about optimizing MCT knowledge-exchange processes to guide future research and practice.

**Research Question**
How does a multinational corporation's diversity climate influence knowledge exchange processes in culturally diverse teams? How do these knowledge exchange processes influence team effectiveness?

**Mechanism Process**
- Organizational contexts: Policy-focused, awareness-focused, and engagement-focused diversity climates in multinational corporations.
- Linking processes: Navigating differences, coping with exceptions, and legitimizing inflexibility or flexibility.
- Knowledge exchange modes: Assertive exchange, cooperative exchange, and oscillation between assertive and cooperative exchange.
- Emergent team features: Quality of relationships, goal accomplishment, and task processes.
- Boundary conditions: Diversity climate shapes but does not mechanically determine team effectiveness; some teams deviate from their dominant organizational climate through local team practices and leadership behavior.

The process theorized in the paper is cross-level. Organizational diversity climates encourage different ways of navigating cultural differences, responding to norm exceptions, and setting limits on flexibility. Policy-focused climates tend to prompt assertive exchange that surfaces differences but can create distrust, low goal acceptance, and inefficient task work. Awareness-focused climates tend to prompt cooperative exchange that protects harmony but can hide conflict, obscure information flow, and weaken task focus. Engagement-focused climates are most likely to support oscillation between direct assertion and cooperative openness, which helps teams build healthier relationships, clarify goals, and complete work effectively.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a qualitative theory of diversity climate in multinational corporations, where national cultural diversity rather than only domestic race or gender diversity is central. It clarifies a cross-level link between organizational diversity climates and multicultural team effectiveness by identifying knowledge exchange processes as the intervening team process. It also challenges the assumption that cooperation alone is best for multicultural teams, arguing that effective teams need oscillation between cooperative and assertive knowledge exchange.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of multicultural teams should not rely only on formal antidiscrimination policies or generic harmony-building practices. The findings suggest that teams need climates and leadership practices that let members voice culturally grounded knowledge directly while also preserving openness, respect, and willingness to adapt. Organizations should build engagement-focused diversity climates that connect cultural differences to core work processes, not merely to representation, compliance, or interpersonal goodwill.

**Limitations**
The paper does not quantitatively test the proposed model, so the mediation logic remains theory generated from qualitative evidence rather than statistically estimated. The study keeps the home-country context constant by focusing on companies based in Austria, which produced a disproportionate number of Austrian respondents and may limit transferability. The authors also note that other regions, nationalities, corporate contexts, team contexts, task types, and contextual factors such as power dynamics, control mechanisms, and corporate strategies may shape the observed processes.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the model with broader samples of organizations and teams and use statistical mediation models to examine whether diversity climate and multicultural team effectiveness are related through knowledge exchange. Studies should examine additional regions, nationalities, corporate contexts, team contexts, and team tasks, including tasks requiring different levels of innovation. The paper also calls for work on contextual factors such as power, control, strategy, time perspectives, other team interaction processes, and the reverse possibility that multicultural teams may reshape organizational context.
