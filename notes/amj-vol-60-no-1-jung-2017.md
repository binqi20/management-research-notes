# How Do Entrepreneurial Founding Teams Allocate Task Positions?

**Abstract**
How do founding team members allocate task positions when launching new ventures? Answering this question is important because prior work shows both that founding team members often have correlated expertise, thus making task position allocation problematic; and initial occupants of task positions exert a lingering effect on venture outcomes. We draw on status characteristics theory to derive predictions on how co-founders’ specific expertise cues and diffuse status cues drive initial task position allocation. We also examine the performance consequences of mismatches between the task position and position occupant. Qualitative fieldwork, combined with a quasi-experimental simulation game and an experiment, provides causal tests of the conceptual framework. We find that co-founders whose diffuse status cues of gender (male), ethnicity (white), or achievement (occupational prestige or academic honors) indicated general ability were typical occupants of higher-ranked positions, such as chief executive officer role, within the founding team. In addition, specific expertise cues that indicated relevant ability predicted task position allocation. Founding teams created more financially valuable ventures when task position occupants’ diffuse status cues were typical for the position; nonetheless position occupants with high diffuse status cues also appropriated more of the created value. Our results inform both entrepreneurship and status characteristics literature.

**Research Question**
Which co-founder initially occupies each formal task position when an entrepreneurial founding team launches a new venture? The paper asks how specific expertise cues and diffuse status cues shape this allocation, and how fit or mismatch between a task position and its occupant affects venture value creation and individual value appropriation.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Specific expertise cues for CEO, CFO, CMO, and COO positions; diffuse status cues, including ascribed cues such as gender and ethnicity and achieved cues such as academic honors and prestigious prior employers; team functional background homogeneity; and team fit between task positions and occupants' diffuse status cues.
- DV(s): Allocation to CEO, CFO, CMO, COO, or idiosyncratic positions; ordered task-position rank; venture valuation; individual share of the founder equity pool; and Study 2 CEO/CMO appropriateness ratings.
- Mediators: Status conflict and transactive memory are theorized as venture-level mechanisms, with post-hoc subsample evidence for transactive memory; double standards and attribution processes are theorized for individual value appropriation.
- Moderators: Congruence between specific expertise cues and diffuse status cues, team functional diversity, and the Study 2 interaction between experimentally manipulated specific expertise and gender-based diffuse status.

The mechanism is that founding teams use observable cues to infer competence under uncertainty. Specific expertise cues indicate task-relevant ability for a particular role, while diffuse status cues signal generalized ability and make some co-founders seem more fitting for higher-ranked positions. When role occupants match widely shared status expectations, the team experiences less status conflict and creates more financial value, but high-status occupants also receive more credit and appropriate a larger share of the created value.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconceptualizes entrepreneurial founding teams as varying not only in horizontal expertise differences but also in vertical status differences among otherwise peer co-founders. It applies status characteristics theory to initial role allocation in founding teams and shows that specific expertise cues and diffuse status cues jointly structure who occupies formal task positions. It also extends position-imprinting and new-venture-team research by arguing that the typicality of initial occupants' diffuse status cues can affect both venture performance and later stratification.

**Practical Implication**
The findings show that title and task-position allocation is consequential rather than a neutral reflection of expertise. Teams may create more value when members accept a hierarchy that others perceive as typical, but this same process can reproduce unequal credit and value appropriation for members with higher diffuse status cues. Role assignments in founding teams can therefore carry gender, ethnicity, and prestige cues in addition to task-relevant expertise.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the causal leverage of the simulation and experiment comes with external-validity limits because the main quantitative evidence uses postgraduate business-school participants rather than a large field sample of operating ventures. Study 1 is a quasi-experimental simulation rather than naturally occurring venture formation, and Study 2 uses scenario ratings rather than real role-allocation decisions. The evidence for reduced status conflict through transactive memory is also post-hoc and based on a smaller subsample.

**Future Research**
The authors call for large-sample field studies of founding teams in varied settings to establish the external validity of their findings. They suggest examining task-position reallocation as venture requirements change, including whether reallocations occur with founder entry or exit and how they relate to organizational inertia. They also propose future work on whether formal titles convey specific task expertise, diffuse status, or both to external stakeholders.
