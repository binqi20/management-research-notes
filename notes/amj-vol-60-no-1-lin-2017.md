# Why is Underemployment Related to Creativity and OCB? A Task-Crafting Explanation of the Curvilinear Moderated Relations

**Abstract**
Based on the job-crafting perspective, we theorized a serial curvilinear mediated moderation model that links underemployment to two outcomes that benefit the organization: creativity and organizational citizenship behavior. A three-waved time-lagged survey of teachers and a field study of technical workers provided convergent support for this model. In Study 1, using data from 327 teachers and their immediate supervisors, we found support for our hypotheses that perceived underemployment had an inverted U-shaped relationship with task crafting and that this relationship was moderated by organizational identification. When the teachers’ organizational identification was high, they engaged in more task crafting for the organization at intermediate levels of perceived underemployment. We also found that task crafting was positively related to creativity and organizational citizenship behavior. In Study 2, the simulation tasks for 297 technical workers provided convergent evidence for the idea that objective underemployment indirectly influences objective task crafting through perceived underemployment with the inverted U-shaped path from perceived underemployment to task crafting. We discussed the theoretical and practical implications of this research.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why underemployment can be related to positive organizational outcomes rather than only negative employee reactions. Specifically, it examines whether objective underemployment shapes perceived underemployment, whether perceived underemployment has an inverted U-shaped relationship with task crafting, and whether organizational identification moderates that perceived-underemployment-to-task-crafting relationship before task crafting relates to creativity and OCBO.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Objective underemployment and perceived underemployment, including the squared term for perceived underemployment.
- DV(s): Study 1 supervisor-rated creativity and OCBO; Study 2 objective task crafting in a simulation exercise.
- Mediators: Perceived underemployment links objective underemployment to task crafting; task crafting links the curvilinear underemployment process to creativity and OCBO.
- Moderators: Organizational identification moderates the curvilinear relationship between perceived underemployment and task crafting.

The mechanism is that employees who perceive low to moderate underemployment still see room to repair their positive self-image by crafting tasks that better use their surplus capacity. When perceived underemployment becomes too high, the gap between capacity and job requirements appears too large, so task-crafting effort declines. Organizational identification keeps employees oriented toward benefiting the organization, making task crafting more likely at intermediate levels of perceived underemployment and, in turn, supporting creativity and OCBO.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to underemployment research by showing that underemployment can have positive organizational consequences under bounded conditions, rather than producing only withdrawal, deviance, or dissatisfaction. It introduces task crafting as an active coping mechanism that translates perceived underemployment into creativity and OCBO. It also extends job-crafting theory by identifying underemployment as an antecedent of task crafting and by linking task crafting to employee creativity and organizational citizenship behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not assume that underemployed or overqualified employees will always respond negatively. The findings suggest that employees with low to moderate underemployment can use surplus capacity productively when managers support task crafting and foster organizational identification. At the same time, large discrepancies between employee capacity and job requirements remain risky because the inverted U-shaped pattern implies that task-crafting effort declines when perceived underemployment becomes too high.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Study 1's time-lagged design cannot establish causality unequivocally across all paths. Both studies used Chinese employees, so labor-market transition, power distance, collectivism, and task-crafting latitude may shape the observed relationships. The paper also did not directly test attribution processes or determine how long task crafting as a coping response can be sustained under prolonged underemployment.

**Future Research**
Future work could use longitudinal field studies or experiments to establish causal direction more rigorously. Research could test whether power distance, collectivism, and other cultural conditions change the underemployment-task crafting-creativity and OCBO pathways. The authors also call for research on attribution, the durability of task-crafting responses over time, and settings where jobs are too tightly constrained to allow task crafting.
