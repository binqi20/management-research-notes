**Mechanism Process**
- Editorial transition: Shaw marks the official transition to AMJ's 21st editorial team and thanks the AOM membership, authors, reviewers, prior editors, and outgoing editorial team.
- Team structure: The editorial introduces a deputy-editor structure, 18 associate editors, qualitative and quantitative editorial streams, a 320-member editorial review board, and managing-editor support.
- Mission implementation: The team aims to publish high-quality, innovative, bold, and inclusive empirical papers while being open to methods, levels of analysis, disciplinary origins, and national contexts.
- Review process: Shaw describes a three-tier process for matching manuscripts to editors and emphasizes a review process that is developmental, inclusive, rigorous, and fair.
- Outreach and theme: The team plans outreach to research communities and regions underserved by AMJ, and frames "new ways of seeing" as an invitation to develop fresh theoretical positions, assumptions, and frameworks for management topics.

The process logic is editorial rather than empirical. Shaw uses the change in editorial leadership to connect AMJ's mission, team design, review operations, outreach activities, and the New Ways of Seeing theme into a three-year agenda for the journal.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial does not develop a new theory. Its contribution is to articulate AMJ's editorial stance on theoretical contribution, arguing that novelty, interestingness, boldness, knowledge accumulation, and theory-driven quantitative research can coexist within the journal's mission.

**Practical Implication**
For authors, the editorial implies that AMJ submissions should foreground well-situated theory, rigorous methods, and management relevance rather than relying on patterns of supportive results. For reviewers and editors, it emphasizes developmental, inclusive, rigorous, and fair reviewing, timely turnaround, and outreach to broaden the journal's research communities.

**Future Research**
The editorial encourages future AMJ research to offer "new ways of seeing" management issues by using frameworks, perspectives, and lenses from outside the management and organizations literature and perhaps outside common base disciplines such as psychology, sociology, and economics. It also points authors toward empirical submissions for the New Ways of Seeing Special Research Forum, including qualitative, quantitative, field, laboratory, meta-analytic, and mixed methods designs.
