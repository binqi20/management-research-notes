# Effects of Employees’ Positive Affective Displays on Customer Loyalty Intentions: An Emotions-as-Social-Information Perspective

**Abstract**
Employees’ positive affective displays have been widely used as a strategic tool to enhance service experience and strengthen customer relationships. Companies have primarily focused their employee training programs on two dimensions of display: intensity and authenticity. Yet there is limited research on when, how, and why these two dimensions affect customer reactions. Drawing on the emotions as social information (EASI) framework (Van Kleef, 2009), we develop a conceptual model in which display intensity and display authenticity differentially influence customer loyalty by changing customers’ affective reactions and cognitive appraisals. Further, we propose that the relative impact of either dimension depends on customers’ motivation to understand the environment deeply and accurately (i.e., their epistemic motivation). We tested our model in one field study and one laboratory study. Results across these two studies provide consistent support for the proposed model and advance our understanding about how different dimensions of employees’ positive affective displays enhance customer reactions. Thus, findings of this research contribute to knowledge on the interpersonal effects of emotions in customer–employee interactions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when, how, and why the intensity and authenticity of employees’ positive affective displays shape customer responses in service encounters. It tests whether intensity and authenticity affect customer loyalty intentions through different psychological routes, and whether customers’ epistemic motivation changes the relative strength of those routes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employees’ positive affective display intensity and display authenticity.
- DV(s): Customer loyalty intentions.
- Mediators: Customers’ affective reactions and customers’ cognitive appraisals of employee service performance.
- Moderators: Customers’ epistemic motivation, modeled as conditioning the indirect paths from affective reactions and cognitive appraisals to loyalty intentions.

The mechanism is grounded in the EASI framework. Display intensity works mainly through the affective route: intense positive displays make customers feel more positive through emotional contagion, which increases loyalty intentions, especially when epistemic motivation is low. Display authenticity works mainly through the cognitive route: authentic positive displays lead customers to infer better employee service performance, which increases loyalty intentions, especially when epistemic motivation is high. The field and laboratory studies provide convergent support for this moderated mediation logic.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to emotional labor and service interaction research by separating two dimensions of the same positive emotional display: intensity and authenticity. It shows that both matter, but through different mechanisms and for different customers, rather than treating “service with a smile” as a single undifferentiated signal.

It also extends the EASI model beyond discrete emotions and beyond settings with shared outcomes, applying it to customer-employee service encounters. The paper demonstrates that dimensions of one emotion can convey different social information and that receivers’ epistemic motivation determines whether affective reactions or cognitive appraisals dominate.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not assume that one display rule fits all service contexts. In routine or low-involvement contexts where customers are less motivated to scrutinize employee emotions, clear requirements for frequent and intense positive displays may be easier to train, monitor, and reward.

When customers are highly motivated to interpret the service encounter, such as in unfamiliar, personalized, or consequential services, authenticity becomes more important. In those settings, firms may benefit more from recruiting employees with genuine positive emotionality, training deep acting, and empowering frontline employees than from relying only on scripted smiles.

**Limitations**
The field study improves external validity but cannot fully control ambient conditions, interaction dynamics, or causal ordering, and it relies on observer ratings of affective displays rather than customers’ immediate perceptions. The laboratory study improves control but uses video-simulated service encounters, which may not fully reproduce real customer-employee interactions.

The paper also studies a limited set of affective displays, mainly smiling and related positive expressive behaviors, and acknowledges that intensity and authenticity are distinct but correlated. The country or regional location of the empirical samples is not reported in the extracted text, and cultural variation in emotion encoding, decoding, and epistemic motivation remains untested.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other nonverbal behaviors in service interactions, including attentiveness, mimicry, rapport-building, and common grounding. It could also test situational antecedents of epistemic motivation, such as power, service familiarity, purchase involvement, relationship strength, and time pressure.

Additional work should examine cultural variation in how customers interpret employee affective displays and whether in-group or out-group status changes epistemic motivation. Future studies could also test alternative paths in which intensity affects cognitive appraisals or authenticity affects affective reactions under specific service conditions.
