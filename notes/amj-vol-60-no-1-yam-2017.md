# From Good Soldiers to Psychologically Entitled: Examining When and Why Citizenship Behavior Leads to Deviance

**Abstract**
Research has consistently demonstrated that organizational citizenship behaviors (OCBs) produce a wide array of positive outcomes for employees and organizations. Recent work, however, has suggested that employees often engage in OCBs not because they want to but because they feel they have to, and it is not clear whether OCBs performed for external motives have the same positive effects on individuals and organizational functioning as do traditional OCBs. In this article, we draw from self-determination and moral licensing theories to suggest a potential negative consequence of OCB. Specifically, we argue that when employees feel compelled to engage in OCB by external forces, they will subsequently feel psychologically entitled for having gone above and beyond the call of duty. Furthermore, these feelings of entitlement can act as moral credentials that psychologically free employees to engage in both interpersonal and organizational deviance. Data from two multisource field studies and an online experiment provide support for these hypotheses. In addition, we demonstrate that OCB-generated feelings of entitlement transcend organizational boundaries and lead to deviance outside of the organization.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and why organizational citizenship behavior can lead employees to engage in later deviance rather than only producing positive individual and organizational outcomes. It specifically tests whether OCB performed under external motivation increases psychological entitlement and, through that entitlement, interpersonal, organizational, and nonwork deviance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Organizational citizenship behavior and, in Study 3, OCB recall versus control recall.
- DV(s): Interpersonal deviance, organizational deviance, and nonwork deviance; psychological entitlement is also modeled as the proximal outcome in the first-stage interaction.
- Mediators: Psychological entitlement.
- Moderators: External motivation toward OCB, modeled as the condition under which OCB increases psychological entitlement and subsequent deviance.

The mechanism integrates self-determination theory with moral licensing theory. When employees perform OCB because they feel externally compelled, they construe the extra-role behavior as underrewarded and develop psychological entitlement. That entitlement functions like a moral credential, making later deviance feel psychologically permissible. Across the three studies, the OCB-to-deviance indirect effect through entitlement appears mainly when external motivation toward OCB is high, and Studies 2 and 3 extend the effect to deviance outside the workplace.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends self-determination theory by showing that controlled motivation for a seemingly prosocial workplace behavior can generate negative attitudinal and behavioral consequences. It also contributes to moral licensing and behavioral ethics research by identifying psychological entitlement as a mediating route through which externally motivated good deeds can license later deviance.

The study further clarifies the coexistence of OCB and counterproductive behavior. Rather than treating citizenship and deviance as simple opposites, it shows that the motive for citizenship behavior changes whether OCB is followed by entitlement and bad behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize the tradeoff in pressuring, rewarding, or otherwise externally inducing employees to perform OCB. Such inducements may increase citizenship behavior, but they may also make employees feel entitled and psychologically licensed to behave deviantly at work or outside work.

The paper advises organizations to reduce reliance on compliance-based or extrinsic OCB inducements and instead cultivate a culture that emphasizes the intrinsic value of good citizenship behavior. If extrinsic rewards for OCB are used, organizations should also communicate ethical standards, monitor conduct, and reinforce ethical behavior.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that OCB and deviance each have many behavioral forms, and specific forms of citizenship or deviance may not follow the same pattern as the general measures used in these studies. Although the studies triangulate across China, the United States, and an online experimental sample, culture is not directly modeled as a factor.

The authors also acknowledge that social exchange alternatives are not fully exhausted, because Study 3 controls for one social exchange indicator but does not measure all possible fairness or source-specific imbalance explanations. For cross-domain deviance, the studies do not directly measure whether employees reconstrue the nonwork deviance itself as morally permissible, so the credentialing interpretation remains inferential.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether different types of OCB, such as relational versus task-focused or affiliative versus challenging citizenship, have different consequences when externally motivated. It should also test other boundary conditions that determine whether employees use a moral license at work or in nonwork domains, including job attitudes, identity orientation, and work-home boundary permeability.

The authors encourage work on other prosocial workplace behaviors, such as constructive voice, as possible antecedents of similar entitlement and licensing dynamics. They also call for research that examines moral credits, moral credentials, and psychological entitlement together, including alternative operationalizations of moral credentials.
