# Why and When Does the Gender Gap Reverse? Diversity Goals and the Pay Premium for High Potential Women

**Abstract**
Abundant research has documented a gender pay gap; women earn less than men, all else being equal. Against the backdrop of an overall female penalty, we propose that the widespread adoption of diversity goals in organizations creates a female premium for certain women. We integrate the economic principle of supply and demand with theory from the field of strategic human resource management and theorize that individuals perceive high-potential women—who have the abilities needed to reach the upper echelons of organizations, where women remain underrepresented—as more valuable for achieving organizational diversity goals than high-potential men and, in turn, reward them with higher pay. Two field studies (Studies 1 and 3) and two laboratory experiments (Studies 2 and 4) reveal a female premium that is unique to high-potential women (Studies 1 and 2), driven by perceptions that high-potential women have more diversity value than high-potential men (Studies 2 and 4), and larger in contexts where diversity goals are stronger (Studies 3 and 4). Our theory and findings challenge the assumption that the gender pay gap uniformly disadvantages women and offer new insight into why and when the female penalty reverses and becomes a female premium.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why a female pay premium emerges against the backdrop of an overall gender pay penalty and when the gender gap reverses. It tests whether organizational diversity goals create demand for high-potential women, whether perceived diversity value explains pay rewards, and whether the premium grows stronger when diversity goals are stronger.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee gender, employee potential, and the strength or context of organizational diversity goals.
- DV(s): Pay rewards, total compensation, and career rewards.
- Mediators: Diversity value perceptions; in Study 4, retention perceptions further link diversity value to rewards.
- Moderators: Potential moderates the gender-pay relationship; industry and manipulated diversity goals moderate the female premium among high-potential employees.

The mechanism is cross-level. Organizational diversity goals and the scarcity of women in upper echelons increase perceived demand for high-potential women. Managers and evaluators therefore see high-potential women as more valuable for achieving diversity goals, infer stronger retention expectations, and allocate higher pay or rewards. The premium is bounded: it appears for high-potential women, not low-potential women, and is larger where diversity goals are stronger.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the assumption that the gender pay gap uniformly disadvantages women by showing that a female penalty can reverse for high-potential women. It contributes a mechanism, perceived diversity value, that explains why a premium can arise, and a boundary condition, high potential, that explains when it arises. It also develops a meso-level account linking macro organizational diversity goals to micro evaluator perceptions and pay decisions.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that pay-equity audits should not only compare men and women overall, because an aggregate female penalty can mask a high-potential female premium. Organizations should examine pay by potential rating and job level, and should clarify whether diversity value is an appropriate factor in reward decisions. The paper also warns that diversity goals may generate legal, fairness, or backlash concerns if they unintentionally produce gender-based pay disparities.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the field studies have strong external validity but do not permit causal inference or direct tests of all individual-level mechanisms. The laboratory studies provide internal validity and all-else-equal comparisons but rely on simulated tasks and include only some participants with management experience. Study 3 uses industry as a proxy for diversity-goal strength, so other industry differences may also contribute to the observed premium.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other sources of contextual variation, including whether legal, social, moral, or business-case motives for diversity goals change the size of the female premium. The authors also suggest extending the theory to other groups targeted by diversity initiatives, including racial minorities.
