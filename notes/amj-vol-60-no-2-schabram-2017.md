# Negotiating the Challenges of a Calling: Emotion and Enacted Sensemaking in Animal Shelter Work

**Abstract**
An important and underexamined issue in the study of callings concerns the challenges people face in pursuing a calling and how they negotiate those challenges. This process may be especially intense and consequential because callings involve work that is rooted in people’s values and that matters a great deal to them. Drawing on narrative interviews with 50 animal shelter workers, we identify three different “calling paths” that evolve as employees respond to the challenges they encounter. While all individuals in our study entered animal shelter work with similar passion and purpose, and faced similar kinds of challenges, those on different paths interpreted challenges differently, had different emotional responses to them, engaged in different kinds of enacted sensemaking, and developed different accounts of themselves and their guiding purpose, through which they interpreted subsequent challenges. The result was the emergence of three increasingly divergent calling paths over time, culminating in different emotional, psychological, and behavioral outcomes. Based on these findings, we develop a model of the recursive process through which individuals negotiate the challenges of a calling, contributing to the literatures on callings and employee responses to workplace challenges.

**Research Question**
The study asks how the ways individuals negotiate the challenges of a calling vary across individuals and over time. It focuses on animal shelter workers because this setting makes the emotional, identity, and sensemaking dynamics of pursuing a prosocial calling transparently observable.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: challenges of animal shelter work, accounts of self and purpose, emotional responses, enacted sensemaking, calling paths, burnout, thriving.
- Process / phases: workers enter with passion and purpose, encounter similar challenges, interpret those challenges through accounts of self and purpose, respond through cycles of emotion and enacted sensemaking, and gradually enact divergent work environments and outcomes.
- Calling paths: identity-oriented workers preserve a sense of special gifts for animals; contribution-oriented workers seek to apply valuable skills for broader impact; practice-oriented workers treat challenges as opportunities for learning and mastery.
- Emergent outcomes: identity and contribution paths tend toward broken or defeated burnout and exit from shelter work, while the practice path tends toward learning, growth, continued animal welfare work, and community building.

The process is recursive rather than linear. Initial accounts of self and purpose frame how workers interpret challenges; emotional responses then energize enacted sensemaking, including experimenting with tasks, relationships, roles, and boundaries. These actions change the work environment and generate new cues, so small early differences in framing and enactment accumulate into distinct calling paths.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends callings research by showing that a calling is not a unitary work orientation with uniform consequences. It identifies three calling paths and explains how people pursuing the same calling can end up thriving, leaving in a defeated state, or leaving with a broken sense of fit. It also contributes to research on workplace challenges by theorizing how emotion and enacted sensemaking recursively shape burnout and thriving trajectories over time.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations that employ people with callings should not assume passion and dedication are self-sustaining resources. The study suggests that called employees may work unpaid hours, take on difficult tasks, bring work home, and become exhausted unless organizations help them negotiate challenges constructively. Support should be tailored to the path: calibrating identity expectations, giving realistic previews of contribution limits, and supporting learning and growth for practice-oriented workers.

**Limitations**
The sample was designed for theory development and consisted mostly of women in North American animal welfare organizations, so transferability to other gender compositions, cultures, and calling domains remains uncertain. The study also relies on one-time narrative interviews with current and former employees rather than real-time longitudinal observation, so retrospective accounts may reflect participants’ current energy, satisfaction, and distance from shelter work.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether similar identity-, contribution-, and practice-oriented calling paths appear in other occupations, especially in different cultural and gendered contexts. Longitudinal studies could observe how accounts of self and purpose, emotional responses, and enacted work environments change in real time.
