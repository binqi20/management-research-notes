# The Evolution of Issue Interpretation within Organizational Fields: Actor Positions, Framing Trajectories, and Field Settlement

**Abstract**
In this study, we seek to understand how actors’ interpretations of contentious issues evolve over time within organizational fields and how these interpretations may lead to field settlement. Empirically, we examine how groups of actors in the field of civil aviation interpreted the environmental issues of noise and emissions during the period 1996–2010. Actors employed various cultural frames to interpret these issues as they rose and fell in prominence within the field. We develop a framework to track actors’ framing trajectories over time; in particular, the extent to which these frames reveal actors’ stance toward buffering versus integrating issues into their core operations. We reveal four prototypical framing trajectories and find that actors’ framing trajectories were influenced by the extent to which these actors were directly linked to issues in societal discourse and had direct contact with concerned audiences. Based on our analysis, we build theory of how actor framing of issues evolves over time and leads to field settlement of contentious issues.

**Research Question**
How does issue interpretation evolve over time within organizational fields, and how do fields reach settlement around contentious issues? The paper studies this question through civil aviation actors' shifting interpretations of aircraft noise and emissions between 1996 and 2010.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / frames: Buffering frames, integrating frames, actor framing trajectories, contentious issue salience, and field settlement.
- Actor positions: Front-stage actors are directly linked to an issue and in direct contact with concerned audiences; middle-stage actors contact concerned audiences without being directly linked to the issue; backstage actors are neither directly linked nor directly exposed.
- Process / phases: Issue emergence, issue contestation, and issue settlement, traced separately for noise and emissions.
- Triggering conditions: Negative moral judgments from the public, threat of external regulation, and peer pressure from other industry actors.
- Emergent outcomes: Four framing trajectories: integrating dominant, buffering dominant, mixed buffering/integrating, and buffering to integrating.

The process account is that actor position shapes how organizations interpret a contentious issue as it rises and falls in salience. Backstage actors maintain relatively stable buffering- or integrating-dominant frames, middle-stage actors use mixed framing, and front-stage actors initially buffer because they are under threat. As public moral judgment, regulatory threat, and peer pressure accumulate, front-stage actors can shift toward integrating frames, treating the issue as part of normal field activity. This shift is theorized as an antecedent to field settlement because it enables a common framework for action even without full consensus.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a longitudinal framework for studying issue framing as trajectories rather than static positions. It distinguishes buffering and integrating frames and shows how these categories capture whether actors resist an issue as external disruption or accept it as part of core operations.

The paper also reconceptualizes actor position in organizational fields as issue-contingent exposure to concerned audiences rather than only resource position, identity, or status. Its field-settlement argument links actor-level interpretive shifts, especially among front-stage actors, to field-level movement from contention toward a workable framework for action.

**Practical Implication**
Managers, activists, and policy makers can use the framework to map which actors occupy front-, middle-, and backstage positions around a contentious issue. The study suggests that field settlement is more likely when the actors most exposed to public scrutiny and regulatory threat move from defensive buffering toward integrating the issue into ordinary operations.

For environmental issues in aviation, the paper implies that public pressure, regulation, and industry peer pressure can make environmental concerns harder to treat as peripheral. However, the authors caution that field settlement does not necessarily mean definitive resolution or improved environmental performance.

**Limitations**
The study examines one organizational field, civil aviation, so the authors treat generalizability as limited and note that aviation is highly regulated. The typology of framing trajectories may therefore be incomplete or partly specific to this setting.

The archival content analysis relies on a single trade publication, Aviation Daily, which may underrepresent NGOs, regulators, and aviation actors outside North America and Europe. The study also does not directly test whether changes in issue interpretation produce changes in firm behavior or environmental performance.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine additional fields to identify other framing trajectories and scope conditions. Studies could also analyze how NGOs, activists, and regulators propel industry actors to the front stage of contentious issues.

The authors call for work that tests whether integrative framing is associated with better environmental performance rather than only interpretive settlement. Additional research could also examine issue dormancy, crowding-out between meta-issues such as environment and security, and the conditions under which settled issues return to contestation.
