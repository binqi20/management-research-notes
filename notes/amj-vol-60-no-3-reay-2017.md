# Getting Leopards to Change their Spots: Co-creating a New Professional Role Identity

**Abstract**
We investigated how professional role identity change can be accomplished in highly institutionalized contexts characterized by resiliency. We show that the collective professional role identity of family physicians was changed through a process of reinterpreting multiple logics and their relationships. Through our inductive analyses, we identified four mechanisms that occurred through social interactions and collectively served to rearrange the constellation of logics guiding physician role identity: (1) revealing the influence of a hidden logic; (2) reinforcing the conflict between logics; (3) reframing the meaning of a dominant logic; and (4) re-embedding the new arrangement of logics. We found that the change in physician professional role identity required significant identity work by a group of actors, but particularly by the managers who had been charged with leading the reform initiative. We contribute to the professional role identity and institutional literatures by showing how others can engage in social interactions with professionals to facilitate the reinterpretation and rearranging of institutional logics that guide collective professional role identity.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how social actors can facilitate changes in collective professional role identity by reinterpreting institutional logics and their relationships. Empirically, it examines how family physicians in a highly institutionalized health care setting shifted from an "autonomous expert" role identity to a "head of team" role identity during primary health care reform.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / starting point: Physicians initially understood themselves as autonomous experts guided by a strong professional logic, with market, state, and corporate logics low or hidden.
- Process / mechanisms: The change unfolded through revealing the influence of a hidden market logic, reinforcing the conflict between professional and corporate logics, reframing the professional logic around informed team leadership, and re-embedding the new logic constellation in routines and standards.
- Actors / relational spaces: Managers, renegade physicians, other physicians, nurses, pharmacists, and the medical association interacted through one-on-one meetings, group meetings, workplace interactions, professional association interventions, and presentations.
- Emergent outcome: The physician role identity shifted toward "head of team," supported by a strong but reframed professional logic, moderate market and state logics, and a corporate logic kept separate from physician identity.

The process centers on identity work performed collectively, especially by managers charged with leading the reform. Private interactions made taboo issues such as financial incentives discussable, while more public group and workplace interactions allowed physicians and other professionals to renegotiate what counted as professional work. The new role identity became durable when standardized care, evidence-based protocols, patient access, clinic revenues, and professional responsibility were linked into a coherent arrangement of institutional logics.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to professional role identity research by showing that collective professional role identity change can be orchestrated partly by actors outside the focal profession, not only by professionals themselves. It contributes to institutional logics theory by showing how a hidden logic can be revealed, how relationships among logics can be reinterpreted, and how social interactions in private and public spaces make that reinterpretation possible. It also connects relational identity work with institutional context, showing how macro-level logics and local workplace interactions jointly shape professional identity change.

**Practical Implication**
For professionalized settings, change initiatives may need to create protected spaces where professionals can reconsider sensitive issues without public reputational cost, as well as public or workplace spaces where new meanings can be enacted and shared. Managers can facilitate professional change by respecting core professional values while reframing how those values are expressed in new work arrangements. In this case, multidisciplinary teamwork became acceptable when it was connected to physician expertise, informed control, patient access, and better care.

**Limitations**
The paper does not present a separate formal limitations section. Its empirical basis is one longitudinal case of primary health care reform in Alberta, so the process model is most directly grounded in highly institutionalized professional settings with strong role expectations and multiple interacting actor groups. The authors frame the findings as particularly relevant to contexts where entrenched norms, values, and beliefs make role identity change highly resilient rather than universally straightforward.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether similar mechanisms operate in other professionalized settings where role identities are strongly institutionalized, such as architects and engineers working concurrently on specialized buildings. The paper also suggests value in studying hidden logics in other settings because bringing such logics into interactional spaces may help explain either institutional change or institutional resilience. Further work could compare how private and public interaction spaces differently enable reinterpretation of institutional logics.
