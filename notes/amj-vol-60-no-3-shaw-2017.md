# Advantages of Starting with Theory

**Abstract**
Not reported in paper

**Research Question**
The editorial asks whether post-result theorizing, or HARKing, is actually an effective route for publishing hypothesis-driven deductive quantitative research in AMJ. Shaw reframes the issue from a review-process perspective and asks why starting with theory produces stronger manuscripts.

**Mechanism Process**
- Editorial problem: Post-result theorizing can make authors retrofit significant findings to theory and present the work as if the theory came first.
- Warning signs: Reviewers detect contorted theory, poorly defined constructs, construct-measurement mismatch, and theory-design mismatch.
- Theory-first advantages: Beginning with theory helps authors build coherent predictions, define constructs carefully, align measures with concepts, design studies around mechanisms, and write stronger theoretical implications.
- Legitimate uses: Post-result findings can motivate follow-up deductive theory development or be reported transparently as exploratory analyses.

The process logic is editorial rather than empirical. Shaw argues that HARKing leaves recognizable traces in manuscripts that generate rejection-leaning reviewer commentary, whereas a theory-first approach improves conceptual framing, study execution, construct clarity, and the contribution to theory.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial contributes to methods and theory-development guidance by shifting the HARKing discussion from ethics alone to manuscript quality and review-process consequences. It argues that the practical advantage of starting with theory is not only avoiding bias, but also producing sharper theoretical contribution, better design alignment, and more credible discussion of how a study changes or advances theory.

**Practical Implication**
For authors, the editorial implies that AMJ submissions should begin from a clear theoretical frame rather than searching results for a publishable story. Exploratory or surprising findings should be used transparently as inputs for further theory development or explicitly labeled additional analyses, not disguised as a priori hypotheses.

**Limitations**
Shaw explicitly notes that his judgment is based on editorial and reviewing experience rather than a large prospective study linking authors' research processes to review outcomes. The editorial therefore offers reasoned guidance from AMJ's review context rather than systematic empirical evidence about all management journals.

**Future Research**
The editorial points toward future work that could study authors' approaches to conducting research and connect those approaches to review-process outcomes. It also encourages future deductive quantitative studies to use unexpected findings as starting points for more comprehensive theory building and follow-up testing.
