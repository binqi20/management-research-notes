# Multinational Enterprises within Cultural Space and Place: Integrating Cultural Distance and Tightness–Looseness

**Abstract**
Prior research into the effects of cultural differences between multinational enterprises’ (MNEs’) home and host countries on expatriate staffing decisions in foreign subsidiaries has produced a large number of conflicting findings. We address some of these conflicting findings and aim to advance theory in two ways. First, we draw on transaction cost economics to explain why and how the effects of cultural distance on the proportion of expatriate parent-country nationals form a curvilinear relationship, instead of a linear one as commonly proposed. Second, we integrate the values-based cultural distance concept with the norms-based tightness–looseness concept. This allows us to simultaneously account for cultural differences between countries and location-bound normative cultural effects within countries, which cannot be overcome solely through expatriate learning and adaptation. Using a large global dataset of Japanese MNEs, we find support for a convex relationship between cultural distance and the proportion of expatriate parent-country nationals. We also find a moderating (steepening) effect of tightness–looseness on this relationship. The results reconcile some of the tensions between the subjectivists’ values-based approach, which positions culture in the shared cognitions realm, and the structuralists’ approach, which places culture in a normative situational environment.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why, when, and in which direction cultural distance between an MNE's home and host country affects the proportion of expatriate parent-country nationals in foreign subsidiaries. It also asks whether host-country tightness–looseness amplifies the curvilinear cultural distance-staffing relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Cultural distance between Japan and host countries; host-country tightness–looseness.
- DV(s): Proportion of expatriate parent-country nationals in a subsidiary.
- Mediators: Not modeled as formal mediators; the theoretical process operates through ex ante and ex post transaction costs.
- Moderators: Host-country tightness–looseness moderates the curvilinear relationship between cultural distance and expatriate PCN proportion.

The mechanism is grounded in transaction cost economics. At low to medium cultural distance, rising ex ante costs of expatriate deployment make local staffing more attractive, reducing the expatriate PCN proportion. At medium to high cultural distance, rising ex post costs from monitoring and opportunism make expatriate PCNs more valuable for coordination and control, increasing their proportion. Tight host-country norms amplify this pattern because they make expatriate adjustment costlier at lower distance levels and make control concerns more salient at higher distance levels.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to cross-cultural management theory by replacing a simple linear cultural distance prediction with a transaction-cost account of a convex, partly U-shaped relationship between cultural distance and expatriate PCN staffing. It reconciles prior positive, negative, and mixed findings by distinguishing ex ante and ex post cost logics across the cultural-distance continuum.

It also integrates values-based cultural distance with norms-based tightness–looseness. By treating cultural distance as cultural space and tightness–looseness as location-bound cultural place, the paper shows that country-level norm strength moderates how cultural distance affects MNE staffing decisions.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not make expatriate-versus-local staffing decisions from cultural distance alone. The paper recommends a more deliberate cost analysis that includes both ex ante costs of expatriate deployment and ex post costs of monitoring and control in culturally distant subsidiaries.

The findings also imply that expatriate preparation should address both cognitive cultural differences and place-bound normative pressures. Cross-cultural training may help with values-based distance, but tight local norms may require additional mechanisms such as mentoring, local relationships, scenario-based preparation, and safeguards against opportunistic behavior.

**Limitations**
The study does not directly measure ex ante and ex post transaction costs, so the cost mechanism is inferred from the staffing pattern rather than observed directly. The dataset has substantial advantages but is limited by the common denominator of Japanese ownership, and Japanese MNEs are known to rely heavily on long-term expatriate PCNs.

The study also depends on available tightness–looseness scores, limiting country coverage. It does not examine different managerial assignee types, alternative expatriate assignment forms, or individual-level variance such as managerial status, cultural background, personality, spousal adjustment, and career goals.

**Future Research**
Future research could replicate the model in other home-country settings and extend tightness–looseness scores to a larger set of countries. The paper also points to the value of studying Quadrant 4 in its cultural-space/place framework and examining the qualitative specificity of local norms rather than treating tightness–looseness as a distance measure.

Additional work could directly measure ex ante and ex post transaction costs, incorporate transaction benefits, and study alternative assignee forms such as inpatriates, third-country nationals, short-term assignments, commuter assignments, and virtual assignments. The authors also suggest multilevel research on individual differences, organizational structures, job characteristics, and polycultural abilities.
