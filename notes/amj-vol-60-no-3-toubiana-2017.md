# The Message is on the Wall? Emotions, Social Media and the Dynamics of Institutional Complexity

**Abstract**
In this paper, we explore how emotions influence organizations in situations of institutional complexity. In particular, we study members’ and leaders’ emotive responses and influence activities in response to a disruptive event that led to a violation of expectations. Our findings show that when people’s expectations of an organization’s actions are violated it can trigger a process of emotional escalation that leads to destabilization of the organization through the emotion-laden influence activities of shaming and shunning. The violation in our case resulted in strong negative emotions expressed on Facebook. Facebook acted as an emotional echo chamber where negative emotions were amplified and led to members’ emotion-driven influence activities, eventually triggering regret and adaptation by the organization. We discuss implications for the study of emotions in institutional dynamics.

**Research Question**
How are organizations that face institutional complexity affected by emotions when they confront disruptive events? The paper examines this question through the conflict between the DDF, an anonymized Canadian nonprofit federation, and members whose expectations were violated when the organization prioritized a research logic over a care logic after a controversial treatment emerged.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Institutional complexity between research and care logics; different emotional registers attached to those logics; members' expectations of care-based advocacy; leaders' research-based, nonemotive response.
- Process / phases: Activation of different logics and investment of hope; members' betrayal and shaming while leaders justified authority; members' anger and shunning; DDF regret and mollification.
- Influence activities: Members moved from hope to betrayal, shaming, anger, and shunning, amplified through Facebook and media attention.
- Organizational response: The DDF initially defended its research-logic position, then expressed regret, reintroduced care-logic communication, and adapted through more personal local engagement.
- Boundary conditions: The case involves serious illness, a nonprofit advocacy setting, high emotional investment, and social media activity that may not represent all members.

The process is cross-level. A disruptive treatment activated members' care logic and their hope that the DDF would advocate for access; the DDF instead enacted a research logic grounded in evidence, safety, and professional expertise. Facebook amplified members' betrayal and anger, turning emotion into shaming and then shunning that destabilized the organization through lost support, legitimacy pressure, and public conflict. Resettlement began when DDF leaders expressed regret and communicated in ways that acknowledged members' care logic and emotions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional theory by showing that institutional logics differ not only in goals, means, and sources of legitimacy, but also in emotional registers that prescribe legitimate emotional expression and reasoning. It extends research on institutional complexity by explaining how disruptive events can surface emotional incompatibilities between logics and destabilize an organizational settlement. It also theorizes social media as an emotional echo chamber that amplifies discrete emotions and converts them into influence activities such as shaming and shunning.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations facing emotionally invested constituents should not treat social media communication as one-way transmission of official positions. The study suggests that justifying a rational or expert position may intensify conflict when the audience is embedded in a different logic with a different emotional register. Managers should communicate in the audience's logic, acknowledge emotions after perceived breaches, and respond early before online amplification pushes supporters away and escalates negative affect.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study focuses predominantly on members posting on Facebook, even though these members may differ from stakeholders who did not engage online. They also state that their data do not conclusively show whether emotive media coverage itself activated the care logic.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether stakeholders who engage online differ significantly from those who do not and how such differences affect organizational response. The authors also call for research on whether disruptive triggers with particular emotional content activate logics with matching emotional registers. More work is needed on social media, emotional amplification, and advocacy for uncertain ideas in other institutionally complex contexts.
