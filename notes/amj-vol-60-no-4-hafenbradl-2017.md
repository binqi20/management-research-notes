# Ideology and the Micro-foundations of CSR: Why Executives Believe in the Business Case for CSR and how this Affects their CSR Engagements

**Abstract**
Existing research on executives’ belief in the business case for corporate social responsibility (CSR) is built on two premises. The first is that, in order to believe in the business case, executives need factual evidence that this business case indeed exists. The second premise is that those executives who do believe in the business case will readily invest in CSR-related activities. The results from our four studies tell a different story. We show that managers, rather than focusing on factual evidence, believe in the business case because they espouse a fair market ideology—the tendency to justify and idealize the market economy system. At the same time, even though managers espousing a fair market ideology believe in the business case for CSR, they are not more inclined to engage in CSR than managers who do not hold such an ideology, because they also experience weaker moral emotions when confronted with ethical problems. By drawing on system justification theory, we simultaneously explore antecedents and consequences of executives’ belief in the business case for CSR and of their moral emotions. In doing so, we help advance knowledge about the micro-foundations of CSR.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why executives believe in the business case for CSR and whether that belief actually makes them more inclined to engage in CSR. It examines whether fair market ideology explains executives' belief in a positive CSP-CFP link while also weakening moral outrage toward ethical problems, thereby offsetting the expected positive effect of business-case belief on CSR engagement.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Fair market ideology and educational background in business, economics, or law.
- DV(s): Belief in the CSP-CFP link and tendency for CSR engagement.
- Mediators: Fair market ideology mediates the educational-background-to-business-case-belief relationship; belief in the CSP-CFP link and moral outrage mediate the relationship between fair market ideology and CSR engagement.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is that fair market ideology motivates managers to justify and idealize the market economy system. This makes them more likely to believe that CSR must have economic value because firms already engage in CSR, but it also dampens moral outrage when they confront ethical problems in the market system. In Study 4, the positive indirect path from fair market ideology to CSR engagement through belief in the CSP-CFP link is neutralized by the negative indirect path through weaker moral outrage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to micro-foundations research on CSR by showing that executives' CSR-related beliefs and moral emotions are not independent drivers but can originate in the same ideology. It cautions against the assumption that belief in the business case for CSR is enough to motivate CSR engagement, because the same fair market ideology that produces business-case belief also reduces moral responsiveness to ethical problems. It also advances upper echelons research by directly measuring belief in the CSP-CFP link through a prediction game rather than relying only on distant biographical proxies.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that persuading executives about the financial merits of CSR may be insufficient if those executives also have weaker moral outrage toward social, environmental, or ethical problems. CSR engagement may require attention not only to instrumental business-case arguments but also to the moral sensitivity that makes executives perceive problems as requiring action. Organizations should be cautious about treating business, economics, or law educational backgrounds as simple proxies for higher CSR orientation.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the studies took place in controlled classroom settings, which supported clean measurement and causal inference but may limit external validity. Their measure of business-case belief represents the CSP-CFP relationship as one linear coefficient, even though executives may believe in more complex or nonlinear returns to CSR. The analysis is also at the individual-executive level, while real CSR decisions are often made in teams.

**Future Research**
Future research could test the framework with field data on actual CSR engagement and follow executives over time to examine whether changes in fair market ideology affect business-case beliefs, moral outrage, and firm CSR behavior. Researchers could also test whether executives perceive nonlinear returns to CSR and whether executive-team diversity in ideology, education, and moral outrage changes CSR decisions. The authors also suggest extending the framework to consumers, investors, regulators, and other evaluators of corporate activity.
