# Unpacking the CEO–Board Relationship: How Strategy Making Happens in Entrepreneurial Firms

**Abstract**
We examine how venture CEOs effectively engage their boards in the strategy-making process. Using the inductive multiple-case study approach, we track CEO–board interactions inside and outside the boardroom in depth and over time through rare observations of board meetings and rich interview access to CEOs and their boards of directors. Our primary theoretical contributions are to the resource dependence perspective. We clarify the resource versus power tradeoff as a fundamental tension in venture CEO–board relationships. Further, we add a much-needed process framework to resource dependence by highlighting how venture CEOs use four behaviors to resolve this tradeoff in an effective strategy-making process. Finally, we contribute a fresh view of the venture CEO–board relationship—i.e., spotlighting the CEO (not board) and boards as CEO–director dyads (not groups). We conclude by noting implications for other key corporate governance perspectives, and indicating boundary conditions for our framework. Overall, we deepen the conversation at the nexus of resource dependence theory and venture governance.

**Research Question**
How do venture CEOs effectively engage in strategy making with their boards? The paper asks how CEOs in professionally funded young firms draw useful advice from knowledgeable but busy or potentially misaligned directors while retaining enough power to move the strategy-making process forward.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core tension: Venture CEOs need board resources such as advice, legitimacy, funding, and connections, but board engagement can reduce CEO power when directors are busy, conflicted, or misaligned.
- Effective process behaviors: More effective CEOs use dyadic interactions and unique director roles, propose single formal alternatives in board meetings, run dedicated strategic brainstorming sessions outside regular board meetings, and use political action to close the strategy-making process.
- Ineffective contrasts: Less effective CEOs treat the board as an undifferentiated group, rely on broad formal-board brainstorming, avoid strategy discussion, or use political action to advance self-interest rather than close strategy making.
- Emergent outcome: These process behaviors help CEOs obtain better director advice while preserving control over the direction, pace, and closure of board-level strategy making.

The process logic is "divide and conquer": effective venture CEOs divide board attention into dyadic roles, distinct meeting formats, and selective alliances, then place themselves at the center as orchestrators of the strategy process. The framework explains why CEO-board relationships are not only a matter of board composition or incentives, but also a sequence of behaviors through which CEOs manage the resource-versus-power tradeoff.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper clarifies the resource-versus-power tradeoff as a central tension in venture governance and returns resource dependence theory to its exchange-theoretic roots. It adds a process component to resource dependence by identifying four CEO behaviors that resolve this tension during board-level strategy making. It also reframes the venture CEO-board relationship from the CEO's perspective and treats CEO-director dyads, rather than the board as a single group, as the more fundamental exchange relationship.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that venture CEOs should not rely only on formal board meetings or broad group problem solving when strategy is uncertain. They can gain more useful board input by cultivating director-specific roles, bringing focused alternatives to meetings, separating strategic brainstorming from operating updates, and using alliances or endorsements to close decisions when delay becomes costly. The analysis also cautions directors and CEOs that board advice can be valuable but may be shaped by conflicts of interest and power dynamics.

**Limitations**
The framework is bounded by the paper's assumptions that CEOs are relatively well aligned with firm goals, board members have valuable advice but may be busy or misaligned, and strategy matters in the focal setting. The study is based on four information-technology ventures in strategically demanding markets, so the authors discuss generalizability to other firm types, groups, trust conditions, and geographies as a boundary-condition issue. Because the paper is an inductive theory-building study, it does not test the emergent framework with a larger sample.

**Future Research**
The authors identify empirical examination of the framework with larger samples and across types of firms and geographies as the next step. They also suggest that the framework may have implications beyond venture governance for resource dependence relationships where actors must gain resources without surrendering power. Future work could further examine when trust changes the need for these CEO behaviors and whether similar dynamics hold in public firms or other strategically demanding contexts.
