# Red Queen Competitive Imitation in the U.K. Mobile Phone Industry

**Abstract**
This paper uses Red Queen competition theory to examine competitive imitation. We conceptualize imitative actions by a focal firm and its rivals along two dimensions: imitation scope, which describes the extent to which a firm imitates a wide range (as opposed to a narrow range) of new product technologies introduced by rivals; and imitation speed, namely the pace at which it imitates these technologies. We argue that focal firm imitation scope and imitation speed drive performance, as well as imitation scope and speed decisions by rivals, which in turn influence focal firm performance. We also argue that the impact of this self-reinforcing Red Queen process on firms’ actions and performance is contingent on levels of product technology heterogeneity—defined as the extent to which the industry has multiple designs, resulting in product variety. We test our hypotheses using imitative actions by mobile phone vendors and their sales performance in the U.K. from 1997 to 2008.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how far and how fast firms should imitate rivals' new product technologies in rapidly changing technology-intensive industries. It examines whether focal firms' imitation scope and imitation speed affect their own sales performance, whether these imitative actions trigger rivals' imitation, and whether product technology heterogeneity changes the Red Queen competitive imitation cycle.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Focal firm's imitation scope, focal firm's average speed of imitation, rivals' imitation scope, rivals' average speed of imitation, and product technology heterogeneity.
- DV(s): Focal firm sales performance, rivals' imitation scope, and rivals' average speed of imitation.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Product technology heterogeneity moderates focal-firm imitation effects on performance, focal-firm imitation effects on rivals' imitation, and rivals' imitation effects on focal-firm performance.

The mechanism is a Red Queen action-reaction process. Focal firms imitate new product technologies to maintain or improve competitive position; those moves can improve performance directly, but they also push rivals to imitate, and rivals' imitation then erodes focal firm performance. Product technology heterogeneity shapes this process because multiple competing designs increase technological uncertainty, changing both the performance value of broad imitation and rivals' willingness or ability to respond.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Red Queen competition research by examining imitation of new product technologies in a technology-intensive industry rather than treating competitive action as a general count of moves. It distinguishes imitation scope from imitation speed and shows that these two forms of imitation have different performance and rival-response implications. It also contributes by theorizing and testing product technology heterogeneity as a boundary condition for the Red Queen cycle.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that managers in technology-intensive industries should not treat imitation as a simple copy-or-not-copy decision. Fast imitation is associated with higher focal firm performance, while broad imitation has more conditional effects: it can help when product technology heterogeneity is high but can hurt when heterogeneity is low. Managers should also expect successful imitation to invite rivals' responses, so gains from imitation may be temporary unless firms keep adapting as the technological environment changes.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study captures only observable strategies based on information reported in the press and industry trade journals. The empirical setting is a single-product industry, which helps isolate Red Queen competition around one device but may limit generalization to multi-product industries. The paper also does not directly measure different organizational learning modes, such as mimetic, vicarious, and experiential learning.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how country and industry boundaries influence Red Queen competitive imitation, especially because mobile phone technologies may be affected by decisions made in adjacent product categories. Scholars could test whether the findings generalize to multi-product industries where firms can absorb losses in one product segment through broader portfolios. Future work could also develop measures of different learning modes and study how Red Queen competition links micro-level interfirm rivalry with macro-level industry evolution.
