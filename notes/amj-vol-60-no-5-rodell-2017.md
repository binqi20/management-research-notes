# Corporate Volunteering Climate: Mobilizing Employee Passion for Societal Causes and Inspiring Future Charitable Action

**Abstract**
Today’s corporations are increasingly engaging in efforts to address societal concerns ranging from hunger and poverty to education and financial stability, predominantly through corporate volunteering. Yet, because research has been focused on the individual volunteer we still know relatively little about how corporate volunteering can help address grand challenges. In this study, we introduce the concept of “corporate volunteering climate” in order to examine the broader, more systemlevel functioning of corporate volunteering in workplaces. Drawing on the sensemaking process, we theorize about how this climate develops—to what extent is it driven by company-level policies versus employee convictions for a cause? We also explore the potential influence of corporate volunteering climate for volunteers and non-volunteers, in terms of the workplace (through employee affective commitment) and the community (through employee intentions to volunteer, whether through corporate opportunities or personally). The results of a study conducted with United Way Worldwide suggest that corporate volunteering climate arises through both employee belief in the cause and corporate policies, and that these forces act as substitutes for each other. Moreover, by fostering a sense of collective pride among employees, this climate is related to affective commitment, and corporate and personal volunteering intentions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what conditions foster a corporate volunteering climate and what implications such a climate has for employees inside and outside the workplace. It compares company-driven resources with employee-driven belief in the volunteering cause, then examines whether corporate volunteering climate relates to affective commitment and future corporate and personal volunteering intentions through collective pride.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Company-provided resources and aggregate employee belief in the volunteering cause as antecedents of corporate volunteering climate; corporate volunteering climate as the focal predictor of employee outcomes.
- DV(s): Employee affective commitment, corporate volunteering intentions, and personal volunteering intentions.
- Mediators: Corporate volunteering climate links company-provided resources and aggregate employee belief in the cause to employee outcomes; collective pride carries the climate relationship with affective commitment and personal volunteering intentions, while the corporate volunteering-intention indirect effect is significant for non-volunteers but not volunteers.
- Moderators: Corporate volunteering participation moderates whether the indirect climate-pride relationship differs for volunteers and non-volunteers; company-provided resources and employee belief in the cause also interact in predicting corporate volunteering climate.

The mechanism is cross-level and sensemaking-based. Employees infer a corporate volunteering climate from company policies and from peers' passion for the volunteering cause; these two sources act as substitutes, so either top-down support or bottom-up belief can foster the climate when the other is weaker. The climate then creates collective pride, which is associated with higher affective commitment for both volunteers and non-volunteers and with personal volunteering intentions overall. For corporate volunteering intentions, the indirect relationship through collective pride is significant for non-volunteers but not for volunteers.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces corporate volunteering climate as a group-level perception, shifting corporate volunteering research beyond individual volunteers and individual volunteer experiences. It contributes to work climate and corporate volunteering research by showing how top-down company resources and bottom-up employee belief can jointly shape a volunteering climate, and by showing that this climate can matter for both volunteers and non-volunteers. It also extends the volunteering literature by distinguishing corporate volunteering intentions from personal volunteering intentions, showing how a work-domain climate may be associated with nonwork charitable intentions.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that companies can cultivate a corporate volunteering climate either by providing resources and formal support or by supporting employee passion for causes that employees already value. For firms with limited existing volunteering activity, a formal program may help make volunteering salient; for firms with an existing volunteer base, allowing employees to pursue causes they care about may organically strengthen the climate. Managers should also recognize that corporate volunteering climate may influence non-volunteers, not only employees already participating in company programs.

**Limitations**
The authors note several limitations: the study relies on self-reports, although focal antecedents and outcomes were separated in time and modeled at different levels where possible. The sample includes 50 companies but relatively few respondents per company, raising concerns about whether climate perceptions fully represent the broader organizations. The companies were recruited through existing United Way Worldwide partnerships, which may limit between-company variance and introduce selection bias. The study also uses volunteering intentions rather than subsequent volunteering behavior and does not observe direct beneficiaries or societal impact.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research with broader company samples, including organizations with strong volunteering programs and organizations without existing programs, as well as more representative employee samples within each organization. Future work could examine other employee motives, such as socializing or skill development, and whether perceived sincerity of colleagues' motives shapes non-volunteer reactions to corporate volunteering climate. The authors also suggest examining how communication and attributions for company-provided resources affect employee reactions to corporate volunteering programs.
