# Self-reliance: A Gender Perspective on its Relationship to Communality and Leadership Evaluations

**Abstract**
We posit a female advantage in the relationship between self-reliance and leadership evaluations. We test this prediction in four studies. First, using multi-rater evaluations of young managers, we find that self-reliance relates positively to leadership evaluations for women, but not for men. Next, in each of three experiments, we manipulate the gender of a leader and the agentic trait he or she displays (e.g., self-reliance, dominance, no discrete agentic trait). We find that self-reliant female leaders are evaluated as better leaders than self-reliant male leaders are. In contrast, we find a male advantage or no gender advantage for dominant leaders or leaders who are described positively, but not in terms of any discrete agentic trait. Consistent with expectancy violation theory, the female advantage in the relationship between self-reliance and leadership evaluations emerges because self-reliant female leaders are seen as similarly competent, but more communal, than self-reliant male leaders are. We discuss the implications of these findings for understanding the effects of self-reliance, gender stereotypes, and stereotype violations on leadership evaluations.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether self-reliance, as a discrete agentic trait, is more positively associated with leadership evaluations for women than for men. It also asks whether perceived communality explains this female advantage relative to self-reliant male leaders and to leaders described as dominant or only generally positive.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader gender; displayed agentic trait, including self-reliance, dominance, or no discrete agentic trait; self-reliance in the field survey; explicit communality information in Study 3.
- DV(s): Leadership evaluations, including 360-degree leadership ratings, voting or hiring intentions, perceived leadership ability, and investment or valuation judgments.
- Mediators: Perceived communality.
- Moderators: Leader gender, agentic-trait condition, and explicit communality information.

The mechanism is grounded in expectancy violation theory. Self-reliance signals agency for both men and women, but the authors argue that observers assimilate women's self-reliance to feminine stereotypes of communality rather than treating it as evidence of low communality. As a result, self-reliant women are seen as similarly competent but more communal than self-reliant men, which improves their leadership evaluations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the undifferentiated treatment of agency in role congruity accounts of gender and leadership evaluation. It shows that a desirable masculine trait can operate as a positive expectancy violation for female leaders, so gender stereotypes can sometimes buffer rather than penalize women's displays of agency. It also brings self-reliance into trait theories of leadership by showing that its evaluative meaning depends on both leader gender and perceived communality.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that female leaders' displays of agency are not uniformly penalized and that self-reliance can improve evaluations when it is not read as low communality. For evaluators and organizations, the results suggest that leadership assessments may depend on trait-specific gender inferences rather than on agency as a single broad category.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the experimental effects in Studies 2-4 may not fully generalize to evaluations of one's own leader, especially in longstanding relationships where beliefs about a leader's communality may diverge from gender stereotypes. They also state that the paper focuses on broad leadership evaluations, which may mask more specific judgments about leadership styles or behaviors. The evidence for a male advantage in dominance was weaker than role congruity theory and expectancy violation theory would predict, possibly because the experiments focused on high-status leaders.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on whether self-reliance shapes evaluations of one's own leader differently from evaluations of unfamiliar or vignette-based leaders. It suggests examining more specific evaluations of self-reliant men and women, such as perceived transformational, transactional, or servant leadership styles. It also proposes studying whether other prescriptive masculine traits, such as being analytical, rational, or logical, can produce similar female advantages and whether knowledge of such advantages affects women's efficacy in pursuing leadership roles.
