# Shady Characters: The Implications of Illicit Organizational Roles for Resilient Team Performance

**Abstract**
In this paper we theorize about illicit roles and explore their effects on resilient team performance. We define an illicit role as one whose occupants specialize in activity forbidden by the law, regulatory bodies, or professional societies, in the belief that doing so provides a competitive advantage. Using longitudinal data on professional hockey teams, we examine the enforcer—a player who specializes in the prohibited activity of fighting. We find that team performance is more disrupted by the injury of an enforcer than by the injury of occupants of other formal roles on the team. In addition, team performance recovers more slowly after this setback to the extent the team tries to replace an enforcer, and the performance disruptions associated with his exit are magnified as a function of his experience with his team. We use these findings to develop new theory about organizational roles that operate outside official channels and formal structures. We suggest that such role occupants are more difficult to replace than their formal counterparts, in part because to enact these roles effectively requires experience in the local social context.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how illicit organizational roles affect resilient team performance when their occupants depart. It specifically examines whether losing an NHL enforcer disrupts team performance more than losing formal role occupants, whether replacement attempts slow recovery, and whether the enforcer's team experience magnifies the disruption.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Injury-related departure of an illicit role occupant, operationalized as the injury of an NHL enforcer; replacement attempts after an enforcer injury, proxied by team penalty concentration; enforcer team experience.
- DV(s): Team performance and performance recovery, measured by team points per game in pre- and post-injury windows.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Team experience of the enforcer; replacement attempts after the enforcer's injury.

The mechanism is that illicit role occupants specialize in work that cannot be formally codified, openly trained, or transparently replaced because the activities are forbidden but believed to support organizational goals. When an enforcer leaves, the team loses a role occupant whose effectiveness depends on trust, local social context, and context-specific knowledge. Attempts to substitute another player into that role slow recovery because replacements lack the same credibility and embedded experience, while longer team tenure makes the departed enforcer more locally valuable and therefore more disruptive to lose.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to role theory by theorizing illicit organizational roles: roles that specialize in prohibited activity and operate outside formal organizational structures. It extends research on teams by showing that non-core, informal, and illicit roles can affect resilient performance even though they are marginal to formal workflow. It also contributes to organizational misconduct research by showing that teams may organize prohibited activity through a role-based allocation of labor.

**Practical Implication**
The paper explicitly cautions that the findings do not imply teams should create illicit roles; instead, such roles create vulnerability for resilient performance. For managers and regulators, the findings imply that identifying people who specialize in illicit activity may help organizations and enforcement bodies detect and discourage misconduct. The authors also suggest that rotating personnel in ethically risky areas may hinder illicit activity because competence in these roles depends on time and local relationships.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the injury-based design can speak only to teams that already have an enforcer, not to whether having an enforcer improves performance. They observe only short-run performance immediately after the player's exit, so longer-term implications of not replacing illicit role occupants remain uncertain. They also study one illicit role in professional hockey, which raises generalizability concerns for other illicit roles, less specialized teams, and settings where informal role requirements are more ambiguous.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research that replicates the findings across other illicit roles and organizational contexts, such as rogue traders or convicted insider traders. It also suggests studying how illicit roles emerge, why some organizations resist adopting them, why they spread or persist across industries, and what incentives help make them obsolete. Future work could also directly test micro mechanisms such as coordination loss, lack of trust in replacements, and the conditions under which teams can avoid replacing illicit role occupants while sustaining performance.
