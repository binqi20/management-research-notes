# Language and Competition: Communication Vagueness, Interpretation Difficulties, and Market Entry

**Abstract**
Firms have a lot to lose from the entry of competitors into their markets. Grounded in the research on interfirm rivalry and strategic communication, we proposed and tested hypotheses suggesting that, when the managers of incumbent firms perceive a high threat of entry, they are more likely to use vagueness in their corporate communications to make their strategies and actions harder to discern. This lessened interpretation results in fewer competitive entries by potential entrants. We used computerized content analysis to quantify the use of vague language in incumbent firms' annual reports and empirically tested our hypotheses through data from the U.S. domestic airline industry. We found robust support for our hypotheses. By revealing that strategic use of language shapes competitive interactions, our research sheds new light on the process through which information is delivered, received, and interpreted by rivals. This process is at the heart of competitive dynamics and strategy research.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when managers of incumbent firms are more likely to use vague language in corporate communication and how rivals react to such vagueness. It specifically examines whether market-entry threats increase incumbents' communication vagueness and whether that vagueness is associated with fewer entries by potential entrants.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Market profitability and prevalence of potential entrants for incumbents' use of vague language; incumbent vagueness for potential entrants' market entry.
- DV(s): Firm vagueness in annual reports and likelihood of entry by potential entrants.
- Mediators: Interpretation difficulty is theorized as the process linking vague language to entrants' decisions, but it is not directly observed.
- Moderators: Average incumbent size and market concentration amplify the relationship between incumbent vagueness and potential entrants' likelihood of entry.

The proposed mechanism is that incumbents facing stronger entry threats tilt corporate communication toward vaguer language, making their strategies and actions harder for rivals to interpret. For potential entrants, vague information can lengthen decision-making and increase the perceived risk of entry, reducing the likelihood of entering a market. The effect is expected to be stronger when entrants pay more attention to incumbents, as in markets with larger incumbents or higher concentration.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to competitive dynamics by separating attention to rivals from interpretation of rivals' information, arguing that awareness requires more than noticing competitive signals. It extends competitive signaling theory by showing that vague and equivocal communication, not only explicit aggressive signaling, can shape competitive interaction. It also adds a competitive-rivalry setting to research on language as a strategic tool in organizations.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that communication and competitive intelligence are important managerial skills. For managers analyzing rivals, the findings imply attention to both what competitors communicate and what they leave unclear. For managers whose firms are being watched, the paper suggests that public communication can be interpreted by competitors as a strategic signal, especially when multiple audiences have divergent interests.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the findings are based on firms in a single industry over a specific time period, which may limit generalizability. They also note that environmental uncertainty is controlled through proxies and that interpretation difficulty is assumed from vague language rather than directly observed. The study relies primarily on annual reports and automated text analysis, limiting attention to other communication channels and within-document variation.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for direct measures of interpretation difficulty, such as surveys or cognitive maps, to unpack links between language use, interpretation, and interfirm behavior. It also suggests studying vagueness across communication channels and developing more fine-grained qualitative measures of language use. Future work could examine other language characteristics, resource diversion stratagems, rival-specific characteristics, mental maps, cognitive frameworks, identity domains, sensemaking processes, and issue framing in competitive dynamics.
