# Is There a Doctor in the House? Expert Product Users, Organizational Roles, and Innovation

**Abstract**
We explore the impact on innovation that domain experts, i.e., professional end users of a product have as inventors, executives, and board members in a young organization. Using a dataset of 231 surgical instrument ventures spanning a 25-year period alongside in-depth qualitative fieldwork, we find that professional physician–users (surgeons) strengthen innovation in some roles but block it in others. These experts are related to an increase in a firm’s innovation when they take a technology role as inventors, and particularly when they take a governance role on a fledgling firm’s board. However, despite their frequent involvement in executive roles, surgeon– executives are less likely to be helpful, and especially likely to block innovation, as chief executives. Our results emphasize expert users as a critical external dependency for a young firm’s innovation, but show that expertise can backfire when there is a mismatch with a particular organizational role. A key finding is that expert users are more helpful in suggesting a broad variety of solutions to a firm’s innovation problems but less helpful in selecting the best ones for the organization to pursue. Our findings have implications for research on the evolutionary perspective of innovation, user experts and their organizational roles in young firms, and entrepreneurial policy.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how professional users in different organizational roles are related to product innovation in young firms. It examines whether physician-users help or hinder innovation when they occupy inventor, executive, CEO, and board-member roles in surgical instrument ventures.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Proportion of user-inventors, proportion of user-executives, user-CEO indicator, and proportion of user-board members.
- DV(s): Young firm innovation measured as yearly counts of FDA product approvals.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Nonlinear role effects are modeled through squared terms; role type conditions whether user expertise emphasizes variation, selection, or both.

The mechanism is role activation of expert-user knowledge. User-inventors and user-board members are theorized to improve innovation because their tasks emphasize variation or combine variation and selection, allowing physician-users to broaden product ideas and improve relevance. User-executives, especially user-CEOs, are less beneficial because executive roles emphasize selection, and the paper argues that expert users may then rely too narrowly on prior domain knowledge and familiar solutions. The empirical results support positive curvilinear effects for user-inventors and user-board members, no overall support for user-executives, and a negative relationship for user-CEOs.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the evolutionary perspective on innovation by showing that variation and selection effects depend on the organizational role through which expert users participate. It extends user innovation research by moving beyond whether professional users are present to how their expertise is activated or constrained by specific internal roles in young firms. It also contributes to organizational roles research by showing that role tasks can change the innovation consequences of the same domain expertise.

**Practical Implication**
Young ventures should not treat professional user expertise as uniformly beneficial. The findings suggest involving physician-users in technical and governance roles, especially in moderation and together with non-users, while being cautious about placing users in CEO or heavily selection-oriented executive roles. The policy implication is also role-specific: broad restrictions on physician involvement in medical-device firms may reduce conflicts of interest, but may also weaken innovation when they block useful technical or governance contributions.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that the design cannot completely rule out reverse causality or selection into roles, so they use fixed effects, rich controls, lagged variables, instrumental variables, IPTW regressions, and robustness tests to address alternative explanations. The setting is surgical instrument ventures, where user influence is especially salient, so the findings may be weaker in other medical-device segments or industries. The paper also notes limited data on founders and on users' medical practice experience.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on the specific roles that user-founders take after founding and how those paths affect innovation. They also suggest examining how differences in users' capabilities and backgrounds, including educational, professional, and medical practice experience, moderate the relationships between user roles and innovation.
