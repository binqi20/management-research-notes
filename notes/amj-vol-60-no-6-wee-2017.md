# Moving from Abuse to Reconciliation: A Power-Dependence Perspective on When and How a Follower Can Break the Spiral of Abuse

**Abstract**
Despite the burgeoning research on abusive supervision, the literature lacks an in-depth understanding of how followers can successfully break the spiral of abusive supervision over time and influence their leaders to engage in reconciliatory behaviors following abusive supervision. Using power-dependence theory as our framework, we first examine the specific state of power dependence that predicts abusive supervision. We then theorize balancing operations as coping strategies that the follower can use to address the persistence of abusive supervision over time by changing the power imbalance within the dyad. We hypothesize that through the follower’s approach balancing operations, the leader is more likely to regard the abused follower as someone who is instrumental to his or her pursuit of goals and resources, resulting in a reduction in future abuse and an increase in the leader’s future reconciliation. After developing and validating measures of balancing operations, we test the hypotheses using a three-wave panel design with field data from a real estate company (Study 1). In addition, we strengthen our conclusions by replicating our results through a different field sample in a commercial bank (Study 2). The findings’ theoretical and practical implications for abusive supervision and followership are discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks which state of power dependence in a leader-follower dyad predicts abusive supervision and how followers can change that dyadic power imbalance over time. It further asks whether followers' approach balancing operations can reduce future abusive supervision and increase leaders' reconciliatory behavior after abuse.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Follower's asymmetric dependence on the leader; follower's approach balancing operations versus avoidance balancing operations; abusive supervision at time 2.
- DV(s): Abusive supervision at time 2, abusive supervision at time 3, and leader's reconciliation at time 3.
- Mediators: Leader's dependence on the follower at time 2 in the mediated moderation model.
- Moderators: Follower's approach balancing operations and leader's dependence on the follower moderate the persistence of abusive supervision and the link between abusive supervision and leader reconciliation.

The mechanism is power-dependence based. When the follower is asymmetrically dependent on the leader, the leader has greater power in the dyad and is more likely to abuse the follower. Approach balancing operations, especially coalition formation and value enhancement, increase the leader's dependence on the follower by making the follower more instrumental to the leader's goals and resources. As the leader becomes more dependent on the follower, abusive supervision is less likely to persist and the leader is more likely to engage in reconciliation after abuse; avoidance balancing operations do not produce the same relational change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to abusive supervision research by specifying follower asymmetric dependence on the leader as the dyadic power state most likely to produce abuse, rather than treating leader power as a static hierarchical fact. It contributes to followership by presenting followers as agentic actors who can use approach balancing operations to alter power dependence, reduce abuse over time, and prompt leader reconciliation. It also develops and validates a balancing operations scale organized around approach and avoidance motivation.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that followers may have coping strategies beyond leaving the organization or managing the personal consequences of abuse. Approach balancing operations can help followers change their relationship with the leader by increasing their instrumental value or collective leverage. The authors also suggest that leaders, followers, and organizations should monitor relational power dependence and encourage mutual high dependence, such as through shared performance goals, before dyadic power imbalance becomes too polarized.

**Limitations**
The authors state that they did not study leaders' strategies to counter followers' balancing operations, including possible power conflict when leaders feel threatened. The research focused on dyadic relations rather than power dependence within teams with multiple followers under one leader. The two empirical studies were based in China, so cultural setting may condition the strength or form of the effects.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on leaders' counter-strategies when followers use balancing operations and on power-dependence patterns in teams rather than only dyads. They also recommend studying antecedents and contingencies of followers' use of balancing operations, including legitimacy perceptions, self-efficacy, locus of control, and organizational events. Future research should further investigate moral licensing and cleansing in abusive supervision and examine balancing operations in other cultural settings.
