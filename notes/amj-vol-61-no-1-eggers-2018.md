# Motivation and Ability? A Behavioral Perspective on the Pursuit of Radical Invention in Multi-Technology Incumbents

**Abstract**
We examine the role of incumbent firms in developing radical technologies, distinguishing between their motivation and ability to develop radical inventions. We argue that the motivation to pursue radical invention will be strongest when performance is moderately below aspiration, and will weaken as performance either falls substantially below aspiration or rises substantially above aspiration. At the same time, ability will increase with performance, resulting in a behavioral mismatch between ability and motivation—firms overinvest in radical invention when performance is moderately below aspiration and underinvest when performance is substantially above aspiration, with these effects being stronger for broad, multi-technology firms. We show empirical support for our arguments using patent data, using an empirical approach that distinguishes the potential radicalness of an invention from its eventual success. Our study highlights the role of established firms as a source of radical technologies, and examines the behavioral biases that may limit firms from pursuing competenceenhancing radical technologies.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and why incumbent firms pursue potentially radical inventions and whether their motivation to pursue radical invention aligns with their ability to develop successful radical technologies. It focuses on variation within established, multi-technology firms rather than the standard entrant-versus-incumbent framing of radical technological change.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Technology-area performance relative to aspirations, split into above-aspiration and below-aspiration performance; local technological productivity; technological diversification; overall firm technological performance.
- DV(s): Whether a patent is potentially radical, logged forward citations for potentially radical patents, and whether a potentially radical patent becomes a breakthrough.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Technological diversification and overall firm technological performance relative to aspirations.

The mechanism combines behavioral theory and a capability-based account of invention. Firms are most motivated to pursue radical invention when technology-area performance is moderately below aspirations because underperformance encourages riskier search, but this motivation falls when performance is far below aspirations or substantially above aspirations. Ability moves differently: stronger prior performance in a technology area indicates stronger capabilities and therefore higher expected success for potentially radical inventions. This creates a mismatch in which firms may overinvest in radical invention when moderately below aspirations and underinvest when substantially above aspirations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends behavioral theory of the firm by linking performance feedback not only to the choice to search radically but also to the likely success of that search. It shows that the same performance conditions that motivate distant search may not be the conditions under which firms are most capable of succeeding.

It also contributes to radical innovation research by recentering incumbent firms as sources of competence-enhancing radical inventions. The paper separates motivation from ability and demonstrates that incumbents' difficulty is not simply lack of willingness or capability, but a systematic mismatch between where they are motivated and where they are able.

**Practical Implication**
Not reported in paper.

**Limitations**
The study relies on patent data, so it observes patented invention rather than all inventive effort and does not directly observe commercialization outcomes. Patent citations and breakthrough status are useful measures of technological success, but they do not fully capture market value or strategic value to the firm.

The empirical design requires prior performance in a technology class, which limits the main analysis to existing firms and technology areas where firms already have patenting experience. The authors also note that they examine the development of radical inventions rather than the later decision to commercialize them.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether the motivation-ability mismatch also appears in commercialization decisions after radical inventions have been developed. This would clarify whether performance relative to aspirations shapes a firm's motivation and ability to commercialize successful radical inventions.

Additional work could use alternate measures of technological invention, such as scientific publications, and could examine radical invention by established firms outside technology areas in which they are already active.
