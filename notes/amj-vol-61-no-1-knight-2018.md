# Organizational Affective Tone: A Meso Perspective on the Origins and Effects of Consistent Affect in Organizations

**Abstract**
Grounded in an open systems perspective, we build and test new theory about how the kinds of industries in which an organization participates influence organizational affective tone and connect to workforce strain. We propose that the more an organization’s activities lie in consumer-centric industries (e.g., service, retail), the more positive and less negative the organization’s affective tone. We connect consumer-centric industry participation and affective tone by explaining how personnel policies and organizational structure generate and sustain consistent positive and negative affect throughout an organization. Additionally, we examine the effects of organizational affective tone on workforce strain. The results of a survey-based study of 24,015 human resource managers, top management team members, and employees of 161 firms largely support our predictions. We discuss the implications of considering macro contextual factors for understanding affect in organizations.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why some organizations develop a more positive or negative affective tone and whether that tone matters for workforce strain. It examines whether consumer-centric activity shapes organizational affective tone through personnel practices and organizational design.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Consumer-centric activity, measured as the firm's participation in service and retail activities.
- DV(s): Positive organizational affective tone, negative organizational affective tone, employee exhaustion, and employee sick days.
- Mediators: Emotion-focused personnel practices and centralization; formalization was hypothesized and tested but not supported as a mediator.
- Moderators: Post-hoc analyses examine whether consumer-centric activity moderates the relationship between affective tone and workforce strain.

The central mechanism is a meso open-systems process. Organizations whose domains include more consumer-centric activity place greater value on positive affect and lower negative affect, and this logic is embedded through emotion-focused personnel practices and lower centralization. Formalization was hypothesized as part of the structural pathway but was not supported in the results. Organizational affective tone then functions as a contextual resource or demand: positive tone is expected to reduce workforce strain, while negative tone is expected to increase it.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a meso explanation for collective affect by linking organizational affective tone to the macro domain in which an organization creates value. It extends affect research beyond small-group emotional contagion by showing how organizational design and personnel practices can embed consistent affect across a whole firm. It also connects organizational affective tone to workforce strain as a contextual demand or resource.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that leaders can shape organizational affective tone through choices about personnel practices and organizational design, especially centralization. Firms moving toward more consumer-centric activity may need to cultivate affective capacity slowly through selection, promotion, structure, leader behavior, and training. The results also suggest that affective tone is relevant to employee exhaustion and sickness absence, although the strain effects vary across contexts.

**Limitations**
The study uses cross-sectional data, so causal conclusions remain tentative, especially for the relationship between affective tone and workforce strain. The sample is limited to medium-sized German firms, which restricts generalizability to other countries and larger multinational organizations. The split-sample design supports multi-source measurement but prevents tests of individual-level and cross-level mechanisms such as trait affectivity.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other macro factors, such as economic conditions, that may shape organizational affective tone. Studies could compare firms within the same industry to explain strategic deviations from the average affective tendency of consumer-centric organizations. Longitudinal or quasi-experimental designs are also needed to test causal ordering and cross-level interactions between organizational affective tone and individual strain.
