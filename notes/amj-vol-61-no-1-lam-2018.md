# Does Proactive Personality Matter in Leadership Transitions? Effects of Proactive Personality on New Leader Identification and Responses to New Leaders and their Change Agendas

**Abstract**
Despite the growing frequency of leadership transitions and their significant impact on team and organizational performance, little research has examined why and how teams develop identification with a new leader, or their subsequent receptiveness to the new leader’s change initiatives. Drawing from the contrast and congruence effects and the theoretical perspectives of leader identification, this study empirically tests a model in which the congruence of new leaders’ and their teams’ proactive personalities foster new leader identification, as well as the team’s behavioral responses to the new leader’s change agenda. This effect is strongest when the new leader’s proactive personality is higher than that of the former leader’s proactive personality (positive contrast). Our findings of a four-wave “before-and-after” transition survey of 155 hotel employees and 51 new leaders, achieved through polynomial regression analyses, proved very insightful. We found that the congruence between a new leader’s and his or her team’s proactive personalities, and the positive contrast between a former leader’s and the new leader’s proactive personalities, enhanced new leader identification and the team’s shared identification with the new leader’s change agenda, and thereby led the team to exhibit more behavioral engagement with, and voice behavior about, the new leader’s change agenda.

**Research Question**
Why and how do teams develop identification with a new leader during leadership transitions, and how does that identification shape teams' receptiveness to the new leader's change agenda? The paper asks whether congruence between team and new-leader proactive personalities, conditioned by the contrast between former and new leaders' proactive personalities, predicts new leader identification, change-agenda identification, engagement, and voice.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Congruence between the team's proactive personality and the new leader's proactive personality; contrast between the former leader's and new leader's proactive personalities.
- DV(s): Team engagement with the new leader's change agenda and team voice behavior about the new leader's change agenda.
- Mediators: New leader identification and the team's shared identification with the new leader's change agenda.
- Moderators: Former-new leader proactive personality contrast, especially positive contrast where the new leader is more proactive than the former leader.

The mechanism is an identification process activated by leadership transition. When a proactive new leader is paired with a proactive team, the leader and team are more likely to share norms, values, and a sense of oneness, which strengthens identification with the new leader and the leader's change agenda. This congruence effect is stronger when the new leader's proactive personality positively contrasts with the former leader's proactive personality, because team members evaluate the new leader against the former leader as a salient reference point. Stronger identification then carries through to more engagement with, and voice about, the change agenda.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends leadership-transition research beyond CEO and top-management succession by explaining middle- and lower-level leader transitions through team-level identification mechanisms. It shows that reactions to a new leader depend not only on new leader-team congruence but also on the contrast between the former and new leader.

The paper also extends proactivity research by integrating proactive personality congruence with leader identification, rather than treating proactive personality as only an individual-level predictor of proactive behavior. It adds a before-and-after transition design with former-leader, new-leader, and team-member data, improving on designs that rely only on posttransition perceptions.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should consider both fit with the team and contrast with the former leader when appointing new leaders during transitions. A proactive new leader may be especially effective when the team is also proactive and the former leader was less proactive.

The findings also imply that newly appointed leaders can benefit from visibly demonstrating their positive proactive characteristics early in the transition. Training and onboarding can help new leaders establish identification with teams and build receptiveness to change agendas.

**Limitations**
The study's data were collected only in China, so the authors caution that the findings may not generalize to cultures with different power-distance, relationship-orientation, or communication patterns. The research context is also a hospitality setting undergoing leadership transitions, which may differ from more routine or less dynamic work contexts.

The study focuses on proactive personality and therefore cannot rule out alternative trait-based explanations such as psychological resilience, openness to change, perspective taking, or political skill. It also measures proactivity primarily as proactive personality, leaving room for future work on different forms and meanings of proactive behavior, including potentially harmful proactivity.

**Future Research**
Future research could compare high- and low-context cultures to test whether new leader-team proactive personality congruence operates similarly across cultural settings. Additional studies could examine whether congruence in other traits, such as resilience or openness to change, affects leadership transitions.

The authors also call for work on routine work contexts and on different forms of proactivity. Future studies could investigate whether proactive leader-team congruence remains beneficial when tasks are structured and repetitive, or when proactive behavior is viewed as disruptive rather than constructive.
