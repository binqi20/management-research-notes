# The Mixed Blessing of Leader Sense of Humor: Examining Costs and Benefits

**Abstract**
Workplace humor is ubiquitous, yet scholars know little about how it affects employees’ behaviors in organizations. We draw on an emerging psychological theory of humor— benign violation theory—to suggest that a leader’s sense of humor often conveys counternormative social information in organizations. We integrate this theory with social information processing theory to develop hypotheses about the effects of a leader’s sense of humor on follower behavior. We suggest that although a leader’s sense of humor is positively associated with leader2member exchange and ultimately work engagement, it can also signal to followers the acceptability of norm violation at work. These perceptions in turn are positively associated with followers’ deviance. Furthermore, we propose that these indirect effects are moderated by leader aggressive humor. Data from two three-wave field studies in China and the United States provide support for our hypotheses. Taken together, our results suggest that a leader’s sense of humor can be a mixed blessing and elicit unforeseen negative behaviors from their followers.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how and when a leader's sense of humor affects follower behavior in both beneficial and harmful ways. It specifically tests whether leader humor increases follower deviance through perceived acceptability of norm violations while also increasing work engagement through leader-member exchange.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader sense of humor.
- DV(s): Follower workplace deviance and follower work engagement.
- Mediators: Perceived acceptability of norm violations for the deviance path; leader-member exchange for the work-engagement path.
- Moderators: Leader aggressive humor, which strengthens the deviance path and weakens the work-engagement path.

The central mechanism is social signaling. Drawing on benign violation theory, the authors argue that humor often involves benign norm violations; integrating social information processing theory, they argue that followers treat leader humor as information about what kinds of conduct are acceptable. This creates a mixed process: leader humor can make norm violation seem acceptable and thereby relate to deviance, but it can also signal openness and relational permissiveness, improving LMX and work engagement. Aggressive humor changes the signal by making the norm violation more hostile, amplifying the negative path and attenuating the positive path.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces benign violation theory into organizational humor research and integrates it with social information processing theory to explain why leader humor can have both positive and negative follower outcomes. It challenges the assumption that leader humor is uniformly beneficial by showing that the same broad behavior can relate to deviance and engagement through different mediators. It also advances humor research by distinguishing general leader sense of humor from aggressive humor style as a boundary condition.

**Practical Implication**
The authors caution leaders and organizations against treating humor as an unqualified leadership asset. Leaders can use humor to support relational quality and engagement, but should minimize aggressive humor because it can damage relationships and communicate tolerance for norm violations. Organizations can also reduce the deviance risk by clarifying conduct norms and socializing employees into shared organizational values.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their leader sense-of-humor measure may not be fully valence-free, even though supplemental evidence and Study 2 correlations reduce this concern. The studies examine only one boundary condition, leader aggressive humor, and rely on self-reported deviance. The field designs are correlational, so the evidence supports the proposed direction more strongly than alternative models but does not definitively establish causality.

**Future Research**
Future research should develop stronger organizational measures of humor and test other leader humor styles, such as self-deprecating humor. It could examine follower characteristics, additional outcomes such as creativity or group conflict, and other mediating mechanisms. Experimental studies and research in industries where humor is explicitly expected would help clarify causality and boundary conditions.
