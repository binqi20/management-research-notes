# Gender-inclusive Gatekeeping: How (Mostly Male) Predecessors Influence the Success of Female CEOs

**Abstract**
Male-typed leadership schemas have been widely acknowledged as barriers to women’s success in leadership roles. We explore how local organizational agents and contexts enable women leaders to overcome these barriers and achieve success at the highest levels in firms. Specifically, we focus on chief executive officer (CEO) succession events and study how several facets of predecessor CEOs and the succession context combine to influence incoming women’s post-succession performance. We conduct a qualitative comparative case study of all CEO successions that involved female successors between 1989 and 2009 across the largest corporations in the United States. Our findings suggest that women’s success occurred when a confluence of local firm-level factors and attributes of the (mostly) male predecessors promoted genderinclusive gatekeeping during succession. Our qualitative comparative analysis approach reveal three recipes for female success: “handing over the legacy,” “partnering the legacy,” and “turning around the legacy.” Moreover, a comparison to a matchedsample of men CEO succession events shows that these three recipes for success are unique to women. Based upon our findings, we propose that male predecessors’ gender-inclusive gatekeeping facilitates female leaders’ success and occurs when local enabling conditions and the embedded context enact agentic and structural mechanisms to alter leadership schemas.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how predecessor CEOs and local succession contexts shape whether incoming women CEOs overcome male-typed leadership schemas and achieve high post-succession firm performance. It focuses on why some female CEO successors succeed while others do not, rather than treating CEO gender as a uniform performance effect.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core conditions: predecessor legacy, successor insider origin, governance structure, inherited performance, and embedding context.
- Success recipes: handing over the legacy, partnering the legacy, and turning around the legacy.
- Agentic mechanisms: predecessor mentoring, sponsorship, endorsement, and post-succession partnering.
- Structural mechanisms: local role and power imprints, inclusive organizational practices, and governance arrangements that alter local leadership expectations.
- Comparative boundary: the sufficient configurations for women CEOs do not map cleanly onto matched male CEO successions.

The process account is configurational: women CEOs' success emerges when predecessor legacy and successor insider origin combine with governance and context to make gender-inclusive gatekeeping possible. Long-tenured or founder predecessors can sponsor, endorse, and partner with insider female successors while local role imprints and inclusive practices reduce the salience of global male-typed leadership schemas. The three recipes differ in whether power is fully handed over, shared through partnership, or retained by the predecessor to support a turnaround.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a midrange theory of gender-inclusive gatekeeping in which mostly male predecessors can help alter local leadership schemas for women entering CEO roles. It shifts research on women executives away from sex-difference comparisons and toward within-gender variability shaped by local organizational agents, governance structures, and succession histories. It also extends executive succession and gatekeeping research by showing that predecessor influence can be inclusive rather than only constraining or exclusionary.

**Practical Implication**
Boards and incumbent CEOs should treat CEO succession as a local context in which predecessor behavior, governance design, and inclusion practices can materially affect women successors' chances of success. Long-tenured predecessors can support women successors through visible sponsorship, careful handoff or partnering, and role-specific development before succession. Firms in male-dominated contexts may need structural inclusion practices and clear power transfer to counteract male-typed leadership expectations.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that generalizing fsQCA findings requires care, and the empirical setting is restricted to rare female CEO successions in large U.S. firms. The analysis captures observable predecessor, successor, governance, and performance conditions, but other predecessor attributes such as prestige, openness, empathy, or leadership style remain unexamined. The matched male comparison is informative but does not exhaust the gendered dynamics of executive succession.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether predecessor legacy and successor insider origin also enable gender-inclusive gatekeeping in lower-level leadership transitions and other professions. Scholars could examine additional predecessor attributes, successor characteristics, professional-network relationships, internal labor markets, and other local governance or institutional conditions. The paper also calls for more work on men in leadership roles, including whether masculine role expectations shape why similar succession conditions work differently for male successors.
