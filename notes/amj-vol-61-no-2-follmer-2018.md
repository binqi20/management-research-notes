# Resolution, Relief, and Resignation: A Qualitative Study of Responses to Misfit at Work

**Abstract**
Research has portrayed person–environment (PE) fit as a pleasant condition resulting from people being attracted to and selected into compatible work environments; yet, our study reveals that creating and maintaining a sense of fit frequently involves an effortful, dynamic set of strategies. We used a two-phase, qualitative design to allow employees to report how they become aware of and experience misfit, and what they do in response. To address these questions, we conducted interviews with 81 individuals sampled from diverse industries and occupations. Through their descriptions, we identified three broad responses to the experience of misfit: resolution, relief, and resignation. Within these approaches, we identified distinct strategies for responding to misfit. We present a model of how participants used these strategies, often in combination, and develop propositions regarding their effectiveness at reducing strain associated with misfit. These results expand PE fit theory by providing new insight into how individuals experience and react to misfit—portraying them as active, motivated creators of their own fit experience at work.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how people become aware of and experience misfit at work, and what people do in response to misfit. It focuses on employees' own descriptions of misfit rather than treating misfit only as low scores on person–environment fit measures or as a simple antecedent of dissatisfaction and turnover.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering conditions: Misfit may follow organizational change, role change, new supervisors or coworkers, gradual disillusionment with values in use, or social signals from others.
- Core experience: Employees describe misfit as painful, stressful, isolating, and salient even when they fit other parts of the work environment.
- Response approaches: Resolution seeks to reduce the source of misfit through exit, internal transfer, changing the environment, or changing the self.
- Relief-seeking: Surface-level behavior change, buffering misfit with areas of fit, and temporal framing reduce the pain of misfit without removing the underlying incompatibility.
- Resignation: When resolution and relief-seeking fail, employees may distance themselves from work or take pride in misfit while continuing to experience strain.

The process begins when employees reassess fit after change, disillusionment, or social cues and experience misfit as a problem requiring action. Employees often first consider leaving but frequently defer exit because of limited mobility, embeddedness, career investment, or concern about job-hopping. They then combine resolution and relief-seeking strategies over time; if these do not work, resignation strategies allow them to endure misfit until exit becomes possible or the condition remains tolerable.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends person–environment fit theory by showing that fit is not only a selection or attraction outcome but an active, effortful, and dynamic process of maintaining compatibility at work. It develops a qualitative model of misfit responses that goes beyond dissatisfaction and turnover by distinguishing resolution, relief-seeking, and resignation. It also links misfit to stress and coping processes, showing how employees appraise whether to leave, change the environment, change themselves, or manage the strain of unresolved incompatibility.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can retain and develop employees by recognizing when employees are trying to resolve misfit and supporting personal change, mentoring, internal transfer, or environmental reform. Because misfit is multidimensional, managers should look for areas of fit that can buffer misfit elsewhere while also addressing the source of incompatibility. The paper cautions that asking employees to change values, ethics, or authenticity may create proud misfits who resent the organization rather than employees who genuinely fit better.

**Limitations**
The authors state that their sampling strategy limits generalizability: Phase 1 participants self-selected into a study about fit and misfit, and Phase 2 purposively sampled people who had recently experienced work changes and already identified themselves as misfits. The study did not deliberately model national culture, even though data collection began in the United Kingdom and ended in the United States. The qualitative design develops propositions and process insight rather than testing causal effects or estimating population prevalence.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the propositions on broader samples and examine whether the model extends beyond the United Kingdom and United States, especially in Eastern cultural contexts where fit may be viewed differently. Additional work could study individual differences and contextual factors that shape whether employees choose personal change, environmental change, buffering, internal transfer, or pride in misfit. The authors also call for research connecting workplace and nonwork responses to misfit and for work on the long-term consequences of surface-level behavior change and taking pride in misfit.
