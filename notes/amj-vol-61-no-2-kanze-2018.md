# We Ask Men to Win and Women Not to Lose: Closing the Gender Gap in Startup Funding

**Abstract**
Male entrepreneurs are known to raise higher levels of funding than their female counterparts, but the underlying mechanism for this funding disparity remains contested. Drawing upon regulatory focus theory, we propose that the gap originates with a gender bias in the questions that investors pose to entrepreneurs. A field study conducted on question-and-answer interactions at TechCrunch Disrupt New York City during 2010 through 2016 reveals that investors tend to ask male entrepreneurs promotion-focused questions and female entrepreneurs prevention-focused questions, and that entrepreneurs tend to respond with matching regulatory focus. This distinction in the regulatory focus of investor questions and entrepreneur responses results in divergent funding outcomes for entrepreneurs whereby those asked promotion-focused questions raise significantly higher amounts of funding than those asked prevention-focused questions. We demonstrate that every additional prevention-focused question significantly hinders the entrepreneur's ability to raise capital, fully mediating gender's effect on funding. By experimentally testing an intervention, we find that entrepreneurs can significantly increase funding for their startups when responding to prevention-focused questions with promotion-focused answers. As we offer evidence regarding tactics that can be employed to diminish the gender disadvantage in funding outcomes, this study has practical as well as theoretical implications for entrepreneurship.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether gender-based differences in investor questions during startup funding Q&A interactions help explain why male entrepreneurs raise more venture funding than female entrepreneurs seeking comparable capital. It further asks whether entrepreneurs can reduce this disadvantage by switching from prevention-focused to promotion-focused answers.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Entrepreneur gender; investor question regulatory focus; experimentally manipulated promotion-focused versus prevention-focused questions and answers.
- DV(s): Startup funds raised in the field study; experimental funding allocations to startup vignettes.
- Mediators: The prevalence of prevention-focused investor questions mediating gender's effect on funding; entrepreneur responses with matching regulatory focus helping perpetuate the Q&A dynamic.
- Moderators: Investor gender was tested and did not qualify the main question-focus effect; the experiment crossed question focus, answer focus, and entrepreneur gender.

The mechanism is that gender stereotypes lead investors to ask male entrepreneurs promotion-focused questions about gains, growth, and opportunity, while asking female entrepreneurs prevention-focused questions about risk, losses, and defensibility. Entrepreneurs tend to match the regulatory focus of the questions they receive, so female entrepreneurs are induced to position their ventures as avoiding losses rather than pursuing gains. This Q&A dynamic lowers funding outcomes, while switching prevention-focused questions into promotion-focused answers can improve allocations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on gender inequality in venture funding by shifting attention from direct investor preferences and entrepreneur demand to the processual Q&A interaction through which bias is enacted. It extends regulatory focus theory to venture funding and introduces motivational matching as a mechanism connecting question framing, entrepreneur positioning, and capital allocation. The paper also bridges micro-level social psychology and macro-level startup funding outcomes by showing how individual interaction patterns can reproduce gendered resource disparities.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that entrepreneurs, especially women, can improve funding outcomes by answering prevention-focused questions with promotion-focused responses that redirect attention to growth, market opportunity, and upside potential. The paper also implies that venture capitalists should monitor and balance the regulatory focus of their questions so they do not systematically ask women to prove they will avoid losses while asking men to describe how they will win.

**Limitations**
The field study is correlational, even though it controls for startup age, quality, capital needs, past experience, and related factors. The setting is TechCrunch Disrupt New York City, a public pitch-competition context with 23 female entrepreneurs among 189 ventures, so the pattern needs testing in other funding contexts. The experiment improves causal identification but uses scripted vignettes and immediate hypothetical funding allocations rather than real investment decisions over time.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on whether the regulatory focus of investor questions varies by gender in online settings such as crowdfunding platforms, where face-to-face gender cues may be less vivid. They also suggest studying contexts where women are not a minority or where startup uncertainty is reduced by stronger track records. Future work could examine specialized domains such as social entrepreneurship or corporate social responsibility, where prevention-oriented framing may be more positively valued, and test whether the effect changes after temporal delay.
