# Are Family Firms Good Employers?

**Abstract**
Family firms employ about 60% of the global workforce. While it is widely assumed that they are good employers, data about their conduct are mixed. In this study, we extend stewardship and agency theories to test competing propositions about the impact of family on employment practices using data from 14,961 private Belgian firms over a 19year period. Higher investments, lower dividend payout, and higher risk tolerance indicate that family firms are better financial stewards of their companies than nonfamily firms. However, family firms are worse organizational stewards than nonfamily firms: they offer lower compensation, invest less in employee training, and exhibit higher voluntary turnover and lower labor productivity. Further, and contrary to earlier research, we find that financial practices in private family firms do not change over time, and that the deleterious influence of family on employment practices rises with both firm age and with heightened family involvement. Together, our findings suggest that a more nuanced understanding of stewardship and agency theory is needed to understand the impact of family on the governance of private firms.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether family firms are good employers when compared with nonfamily firms. It tests whether family control is associated with financial stewardship and organizational stewardship, especially compensation, off-site training, dismissals, voluntary turnover, and labor productivity.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Family firm status, family board involvement, family-firm identity overlap, and firm age.
- DV(s): Financial stewardship, compensation, off-site training, dismissals, voluntary turnover, and labor productivity.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Firm age, family board involvement, and family-firm identity overlap in post hoc moderation tests.

The mechanism contrasts stewardship and agency accounts of family control. Stewardship theory predicts that family identification, long-term orientation, and concern for stakeholders should support investment in the firm and its workforce. Agency theory predicts that controlling families may protect family welfare and control while shifting costs onto nonfamily employees. The findings separate these dimensions: family firms appear to be better financial stewards but worse organizational stewards, and the negative employment-practice pattern strengthens with age and family involvement.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes by distinguishing financial stewardship from organizational stewardship rather than treating either dimension as a proxy for the whole construct. It extends agency and stewardship theory from governance and performance to workforce-wide employment practices in private firms. It also adds evidence that family control can support long-term financial investment while still producing poorer employee-facing practices.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that Belgian employees should be cautious about assuming family firms offer better employment conditions, because the study finds lower pay, less training, higher voluntary turnover, and lower productivity in family firms. Family business owners should attend to how family control may weaken workforce commitment and human capital development. Policymakers concerned with job quality may need to consider family-firm employment practices because family firms employ a large share of the workforce.

**Limitations**
The study examines a restricted set of employment practices and does not observe climate, job satisfaction, commitment, citizenship behavior, teamwork, or performance appraisal. Its Belgian setting provides strong administrative data but limits direct generalization to countries with different labor regulations, unionization, or employee involvement institutions. Endogeneity remains a general concern, although the authors argue that propensity-score matching, longitudinal data, and robustness tests reduce this risk.

**Future Research**
The authors call for fresh theorizing on firm governance, strategic human resource management, and cooperation in closely held firms. Future research should examine whether stewardship is better captured through organizational climate and employee experiences, and should study how private family firms can address human capital and employee motivation problems. Additional work could test whether similar family-firm employment patterns appear under different national labor-market institutions.
