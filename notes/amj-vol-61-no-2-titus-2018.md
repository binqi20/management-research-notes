# Ripping off the Band-Aid: Scrutiny Bundling in the Wake of Social Disapproval

**Abstract**
Activities that hazard the possibility of increased scrutiny are an unavoidable reality for many firms. While managers may face the need to engage in these activities, there is little research on when managers decide to do so. Existing theoretical perspectives on status quo deviations have not sufficiently addressed how managers order the firm’s essential activities that differ primarily in terms of the scrutiny those activities engender. Drawing from concepts in the accounting and political science literatures, we advance a “scrutiny-bundling” perspective that suggests that firms engage in scrutiny-hazarding action in the wake of social disapproval, assessed in this study via negative media coverage. We further theorize that a strong linkage between the focus of media coverage and the specific scrutiny-hazarding action exacerbates this relationship. We then contend that managers at firms that are either large in size or that perform well relative to their aspirations are less sensitive to social disapproval, and are therefore less likely to engage in scrutiny bundling. We test our hypotheses on a sample of 100 firms in the upstream petroleum industry and find general support for our theories.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when managers engage in scrutiny-hazarding action: core operational activity that is strategically necessary but likely to attract burdensome stakeholder attention. It examines whether social disapproval, measured through negative media coverage, leads upstream petroleum firms to shift more strongly toward resource development relative to resource access.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Social disapproval, measured as prior-year negative media coverage.
- DV(s): Scrutiny-hazarding action, measured as investment in resource development relative to resource access.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Conceptual closeness between media coverage and resource development, aspiration-relative performance, and firm size.

The mechanism is scrutiny bundling: managers facing social disapproval may cluster necessary scrutiny-hazarding actions into an already negative period to reduce prolonged attention over time. The paper argues that stakeholder attention is bounded, so additional scrutiny attached to related actions may be less damaging when bundled with existing disapproval. The full model does not fully support the main social-disapproval effect or the media-focus interaction, but it supports the buffering logic: strong aspiration-relative performance and larger firm size weaken the social disapproval-scrutiny-hazarding action relationship.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a scrutiny-bundling perspective to social evaluation and strategic decision-making research. It shifts attention from avoidance, distraction, problem resolution, or status quo deviation to the timing of unavoidable core actions that may attract scrutiny. It also extends buffering arguments by showing that firm size and aspiration-relative performance can reduce managers' sensitivity to social disapproval.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that managers should treat media disapproval as a signal that may shape rivals' timing of scrutiny-hazarding actions, especially among smaller or lower-performing firms. Firms may use this insight to anticipate competitors' development moves, partnership needs, or asset transactions when those competitors are under social disapproval. The paper does not prescribe scrutiny bundling as normatively appropriate or effective, and managers should not read the evidence as proof that bundling reduces long-term scrutiny.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the relationship may reverse at extreme levels of organizational vulnerability that their data cannot capture. They also acknowledge that, despite controls and robustness tests, they cannot account for every issue shaping investment decision-making. The empirical setting is Canadian upstream petroleum, so the study theorizes scrutiny-hazarding action through a context where resource-development activity is strategically central and likely to attract scrutiny.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine scrutiny bundling at extreme levels of firm vulnerability, where very fragile firms may avoid concentrated scrutiny and very robust firms may invite it. Additional studies could test whether scrutiny bundling appears in other industries, other kinds of social evaluation, and other forms of core but controversial activity. Further work could also examine whether scrutiny bundling actually reduces long-term scrutiny or merely changes its timing.
