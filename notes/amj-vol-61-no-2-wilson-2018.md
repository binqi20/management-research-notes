# Misery Loves Company: An Investigation of Couples’ Interrole Conflict Congruence

**Abstract**
Previous research on interrole (family-to-work and work-to-family) conflict has demonstrated that such conflict is detrimental for outcomes in the work and home domains for employees and their family members. Although research has begun to integrate multiple parties into the interrole conflict literature, studies have overlooked how employee interrole conflict and partner interrole conflict can jointly influence employee outcomes. We advance work–family research by integrating balance theory with the interrole conflict literature to investigate dyadic interrole conflict congruence and challenge the implicit assumption that less interrole conflict always results in superior outcomes. Using a polynomial regression analysis of 141 employee and romantic partner dyads, we demonstrate that congruence between couples’ experiences of family-to-work (but not work-to-family) conflict is positively associated with balance satisfaction, and ultimately employee job satisfaction and partner relationship satisfaction. Thus, when it comes to balance satisfaction and its downstream correlates, the harmful effects of high family-to-work conflict (FWC) are largely mitigated if an employee’s partner shares a similarly high level of FWC, and the beneficial effects of low FWC are largely eliminated if an employee’s partner does not share a similarly low level of FWC.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how congruence or incongruence between an employee's and romantic partner's interrole conflict shapes the employee's balance satisfaction and downstream satisfaction outcomes. It specifically tests whether family-to-work conflict and work-to-family conflict congruence operate differently when both partners' own conflict experiences are considered jointly.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee FWC/WFC, significant-other FWC/WFC, and the polynomial terms representing congruence and incongruence between the two partners' conflict levels.
- DV(s): Employee balance satisfaction as the proximal outcome; employee job satisfaction and significant-other relationship satisfaction as downstream outcomes.
- Mediators: Employee balance satisfaction mediates the relationship between the interplay of employee/SO interrole conflict and downstream satisfaction outcomes.
- Moderators: Gender and number of children were examined in supplemental moderated polynomial regressions and did not significantly moderate the congruence effects.

The mechanism is grounded in balance theory: partners experience congruent levels of interrole conflict as a more balanced and validating state, while incongruence creates tension around work and family role sacrifices. The supported effect is strongest for family-to-work conflict because the family domain is shared by both partners. When both partners experience similar levels of FWC, the employee reports higher balance satisfaction than when only one partner carries high FWC, and this balance satisfaction helps explain employee job satisfaction and partner relationship satisfaction.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces dyadic interrole conflict congruence as a work-family construct and shows that partner conflict is not merely background context but a boundary condition for interpreting an employee's own interrole conflict. It challenges the individual-level assumption that less conflict is always better by showing that high FWC can be less harmful when the partner experiences similarly high FWC. The study also contributes to work-family balance research by linking balance satisfaction to dyadic fit between partners' conflict experiences.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that organizations and supervisors should pay attention to employees' family systems rather than treating work-family conflict as a purely individual issue. Benefits that reduce family demands for both partners, such as reliable childcare support or household-service assistance, may be especially relevant for lowering FWC in dual-earner couples. Supervisors may also need to identify employees whose FWC is higher than their partner's and provide family-supportive emotional and instrumental support.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the research design was mostly cross-sectional, so the study cannot establish causal ordering among interrole conflict congruence, balance satisfaction, job satisfaction, and relationship satisfaction. The study included SO relationship satisfaction as one crossover outcome but did not test a full crossover model with both partners' balance satisfaction and downstream outcomes. The authors call for future work using different and more heterogeneous samples to assess whether the same patterns hold beyond the sampled employees and significant others.

**Future Research**
The authors call for longitudinal research that examines interrole conflict congruence across multiple time periods and tests whether changes in congruence precede changes in job and relationship satisfaction. They also encourage fuller crossover models that include both partners' outcomes, additional outcomes such as performance, health, and general well-being, and international samples across different industries. Future work could examine antecedents of congruence, including shared values and provider-role attitudes, and could compare partner-reported conflict with focal employees' perceptions of both partners' conflict.
