# Saving Face: How Exit in Response to Negative Press and Star Analyst Downgrades Reflects Reputation Maintenance by Directors

**Abstract**
This paper explores the extrinsic and intrinsic motivations driving individual-level responses to reputational threats in the context of the director labor market. Integrating work on reputation with self-determination and identity theories, we theorize that negative attention from the media and star equity analysts threatens directors’ reputations, motivating proactive behavior to mitigate both the external and internal consequences of reputation damage. Using a sample of directors of S&P 1500 firms between 2003 and 2014, we argue and find that negative media coverage and downgrades by star equity analysts are positively related to director exit, even after controlling for firm performance, overall media visibility, and negative events such as lawsuits and financial restatements. We also find that director status intensifies the effect of negative media coverage on exit, serving as the board chair attenuates the effect of star analyst downgrades on exit, and director tenure intensifies the effects of both negative media coverage and star downgrades on exit. In post-hoc testing, we provide further evidence of director reputation maintenance by demonstrating the counterintuitive finding that negative attention from the media and star analysts also increases directors’ likelihood of joining the boards of other S&P 1500 firms.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how prominent individual directors respond when negative external attention toward the firms whose boards they serve on threatens their reputations. It specifically examines whether negative media coverage and star equity analyst downgrades increase director exit, and whether director status, board chair position, and director tenure condition those effects.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Negative media sentiment; star analyst downgrades.
- DV(s): Director exit from the focal firm within a three-year window.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Director status; board chair position; director tenure.

The mechanism is that negative external attention toward a firm threatens directors' reputations and activates both extrinsic motives to avoid external stigma and intrinsic motives to protect identity, competence, fulfillment, and identification with board service. Directors are therefore more likely to exit boards associated with negative media sentiment or star analyst downgrades. The results support positive main effects of both negative media sentiment and star analyst downgrades on director exit; they also show that status intensifies the media effect, board chair position attenuates the star-downgrade effect, and tenure intensifies both effects. Post hoc analyses suggest that directors may also seek additional S&P 1500 board appointments as a reputation-maintenance response.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends reputation research by shifting reputation maintenance from mostly organizational responses to individual-level responses by prominent actors. It integrates reputation theory with self-determination and identity theories to argue that reputational threats can create simultaneous extrinsic and intrinsic motivations for action. It also contributes to corporate governance research by challenging the view that director exit is mainly involuntary settling up and by showing how external market actors shape director labor-market behavior.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that boards and firms should treat negative press and star analyst downgrades as director-retention risks, not only as investor-relations or performance signals. Because longer-tenured directors and some high-status directors are especially responsive to negative attention, firms facing reputational threats may need to manage director identification, role commitment, and perceived reputational exposure if they want to retain board members.

**Limitations**
The authors state that, as with much board research, the study is inferential and uses secondary data, so other motivations beyond reputation or identity may influence director exit and new appointments. They note that directors could leave because negative attention creates additional time demands, or could receive new appointments because other firms target or poach them. They also caution that the context of directors in large corporations may be somewhat unique and may limit generalizability to other prominent individuals.

**Future Research**
The authors call for primary-data or qualitative research that can measure reputation and director identification more directly. They encourage work in other contexts to develop theory on individual-level reputation maintenance and the possible actions individuals use to protect reputations. They also suggest further theory and analysis on additional board appointments, promotions at directors' home firms, and whether internal prestige or positive external attention increases positive outcomes.
