# Not in the Same Boat: How Status Inconsistency Affects Research Performance in Business Schools

**Abstract**
This study examines the consequences of status inconsistency for the performance of multi-unit organizations. Status inconsistency refers to the extent to which social actors occupy status positions accorded different amounts of social esteem and prestige in different social systems. Status inconsistency affects the performance of multi-unit organizations by weakening the well-established positive effects of status on performance because it creates status ambiguity that makes external evaluations of the organization and its individual units more difficult. Distinguishing between high-status and low-status multi-unit organizations, we argue further that status inconsistency is particularly problematic for high-status multi-unit organizations, whereas low-status multi-unit organizations may actually benefit from status inconsistency. We test our arguments using a longitudinal sample comprised of 109 international business schools and their finance, accounting, marketing, management, and operations departments from 2002 to 2013. Our study concludes with a discussion of the contributions our research makes to status theory and research, and the managerial relevance of our findings.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how status inconsistency affects the performance benefits of status in multi-unit organizations. In the business school setting, it examines whether school-level status inconsistency weakens the positive associations between business school status, department status, and department research performance, and whether these effects differ for high- versus low-status schools and departments.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Department status, business school status, and business school status inconsistency.
- DV(s): Department research performance measured with the UTD research contribution score.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Business school status inconsistency moderates the effects of department status and business school status on department research performance; high- versus low-status business schools and departments condition the moderation pattern.

The mechanism is a status-ambiguity argument. Status normally helps external evaluators infer quality and justify decisions, but inconsistent status positions across units make the status of the multi-unit organization and its departments harder to interpret. This weakens status as a signal of perceived quality and as a tool for reducing accountability pressures, especially for high-status schools and high-status departments that have more to lose from status deflation; low-status departments may instead benefit from status inflation through association with higher-status units.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends status inconsistency research from single-unit actors ranked differently across status dimensions to multi-unit organizations whose units differ in status within comparable social systems. It identifies status inconsistency as a boundary condition on cumulative status advantage: status remains valuable, but inconsistent unit-level status can make status less informative and less useful for external evaluation. The paper also adds involuntary co-location within multi-unit organizations to status theory, showing that associations not chosen as exchange ties can still reshape audience status beliefs.

**Practical Implication**
For high-status business schools, the findings imply that large status gaps across departments can reduce the research-performance benefits associated with both school and department status. The authors suggest that, rather than only allocating additional resources to already high-status departments, business schools may improve overall research performance by helping lower-status departments move up the status hierarchy, while still preserving resources needed for high-status departments to remain competitive. They also argue that the importance of status consistency generalizes to other multi-unit organizations that depend on external audiences.

**Limitations**
The authors state that they do not fully explore the scope conditions of the theory. The business school setting is a conservative test because editors usually know their own field's status hierarchy best and may know less about other departments; effects may be stronger when audiences attend closely to all units of a multi-unit organization. The paper also cannot determine the relative importance of the two proposed mechanisms, signaling quality and accountability reduction.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine scope conditions in settings where audiences pay closer attention to all units, such as stock markets where investors and analysts monitor diversified public firms. The authors also point to internal responses to status inconsistency as an open direction, including interdepartmental power struggles and leadership-mandated closing of low-status departments. The discussion further suggests that status inconsistency may complement product-diversification theory by adding a vertical-unrelatedness lens to the horizontal-relatedness focus.
