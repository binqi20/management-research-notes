# Geographic Co-location of Partners and Rivals: Implications for the Design of R&D Alliances

**Abstract**
This study advances previous research on the competitive aspects of research and development (R&D) collaborations that has been mainly interested in knowledge protection concerns in alliances between direct rivals. We join the alliance and agglomeration literatures and argue that geographic co-location between a focal firm’s partner and rivals introduces potential indirect paths of knowledge leakage to rivals. Geographic colocation creates significant risks of unintentional knowledge spillovers to rivals, while it also increases the likelihood of transactions between the partner firm and the rivals in which firm knowledge can be misappropriated. As a consequence of these risks associated with the co-location of partners and rivals, the focal firm is more likely to employ defense mechanisms when designing alliances. In particular, the focal firm will use equity structures to provide greater monitoring, control, and incentive alignment and will reduce the alliance’s scope, as well as task interdependence, to address knowledge leakage concerns.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how geographic co-location between an R&D alliance partner and the focal firm's rivals creates indirect knowledge-protection risks, even when the partner is not itself a direct rival. It examines whether greater risk from partner-rival co-location is associated with more protective alliance governance and design choices.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Risk from partner-rival co-location, measured from the number of nearby rivals co-located with the partner, those rivals' market shares in relevant therapeutic classes, and the importance of those markets to the focal pharmaceutical firm.
- DV(s): Equity alliance, alliance scope, and reciprocal interdependence.
- Mediators: Not directly measured; the theorized channels are unintentional knowledge spillovers, intentional misappropriation through partner-rival transactions, monitoring, control, incentive alignment, restricted knowledge access, and reduced interaction.
- Moderators: Not directly modeled as moderators; the risk construct incorporates more rivals, stronger rival market positions, and greater importance of the relevant product markets to the focal firm.

The mechanism is that partner-rival co-location creates informal and formal routes through which knowledge shared in an R&D alliance can reach the focal firm's rivals. Focal firms respond by using equity structures to improve monitoring, control, and incentive alignment, by narrowing alliance scope to reduce exposed knowledge, and by lowering task interdependence to limit interaction and knowledge transfer. The empirical results support all three predicted associations: higher partner-rival co-location risk increases the likelihood of equity governance and decreases the likelihood of broad scope and reciprocal interdependence.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends alliance research beyond direct rival dyads by showing that competitive risks can arise through indirect geographic links between partners and rivals. It connects alliance governance and agglomeration theory by identifying partner-rival co-location as a downside of clustered knowledge environments. It also contributes to research on incumbent firm-technology venture alliances by shifting attention to incumbents' knowledge-loss risks and by treating task interdependence as an alliance design choice rather than only an exogenous task attribute.

**Practical Implication**
Firms designing R&D alliances should consider not only who the partner is, but also where the partner is located relative to product-market rivals. When partner-rival co-location makes knowledge leakage more damaging, the paper suggests that equity participation, narrower scope, and lower task interdependence can function as protective design choices.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on the risk of knowledge spillovers and misappropriation, while potential knowledge spill-ins from rivals through the partner are treated as secondary and not directly tested. The empirical setting is domestic U.S. biopharmaceutical R&D alliances, so the findings may not transfer directly to other industries or alliance purposes. The use of secondary data also limits the measurement of task interdependence and does not directly observe the specific pathways through which different kinds of knowledge leak.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether partner-rival co-location affects firm behavior and outcomes outside the R&D alliance context. Studies could test other responses to these risks, such as partner selection, relational governance, acquisitions, nondisclosure agreements, or project choice. Primary-data research could also distinguish formal and informal leakage pathways and examine whether different kinds of knowledge travel through different mechanisms.
