# A Social Mindfulness Approach to Understanding Experienced Customer Mistreatment: A Within-person Field Experiment

**Abstract**
We apply a social mindfulness lens to understand the phenomenon of perceived customer mistreatment. Recognizing that both recall of prosocial acts and perspective taking invoke the motivation to be mindful in social interactions, we investigated whether these two types of interventions affect customer service employees' experience of customer mistreatment. Additionally, we investigated whether these two interventions might also buffer the relation of employees' daily experience of customer mistreatment and their negative mood at the end of the workday. Finally, we examined whether the interventions, via their effects on daily experience of customer mistreatment and afternoon negative mood, could reduce dysfunctional coping responses in the evening (i.e., employee rumination and maladaptive shopping). We conducted a within-person field experiment utilizing a daily experience sampling approach with 94 customer service employees whom we surveyed for 15 consecutive workdays. Consistent with our expectations, both interventions significantly reduced the daily experience of customer mistreatment compared to a control condition. Recall of prosocial action also significantly buffered the positive relation of daily experience of customer mistreatment with afternoon negative mood. Moreover, both interventions had significant indirect effects on dysfunctional coping responses in the evening. We discuss theoretical and practical implications of these findings.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether daily social mindfulness interventions can reduce service employees' subjective experience of customer mistreatment and its affective and behavioral consequences. It specifically tests whether morning recall of prosocial action and perspective taking reduce daily perceived customer mistreatment, buffer the customer mistreatment-negative mood link, and indirectly reduce evening rumination and maladaptive shopping.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Morning recall of prosocial action intervention; morning perspective taking intervention; daily perceived customer mistreatment in the downstream affective and coping paths.
- DV(s): Daily customer mistreatment experience; afternoon negative mood; evening rumination; evening maladaptive shopping.
- Mediators: Daily customer mistreatment experience and afternoon negative mood in the intervention-to-evening-outcome paths; afternoon negative mood in the customer mistreatment-to-evening-outcome paths.
- Moderators: Recall of prosocial action intervention on the daily customer mistreatment-afternoon negative mood relation; perspective taking intervention was hypothesized as the same moderator but was not supported.

The mechanism is that recall of prosocial action and perspective taking activate other-oriented motivation, making employees more socially mindful in interpreting customer behavior. Through motivated information processing, these interventions are expected to direct attention toward customers' needs and situational explanations, reducing perceived mistreatment and weakening negative affective reactions. Afternoon negative mood then carries the effects of perceived customer mistreatment into self-focused maladaptive evening outcomes, namely rumination and maladaptive shopping.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends customer mistreatment research by distinguishing actual customer mistreatment from employees' subjective experience of mistreatment and by identifying malleable antecedents of that experience. It integrates the social mindfulness framework with motivated information processing theory to explain why other-oriented daily interventions can shape employees' perception and appraisal of customer behavior. It also extends work-to-nonwork spillover research by linking customer mistreatment to evening maladaptive shopping as well as rumination.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that low-cost morning routines, such as asking service employees to recall recent prosocial actions or take customers' perspective, can reduce perceived mistreatment and downstream maladaptive coping. The authors caution that organizations should not use these interventions to normalize abusive customer behavior or pressure employees to accept mistreatment. They argue that such routines should be paired with employee-oriented support, including supervisor support and emotion regulation training.

**Limitations**
The authors note that, except for the interventions, all study variables were self-reported by employees, although they used time separation, baseline controls, and group-mean centering to reduce method concerns. Generalizability may be limited by the Chinese call center sample and by cultural differences that could shape perspective taking and customer mistreatment perceptions. They also discuss possible practice, Hawthorne, and demand effects, the incomplete counterbalancing of the crossover design, small raw effect coefficients, and the lack of direct evidence about whether intervention effects persist beyond the same evening.

**Future Research**
Future research should test boundary conditions of the interventions, including individual differences in other-orientation, personality, customer service quality, and supportive or empowering organizational contexts. The authors call for studies in other cultures and with objective or other-reported measures, including actual customer mistreatment behaviors and customer outcomes. They also encourage research that directly examines the mechanisms behind perspective taking and prosocial recall, identifies when prosocial recall is beneficial versus detrimental, and studies how long intervention effects persist and how frequently interventions should be administered.
