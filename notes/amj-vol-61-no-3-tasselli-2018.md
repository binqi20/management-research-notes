# When Brokerage Between Friendship Cliques Endangers Trust: A Personality–Network Fit Perspective

**Abstract**
Workplace friendship obligations of openness and favoritism are likely to conflict with organizational norms of discretion and neutrality. This dilemma is especially apparent for Simmelian brokers, who divide time and attention across multiple, otherwise disconnected, friendship cliques. In two samples, we found support for the core idea that the fit between the requirements of the network role and the personality of the individual facilitates trust. Simmelian brokers are trusted by their friends if they exhibit a role-appropriate diplomatic personality style involving flexibility of self-presentation (high self-monitoring) and inhibition of verbal loquaciousness (low blirtatiousness). Of course, not everyone engages in Simmelian brokerage. Some individuals experience a strongly cohesive situation—i.e., a single friendship clique within which they are embedded. For these nonbrokers, we hypothesized and found that the most appropriate trait combination likely to maintain the trust of a group of tightly bound colleagues involved a forthright, be-true-to-yourself, loquacious personality style (i.e., low self-monitoring, high blirtatiousness). In introducing a personality–network fit perspective concerning whether Simmelian brokers are trusted by their colleagues, we help reconcile discrepancies in prior literature concerning whether these brokers are paralyzed into indecision by cross-pressures. Brokers who flexibly and guardedly manage individuality facilitate interconnection across cliques.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether personality affects whether Simmelian brokers are trusted by their friends. It examines whether the fit between a person's network role across disconnected friendship cliques and their self-monitoring and blirtatiousness shapes affect-based trust from clique members.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Simmelian brokerage, self-monitoring, blirtatiousness, and their two-way and three-way interactions.
- DV(s): Affect-based trust from clique members; cognition-based trust is used as a Hospital-sample robustness outcome.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Simmelian brokerage moderates the effects of self-monitoring and blirtatiousness on trust, and the focal model is a three-way interaction among Simmelian brokerage, self-monitoring, and blirtatiousness.

The mechanism is a personality-network fit argument. Simmelian brokers face competing expectations from disconnected friendship cliques, so trust is maintained when high self-monitoring enables flexible self-presentation and low blirtatiousness restrains disclosure across clique boundaries. For nonbrokers embedded in a single clique, the better fit reverses: low self-monitoring and high blirtatiousness support an authentic, forthright, and loquacious style that preserves trust inside a cohesive friendship group.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to micro-foundations research on organizational social networks by showing that brokerage outcomes depend not only on network position but also on the fit between structural demands and individual personality. It helps reconcile pessimistic accounts of Simmelian brokerage as paralyzing with more optimistic accounts of brokerage as generative by showing that some brokers maintain trust when their personality style fits the role. It also extends personality-network fit beyond brokers by theorizing a contrasting personality configuration for trusted nonbrokers inside single cliques.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that managers should recognize Simmelian brokers who connect otherwise disconnected friendship cliques, because such brokers can support knowledge transfer and coordination when they maintain trust. It also suggests that role effectiveness may be supported by encouraging diplomatic behaviors, such as flexible self-presentation and guarded disclosure, among people who operate across cohesive social groups.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the cross-sectional data prevent them from establishing causality or completely excluding spurious effects. They also note that distinctive expertise, abilities, or social skills could explain both trust and network position, although they use covariates and robustness tests to reduce this concern. The study focuses on interpersonal trust rather than testing whether brokerage-personality fit directly affects performance outcomes.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how personality explains success over time when individuals oscillate between brokerage across groups and deep engagement within a group. The authors also point to research on blirtatiousness in authentic leadership, personality profiles of the cliques being bridged, and personality differences in producing and consuming gossip. Additional work could examine whether Simmelian brokerage roles elicit existing personality expression or nurture longer-term personality change.
