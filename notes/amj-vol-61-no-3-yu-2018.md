# Is Leader–Member Exchange Differentiation Beneficial or Detrimental for Group Effectiveness? A Meta-Analytic Investigation and Theoretical Integration

**Abstract**
Despite the burgeoning number of studies that have examined leader–member exchange (LMX) differentiation, definitive conclusions regarding its effects remain scarce. We propose a theoretical framework for studying LMX differentiation through an equity– equality perspective derived from allocation preferences theory, allowing us to elucidate both the beneficial and detrimental influences LMX differentiation can have on workgroups. In a meta-analytic investigation including 4,114 workgroups and 21,745 individuals, we found that LMX differentiation was detrimental to collective harmony and solidarity, as indicated by a consistent negative relationship with emergent states and group processes. A theoretical integration of our predictions within an input– mediator–outcome model of group effectiveness revealed a more complex pattern of relationships with group performance. By simultaneously considering the proximal and distal nature of group outcomes, we found that emergent states and group processes not only mediated the negative indirect relationship, but also suppressed the positive direct relationship between LMX differentiation and group performance. These findings demonstrate the utility of an equity–equality framework for understanding LMX differentiation in workgroups and imply that there are tradeoffs associated with differentiation that must be considered when predicting group effectiveness criteria.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether LMX differentiation is beneficial or detrimental for group effectiveness. It specifically examines how an equity-equality perspective and an input-mediator-outcome model can explain why LMX differentiation may support group performance while harming collective harmony and solidarity.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): LMX differentiation, defined as variability in LMX relationship quality among members of the same workgroup.
- DV(s): Group performance, emergent states, and group processes.
- Mediators: Emergent states and group processes mediate the negative indirect relationship between LMX differentiation and group performance.
- Moderators: Task interdependence and cultural collectivism are theorized moderators; supplemental analyses examine power distance, workgroup LMX quality, group size, job type, and operationalization of LMX differentiation.

The mechanism is a tradeoff between equity and equality principles. Differentiation can align with equity by directing scarce leader resources toward members who contribute more to collective performance, creating a positive direct path to performance. At the same time, differentiation violates equality expectations, weakens emergent states and group processes, and thereby creates negative indirect paths to performance. The meta-analytic path model shows that these negative indirect paths suppress the positive direct relationship, producing a near-zero total relationship between LMX differentiation and group performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides a parsimonious group-level framework for reconciling positive and negative perspectives on LMX differentiation. By integrating allocation preferences theory with the input-mediator-outcome model, it distinguishes proximal social outcomes from distal performance outcomes and shows why LMX differentiation should not be treated as unilaterally beneficial or detrimental.

The study also extends LMX differentiation research beyond individual and dyadic theorizing by synthesizing group-level evidence across 41 samples. It shows that emergent states and group processes carry consistent negative relationships with LMX differentiation, while the performance relationship depends on separating direct and indirect pathways.

**Practical Implication**
The paper cautions leaders that differentiating exchange relationships creates tradeoffs rather than a simple performance lever. Leaders should align differentiation decisions with the workgroup's goals and recognize that motivating higher output from some members can increase dissatisfaction and antagonism in the broader group.

The authors also suggest that procedural fairness may help mitigate the negative effects of either differentiating or not differentiating. Organizations may therefore benefit from developmental programs that train managers to enact justice principles when allocating scarce leader resources.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the meta-analysis contains a relatively limited number of group-level LMX differentiation studies, even though these studies represent the available body of work at the time. They also abstract operationally distinct outcomes into broader categories of performance, emergent states, and group processes, which supports synthesis but may obscure more specific construct-level differences.

The paper further notes that most primary studies did not use designs that can definitively establish causality. Moderator tests for task interdependence and cultural collectivism were also limited by the amount of information reported in primary studies.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the proposed causal ordering with designs better suited to causal inference, such as controlled laboratory or longitudinal designs. The authors also point to job categories, workgroup size, cultural power distance, and measurement operationalization as promising moderators for future LMX differentiation research.

Future studies should also work toward greater consensus on how LMX differentiation is measured. The paper argues that standard deviation and variance are more appropriate operationalizations of dispersion than agreement indices such as rWG.
