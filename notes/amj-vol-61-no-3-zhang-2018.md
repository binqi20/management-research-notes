# Does “Could” Lead to Good? On the Road to Moral Insight

**Abstract**
Dilemmas featuring competing moral imperatives are prevalent in organizations and are difficult to resolve. Whereas prior research has focused on how individuals adjudicate among these moral imperatives, we study the factors that influence when individuals find solutions that fall outside of the salient options presented. In particular, we study moral insight, or the discovery of solutions, other than selecting one of the competing moral imperatives over another, that honor both competing imperatives or resolve the tension among them. Although individuals intuitively consider the question “What should I do?” when contemplating moral dilemmas, we find that prompting people to consider “What could I do?” helps them generate moral insight. Together, these studies point toward the conditions that enable moral insight and important practical implications.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when individuals facing right-versus-right moral dilemmas find moral insight solutions rather than simply choosing one moral imperative over another. It specifically tests whether prompting people to ask what they could do, rather than what they should do, increases divergent thinking and thereby helps them generate solutions that honor or reconcile competing moral imperatives.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Could mindset versus should mindset; in Study 4, dilemma type also varied between right-versus-right and hybrid dilemmas involving self-gain.
- DV(s): Moral insight, measured through concrete coding of unconventional solutions and abstract ratings of whether responses honored competing moral imperatives; Study 4 also measured ethical violations and ethicality ratings.
- Mediators: Divergent thinking, measured through self-reports, counts of solutions, and independent coder ratings of boundary-spanning or outside-the-box responses.
- Moderators: Dilemma type in Study 4, distinguishing right-versus-right dilemmas from hybrid dilemmas that included self-gain.

The mechanism is that a should mindset narrows attention toward weighing competing obligations and choosing among salient options, while a could mindset makes additional possibilities cognitively available. This broader mindset increases divergent thinking, which helps individuals formulate responses that move beyond forced tradeoffs and preserve multiple moral imperatives. Across the focal studies, this process increased moral insight in hypothetical, incentive-compatible, dyadic, and enacted right-versus-right contexts, but the could mindset did not add moral insight in hybrid dilemmas where self-gain was also present.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to behavioral ethics by shifting attention from misconduct and right-versus-wrong temptation to the problem of resolving right-versus-right moral dilemmas. It introduces moral insight as a distinct ethical problem-solving outcome and theorizes it as an application of creative cognition to moral conflicts. It also qualifies prior work linking creativity to unethical behavior by showing that creative, divergent thinking can support ethical resolution when the task is to reconcile competing moral imperatives rather than justify self-serving wrongdoing.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that managers, ethics representatives, counselors, and employees can improve moral-dilemma problem solving by beginning with the question “What could I do?” before adjudicating what they should do. This prompt can move conversations away from the two most salient options and toward a broader set of responses that preserve competing obligations. The paper cautions, however, that the evidence does not imply replacing should-based moral reasoning; rather, could-based exploration can precede and enrich subsequent ethical evaluation.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Studies 1 to 3 measured what participants said they would do in scenarios rather than directly observing decisions that affected key constituents. They also note that the experiments were conducted in labs or online panels, which limits external validity even though several dilemmas were adapted from real employee situations. A further limitation is possible demand effects from explicitly asking participants to adopt either a could or should mindset.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on when expansive could mindsets lead to moral insight versus unethical creative justification, especially as self-gain becomes more tempting. They also propose field research on whether instilling a could mindset helps employees generate moral insight in real workplace ethical challenges. Additional work is needed on how observers evaluate the ethicality of moral insight decisions, because resolving the focal tension may reduce the salience of ethical sacrifice.
