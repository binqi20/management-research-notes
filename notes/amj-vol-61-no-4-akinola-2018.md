# To Delegate or Not to Delegate: Gender Differences in Affective Associations and Behavioral Responses to Delegation

**Abstract**
Effectively delegating work to others is considered critical to managerial success, as it frees up managers’ time and develops subordinates’ skills. We propose that female leaders are less likely than male leaders to capitalize on these benefits of delegating. Although delegation has communal (e.g., relational) and agentic (e.g., assertive) properties, we argue that female leaders, as compared to male leaders, find it more difficult to delegate tasks due to gender-role incongruence. In five studies, we draw upon social role and backlash theories to show that women imbue delegation with more agentic traits, have more negative associations with delegating, and feel greater guilt about delegating than men. These associations result in women delegating less than men and, when they do delegate, having lower-quality interactions with subordinates. We further show that reframing delegation as communal attenuates women’s negative associations with delegation. These findings reveal that even when a given behavior has both agentic and communal elements, perceptions of agency can undermine women’s engagement in them. However, emphasizing the communal nature of seemingly agentic acts may encourage women’s engagement in such critical leadership behaviors. These findings have theoretical and practical implications for research on gender differences and leadership behavior in the workplace.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether women and men differ in how they perceive, emotionally associate with, and behaviorally enact delegation. It specifically tests whether delegation’s agentic and communal properties create gender-role incongruence for women, leading to more negative affective associations, less delegation, lower-quality subordinate interactions, and whether communal framing can attenuate these responses.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader or participant gender; delegation condition versus no-delegation condition; delegation-as-communal framing versus control framing.
- DV(s): Perceived agency and communality of delegation, negative and positive affective associations, guilt, fear of backlash, delegation choice and amount, interaction quality with subordinates, and task performance.
- Mediators: Negative affect and positive affect in Study 4 mediation tests; guilt and fear of backlash were theorized mechanisms but were measured after delegation and not tested as mediators there.
- Moderators: Communal framing of delegation in Study 5; subordinate gender and confederate gender were manipulated or controlled but were not the focal theoretical moderators.

The mechanism is that delegation is both communal and agentic, but the agentic element is more salient and costly for women because social role and backlash expectations penalize women’s agency more than they reward women’s communality. This produces more negative affective associations with delegation, including anxiety, guilt, and fear of backlash. Those associations can reduce women’s willingness to delegate and degrade the quality of interactions when women do delegate. Reframing delegation as a way to help subordinates develop makes the communal element more salient and reduces women’s negative affective responses.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to gender and leadership research by showing that leadership behaviors can be gender-role paradoxical rather than simply agentic or communal. It provides empirical evidence that women and men differ in delegation behavior and identifies affective associations as a process through which gender-role incongruence shapes delegation. It also extends backlash and social role theory by showing that communal reframing can change women’s responses to a seemingly agentic leadership behavior.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that organizations should not assume that delegation is experienced similarly by male and female leaders, even when delegation is formally encouraged as a developmental practice. Framing delegation as mentoring, developing, and empowering subordinates may help women view delegation as more communal and selfless, reducing negative affect and potentially increasing delegation. The paper also suggests that failure to address these affective barriers may hinder women’s time management, subordinate development, and leadership effectiveness.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Study 3 did not find gender differences in self-reported delegation among managers, while Study 4 found behavioral differences in a student laboratory sample, leaving open whether behavioral gender differences generalize across organizations, experience levels, and hierarchy. They also note that Study 4 involved participants with no ongoing subordinate relationship, which may suppress or exaggerate delegation behavior compared with real work relationships. A further limitation is that Studies 2 and 4 operationalized delegation in a time-scarce, agentic way, which may have minimized delegation’s communal aspects.

**Future Research**
The authors call for behavioral studies of delegation across men and women in organizations with varied work experience and hierarchical levels. They recommend longitudinal work on how familiarity and relationship length with subordinates affect women’s likelihood of and comfort with delegation. They also call for research that distinguishes associated, prospective, and retrospective anxiety; tests broader negative affective mechanisms such as anticipatory guilt; and compares communal with noncommunal or agentic-legitimating reframings of delegation.
