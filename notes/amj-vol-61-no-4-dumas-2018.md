# The Paradox of Family Structure and Plans after Work: Why Single Childless Employees May Be the Least Absorbed at Work

**Abstract**
Existing research has shown that positive family experiences can affect work positively. In this article, however, we consider how family may enhance work even when family experiences are not explicitly positive. We draw on boundary theory and cognitive psychology’s current concerns theory to evaluate how employees’ family structures and associated after-work activities affect their work absorption. A survey of business school alumni (Study 1) revealed that single, childless workers reported lower absorption than workers with other family structures. Further, a daily diary study of university employees (Study 2) showed that employees’ planned after-work activities explained the relationship between family structure and work absorption. Specifically, single, childless workers anticipated fewer domestic after-work activities, resulting in lower work absorption. Due to similarities between domestic responsibilities and work tasks—for example, their obligatory and goal-directed nature—anticipating domestic responsibilities after work reinforces, rather than distracts from, the work mindset, thus keeping employees more immersed psychologically in their work. This finding suggests that having a spouse and/or children can affect employees’ work absorption positively through the anticipation of domestic duties after work. Thus, our study contributes to a more comprehensive view of how employees’ work and nonwork lives are connected.

**Research Question**
How does family structure affect employees' work absorption, and do anticipated after-work domestic or leisure activities explain why single, childless employees may be less absorbed at work than employees with other family structures? The paper asks whether family responsibilities can enhance work absorption even when the relevant family experience is not explicitly positive.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Family structure, especially being single and childless versus all other family structures; anticipated proportions of domestic and leisure after-work activities.
- DV(s): Work absorption, measured as employees' psychological immersion and concentration in work.
- Mediators: Anticipated domestic after-work activities; anticipated leisure after-work activities; supplemental construals of activities as obligatory, goal directed, or an escape from routine.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is anticipatory rather than affect-transfer based. Because domestic responsibilities are obligatory and goal directed, anticipating them can reinforce a work-like mindset and keep employees psychologically immersed in work. Single, childless employees anticipate fewer domestic activities, which reduces this reinforcing process and helps explain their lower work absorption; anticipated leisure activities are associated with lower absorption but do not mediate the family-structure effect.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends work-family enrichment research by showing that family can benefit work through anticipated responsibilities, not only through positive affect, skill transfer, or prosocial motivation. It contributes to boundary theory by linking anticipatory boundary transitions to current work absorption rather than only to after-work recovery or role conflict. It also broadens ideal-worker theorizing by directly examining single, childless workers and showing that being less encumbered by family responsibilities does not necessarily translate into greater work absorption.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not assume that single, childless employees are naturally more available, focused, or work-absorbed because they lack spouse or child responsibilities. Organizations may need broader work-life policies that respect nonfamily commitments and support diverse forms of nonwork life. Employees and managers can also use reminders or planning around goal-directed domestic or nonwork tasks selectively, because anticipated activities can shape absorption before the workday ends.

**Limitations**
The studies rely partly on self-reported survey behavior, creating potential common method variance, although Study 2 uses multiple survey points and family structure is not an attitude measure. The Study 2 and supplement samples are predominantly female, and the authors note that spousal employment status, gender, and unstudied cohabitating heterosexual couples require further exploration. The paper studies absorption rather than time spent at work or productivity, so the findings do not establish effects on objective performance.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine how spousal employment status, gender, parental status, marital status, and cohabitation jointly shape the family-structure effects documented here. Additional work should compare the anticipatory effects of after-work activities with their following-day recovery effects, including moderators such as how active, passive, challenging, goal directed, or routine the activities are. Researchers should also examine whether work absorption, rather than face time or constant accessibility, predicts valued outcomes in professional knowledge work.
