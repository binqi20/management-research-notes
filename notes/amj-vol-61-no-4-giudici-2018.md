# Open-System Orchestration as a Relational Source of Sensing Capabilities: Evidence from a Venture Association

**Abstract**
Research on innovation networks has highlighted the pivotal role that actors with more prominence and power, such as hub firms, may play in orchestrating the activities of other network members along a collective innovation effort. Our study examined the under-theorized, but no less important, type of orchestration that characterizes other organizations, such as business incubators and venture associations, who seek to support the dispersed entrepreneurial efforts of network members. We refer to this type as “open-system” orchestration, as opposed to the commonly studied “closed-system” type performed by hub firms. Our findings reveal how the processes of open-system orchestration differ markedly from those of closed-system orchestration, and detail how these processes influence the micro-foundations of network members’ sensing capabilities. By doing so, we also offer empirical substantiation and theoretical elaboration to the idea that dynamic capabilities might not reside exclusively inside firms, but could be co-created relationally with other parties in the business ecosystem.

**Research Question**
How do open-system orchestrators support the dispersed entrepreneurial efforts and sensing capabilities of network members? The paper asks how this form of orchestration differs from closed-system orchestration by hub firms and how it helps firms recognize new opportunities and form new business relationships.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: open-system orchestration, closed-system orchestration, sensing capabilities, mutual trustworthiness, organizational self-awareness, positive emotional experience, experiential learning, and dispersed collaboration.
- Orchestration processes: building collaborative engagement, facilitating members' introspection, encouraging members' exploration of complementarities, and fostering dispersed collaboration.
- Cognitive and emotional micro-foundations: assumptions of mutual trustworthiness lowered relational barriers, enhanced organizational self-awareness clarified search purposes, and positive emotional experience energized interaction.
- Emergent outcomes: recognition of new entrepreneurial opportunities and formation of new business relationships.
- Boundary conditions: the case was a highly successful, larger-than-average matchmaking initiative, mostly involving SMEs, and WT predominantly engaged in open-system orchestration.

The process model begins with a venture association creating a collaborative context around B2BMatch rather than directing members toward a centrally controlled innovation goal. Through preparatory activities, referrals, event design, and interaction monitoring, WT helped firms reflect on their own resources and identify complementarities with other members. These interactions supported members' sensing capabilities as relationally co-created capacities, while repeated participation also helped some firms internalize search routines beyond the event.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends network orchestration research by theorizing open-system orchestration as a distinct form used by organizations such as incubators, regional agencies, and venture associations to support decentralized entrepreneurial search. It contributes to dynamic capability theory by showing how sensing capabilities can be co-created relationally rather than residing exclusively inside firms. It also elaborates the cognitive and emotional micro-foundations through which trust, self-awareness, and positive affect support opportunity search in innovation networks.

**Practical Implication**
The study suggests that venture associations, incubators, and similar intermediaries can improve members' opportunity search by designing collaborative norms, reflection spaces, high-quality referrals, and interaction settings that lower barriers among participants. It also implies that member firms need to engage thoughtfully and systematically; the benefits of open-system orchestration depend on their preparation, willingness to collaborate, and repeated learning from participation.

**Limitations**
The authors state two boundary conditions. First, most B2BMatch participants were SMEs, where strategic decisions about business development, purchasing, and sales were concentrated in a small team, often including owner-managers. Second, WT engaged predominantly in open-system orchestration, so settings that combine open- and closed-system orchestration may show different or interfering processes.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine open-system orchestration in larger organizations where responsibility for search and business development is more dispersed. The authors also call for research on how open-system orchestration affects new business models, leadership development, and tensions among different classes of dynamic capabilities. Additional work could compare orchestrators that combine open- and closed-system processes to test whether the mechanisms are compatible.
