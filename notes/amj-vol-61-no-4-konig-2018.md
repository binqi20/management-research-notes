# Silver Bullet or Ricochet? CEOs’ Use of Metaphorical Communication and Infomediaries’ Evaluations

**Abstract**
We combine literature on rhetoric and socially situated sensemaking to illuminate the challenges that emerge when chief executive officers (CEOs) try to influence infomediaries by using metaphorical communication—figurative linguistic expressions that convey thoughts and feelings by describing one domain, A, through another domain, B. Specifically, we theorize that because different infomediaries are situated in different thought worlds, CEOs’ use of metaphorical communication has contradictory effects on journalists’ and securities analysts’ evaluations: while it triggers more favorable statements from journalists, it prompts more unfavorable assessments from analysts. Moreover, we integrate findings from cognitive psychology to argue that these contradictory effects increase the more a firm’s performance falls behind market expectations. Our hypotheses find support in an extensive analysis of 937 quarterly earnings calls in the U.S. pharmaceutical, hardware, and software industries, and of journalists’ statements and analysts’ earnings forecasts and recommendations. Our novel theorizing and findings suggest that the use of discursive frames, especially in the form of metaphorical communication, in firms’ interactions with critical audiences creates thought-provoking and thus-far neglected dilemmas. In developing and testing these thoughts, we contribute to and link ongoing conversations in management science, especially discussions of organizational reputation, executive communication, and impression management.

**Research Question**
How does the degree to which a CEO uses metaphorical communication affect the favorability of different infomediaries' assessments of the CEO's firm? The paper focuses on whether journalists and securities analysts respond differently to the same CEO rhetorical device because their sensemaking is socially situated in different professional thought worlds.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO's use of metaphorical communication in quarterly earnings conference calls.
- DV(s): Favorability of journalists' reporting about the firm; favorability of analysts' evaluations, measured through EPS forecast revisions and buy-hold-sell recommendation changes.
- Mediators: Not formally modeled as statistical mediators; the theorized process is infomediaries' socially situated, constituent-minded sensemaking and reliance on rhetorical cues as cognitive shortcuts.
- Moderators: Negative earnings surprise, with positive earnings surprise included as a check.

The mechanism is audience-specific. Metaphorical communication provides broad, familiar, engaging frames that reduce journalists' effort in translating firm information for a mainstream audience, so journalists evaluate firms more favorably when CEOs use more metaphorical communication. Analysts, by contrast, work in a fact-oriented thought world and need precise, unambiguous information for investor audiences, so metaphorical communication becomes distracting or suspect and leads to less favorable evaluations. Negative earnings surprises intensify both effects because poor performance increases reliance on cognitive shortcuts under uncertainty.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on executive communication and CEO-infomediary relations by showing that the rhetorical form of CEO communication, not only the amount or content of information, shapes firm evaluations by critical audiences. It also complicates the common view of metaphor as a rhetorical asset by showing that metaphorical communication can be beneficial with journalists and harmful with analysts, creating a rhetorical dilemma when CEOs address multiple audiences simultaneously. More broadly, it links organizational reputation, executive communication, and impression management through the lens of socially situated sensemaking.

**Practical Implication**
Corporate leaders, coaches, and speechwriters should treat metaphorical communication as an equivocal lever rather than a universal persuasive tool. When communicating with infomediaries, CEOs may need to decide which audience matters most at a given time and adjust rhetorical style accordingly, especially when firm performance disappoints market expectations. More metaphor may help journalists produce favorable coverage but can reduce analysts' favorability; less metaphor may preserve analyst confidence while forfeiting some journalistic appeal.

**Limitations**
The authors note that they use only preliminary measures for whether CEOs' metaphors were aligned with the message being conveyed, leaving open whether metaphor quality, fit, or source-domain choice changes the effects. The design analyzes conference calls and cannot fully rule out endogeneity in CEOs' use of metaphorical communication, although the paper reports extensive robustness checks, simultaneity tests, and residualized models. The setting is limited to U.S. pharmaceutical, hardware, and software firms and to two types of infomediaries, so effects may differ for other industries, cultures, communication channels, or audiences.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine characteristics of metaphorical communication, including whether specific source domains such as sports, journeys, or violence have different effects and whether automated methods can classify such domains reliably. Scholars could also study other CEO communication vehicles, such as interviews, public speeches, presentations, and roadshows, as well as nonverbal delivery cues such as facial expressions, gestures, and tone of voice. The paper further calls for work on other audiences, including customer-advocacy groups, rating agents, shareholder groups, and internal organizational members, where metaphorical communication may produce additional rhetorical dilemmas.
