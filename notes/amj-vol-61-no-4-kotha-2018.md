# Experience and Signaling Value in Technology Licensing Contract Payment Structures

**Abstract**
When commercializing technology, the lack of proven results and a reluctance to invest upfront in resources hamper efforts by firms to work jointly with inventors to bring new discoveries to market. An effective contract payment structure—a mix of upfront and royalty payments—can help overcome these hurdles. We conduct our research in university technology licensing, where licensing managers act as intermediaries to unite inventors and licensee firms. Rather than leveraging their experience to bargain for maximum payments, highly experienced managers offer contractual payment structures that trade lower upfront payments for higher royalty payments in order to signal value. The signal instills confidence in the value of the partnership for skeptical licensee firms, and experienced licensing managers can amplify signals as needed to overcome the uncertainties inherent in technology commercialization. By explicitly addressing these variations in signal strength, we develop new theory that builds on classical signaling principles. We test and confirm these predictions in a sample of over 950 invention-licensing contracts. In addition to advancing signaling theory, our work has implications for academic entrepreneurship, and for how experience shapes valuesharing agreements in collaborative innovations.

**Research Question**
How does licensing manager experience shape technology licensing contract payment structures, and when do experienced managers use lower upfront fixed fees and higher royalty rates as a signal of value rather than bargain for maximum immediate payments? The paper examines whether signaling ability and signaling need jointly explain contract terms in university technology licensing.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Licensing manager experience, measured as the number of prior contracts negotiated and signed by the focal licensing manager at the time of invention licensing.
- DV(s): Commercial success of the licensed invention; negotiated upfront fixed-fee payment; negotiated royalty rate.
- Mediators: The paper theorizes selection of promising inventions and matching with capable licensees as the mechanisms, and tests them in post-hoc analyses rather than as formal mediation models.
- Moderators: Science intensity of the invention, inventor experience, and patent scope.

The mechanism is a signaling tradeoff in contract design. Experienced licensing managers are better able to select promising inventions and match them with capable licensees, so they can credibly give up guaranteed upfront payments in exchange for higher, performance-contingent royalties. This lower-upfront, higher-royalty structure signals confidence in the invention-licensee match to skeptical licensee firms. The signal becomes stronger when information asymmetry is higher or when signal need changes, including science-intensive inventions, more experienced inventors, and broader patent scope.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends signaling theory by integrating the sender's ability to signal with the receiver-side need for a signal, rather than assuming superior information and signal need are fixed. It shows that contract payment structures can function as intentional, recipient-specific signals whose strength varies with licensing manager experience and invention conditions. It also contributes to academic entrepreneurship by linking opportunity selection, partner matching, and licensing contract design as mechanisms through which experience shapes commercialization outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Technology transfer offices and other organizations involved in licensing should treat experience as a capability for selecting inventions, matching them with appropriate partners, and crafting credible contract signals. The findings imply that inexperienced licensing managers may benefit from mentoring, pairing with senior managers, and repeated exposure to licensing deals. Experienced managers should not standardize every contract; they can adjust upfront fees and royalty rates to the uncertainty and information asymmetry in each agreement.

**Limitations**
The authors note that although post-hoc analyses isolate selection and matching mechanisms, future work should verify their relative magnitude more directly. The study is based on one technology transfer office, which offers advantages for isolating licensing capabilities but limits direct generalization to other TTOs, corporate licensors, or non-academic licensing contexts. The data also do not provide comprehensive information on unsuccessful licensing attempts, although the analyses control for unlicensed inventions in licensing managers' portfolios.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine how initial negotiation reference points influence contract structure and should distinguish contract experience from prior interactions with multiple inventors more carefully. Studies using licensing data from multiple TTOs and non-academic corporate licensors could test whether the framework generalizes beyond the focal university context. The authors also suggest applying the framework to other settings where intermediaries help buyers and sellers agree on uncertain quality and value, such as artistic and cultural products or professional athletics.
