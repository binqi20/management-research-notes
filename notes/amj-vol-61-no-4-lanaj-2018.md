# The Benefits of Not Seeing Eye to Eye with Leadership: Divergence in Risk Preferences Impacts Multiteam System Behavior and Performance

**Abstract**
Multiteam systems are large structures of interdependent teams that coordinate via planning before embarking on important projects. Although rarely studied, theory suggests that expression of convergent versus divergent goals and preferences during planning by leadership and component teams matters for multiteam system performance. Addressing this important issue, in a sample of multiteam systems consisting of Air Force officers, we examine how convergent versus divergent preferences for risk expressed during planning by the leadership and component teams impact multiteam system behavior and performance during the performance episode. Drawing from literatures on multiteam systems, diversity, and risk we posit and show that divergent (vs. convergent) preferences for risk have positive implications for multiteam system behavior and performance. Specifically, when the leadership and component teams hold divergent risk preferences, multiteam systems engage in more aspirational behavior and perform better. Alternatively, when the leadership and component teams hold convergent risk preferences, multiteam systems perform worse, partially because they display lower aspirational behavior. Consistent with a learning perspective, the beneficial effects of divergent preferences for risk increase over time. We discuss the implications of our findings for research and practice.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how convergence versus divergence in risk preferences between leadership and component teams during planning affects multiteam system behavior and performance during execution. It further asks whether divergent risk preferences facilitate multiteam system learning over time.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Vertical convergence or divergence between component-team risk preferences and leadership-team risk preferences; horizontal convergence or divergence between East and West component-team risk preferences in supplementary assumption tests.
- DV(s): Multiteam system performance, aspirational behavior, unwarranted risk behavior, and growth trajectories for these outcomes over ten simulation rounds.
- Mediators: Aspirational behavior and unwarranted risk behavior, with mediation supported for aspirational behavior and interpreted cautiously for unwarranted risk behavior because the main effect was not supported.
- Moderators: Time was used to test the learning assumption through growth trajectories rather than as a conventional interaction moderator.

The mechanism is that divergent risk preferences prevent premature consensus during planning and stimulate consideration of broader information, contingencies, and alternative plans. In these authority-differentiated multiteam systems, vertical divergence between leadership and component teams helps the system pursue well-vetted, high-reward aspirational behavior, which in turn improves objective performance. Convergence suppresses this aspirational behavior and is associated with weaker performance. The growth-curve results suggest that divergence becomes more beneficial over time for performance and aspirational behavior, consistent with the authors' learning interpretation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the prevailing multiteam systems argument that goal and plan convergence is generally preferable for system performance. It shows that, for risk preferences in divisional, authority-differentiated multiteam systems, divergence between leadership and component teams can improve performance rather than undermine it. It also contributes to team learning, risk, and diversity research by showing that deep-level divergence can be beneficial in a specific meso-level structure and that its effects strengthen over time.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that leaders of multiteam systems should not treat early disagreement about risk as evidence that the system is failing. During planning for complex, high-stakes work, leadership teams may need to create room for component teams to express divergent risk preferences and avoid rapid agreement for its own sake. The paper also suggests that leaders can deliberately elicit component-team perspectives before stating their own risk preferences, while recognizing that the benefits of divergence may emerge during execution rather than immediately.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the design did not collect survey data on members' attitudes or affective processes, such as psychological safety, team identification, satisfaction, or conflict. They also did not observe individual team-member risk preferences or the decision process through which teams formed a shared risk preference. Additional limitations include the simulation-specific operationalization of aspirational and unwarranted risk behavior, inability to record the full communication process, wide confidence intervals around principal-axis tests, and the fact that authority differentiation was a context rather than an experimentally varied construct.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how individual risk preferences become team-level risk preferences and how within-team divergence interacts with multiteam-system-level divergence. They also recommend studying aspirational and unwarranted risk behavior in other contexts, such as medical, disaster relief, or new product launch multiteam systems. Future work should test other strategic goals beyond risk, examine systems with different coordination structures and boundary-spanning arrangements, and distinguish system-level from component-team-level outcomes.
