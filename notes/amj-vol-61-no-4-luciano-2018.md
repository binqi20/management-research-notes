# Shared Team Experiences and Team Effectiveness: Unpacking the Contingent Effects of Entrained Rhythms and Task Characteristics

**Abstract**
This study explores the conditions under which shared team task-specific (STTS) experiences in crew-based arrangements may negatively influence team effectiveness. We suggest that the entrained rhythms featured in social entrainment theory act as a dual-edged sword with the potential to generate complacency detriments in addition to the commonly cited synchronization benefits. We argue that the manifestation and influence of the countervailing forces (i.e., synchronization and complacency) on the STTS experience—team effectiveness relationship will depend on salient task characteristics (i.e., frequency and difficulty). More specifically, frequently performed tasks create conditions for complacency to manifest (generating an inverted-U shaped relationship between STTS experience—team efficiency), whereas infrequently performed tasks do not (generating a positive, linear relationship). We further this distinction by layering on task difficulty that, we posit, acts to amplify the respective negative and positive consequences. Analyses of archival data from 8,236 surgeries performed over one year at a large hospital located in the southwestern region of the United States were consistent with our hypotheses and 30 semi-structured interviews with operating room personnel added richness and precision to our theory. Ancillary analyses on patient post-surgery recovery rate yielded additional insights. Implications and future directions are discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when shared team task-specific experience improves or harms team effectiveness in crew-based teams. It specifically examines whether task frequency and task difficulty shape whether entrained rhythms produce synchronization benefits or complacency detriments for surgical team efficiency and patient post-surgery recovery.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Shared team task-specific experience, including a squared term for curvilinear effects in frequent tasks.
- DV(s): Team efficiency as the focal outcome; patient post-surgery recovery rate as an ancillary team-effectiveness outcome.
- Mediators: Knowledge accrual, synchronized entrained rhythms, and complacency are theorized mechanisms; they are not modeled as formal statistical mediators.
- Moderators: Task frequency and task difficulty.

The mechanism is that prior shared experience with the same team members on the same type of task creates shared knowledge and entrained rhythms that can help crew members coordinate efficiently. For infrequent tasks, the paper argues that these synchronization benefits are less likely to be offset by complacency, producing a positive relationship between STTS experience and efficiency, especially on difficult tasks. For frequent tasks, deeply familiar procedure rhythms can reduce vigilance and system monitoring, so high STTS experience can become inefficient through complacency; task difficulty amplifies these negative consequences because difficult cases have less room for error.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends social entrainment theory by treating entrained rhythms as a dual-edged process that can generate both synchronization benefits and complacency detriments. It contributes to team effectiveness research by showing that shared experience is not uniformly beneficial and that task frequency and task difficulty are important boundary conditions in crew-based arrangements. It also integrates archival and interview evidence to connect shared team experience, task characteristics, efficiency, and patient recovery in a field setting rather than a laboratory context.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that crew staffing decisions should consider both task frequency and task difficulty rather than simply maximizing repeated team membership. For infrequent and more difficult procedures, managers may benefit from building higher levels of shared team task-specific experience; for frequent procedures, especially difficult ones, managers should watch for complacency and use interventions such as briefing, teamwork training, and debriefing to maintain vigilance without destroying useful rhythms. In hospitals, the paper frames these staffing and intervention choices as consequential for operating-room efficiency, patient recovery, and cost.

**Limitations**
The authors note that complacency is difficult to examine outside the laboratory because interviews and observations are vulnerable to evaluation apprehension and Hawthorne effects. The archival data cannot directly measure the strength or dynamics of entrained rhythms during each surgery, so the study infers those processes from records and interview evidence. The empirical setting is a single U.S. hospital and one type of crew-based team, which limits comparison across hospitals, procedure-volume profiles, and team structures.

**Future Research**
The authors call for richer measures of entrained rhythms, including experience-sampling designs that track rhythm dynamics over time and distinguish why surgeries become faster or slower. They also recommend studying additional task characteristics, unanticipated events, attention, and situational cues that may shape complacency and synchronization. Further work should compare hospitals with different procedure volumes, examine other team structures such as intact teams, ad hoc teams, and multiteam systems, and assess individual differences, composition, strategic staffing, and caregiver well-being implications.
