# A Micro-institutional Inquiry into Resistance to Environmental Pressures

**Abstract**
This article contributes to the emerging stream of micro-institutional research, which zooms in on the internal organizational processes that are responsible for organizations’ differential responses to the external environment. Specifically, the investigation offers new knowledge of how organizational identity processes can shape whether decisionmakers will resist versus give in to environmental pressures. Building on the notion that organizational identity acts as a filter through which decision-makers relate to the external environment, I develop the theoretical argument that strong organizational identification increases resistance to environmental pressures due to two mechanisms: (1) it bolsters the decision-maker’s certainty and (2) it deflects the decision-maker’s attention from the environment. A series of laboratory experiments not only test the mediated relationship between organizational identification and resistance to environmental pressures but also contrast different types of organizational identity. The empirical results support the hypothesized positive link between organizational identification and resistance, which becomes particularly strong when the organizational identity is normative (vs. utilitarian). The findings reported here enrich institutional theory by adding microfoundations to organizational practice adoption decisions and shedding new light on relevant enabling conditions for agency and within-field heterogeneity.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why organizational decision-makers facing the same environmental pressures vary in whether they resist those pressures or give in to them. More specifically, it asks whether organizational identification affects resistance, whether certainty and attention explain that effect, and whether normative versus utilitarian identity content changes resistance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Strength of organizational identification; organizational identity type, especially normative versus utilitarian identity.
- DV(s): Resistance to environmental pressures, measured as staying with an initial decision despite mimetic peer-group information or resisting ISO 31000 adoption pressure.
- Mediators: Decision-maker certainty and attention to environmental stimuli.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The proposed mechanism is cognitive and identity-based. Strong organizational identification increases resistance because it bolsters the decision-maker's certainty and reduces attention to environmental stimuli. Normative identity further strengthens resistance relative to utilitarian identity because it turns attention inward toward organizational ideals rather than outward toward competitive or institutional comparison.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional theory by adding microfoundations to explanations of organizational responses to environmental pressures. It shows that organizational decision-makers' identification and identity content help explain within-field heterogeneity rather than treating organizations as passively conforming to macro-level pressures. It also integrates organizational identity research with institutional theory and demonstrates how experiments can be used to test micro-level institutional mechanisms.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests, with explicit caution, that organizations may manage environmental conformity versus resistance partly through identity salience and identity content. When organizations seek to implement practices that challenge established field templates, leaders who strongly identify with the organization may be more willing to resist environmental pressure. The implication is not that resistance is always beneficial, but that identity processes can shape whether organizations automatically conform or maintain agency under pressure.

**Limitations**
The paper largely brackets how organizational identity is constructed and instead focuses on its consequences for resistance. It also treats individual decision-makers as representatives of organizational actors, while acknowledging that organization-level outcomes may emerge through more complex aggregation, interaction, and interdependence. Finally, the experimental findings generalize through theory rather than direct one-to-one correspondence with large organizations in field settings.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how organizational identity develops and is negotiated in organizations, especially through qualitative, practitioner, field-replication, and mixed-method designs. Further work could study how social interaction and collective aggregation translate individual-level responses into organization-level action. The paper also calls for research on competing institutional logics, non-binary responses to institutional pressure, institutional work strategies such as challenging or manipulating institutions, and legitimacy judgments by external audiences.
