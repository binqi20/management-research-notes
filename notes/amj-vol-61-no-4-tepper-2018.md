# Examining Follower Responses to Transformational Leadership from a Dynamic, Person–Environment Fit Perspective

**Abstract**
We invoke the person–environment fit paradigm to examine on a daily basis follower affective, attitudinal, and behavioral responses to transformational leadership needed and received. Results from two experience sampling method (ESM) studies suggested that positive affect was higher on days when transformational leadership received fit follower needs (compared to days when the amount received was deficient or in excess of follower needs) and on days when absolute levels of fit was higher. We also found that positive affect mediated the within-person effects of transformational leadership needed and received on subordinates’ daily work attitudes (Studies 1 and 2) and organizational citizenship behaviors (Study 2). Supplemental analyses in Study 2 revealed that subordinates need more transformational leadership when they experience more challenge stressors, face greater uncertainty at work, and perform more meaningful work.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how followers respond on a daily basis when the transformational leadership they receive fits, falls short of, or exceeds the transformational leadership they need. It also asks whether positive affect explains the within-person links from transformational leadership needed and received to daily work attitudes and organizational citizenship behaviors, and which daily conditions predict stronger need for transformational leadership.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Daily transformational leadership needed, daily transformational leadership received, their fit/misfit, and absolute level of fit.
- DV(s): Positive affect, job satisfaction, satisfaction with supervision, OCB-O, OCB-I, and exploratory daily transformational leadership needed.
- Mediators: Positive affect mediates the joint within-person effects of transformational leadership needed and received on job satisfaction, satisfaction with supervision, and organizational citizenship behaviors.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper as formal moderators; the paper models fit, deficiency, excess, and absolute level of fit through polynomial response-surface terms.

The mechanism combines person–environment fit theory with affective events theory. Transformational leadership is treated as an environmental supply, while followers' daily need for such leadership varies with activated psychological needs and work conditions. Fit between needed and received transformational leadership should raise positive affect; deficiency and excess should reduce it. Positive affect then carries the daily fit experience into work attitudes and discretionary citizenship behavior, while challenge stressors, uncertainty, and meaningful work help explain when followers need more transformational leadership.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to transformational leadership theory by challenging the assumption that more transformational leadership is always better. It theorizes and tests transformational leadership as a dynamic, episodic phenomenon whose effects depend on day-level fit with follower needs. It also extends P–E fit theory by showing the value of modeling need fulfillment within persons over time rather than treating within-person variation as error.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that transformational leadership training should help leaders diagnose followers' dynamic needs and calibrate the amount of transformational behavior they provide. Leaders should avoid both under-supplying and over-supplying transformational leadership, because excess can be less favorable than fit. They should also pay attention to the type of stressors followers face, because challenge stressors and uncertainty evoke need for transformational leadership whereas hindrance stressors may call for a more transactional response.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their conceptualization relies on Bass' model of transformational leadership, despite criticisms of that model and unresolved questions about whether its between-person structure is isomorphic at the within-person level. They also did not examine fit for other forms of leadership needed and received, such as transactional leadership. Finally, the studies focused on a limited set of outcomes and did not test why some leaders are better than others at tailoring transformational behavior to follower needs.

**Future Research**
The authors call for more focused tests of the Bass model at within- and between-person levels, and for dynamic P–E fit research that embeds transformational leadership within broader models of leadership needed and received. They encourage future ESM work on additional outcomes such as justice, stress, intrinsic motivation, creativity, trust, empowerment, and organizational commitment. They also recommend research on leader-side predictors of fit, including depletion, personality, trait empathy, and political skill.
