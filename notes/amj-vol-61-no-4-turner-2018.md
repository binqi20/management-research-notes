# Watching the Clock: Action Timing, Patterning, and Routine Performance

**Abstract**
This study contributes to research on the temporality in routines by proposing that action timing is a patterning mechanism, distinct from the sequence-based patterning that has been the focus of current research. We examine the effect of this mechanism by investigating how recurrent action timing—and its reproduction—affect the effectiveness of routine performance. We further theorize and show that multiplicity in the performative and the ostensive aspects of the routine influence the strength of timingbased patterning and its effects. We test our ideas using a unique dataset that tracks specific garbage collection actions and captures their precise timing, combined with corresponding data on customer complaints about missed collections. Our findings support our theoretical predictions and advance current understanding about different temporal patterning mechanisms in routines.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how the timing of actions affects patterning and routine performance in dispersed, interdependent service routines. It specifically examines whether reproducing action timing improves effective routine performance and whether multiplicity in performative and ostensive aspects strengthens or weakens that timing-based effect.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Reproducing the timing of organizational actions in a focal routine performance, operationalized as same-street/same-time matches between the current route performance and prior performances within the previous 30 days.
- DV(s): Effective routine performance, measured as the count of customer reports of missed waste collection service.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Historical consistency in action timing, customers' bureaucratic experience, customers' co-present point of view, and different performance conditions represented by city-observed holidays.

The mechanism is timing-based patterning. Recurrent action timing creates a temporal structure that lets outsiders anticipate insider actions using clock time rather than the full action sequence of the routine. When current performances reproduce historically established timing, customers' actions align more closely with collection crews' actions, reducing missed-service complaints. This effect is stronger when historical timing consistency and customer co-presence make the timing pattern more recognizable, and weaker when bureaucratic experience makes customers more likely to follow formal rules rather than timing-based expectations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends routine dynamics theory by distinguishing timing-based patterning from sequence-based patterning. It shows that clock time can function as an external temporal patterning mechanism for routines performed across time, space, and organizational boundaries. It also contributes by showing that timing-based patterning is contingent: multiplicity in performative and ostensive aspects shapes whether reproduced timing improves routine effectiveness.

**Practical Implication**
For managers of interdependent service routines, the paper implies that consistent timing can improve customer-perceived effectiveness but can also create stronger customer dependence on historically established timing. Organizations therefore need to recognize that formal rules, such as putting containers out by a specified time, may be displaced by customers' learned timing expectations when service timing becomes predictable.

**Limitations**
The archival data limited the authors' ability to observe customer presence, action, or inaction directly. The study uses demographic proxies for customer point of view, such as retirement age and government employment, which leaves room for alternative explanations. The effectiveness measure captures customer complaints and therefore reflects the customer vantage point more than organizational or crew evaluations of performance.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for future studies that observe customer or outsider cognition and action more directly, for example by combining fine-grained workflow data with surveys or diary accounts. It also encourages research comparing insider and outsider evaluations of routine effectiveness and examining timing-based patterning in settings with greater unpredictability, innovation demands, or interactions across multiple routines.
