# A Signaling Theory of Institutional Activism: How Norway’s Sovereign Wealth Fund Investments Affect Firms’ Foreign Acquisitions

**Abstract**
Combining perspectives from institutional activism and signaling theory literatures, we suggest that an activist sovereign wealth fund (SWF) can serve as an intermediary signaler, providing cues about host countries’ institutional environment to internationalizing firms. By publicizing its investments and engaging in institutional activism, a SWF can signal the institutional quality of host countries to internationalizing firms, thus allowing them to overcome the well-known “lemons problem” in international decisionmaking. We examine the impact of a SWF’s signals on firms’ ownership choices in their foreign acquisitions. Our empirical analysis of Norway’s socially responsible SWF and firms from Norway and Sweden during 1998–2011 shows that firms are more likely to take larger commitments via full equity ownership in acquisitions in host countries where Norway’s SWF holds larger investments. The signaling effect of the SWF weakens for conational firms, suggesting that proximity to the signaler may generate alternative information channels that diminish the signaling value of foreign investments. Similarly, institutional harmonization enabled by intergovernmental organizations connecting the home and host countries weakens the signaling value of SWF investments. Our findings point to intermediary signaling by activist institutional investors and the salience of their signals for firms’ international decision-making.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether investments by an activist sovereign wealth fund can serve as intermediary signals of host-country institutional quality for internationalizing firms. It tests whether Norway’s SWF investments affect firms’ full versus partial ownership choices in foreign acquisitions, and whether that signaling effect is weakened by conationality and by home-host IGO linkages.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Norway SWF cumulative equity investments in a host country.
- DV(s): Acquiring firm ownership choice in a foreign acquisition, operationalized as full versus partial acquisition and checked with percentage of shares acquired.
- Mediators: Not directly measured; the theorized mechanism is intermediary signaling about host-country institutional quality under information asymmetry.
- Moderators: Conationality with Norway’s SWF and joint IGO membership between the acquirer’s home country and the host country.

The mechanism is that a transparent, activist, government-owned SWF can make host-country institutional quality more observable to firms evaluating foreign acquisition commitments. Larger SWF investments are interpreted as credible signals because the SWF has government-linked information access, long-term investment incentives, and institutional activism capacity. The signal is less valuable when firms have alternative information channels through conational proximity or when IGO ties already reduce institutional uncertainty and harmonize expectations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a signaling theory of activist SWFs as third-party intermediary signalers whose investments can unintentionally inform a broad audience about host-country institutional quality. It extends classical two-party signaling and relational-tie signaling by theorizing signals generated through an intermediary’s actions toward an entity of interest rather than through the entity’s deliberate self-presentation. It also specifies boundary conditions: proximity to the signal sender and the broader signaling environment shape when firms rely on SWF investment signals.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that managers of internationalizing firms may treat transparent SWF investments as potentially useful cues about host-country institutional environments. This is especially relevant when firms prioritize long-term objectives and institutional qualities aligned with the SWF’s goals. The implication is not that SWF investment should mechanically determine entry or ownership, but that it can become one input when assessing foreign acquisition commitment under institutional uncertainty.

**Limitations**
The article does not present a standalone limitations section. The empirical setting centers on Norway’s socially responsible and unusually transparent SWF, Norwegian and Swedish acquiring firms, and acquisitions during 1998-2011, so the evidence may not generalize cleanly to less transparent, more politically motivated, or less activist SWFs. The study also treats host-country institutional quality as inferred through SWF investment signals rather than directly observing all institutional changes activated by the SWF.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how different SWF purposes, transparency levels, asset allocations, and forms of activism affect whether their investments become observable and credible signals. Future studies could code the actual ESG policy changes activated by SWFs and examine how those changes affect subsequent foreign investment. The paper also points to research on how SWF investments can deter firms or trigger social activism and regulation when the SWF’s national image or strategic motives are viewed negatively.
