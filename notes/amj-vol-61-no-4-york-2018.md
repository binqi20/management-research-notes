# It’s Not Easy Building Green: The Impact of Public Policy, Private Actors, and Regional Logics on Voluntary Standards Adoption

**Abstract**
Human-induced climate change is one of the most daunting challenges of the 21st century. Addressing climate change will require new sectors that create economically and ecologically sustainable products and services, often supported through voluntary certification standards. For example, the Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) voluntary certification standard seeks to reduce the environmental impacts of buildings through stimulating growth of the “green building” sector. Both public and private actors have attempted to promote such standards; however, we know little about the relative efficacy of such efforts. We know even less about how regional culture conditions the impact of such public and private promotion. In this study, we theorize and test how regional institutional logics filter the efficacy of public and private efforts to promote adoption of standards. Our results demonstrate that, in addition to policy, private actors such as social movement organizations, market intermediaries, and environmental entrepreneurs play a role in bolstering adoption. Yet, the efficacy of such interventions is dependent upon variations in regional culture; only environmental entrepreneurs were able to bridge the cultural divide and be equally effective in regions dominated by either market or community logics. Our findings advance understanding of how new markets are socially constructed and the conditions under which efforts to promote new sectors are more effective.

**Research Question**
How do public policies and private actors affect adoption of a voluntary environmental standard, and how do regional market and community logics condition those effects? The paper asks this in the context of LEED adoption across U.S. metropolitan statistical areas.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Economic incentive policies, TSMO activity through USGBC membership, market intermediaries measured as LEED Accredited Professionals, and environmental entrepreneur entry.
- DV(s): New LEED-certified buildings in an MSA-year.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Regional market logic and regional community logic.

The paper theorizes regional institutional logics as cultural filters that shape how potential adopters interpret public and private efforts to promote LEED. Economic incentives, TSMOs, and market intermediaries were expected or found to work especially through alignment with market-oriented interpretations of green building. Environmental entrepreneurs are theorized as hybrid actors that can make LEED meaningful under both market and community logics by combining economic opportunity with ecological benefit.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends voluntary standards research by shifting attention from coercive regulation and adopter-side isomorphism to the combined role of public policy, TSMOs, market intermediaries, and entrepreneurs in constructing a new market. It contributes to institutional logics research by showing that regional logics filter the effectiveness of interventions rather than simply exerting direct effects. It also links environmental entrepreneurship to voluntary standards adoption by showing how new ventures can facilitate standards diffusion.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that policy-makers and advocates promoting environmental standards should adapt interventions to regional cultural logics rather than assuming uniform policy effects. Market-oriented regions may respond especially well to incentives, professional intermediaries, and economic framing. Environmental entrepreneurs may be particularly useful because their products and services can bridge market and community interpretations of green building.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the study may generalize most directly to practices embedded in institutional complexity. They also note that LEED emergence involved co-evolution among policy, USGBC activity, LEED APs, private actors, and adoption, although they use lagged variables and simultaneous equation approaches to address endogeneity concerns. A further limitation is that some marginal effects are relatively small compared with stronger drivers such as isomorphism and environmental beliefs.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether these findings generalize beyond environmental standards to other practices embedded in institutional complexity, such as alternative health care practices. The authors also suggest fieldwork and process-model development to better explain co-evolution among policy, private actors, and adoption. Additional work could examine actors' position within an industry, TSMO membership composition, and entrepreneurs' roles in renewable energy, local and organic foods, and other environmentally beneficial sectors.
