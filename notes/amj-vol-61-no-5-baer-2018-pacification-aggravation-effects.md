# Pacification or Aggravation? The Effects of Talking about Supervisor Unfairness

**Abstract**
Many employees feel a general sense of unfairness toward their supervisors. A common reaction to such unfairness is to talk about it with coworkers. The conventional wisdom is that this unfairness talk should be beneficial to the aggrieved employees. After all, talking provides employees with an opportunity to make sense of the experience and to "let off steam." We challenge this perspective, drawing on cognitive-motivational-relational theory to develop arguments that unfairness talk leads to emotions that reduce the employee's ability to move on from the unfairness. We first tested these proposals in a three-wave, two-source field study of bus drivers (Study 1), then replicated our findings in a laboratory study (Study 2). In both studies, we found that unfairness talk was positively related to anger and negatively related to hope. Those emotions went on to have direct effects on forgiveness and indirect effects on citizenship behavior. Our results also show that the detrimental effects of unfairness talk were neutralized when the listener offered suggestions that reframed the unfair situation. We discuss the implications of these results for managing unfairness in organizations.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether talking with coworkers about supervisor unfairness helps employees move on from unfairness or instead aggravates the experience by increasing anger and reducing hope. It also asks whether coworker reframing weakens those effects and whether forgiveness links unfairness talk to supervisor-directed citizenship behavior.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Unfairness talk about supervisor unfairness; in Study 2, experimentally manipulated high versus low unfairness talk.
- DV(s): Anger, hope, forgiveness of the supervisor or experimenter, and supervisor- or experimenter-directed citizenship behavior.
- Mediators: Anger and hope mediate the effect of unfairness talk on forgiveness; forgiveness further links the emotion pathway to citizenship behavior.
- Moderators: Coworker or listener reframing response is tested as a first-stage moderator of the unfairness-talk-to-emotion links; Study 2 supports the moderated indirect pathway through hope, while the anger-path difference is not significant.

The mechanism is appraisal-based. Talking about supervisor unfairness keeps the goal-hindering, blame-focused appraisal active, increasing anger, while also making the unfairness feel more solidified and less surmountable, reducing hope. Anger and lower hope make it harder to forgive the supervisor, and lower forgiveness reduces willingness to go beyond role requirements for that supervisor. Reframing responses interrupt this process by offering alternative interpretations and coping options, with the replicated conditional indirect evidence strongest for the hope pathway.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces unfairness talk to the organizational justice literature, shifting attention from unfair events alone to the conversational dynamics that follow unfairness. It tests and extends cognitive-motivational-relational theory by using anger and hope to explain why talking can impair forgiveness and citizenship behavior. It also identifies coworker reframing as a listener-side response that can neutralize otherwise harmful effects of unfairness talk.

**Practical Implication**
The authors caution that employees may worsen the long-term employee-supervisor relationship if they repeatedly talk about unfairness with coworkers who only validate the grievance. They suggest employees should choose conversation partners who can offer alternative perspectives and coping options, while coworkers should reframe with concern rather than dismissiveness. The paper also points to supervisor conflict-management training, forgiveness interventions, reparations, and expressive writing as possible organizational responses to supervisor unfairness.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Study 1 used two sources and three waves, but the anger, hope, and forgiveness relationships could still have been inflated because those variables were not separated into four waves. They also state that they did not capture all variables during each wave, so a true panel design would have provided stronger field evidence for causality. Another limitation is that the field sample was primarily male, although the authors argue prior evidence suggests gender may not strongly affect talking about negative experiences or forgiveness.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether benefits for the employee-coworker relationship, such as social support or strengthened coworker ties, offset the harms found for the employee-supervisor relationship. It could also compare different coworker response types, including validation and reframing, and assess whether reframing reduces future willingness to share with that coworker. The authors also call for experiments that distinguish talking about emotions from talking about thoughts, including structured conversations aimed at obtaining alternative perspectives.
