# Trusting the “Look and Feel”: Situational Normality, Situational Aesthetics, and the Perceived Trustworthiness of Organizations

**Abstract**
We conducted two studies examining how the “look and feel” of an organization shapes newcomers’ trust in that organization. More specifically, we examined the effects of situational normality—the degree to which the work setting appears customary, with everything in proper order. We then introduced the construct of situational aesthetics—the degree to which the work setting has a pleasing and attractive appearance. A field study of new accountants revealed that situational normality and situational aesthetics had indirect effects on trust through perceived trustworthiness, with trust going on to predict coworker ratings of learning behavior. We then replicated those trustworthiness findings in a laboratory setting. Taken together, our results suggest that newcomer trust formation may be shaped by aspects of the work setting that have been heretofore ignored by trust scholars.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether the physical work setting's "look and feel" shapes newcomers' perceptions of organizational trustworthiness and trust before they have much rational or historical information about the organization. It focuses on whether situational normality and situational aesthetics affect perceived ability, benevolence, and integrity, and whether trust then predicts newcomer learning behavior.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Situational normality and situational aesthetics; Study 2 also manipulates psychological contract fulfillment.
- DV(s): Perceived trustworthiness facets, trust in the organization, and learning behavior in Study 1; perceived trustworthiness facets in Study 2.
- Mediators: Perceived ability, perceived benevolence, perceived integrity, and trust in the organization.
- Moderators: Psychological contract fulfillment in Study 2, where breach conditions make normality and aesthetics more consequential for perceived ability.

The mechanism is a trust-formation process based on heuristic and categorical cues. Newcomers may read a setting that seems normal, customary, proper, pleasing, beautiful, or tasteful as a thin-slice cue about the organization's ability, benevolence, and integrity. Those trustworthiness judgments then support trust in the organization, which gives newcomers enough comfort to engage in learning behavior that carries interpersonal and career risk.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges rational/historical models of organizational trust formation by showing that newcomers' trustworthiness judgments can be shaped by physical-setting cues before extensive direct evidence is available. It extends McKnight et al.'s situational normality idea from initial interpersonal trust to trust in an organization and introduces situational aesthetics as a new construct in the trust-formation literature. It also reconnects research on physical work settings with organizational behavior outcomes by showing that workplace appearance can matter for perceived trustworthiness and learning-related behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should treat physical-setting decisions as part of newcomer trust formation, not only as facilities or branding choices. The findings suggest that avoiding atypical or disorderly workplace cues may be a basic step, while aesthetic choices may require attention to employee preferences around lighting, colors, textures, spatial forms, and internal organization. The paper cautions that aesthetics are subjective and can be overdone, but argues that construction, remodeling, and redecorating choices can be managed with trustworthiness in mind.

**Limitations**
The field study did not temporally separate trust in the organization from coworker-rated learning behavior, so that link lacks the temporal precedence normally desired in hypothesis tests. Attrition across waves and sources limited the Study 1 sample size, and situational normality and aesthetics were measured about three weeks after newcomers' first day rather than during their earliest hours at work. Study 2 strengthened causal inference through random assignment, but it used a laboratory scenario rather than observing actual newcomers in a real organization.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether situational normality and situational aesthetics operate through fully heuristic processes or whether newcomers can articulate rational reasons for the work setting's influence. The paper also calls for work on interactions between rational and heuristic antecedents of trustworthiness, differences across ability, benevolence, and integrity, and other early trust heuristics such as reception-staff friendliness or elevator courtesy. It further suggests studying whether managers intentionally use physical settings for impression management and where efforts to look typical or beautiful become excessive or misaligned with employees.
