# Quitting When the Going Gets Tough: A Downside of High Performance Expectations

**Abstract**
High performance expectations often improve performance. When individuals with high external performance expectations encounter early setbacks, however, they face impression management concerns and the prospect of embarrassment. As a result, when the going gets tough, individuals facing high external expectations may be less likely to persist than people facing low external expectations. In a field study of 328,515 men’s professional tennis matches (Study 1), we employ a regression discontinuity design to demonstrate that, after losing the first set of a match, players who are expected to win (favorites) are significantly more likely to quit than players who are expected to lose (underdogs). We replicate this pattern of results in a laboratory experiment (Study 2) and provide evidence for our proposed mechanism: compared to individuals facing low external expectations, those facing high expectations are more easily embarrassed by poor performance and consequently less persistent following early setbacks.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how external performance expectations affect persistence after poor initial performance. It specifically examines whether individuals expected to perform well become less persistent than those expected to perform poorly when early outcomes threaten their public image.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): External performance expectations, operationalized as favorite versus underdog status in Study 1 and middle-school versus expert difficulty expectations in Study 2.
- DV(s): Persistence, operationalized as quitting mid-match in Study 1 and the number of original-topic trivia questions attempted before switching topics in Study 2.
- Mediators: Embarrassment; impression management concerns provide the underlying image-management motive.
- Boundary condition tested: Poor initial performance, captured by losing the first set in Study 1 and poor early trivia performance in Study 2.

The proposed mechanism is that high external expectations create a stricter public reference point. When individuals underperform early, this expectation violation produces embarrassment and impression management concerns, making quitting or switching tasks attractive because it reduces exposure to further public failure and can preserve an alternative explanation for poor performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the dominant view that high external expectations mainly improve effort, persistence, and performance. It contributes to persistence research by showing that image goals can undermine performance goals, and it extends expectation research by identifying a downside of positive expectations under public setbacks.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat positive expectations and ranking systems as potentially costly when employees face visible early setbacks. The paper suggests that managers can reduce premature withdrawal by supporting high-expectation employees after setbacks and by fostering a culture that accepts and learns from failure.

**Limitations**
The field evidence comes from professional men's tennis, a highly visible competitive setting, so the authors call for research in more traditional organizational environments. Both studies involve high performance visibility, leaving uncertainty about whether the same expectation-persistence relationship holds when interim or final performance is not publicly observable.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on whether the pattern reflects a burden of high expectations or a benefit of low expectations. It also suggests examining contexts with less visible performance, the role of internal expectations, and how the perceived legitimacy of quitting excuses moderates the relationship between external expectations and persistence.
