# Strategizing and the Initiation of Interorganizational Collaboration through Prospective Resourcing

**Abstract**
In this paper, we explain how managers establish resource complementarity during their strategizing efforts for interorganizational collaboration. Based on a longitudinal field study at an automotive company, we show that resource complementarity is not given but jointly constructed in interactions with multiple potential partners through recursive cycles of what we refer to as “prospective resourcing.” Prospective resourcing mediates the interplay of strategizing and collaboration, thereby reversing the prevailing logic that strategy precedes and determines collaboration. Our findings offer insight into resourcing as a mechanism for developing strategic initiatives and shows how external actors may influence strategizing.

**Research Question**
How do actors establish resource complementarity when initiating interorganizational collaboration for an innovative strategic initiative? The paper asks how resource complementarity is constructed during strategizing rather than known ex ante as a preexisting partner-selection condition.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: resource complementarity, strategizing, interorganizational collaboration, prospective resourcing, emergent strategy content, partner preferences, and external actors' strategic influence.
- Process / phases: AutoCo moved through recursive cycles of resource exploration, envisioning resource use, and configuring resources across six strategic configurations for the Connect initiative.
- Resource exploration: managers and potential partners interacted to understand what external resources could do and which outcomes could be generated from their use.
- Envisioning resource use: managers used growing familiarity with external resources to specify resource needs and imagine how resources could support elements of the strategic initiative.
- Configuring resources: managers aligned internal resources, external resources, strategic objectives, initiative elements, and preferred partners into increasingly complementary resource combinations.
- Emergent outcomes: resource complementarity increased over time, but repeated reconfiguration also shifted partner preferences and frustrated some potential partners.

The process is not a variable-based causal model. The paper theorizes prospective resourcing as the recursive process through which firms turn external and internal resources into complementary combinations while strategy content and collaboration preferences co-evolve. Potential partners do not simply fill known resource gaps; they help reveal, frame, and sometimes redirect what the focal firm comes to see as strategically valuable.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to interorganizational collaboration research by showing that resource complementarity can be interactively created through prospective resourcing rather than identified before collaboration begins. It contributes to strategizing research by explaining how strategy content emerges relationally through interactions with external actors under novelty and complexity. It also extends resourcing and resource-based perspectives by showing how resource bundles gain strategic value through distributed, joint configuration rather than through the simple orchestration of preexisting resource pieces.

**Practical Implication**
The study implies that managers initiating innovative collaborations should not expect to know all resource needs or partner-selection criteria upfront. They need to keep multiple partner options open long enough to explore possible synergies, while also recognizing that prolonged informal exploration can create unmet expectations and burn social capital. Managers should pay close attention to how potential partners frame the initiative around their own resources.

**Limitations**
The authors identify novelty and complexity as boundary conditions for the model. The study focuses on one innovative strategic initiative at an international automotive firm, where required resources were distant and unfamiliar and where multiple interdependent elements required several types of partners. Less novel and less complex initiatives may require fewer cycles of prospective resourcing.

**Future Research**
The authors call for more research into the connections between interorganizational and intraorganizational processes and further cross-fertilization between research on strategizing and interorganizational collaboration. Future research could examine prospective resourcing in initiatives with lower novelty or complexity, in other kinds of innovative strategic objectives, and in settings where needed resources such as financial resources or distribution channels are more familiar.
