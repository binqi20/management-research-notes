**Mechanism Process**
- Core purpose: The editorial introduces data visualization techniques from outside management that could help management scholars communicate qualitative and quantitative data more effectively.
- Qualitative visualization techniques: The primer discusses word clouds, word trees, social graphs, and history flows, emphasizing how these tools can make text, networks, and editing histories more visible.
- Quantitative visualization techniques: The primer discusses multidimensional scaling plots, funnel plots, maps, bubble plots, dynamic plots, and tree maps as ways to reveal distances, bias symptoms, geographic patterns, multidimensional relationships, temporal change, and hierarchy.
- Tool guidance: The authors identify software options including Python packages, R packages, Tableau, Excel-based tools, NetworkX, NetDraw, Plotly, metafor, and squarify.
- Aesthetic guidance: The editorial argues that visualization choices should make research more understandable, informative, efficient, and aesthetically appealing.

The process logic is editorial and instructional rather than causal. Ertug and colleagues treat visualization as an additional communication mode for management research, first surveying techniques for qualitative and quantitative data and then linking those techniques to caveats, examples, and software choices. The central argument is that management researchers should look beyond standard charts and deliberately match visualization forms to the properties, relationships, and interpretive needs of their data.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial does not develop or test a theory. Its contribution is methodological and programmatic: it broadens the repertoire of visualization practices available to management researchers and frames visualization as a way to improve communication, memory, pattern recognition, and reader engagement.

**Practical Implication**
Management researchers can use the primer to select visualization techniques that fit their data type, analytic purpose, and communication challenge. The article also cautions researchers to preprocess text, avoid over-interpreting visual patterns, consider software and programming requirements, and make visualizations clear, informative, and aesthetically appealing.

**Limitations**
The editorial is selective rather than comprehensive, and it explicitly does not provide detailed step-by-step tutorials. Its examples are illustrative, and the cautions attached to several visualization techniques indicate that visual displays depend on data quality, preprocessing choices, layout choices, and appropriate interpretation.

**Future Research**
The authors call for researchers to contribute open source code to implement history flow visualization for future use. More broadly, the editorial invites management scholars to explore visualization techniques used in other disciplines and adapt them to management research questions, data structures, and communication needs.
