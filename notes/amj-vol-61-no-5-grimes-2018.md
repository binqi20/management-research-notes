# The Pivot: How Founders Respond to Feedback through Idea and Identity Work

**Abstract**
Efforts to incorporate external feedback are central to the process of entrepreneurship and to that of creative work more broadly, yet, because individuals may view aspects of their creative ideas as linked to their self-concepts, this can trigger resistance toward revision. Thus, feedback-induced change, while likely intended to increase the viability of creative ideas, might paradoxically undermine that viability by compromising creative workers’ associated identity-based relationships with their creative endeavors. While existing scholarship has established the importance of creative revision, research has largely overlooked how this vital process intersects with creative workers’ identities. Through a field study of 59 founders and their entrepreneurial ideas, I present an identity-based process model of creative revision that highlights differences in founders’ psychological ownership of their ideas and how those differences affect subsequent revision efforts. The emerging findings contribute to existing theory by revealing that the capacity to extend the novelty and usefulness of one’s ideas is not merely subject to informational constraints but also to identity-based constraints.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how founders respond to external feedback when that feedback requires changes to entrepreneurial ideas that are linked to their self-concepts. More specifically, it examines how psychological ownership of ideas shapes founders' idea work, identity work, and ability to balance differentiation and assimilation within an entrepreneurial community.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: creative revision, identity-sharpening feedback, psychological ownership reappraisal, idea work, identity work, collective sensemaking, breadth of prior creative work experience, and optimal distinctiveness.
- Process / phases: identity-sharpening feedback triggers founders to reappraise psychological ownership of their ideas by reaffirming, abstracting, or relinquishing that ownership.
- Idea work practices: reaffirming ownership is linked to defending; abstracting ownership is linked to repairing; relinquishing ownership is linked to re-engineering.
- Identity work practices: defending supports transcending, repairing supports decoupling, and re-engineering supports professionalizing.
- Conditioning factors: collective sensemaking and broader prior creative work experience make founders more willing to reconsider feedback and loosen psychological ownership.
- Emergent outcome: decoupling is the path most clearly associated with optimal distinctiveness because it lets founders keep commitment to a broader creative vision while revising specific assumptions.

The central process is identity-based rather than only informational. Feedback changes founders' attention and intentions, but those effects depend on whether founders experience ideas as extensions of the self. When founders keep ownership tightly attached to the original idea, feedback is treated as a threat and revision narrows into defense. When they abstract or relinquish ownership, they can repair or re-engineer the idea and reposition the self in relation to competing founder roles.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends creative revision theory by showing that revision is constrained not only by information deficits but also by identity-based constraints rooted in psychological ownership. It bridges research on psychological ownership and founder identities by introducing psychological ownership reappraisal and showing how founder role identities shift during feedback encounters. It also contributes to identity scholarship by showing how competing visionary and scientific founder roles create role equivocality and make optimal distinctiveness central to entrepreneurial revision.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that founders, mentors, and resource providers should treat feedback as both idea work and identity work. Feedback processes are more likely to support viable revision when they help founders preserve a coherent broader purpose while questioning specific business-model assumptions. Incubators and founder teams may also improve revision by enabling collective sensemaking that depersonalizes criticism and helps founders evaluate feedback pragmatically.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on idea-stage founders who were pre-revenue, pre-customer, and pre-funding, which allowed the author to isolate idea and identity work before later accountabilities and dependencies entered the venture. The founders were all highly involved in generating their ideas and were pursuing capital, so the findings are especially suited to contexts where ideas feel novel, personal, and exposed to resource-provider judgment. The paper also notes that equity-based financing contexts represent only a subset of entrepreneurial settings.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how later-stage accountability and resource dependencies condition the identity and idea work that follow feedback. The paper also calls for work on the long-term effects of early idea and identity work, including imprinting on organizational culture, founder resilience, and venture success. Additional studies could test the model with different founder types, such as necessity entrepreneurs, franchise operators, later-recruited founders, and other creative workers such as artists, designers, and scholars.
