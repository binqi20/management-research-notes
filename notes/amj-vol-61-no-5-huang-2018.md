# The Role of Investor Gut Feel in Managing Complexity and Extreme Risk

**Abstract**
Securing financial resources from investors is a key challenge for many early-stage entrepreneurial ventures. Given the inherent uncertainty surrounding a decision to invest in these ventures, prior research has found that experienced investors rely heavily on their investor gut feel—that is, dynamic expertise-based emotion-cognitions specific to the entrepreneurship context. In this paper, I inductively find that rather than being based on rapid, nonconscious impulse, as much of prior literature would suggest, what investors call their “gut feel” is an elaborate “intuiting process.” This process serves a distinct purpose: it emboldens investors to make investments that would otherwise be considered overly risky and likely to lead to failure. In the theoretical model I present, I delineate how investors are guided by a predisposed stance on risk and uncertainty, which dictates the approach investors take toward managing the complexity of an investment opportunity—and how they cognitively and emotionally reframe investment risk into a compelling narrative that transcends avoidance behavior and leads investors to invest. These findings expand our overall understanding of the complex ways in which investors contend with the risks and uncertainties in entrepreneurial finance.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how early-stage investors choose to invest in opportunities they know are extremely risky, and specifically how investor gut feel enables them to see past that extreme risk and substantiate investment action. It examines investor gut feel from the investor perspective as an elaborate intuiting process rather than as a rapid, nonconscious impulse alone.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key phenomenon: Investor gut feel as a dynamic, expertise-based emotion-cognition in early-stage entrepreneurial finance.
- Triggering conditions: Early-stage investment opportunities marked by complex information, uncertainty, extreme risk, and high likelihood of failure.
- Process / phases: Investors start from a predisposed stance on risk and uncertainty, either control-focused or choice-focused; that stance guides either a checklist approach or a syncopated approach to forming gut feel.
- Key constructs / tensions: Business viability data, perceptions of the entrepreneur, cognitive-emotional premises, self-focused narratives, downside-risk bracketing, and nuanced recognition of extraordinary upside.
- Emergent outcome: Investment action is substantiated when investors construct a narrative that reframes risk and gives them a reason to invest despite likely failure.
- Boundary conditions: The model depends on investor-entrepreneur interaction during due diligence and may not transfer directly to contexts where entrepreneurs are less personally accessible.

The process is interpretive rather than a simple causal-variable model. Control-focused investors begin with business viability data, use checklist-like premises around business feasibility and executional capability, and justify investing through a self-focused narrative about their ability to identify and bracket risk. Choice-focused investors begin with perceptions of the entrepreneur, use syncopated premises around distinctiveness and executional exclusivity, and justify investing through a narrative about their ability to recognize exceptional upside. Across both routes, gut feel combines cognitive and emotional information into a narrative that allows action under extreme risk.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to entrepreneurial resource acquisition by showing that early-stage investors do not all make investment decisions in the same way: their stance toward risk shapes whether they use checklist or syncopated approaches. It also challenges the view of gut feel as merely biased, fast, or noisy by showing it can be an extensive intuiting process that integrates economic, behavioral, cognitive, and emotional information. Finally, it reframes investor gut feel as a self-focused narrative that substantiates action rather than as a direct claim about investment performance.

**Practical Implication**
For investors, the study suggests that understanding the basis of their own investment approach may help them manage portfolios and conduct due diligence more efficiently. For entrepreneurs, it implies that investors differ in whether they emphasize feasibility, desirability, risk control, or upside recognition, so all money may not be equally suitable. The paper also warns that investor gut feel may serve as a cover for implicit bias, creating disadvantages for groups subject to gender, race, ethnicity, or other perceptual stereotypes.

**Limitations**
The model is based on early-stage angel investing, where investor-entrepreneur interpersonal interaction during due diligence is central, so it may not apply to equity crowdfunding, late-stage venture capital, private equity, or contexts with less personal access to entrepreneurs. The paper also reports that it could not identify which investor characteristics predict control-focused versus choice-focused stances or checklist versus syncopated approaches. Finally, the analysis relied heavily on interview data, which may introduce retrospective sensemaking bias, although the author used observations and other sources to support the inductive analysis.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine gut feel in other types of investors and financing contexts to test the boundary conditions of the model. It could also study whether investor background, entrepreneurial experience, technology experience, net worth, investment size, or portfolio size predicts the stance and approach investors use. The paper further calls for quantitative laboratory and field studies to test the theory, examine moderators and boundary conditions, and assess more objective outcomes beyond the qualitative process model.
