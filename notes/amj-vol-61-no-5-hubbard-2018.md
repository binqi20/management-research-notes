# Safe Bets or Hot Hands? How Status and Celebrity Influence Strategic Alliance Formations by Newly Public Firms

**Abstract**
Social approval assets derive their value from favorable stakeholder perceptions. Past research has focused primarily on their role as signals that reduce stakeholders’ perceived uncertainty about the firm. However, social approval assets can also serve as frames that influence how other information is interpreted. We theorize how the frames associated with two social approval assets—status and celebrity—influence the interpretation of equivocal information about newly public firms. Specifically, we examine how each frame influences the way underpricing is interpreted, and how these interpretations, as well as the joint effects of possessing status and celebrity, influence newly public firms’ strategic alliance formations. We explore these ideas in the ambiguity-ridden context of “Dot-Com” firms during the commercial dawn of the Internet. Our findings generally support our arguments, providing new theory and evidence about the framing effects of social approval assets with different sociocognitive content, and the dynamics of information and frame (in)congruence.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how status and celebrity, as social approval assets with different sociocognitive content, shape stakeholders' interpretation of equivocal IPO underpricing information. It specifically examines how those frames, separately and jointly, influence newly public firms' strategic alliance formations in the Dot-Com context.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Status affiliations, celebrity, IPO underpricing, and the interactions among status, celebrity, and underpricing.
- DV(s): Number of post-IPO strategic alliances formed during the first year after the IPO.
- Mediators: Not directly modeled; the theory centers on interpretive framing of underpricing as low uncertainty or market excitement.
- Moderators: Status moderating the underpricing-alliance relationship, celebrity moderating the underpricing-alliance relationship, and the joint possession of status and celebrity.

The mechanism is that status and celebrity act as interpretive frames rather than only as signals. Status draws attention to analytical assessments of vetted quality and affiliation, whereas celebrity draws attention to affective excitement and future potential. Because underpricing is equivocal, celebrity amplifies the positive interpretation of high underpricing, while joint status and celebrity can create interpretative uncertainty that reduces the value of possessing status alone.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends research on social approval assets by theorizing and testing their framing effects, not just their signaling effects. It shows that status and celebrity have different sociocognitive content, that celebrity reinforces the affective interpretation of high underpricing, and that incongruent social approval frames can weaken rather than accumulate each other's benefits.

**Practical Implication**
For managers of newly public firms, the findings suggest that high-status affiliations are generally more productive for gaining alliance partners than pursuing media celebrity. The paper also warns that firms with high-status affiliations should be cautious about courting celebrity because the combination may create interpretative uncertainty, although celebrity can help when market sentiment is already strongly positive.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting is limited to high-tech Dot-Com firms in an ambiguity-ridden historical context, so the authors cannot establish that the findings generalize to other time periods or industries. The data are cross-sectional and archival, which means changes in status and celebrity over time and stakeholders' underlying psychological reactions are measured indirectly rather than observed directly.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for additional investigation of interpretive frames in other time periods, contexts, stakeholder audiences, and types of social approval assets. It also suggests using methods such as laboratory studies or policy-capturing designs to test stakeholders' psychological reactions to status, celebrity, and other frames under high uncertainty.
