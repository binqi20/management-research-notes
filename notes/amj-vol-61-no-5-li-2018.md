# From Employee-experienced High-involvement Work System to Innovation: An Emergence-based Human Resource Management Framework

**Abstract**
The influence of human resource management on innovation has attracted considerable research attention over the last decade. However, existing studies have primarily focused on the macro-level human resource management architecture, limiting our understanding about the cross-level origin of innovation. Developing an emergence-based human resource management framework, we propose that an employee-experienced high-involvement work system (HIWS) promotes innovation by eliciting collective interactions for knowledge exchange and aggregation. Further, we investigate the emergence-enabling process that facilitates an employee-experienced HIWS to give rise to organization-level innovation. Specifically, we probe three distinct emergence enablers that amplify the positive influence of HIWS on innovation by shaping the concertedness, direction, and adaptability of collective interactions: (1) the homogeneity of HIWS experiences as the internal mechanism, (2) the strategic importance of innovation as the external mechanism, and (3) the churn in human resources as the temporal mechanism. We tested our theoretical model using data from a nationally representative sample of workplaces in Canada (n 5 2,639). Our results suggest that an employeeexperienced HIWS was positively related to innovation. In addition, this positive effect was amplified by all three emergence enablers (i.e., the homogeneity of HIWS experiences, the strategic importance of innovation, and the churn in human resources).

**Research Question**
The paper asks how employee-experienced high-involvement work systems give rise to organization-level innovation. It focuses on whether collective interactions for knowledge exchange and aggregation are amplified by three emergence enablers: homogeneity of HIWS experiences, strategic importance of innovation, and churn in human resources.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee-experienced high-involvement work system, measured from employee reports of flexible job design, information sharing, problem-solving teams, self-directed teams, gainsharing, and training.
- DV(s): Workplace innovation in 2006, measured as an ordinal product and process innovation launch outcome.
- Mediators: No mediator is directly tested; knowledge exchange and aggregation through collective interactions are the theorized emergence process.
- Moderators: Homogeneity of HIWS experiences, strategic importance of innovation, and churn in human resources.

The theorized mechanism is bottom-up: employee-experienced HIWS creates opportunities for collective interactions, through which employee knowledge is exchanged and aggregated into organization-level innovation. Homogeneous HIWS experiences increase concertedness, strategic importance of innovation directs collective effort toward innovation, and human resource churn increases adaptability by refreshing knowledge and reducing stagnation. The results support the positive HIWS-innovation relationship and show that all three emergence enablers strengthen it.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to strategic HRM by shifting attention from macro-level HRM architecture to the bottom-up emergence process through which employee HRM experiences produce organization-level innovation. It integrates the human capital resource emergence perspective with complex adaptive system theory by specifying internal, external, and temporal emergence-enabling mechanisms. It also extends contingency HRM research by showing that the value of HIWS for innovation depends on homogeneity of employee experiences, strategic innovation priority, and human resource flow.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that organizations competing through innovation should not focus only on adopting HIWS practices. They also need to implement HIWS uniformly across employees, communicate innovation as a clear strategic priority, and actively manage employee inflow and outflow so collective interactions remain adaptive and knowledge-rich.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the archival WES data constrained their measures, including dichotomous innovation items and limited detail on how each HRM practice was implemented across employees. They could measure the quantity of employee mobility but not the quality of mobility. They also could not directly test the mediating processes through which the emergence enablers shape the HIWS-innovation relationship.

**Future Research**
Future research could use field studies to test mediating mechanisms such as transactive memory systems, interpersonal communication, and collective cohesion. The authors also call for work distinguishing exploitative and explorative innovation and for studies of other organizational factors, such as developmental stages and life cycles, that may facilitate or inhibit human resource emergence.
