# The Differential Impact of Knowledge Depth and Knowledge Breadth on Creativity over Individual Careers

**Abstract**
While usually argued to be fostering creativity, the effect of knowledge depth and breadth on creativity is actually mixed. We take a dynamic approach to the knowledge–creativity relationship and argue that the effect of knowledge depth and knowledge breadth is likely to be contingent on career age. We propose that individuals’ knowledge structures become increasingly rigid as career age grows and that, because of this, knowledge depth and breadth have different effects on creativity at different points of the career. More specifically, we hypothesize that knowledge depth is more beneficial for creativity in earlier stages of one’s career, when creators need to increase the complexity of knowledge structures, while knowledge breadth is more beneficial in later stages, when flexibility is most needed. We test and find support for our hypotheses in a longitudinal study set in the context of the Hollywood animation industry, a setting characterized by the presence of a variety of creators involved in knowledge-intensive activities. Theoretical and practical implications of the results are discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when knowledge depth and knowledge breadth are more or less conducive to individual creativity over the course of a career. It examines whether career age changes the creativity effects of depth and breadth because individual knowledge structures become increasingly rigid over time.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Knowledge depth, measured as the number of prior movies a creator worked on in the focal movie's main genre; knowledge breadth, measured as the number of different genres a creator had worked on up to the focal year.
- DV(s): Individual creativity, measured as the number of awards and nominations received by a creator in a given year.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper; the theory centers on knowledge-structure complexity, flexibility, and rigidity as cognitive mechanisms.
- Moderators: Career age, measured as time elapsed between the creator's first contribution to the Hollywood animation industry and the observation year.

The mechanism is temporal and cognitive. Early-career creators have relatively flexible but less complex knowledge structures, so depth helps them build richer domain schemas and generate creative outcomes. As career age increases, linkages within and between knowledge domains become more rigid; depth then becomes less beneficial and can intensify entrenchment, whereas breadth helps loosen knowledge structures by exposing creators to different domains.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconciles mixed findings about knowledge depth, knowledge breadth, and creativity by showing that their effects depend on career age. It contributes to research on career age and creativity by specifying why different knowledge configurations are useful at different career stages. It also adds a temporal microfoundation to creativity theory by connecting changes in knowledge structures to variation in creative output over individual careers.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that learning and development programs should vary by career stage. Newer employees may benefit more from deepening domain knowledge and learning the rules, norms, and procedures of their field. Later-career employees may need exposure to diverse domains and tasks that broaden their knowledge base and help refresh creative cognition.

**Limitations**
The archival design does not directly observe the cognitive mechanisms by which career age changes knowledge structures, so alternative mechanisms such as skill-building dynamics, intrinsic motivation, or job stability cannot be fully ruled out. The setting is a creative, project-based industry, which may limit generalizability to tasks or industries where creativity is less central. Some robustness checks, including the analysis of job stability among permanent hires, had limited sample size.

**Future Research**
The authors call for future research that directly studies intra-individual creative cognition and the joint role of knowledge and cognition in shaping creativity. They also suggest examining linkages between knowledge domains, not only within-domain schemas, and testing whether other creativity antecedents vary in effect across career stages. Further studies should examine the knowledge-depth, knowledge-breadth, and career-age relationship in settings beyond the animation industry.
