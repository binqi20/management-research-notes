# The Social Consequences of Voice: An Examination of Voice Type and Gender on Status and Subsequent Leader Emergence

**Abstract**
This paper explores the impact of two types of voice and gender on peer-rated social status and subsequent leader emergence. Across two studies—a three-wave field study and an experiment—we find that speaking up promotively, but not prohibitively, is positively and indirectly related to leader emergence via status, and that this relationship is conditional on the gender of the speaker. Specifically, men who spoke up promotively benefited the most in terms of status and leader emergence, not only compared to men who spoke up prohibitively, but also compared to women who spoke up promotively. This research extends our understanding of the outcomes of voice by articulating how it impacts one’s place in his or her group’s social structure, and ultimately whether he or she is seen as a leader. We also add to our understanding of leader emergence by suggesting that talking a lot or participating at a high level in a group may not be enough to emerge as a leader—it also depends how you do it and who you are.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how peers respond to promotive versus prohibitive voice and whether these voice types shape peer-rated status and subsequent leader emergence. It further asks whether the social consequences of voice differ by speaker gender.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Promotive voice and prohibitive voice; in Study 2, voice type was experimentally manipulated as promotive versus prohibitive.
- DV(s): Leader emergence, measured as peer rankings in Study 1 and perceived leader emergence ratings in Study 2.
- Mediators: Peer-rated status.
- Moderators: Speaker gender.

The proposed mechanism is that voice can function as a status-claiming behavior, but peers grant status only when the behavior is seen as valuable and legitimate. Promotive voice is theorized to be more status-enhancing than prohibitive voice because it emphasizes future-oriented improvement and positive movement toward an ideal state, whereas prohibitive voice highlights problems and potential harm. Expectation states theory further implies that gender shapes legitimacy judgments, so men receive greater status and leader-emergence benefits from promotive voice than women do.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends voice research by shifting attention from managers' reactions to peers' social evaluations of speakers. It links voice, status, and leader emergence by showing that promotive voice can increase peer-granted status and leadership emergence, while also showing that these benefits depend on gender. It also contributes to expectation states theory by distinguishing between types of assertive behavior rather than treating speaking up as uniformly status-enhancing.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that employees may gain respect and influence by speaking up in ways that focus on ideas for moving the group toward an ideal state. It also warns that these benefits are not evenly distributed: women may speak up without receiving the same status and leadership credit as men. The authors recommend managerial practices such as documenting, recognizing, and amplifying women's voice to counter unequal status-conferral processes.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Study 1 took place in a male-dominated and distinctive military academy competition setting, even though Study 2 used a more gender-balanced context. They also note that Study 1 relied on self-ratings of voice and that the two studies used different measures of leader emergence. The paper did not directly examine the substantive content of specific voice episodes or the role of formal leaders in shaping the voice-status-emergence process.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research in settings with different gender compositions, including teams with mostly women, and for more objective measures of employee voice such as direct observation. It also calls for work on how the content of discrete voice events affects peer perceptions of respect and influence. Additional future research should examine other change-oriented behaviors, clarify leader-emergence measurement, and investigate how formal leaders affect whether voice accrues status.
