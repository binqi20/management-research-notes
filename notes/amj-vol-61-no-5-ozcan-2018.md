# Playing Cat and Mouse: Contests over Regulatory Categorization of Dietary Supplements in the United States

**Abstract**
Regulatory categorization can be a matter of life and death to firms, as it sets legal limitations on the production and sales of their product. In this paper we set out to uncover this critical process, for which there is only anecdotal information in extant literature, by asking how regulatory categories are determined through the strategies and interaction of firms, regulators, and other category audiences. We use extensive archival data to examine how U.S. dietary supplement makers first moved from the drug to the food category in 1976, and, when faced with resistance from the Food and Drug Administration, created an entirely new category in 1994, which then fueled their explosive market growth. Our findings show that regulatory categorization is a contest between firms and the regulator, where firms try to disrupt unfavorable regulation by overpowering the regulator through hard power imposed by other state actors, which is achieved through pressure from another category audience—consumers—who are themselves won over through soft power.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how regulatory categories are determined through the strategies and interaction of firms, regulators, and other category audiences. Empirically, it examines how U.S. dietary supplement makers moved first from the drug category to the food category in 1976 and then into a newly created dietary supplement category in 1994.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key actors / audiences: supplement makers, the FDA, Congress and other state actors, consumers, health stores, pharmaceutical companies, food corporations, public-interest groups, and category incumbents.
- Process / phases: movement between extant categories through the Proxmire Amendment of 1976, followed by creation of a new dietary supplement category through DSHEA in 1994.
- Firms' category work: dissociation from unfavorable categories and category evaluators, association with target categories, and selective representation through less controversial labels such as vitamins.
- Firms' advocacy work: lobbying legislators directly, hooking consumers through cultural meta-narratives, activating them through dramatization and urgency, and coordinating action through health stores and the value chain.
- Regulator work: FDA category work associated supplements with drug or food-additive categories, while maintenance work relied on educating, policing, deterrence, raids, product seizures, labeling restrictions, and scientific authority.
- Emergent outcome: firms used soft power over consumers to activate hard power from Congress, thereby overpowering FDA categorization decisions and changing the legal category governing the industry.

The process is a multi-actor political contest rather than a purely cognitive categorization process. Supplement makers first cognitively prepared audiences for category change by reframing what supplements were and who should evaluate them. They then mobilized consumers and legislators so that Congress could impose legal changes on the FDA. The regulator, by contrast, relied heavily on scientific expertise, education, and coercive enforcement, which made it vulnerable to firms' freedom-of-choice framing and public mobilization.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional work by showing how advocacy work can combine soft power and hard power to produce regulatory change, especially when less powerful firms mobilize other audiences that have leverage over state actors. It contributes to categorization research by defining category work as efforts to influence category attributes, boundaries, and membership, and by distinguishing movement between extant regulatory categories from new regulatory category creation. It also links institutional work and corporate political strategy by treating regulatory categorization as a multi-player contest among firms, regulators, legislators, consumers, value-chain actors, and incumbents.

**Practical Implication**
For firms in regulated markets, the study implies that changing regulation may require more than direct lobbying: firms may need to cognitively prepare audiences for category change and mobilize actors who can influence legislators. For regulators, the case shows the limits of relying only on scientific authority, education, and enforcement when firms can frame regulation as a threat to public values. For entrants facing powerful regulators, the paper suggests that category incumbents' divisions and value-chain access to consumers can create openings for regulatory change.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the study is set in a single industry, where the broader political and cultural climate may have contributed to the outcome. They also note that the alternative medicine movement in the 1970s and 1980s may have widened political support for the dietary supplement movement. A further boundary condition is that the setting involved individuals' health and well-being, making the public especially susceptible to heightened emotions.

**Future Research**
The authors call for studies of regulatory categorization in other markets, such as energy or broadcasting, where the issues may have less dramatic implications for society at large. They also suggest examining newer settings such as peer-to-peer platforms and e-cigarettes to understand when firms can or cannot mobilize public support for favorable categorization. Future work should further investigate hard versus soft power tactics and how power is distributed among different category audiences across regulatory settings.
