# The Short-lived Benefits of Abusive Supervisory Behavior for Actors: An Investigation of Recovery and Work Engagement

**Abstract**
Although empirical evidence has accumulated showing that abusive supervision has devastating effects on subordinates’ work attitudes and outcomes, knowledge about how such behavior impacts supervisors who exhibit it is limited. Drawing upon conservation of resources theory, we develop and test a model that specifies how and when engaging in abusive supervisory behavior has immediate benefits for supervisors. Via two experiments and a multi-wave diary study across 10 consecutive workdays, we found that engaging in abusive supervisory behavior was associated with improved recovery level. Moreover, abusive supervisory behavior had a positive indirect effect on work engagement through recovery level. Interestingly, supplemental analyses suggested that these beneficial effects were short-lived because, over longer periods of time (i.e., one week and beyond), abusive supervisory behavior was negatively related to supervisors’ recovery level and engagement. The strength of these short-lived beneficial effects was also bound by personal and contextual factors. Empathic concern––a personal factor––and job demands––a contextual factor––moderated the observed effects. Specifically, supervisors with high empathic concern or low job demands experienced fewer benefits after engaging in abusive supervisory behavior. We discuss the theoretical and practical implications of these findings, and propose future research directions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether, why, and when engaging in abusive supervisory behavior has proximal benefits for supervisors themselves. It focuses on whether abusive supervisory behavior can improve supervisors' recovery level and work engagement, and whether empathic concern and job demands condition those effects.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Engaging in abusive supervisory behavior, measured or manipulated as supervisors' verbal abuse or daily abusive behavior toward subordinates.
- DV(s): Supervisors' recovery level and work engagement, with supplementary longer-term analyses of recovery, work engagement, and job satisfaction.
- Mediators: Recovery level mediates the relationship between abusive supervisory behavior and work engagement.
- Moderators: Empathic concern weakens the short-term abusive behavior to recovery relationship; job demands strengthen it.

The mechanism is resource-based. The authors argue that abusive supervisory behavior can conserve supervisors' resources by releasing them from the effortful inhibition of aggressive impulses and can generate resources by increasing a sense of control. This resource recovery then supports same-day work engagement. The benefits are bounded: high empathic concern creates additional stress after abuse, low job demands make resource gains less salient, and cumulative abuse becomes detrimental over longer time windows.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends abusive supervision research by shifting the focal actor from subordinates as recipients to supervisors as perpetrators, showing that abusive behavior can have short-term benefits for actors even while remaining harmful overall. It contributes to conservation of resources theory by treating a "bad" behavior as a possible short-term resource-conservation and resource-generation event, while showing that the same behavior becomes resource-consuming over longer time periods. It also identifies empathic concern and job demands as personal and contextual boundary conditions on actor-centric abusive supervision effects.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that organizations should not treat abusive supervision only as irrational or purely destructive from the supervisor's perspective; short-term recovery and engagement benefits may reinforce the behavior. The authors point to less harmful recovery alternatives, including workday breaks, coworker communication, cultivating empathic concern, and reducing or managing supervisors' job demands. Interventions should target the resource pressures and perceived control deficits that make abusive behavior temporarily rewarding.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Study 2 relies on supervisor self-reports of daily abusive behavior, recovery, and engagement, creating potential common method variance despite temporal separation, controls, and supporting experiments. The tested outcomes are limited mainly to recovery and engagement, and the moderators focus on empathic concern and job demands rather than other organizational or subordinate factors. The same-day design captures proximal effects, while longer-term consequences are explored through supplementary analyses rather than a dedicated long-horizon longitudinal design.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on broader actor-based outcomes of abusive supervision, including negative affect, burnout, productivity, promotion potential, and social sanctions from subordinates. They suggest examining additional moderators such as organizational context and subordinate status, and testing whether supervisors are affected differently depending on whom they abuse. They also recommend longitudinal studies with different time frames and further investigation of cultural contingencies in the consequences of abusive supervisory behavior for actors.
