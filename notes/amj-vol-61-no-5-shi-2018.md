# Short Seller Influence on Firm Growth: A Threat Rigidity Perspective

**Abstract**
Whereas most investors seek to maximize their investment, there are some who profit from downward stock price movement. Engaging in such behavior is called “short selling,” and it has increased considerably among publicly traded North American firms in recent years. This study builds on threat rigidity theory to develop arguments about the behavioral consequences of short sellers. We theorize that, when firms have a high level of short interest, managers adopt a defensive stance by halting new growth initiatives and limiting the range of growth modes in which they participate. We also suggest that managers’ ability to process information and exercise control moderate these main effects. Specifically, we argue that managers will be less reactionary when firms have high absorptive capacity, possess high board centrality, maintain sufficient financial slack, and are well regarded by the media. Results largely support our theory, as we show that short sellers can have a profoundly deleterious effect on firm growth prospects.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how high levels of short interest influence firm growth decisions. Specifically, it examines whether short sellers, treated as an indirect external threat, lead managers to undertake fewer growth actions and to use a less diverse mix of growth modes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Level of short interest, measured as short shares as a percentage of total shareholdings; supplementary tests also use a high-short-interest dummy.
- DV(s): Number of growth actions and diversity of growth actions, with supplementary outcomes for consolidation actions, growth ratio, growth complexity, and broader competitive actions.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Absorptive capacity, board centrality, financial slack, and news sentiment.

The mechanism is threat rigidity. The authors argue that short sellers threaten managers by publicly betting against and denigrating the firm, even though they do not directly control it. Under high short interest, managers restrict information processing and narrow their span of control, which makes risky and cognitively demanding growth actions less attractive. Absorptive capacity, board centrality, financial slack, and positive media sentiment are expected to dampen this response because they improve information processing, control, resources, or external support.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends threat rigidity theory by applying it to an indirect threat: short sellers who cannot directly impose organizational harm but can unsettle managers through capital-market pressure and public criticism. It also contributes to investor-influence research by studying short sellers as a hostile investor type rather than as monitors or activists seeking firm improvement. Finally, it adds to managerial risk-taking research by showing that capital-market signals about a firm's prospects can shape growth decisions.

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest that managers should recognize how short-seller pressure may bias them toward defensive, internally focused decisions. Awareness of threat-rigidity tendencies may help managers develop more reasoned responses to short sellers rather than automatically reducing growth activity. The findings also matter for policy makers because short selling may have organizational consequences beyond market-price effects.

**Limitations**
The authors state that they do not directly measure the psychological mechanism of threat rigidity and instead infer it through moderator analyses. They also examine managerial actions rather than managers' public statements or counter-campaigns. In addition, the sample focuses on S&P 1500 companies, so the effects may differ for smaller and younger firms.

**Future Research**
Future research could directly measure the psychological and cognitive processes triggered by short-interest pressure, for example through survey instruments. It could also study how managers publicly respond to short sellers and whether those responses affect strategic decision-making. The authors further suggest expanding samples to smaller and younger firms and parsing competitive actions and competitor countermoves in more detail.
