# Fish out of Water: Translation, Legitimation, and New Venture Creation

**Abstract**
We draw on institutional theory to study a common type of new venture creation that has been neglected in the literature: the translation of an existing organizational form from a different—and misaligned—institutional context. To do so, we conducted an indepth case study of H-Farm, an Italian venture that was founded as a business incubator, a type of organization that first emerged in Silicon Valley and other U.S. technology regions. Our study illuminates the specific configuration of legitimacy pressures inherent in this type of entrepreneurship, and theorizes the strategies that entrepreneurs can enact to address them: local authentication work, category authentication work, and dual optimal distinctiveness work. We also show that the legitimacy pressures experienced by entrepreneurs may vary significantly as ventures mature, and challenge the notion of a specific “legitimacy threshold” that new ventures are required to reach. Finally, our model conceptualizes translation as an iterative, dynamic, and ongoing accomplishment rather than a “one off” activity with clear beginning and end points.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how entrepreneurs create and legitimate a new venture by translating an existing organizational form from a different and misaligned institutional context. Empirically, it examines how H-Farm translated the business incubator/accelerator form from Silicon Valley and other U.S. technology regions into Veneto, Italy.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / pressures: local-level legitimacy, category-level legitimacy, optimal distinctiveness, misaligned translation, and target-form evolution.
- Process / phases: improvising, converging, and optimizing.
- Entrepreneurial work: local-level authentication work, category-level authentication work, and dual optimal distinctiveness work.
- Triggering conditions: perceived shifts in the target organizational form and reflection on the limits of H-Farm's current model.
- Emergent outcomes: a model of entrepreneurship through misaligned translation in which legitimacy pressures vary over time rather than being resolved at a single threshold.
- Boundary conditions: stakeholder diversity and the rapid evolution of the organizational form may shape whether the process is generative or creates muddled meaning.

The process begins with improvising, where entrepreneurs loosely emulate a poorly aligned target form and work to make the venture locally understandable. It then shifts to converging, where entrepreneurs seek category-level acceptance from stakeholders associated with the target form while continuing local authentication. In the optimizing phase, growing local and international competition makes the venture pursue dual optimal distinctiveness: what once authenticated the venture locally helps it stand out at the category level, while category-level authentication helps distinguish it locally.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional entrepreneurship research by identifying the distinct legitimacy pressures faced by ventures created through misaligned translation: local-level legitimacy, category-level legitimacy, and later a dual optimal-distinctiveness challenge. It also theorizes legitimation as gradated and temporal rather than as crossing a single legitimacy threshold. Finally, it extends translation theory by showing that entrepreneurial translation involves a moving target being transposed by a moving object, making translation iterative, dynamic, and ongoing rather than a one-off act of faithful emulation.

**Practical Implication**
Entrepreneurs translating an organizational form into a misaligned context should not treat faithful imitation as sufficient. The H-Farm case suggests that they need to build local resonance first, then seek category-level acceptance when growth requires resources outside the local context, and later manage similarity and differentiation at both levels. The paper also cautions that if dual optimal distinctiveness work backfires, entrepreneurs may create muddled meaning for local and category-level stakeholders.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their case involved local and category-level stakeholders who were mainly entrepreneurs and investors, and that these pressures were sufficiently homogeneous for H-Farm to harness them productively. They caution that translating forms with more diverse stakeholders, such as social enterprises moving between the global north and global south, may create more incompatible legitimacy pressures. They also indicate that the dynamics of dual optimal distinctiveness may differ when the target organizational form is more static and has crisper boundaries.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether the differences between local-level and category-level legitimacy pressures remain or erode as a translated organizational form matures. It could also study misaligned translation in settings with more diverse stakeholder expectations, where local and category-level pressures may become competing tensions. The conclusion further invites work that builds on, extends, and refines the model for other digital ventures and other forms of new venture creation through misaligned translation.
