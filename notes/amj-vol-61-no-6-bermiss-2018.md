# Ideological Misfit? Political Affiliation and Employee Departure in the Private-equity Industry

**Abstract**
Though organizations are increasingly active participants in the political realm, little research has investigated how an organization’s heightened focus on political ideology impacts employees. We address this gap by exploring how an individual’s political ideological misfit with an organization’s prevailing ideology impacts departure. By tracking the movements of over 40,000 investment professionals in the U.S. private equity industry over 10 years, we investigate the consequences of the ideological misfit that arises when individuals’ political ideologies diverge substantially from the prevailing ideology of their firms. We hypothesize that ideological misfit increases the likelihood of departure, though the effects vary with liberal or conservative ideological orientation. Polynomial regression analyses reveal that the fit relationship between individual and organizational political ideology deviates from the idealized congruence relationship that underlies prevailing theory. Specifically, we find that departure is more likely for employees experiencing conservative misfit (a personal ideology more conservative than the firm’s) versus liberal misfit (a personal ideology more liberal than the firm’s). Conservatives are more likely to depart than liberals regardless of fit, and departing misfits tend to join new organizations that are closer to their own ideology.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how ideological misfit between an employee's political ideology and the prevailing political ideology of the employing organization affects departure. It also asks whether the departure effect differs for conservative misfit versus liberal misfit.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Individual-level liberal ideology, firm-level liberal ideology, their squared terms, and the interaction between individual- and firm-level ideology.
- DV(s): Employee departure from a private equity firm.
- Mediators: The paper theorizes hostile climate, alienation, detachment, reduced organizational identification, and lower social cohesion as mechanisms, but it does not estimate them as formal mediators in the main archival models.
- Moderators: Direction of ideological misfit, distinguishing conservative misfit from liberal misfit; additional analyses examine destination-firm ideology and employee satisfaction robustness.

The central mechanism is that political ideology functions as a person–organization fit dimension. When employees' political ideology diverges from the organization's prevailing ideology, the paper theorizes that they experience discomfort, hostile climate, detachment, and weaker identification, making departure more likely. The evidence is asymmetric: conservative misfits are more likely to depart, whereas liberal misfits are less likely to depart in some conditions, which the authors interpret through conservative sensitivity to misfit and through possible misfit strategies such as missionary action or discreet dissent.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to employee mobility research by theorizing departure as a sociocultural process shaped by political ideology rather than only as an economic or vacancy-driven process. It extends person–organization fit theory by introducing political ideology as a fit dimension and showing that misfit effects need not be symmetrical or uniformly detrimental. It also broadens research on political ideology in organizations beyond senior executives by studying rank-and-file professionals.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that managers should treat political ideology as a potentially consequential but difficult-to-observe dimension of fit. It cautions against assuming that ideological homogeneity is always desirable, because some ideological misfits may become sources of productive dissent or learning if the organization provides enough security for minority viewpoints. Managers in small, human-capital-intensive firms should pay attention to whether political discourse creates silence, alienation, or avoidable departures.

**Limitations**
The authors note that political contributions are an imperfect measure of political ideology and that the archival data cannot fully distinguish voluntary from involuntary departure. They also acknowledge that the study does not account for all turnover antecedents, including compensation, leadership, group cohesion, stress, and other demographic predictors. The empirical context is U.S. private equity, so the pattern may not generalize to other industries or to other salient ideologies.

**Future Research**
The authors call for future research integrating ideology with other antecedents of turnover, including job dissatisfaction, job search, and quit intentions. They also suggest studying whether misfit works similarly for other salient workplace ideologies, such as religious or philosophical ideologies. Further work could test the causal mechanisms behind the conservative-versus-liberal asymmetry and examine whether the findings replicate in other industry contexts.
