# Becoming Who We Serve: A Model of Multi-Layered Employee–Customer Identification

**Abstract**
Though employee identification with customers has received little explicit attention in organizational scholarship, and has only recently emerged as an area of focus in marketing, research suggests that customers should play a central role in the identity dynamics of employees. What makes employee–customer identification different from other forms of identification (e.g., employee–organization and customer–organization identification) is that although the organization may be the conduit for identification to occur, it may not be the focus of identification. Indeed, in the organization we explore here, organizational practices lead employees to identify with customers, but to do so in such a way that employees become—at least in part—more like “who they served” than vice versa. Moreover, the bonds we find between employees and customers are multilayered, incorporating nonwork identities, as well as identities at different levels of inclusiveness (e.g., collective and role). This paper explores and models the development and consequences of multilayered employee–customer identification, argues for its theoretical and practical implications, and suggests avenues for future research.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what the nature of employee–customer identification is, what role organizations may have in cultivating such bonds, and what perceived implications employee–customer identification has for employees and the employing organization. It specifically examines an alternative to the assumption that ECID is anchored in customers becoming "like us" through shared organizational values.

**Mechanism Process**
- Organizational practices: Bridging practices helped COP-CU employees understand, appreciate, and become involved with police-officer customers; valuing practices clarified and reinforced why serving police officers mattered.
- Identity process: These practices supported collective-level and role-level self-expansion, producing multilayered employee–customer identification.
- Collective-level expressions: Employees came to see themselves and police officers as part of the same family and as part of the police social collective.
- Role-level expressions: Employees came to see their work as familial emotional support and as sharing elements of policing, especially protecting and serving.
- Perceived implications: Occupational self-expansion contributed to work meaningfulness, familial self-expansion contributed to perceived service effectiveness, and these were linked to organizational identification, growth, and profitability.
- Comparison case: POST-CU showed little evidence of bridging and valuing practices, self-expansion, meaningful work, or distinctive service effectiveness, weakening alternative explanations based only on credit-union form, small size, defined membership, service mission, or personal ties to the customer occupation.

The process is not presented as a statistical causal test; the authors use an inductive comparative design to build a model. COP-CU's bridging practices blurred boundaries between bankers and police officers, while valuing practices promoted social agency by motivating employees to form strong reciprocal connections with police officers. Those connections enabled employees to become more like the customers they served rather than only seeing customers as sharing organizational values. The resulting M-LECID operated through multiple identity layers and was associated with perceived task significance, organizational instrumentality, and identification-based trust.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to workplace identification research by challenging the assumption that employee identification is necessarily anchored in the organization. It shows that employee–customer identification can involve employees taking on customer-linked identity content, so part of "who we are" as organizational employees becomes anchored in customers. It also extends identification and self-expansion theory by showing that identification can be multilayered, with collective and role-level self-expansion occurring in the same employee–customer relationship.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations that rely on repeated service relationships may foster productive ECID by creating bridging practices that help employees understand and interact with the service group, and valuing practices that make the mission of serving that group clear and rewarded. The paper also warns that closeness to customers may create risks: employees may become too customer-anchored, organizational flexibility may be constrained, and practices that encourage closeness may frustrate employees or customers if the organization does not enable or reward the connection.

**Limitations**
The study is inductive, so it supports analytical and naturalistic generalizability rather than population-level statistical generalizability. The comparison design does not directly replicate two credit unions serving the same customer population, so police-officer customer characteristics may still have influenced the identity dynamics observed at COP-CU. The authors also note that they did not collect longitudinal data, leaving the temporal ordering between organizational practices, self-expansion, and identification open to further testing.

**Future Research**
Future research should test multilayered employee–customer identification in other service contexts, including organizations with different customer groups, more heterogeneous customer bases, or other stakeholder targets. Studies could examine when customer categorization and organizational practices foster self-expansion, whether multilayered identifications differ from unidimensional identifications in resilience or behavioral consequences, and how employees respond when the customer group becomes threatened or morally contested. Longitudinal designs could also examine how bridging and valuing practices contribute to self-expansion and M-LECID over time.
