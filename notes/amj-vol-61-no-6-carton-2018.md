# How Can Leaders Overcome the Blurry Vision Bias? Identifying an Antidote to the Paradox of Vision Communication

**Abstract**
Evidence suggests that organizational leaders can inspire employees by communicating a vision of the future with image-based rhetoric—words and phrases that are readily envisioned in the mind's eye (e.g., "our vision is to make moviegoers laugh"). Yet research has demonstrated that most leaders do not craft visions with image-based rhetoric, instead favoring abstract language that cannot easily be visualized. We integrate theory on leadership and dual cognitive processing to argue that this problem is exacerbated when leaders focus on word selection when crafting visions because they overemphasize the meaning-based cognitive system (in which they consider the abstract meaning of words) and underemphasize the experience-based cognitive system (in which they can generate vivid mental images of what the future could look like). We introduce a novel tactic to help leaders activate the experience-based system and, in turn, generate and communicate more impactful visions. We also investigate boundary conditions. We test our predictions with three experiments featuring three distinct samples, including one with senior corporate executives and one in which members of the British government crafted visions on the day Britain announced it would exit the European Union ("Brexit").

**Research Question**
The paper asks why leaders continue to communicate abstract, hard-to-visualize visions even though image-based rhetoric is more inspiring, and whether temporal projection can help them overcome this "blurry vision bias." It also asks when this intervention works best, focusing on whether efficient versus analytical cognitive style changes the effect of mental imagery on image-laden vision communication.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Prescription type, comparing temporal projection or mental time travel against language-based prescriptions and control conditions.
- DV(s): Image-based rhetoric in vision communication, broader vision quality, and in Study 3 employee inspiration.
- Mediators: Mental imagery generated by temporal projection; Study 3 also tests image-laden rhetoric as a mediator between temporal projection and employee inspiration.
- Moderators: Cognitive style, contrasting efficient thinkers with analytical thinkers.

The mechanism is grounded in dual cognitive processing. A direct focus on word selection keeps leaders in the meaning-based system, where they process abstract concepts and therefore continue to craft abstract visions. Temporal projection instead asks leaders to imagine a future scenario as if they were observing it firsthand; this activates the experience-based system, creates mental imagery, and makes image-based rhetoric the natural language for communicating what they have imagined. The effect is stronger for efficient thinkers because they are less likely than analytical thinkers to override imagery with abstract semantic processing.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to leadership and vision communication theory by explaining the descriptive-prescriptive gap in which leaders are advised to choose better words but still produce blurry, abstract visions. It integrates leadership, linguistics, and dual cognitive processing to specify why temporal projection improves vision rhetoric, how mental imagery mediates the effect, and when cognitive style constrains it. It also redirects theory from wordsmithing toward the cognitive system leaders activate before choosing words.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that leaders should not rely only on advice to use vivid words. A more useful intervention is to mentally project themselves to a future moment when the organization's vision has been realized, imagine what people would see and experience, and then translate that scene into words. The findings also imply that analytically oriented leaders may need additional support or pairing with more intuitive visionary communicators when crafting inspiring visions.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that the moderated mediation evidence should be interpreted carefully because the mediator and moderator were nonnormally distributed and the conditional indirect effects were driven partly by a very strong independent-variable-to-mediator relationship. They also note that the word "company" in the mental time travel manipulation may have been less appropriate for some Brexit-study participants who worked in agencies. A further limitation is that some Brexit-study participants did not occupy high-ranking roles, even though Studies 2 and 3 included leaders with larger spans of control and senior executives.

**Future Research**
The authors suggest examining whether temporal projection generalizes to team leaders, including evidence from a supplementary MBA-team experiment. They also call for research on delivery, cadence, follower characteristics, and whether leaders can sustain high-quality vision communication after temporal projection. Future work could also study how leaders refine visions after converting mental imagery into words.
