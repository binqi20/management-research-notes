# Managing Technological Transitions by Building Bridges

**Abstract**
While much research has demonstrated that radical technological transitions challenge incumbents, surprisingly little empirical work has examined which factors drive variation in their ability to invent in the new domain. Through a longitudinal study of photography firms transitioning from analog to digital technologies, we examine a key source of heterogeneity: whether and how incumbents integrate knowledge related to both old and new technologies to bridge generations. Specifically, we explore how the use of three types of intergenerational knowledge bridges—(1) inventor bridges, (2) technology bridges, and (3) hybrid product bridges—influences inventive performance in the new generation. Consistent with theories of inertia, we find that, on average, inventor and technology bridges are associated with lower performance in the new generation. However, the strength of a firm’s inventive performance in the old generation positively moderates this effect. When performance in the old technology is strong, both technology and hybrid product bridges are associated with higher inventive performance in the new generation. This finding suggests that, when combined with a strong R&D program, old-technology knowledge forms a foundation that incumbents can leverage to learn new technologies as they navigate transitions.

**Research Question**
Which factors explain variation in incumbents' inventive performance as they transition from an old technological generation to a new one? The paper asks whether and how firms' use of intergenerational knowledge bridges between analog and digital photography affects digital inventive performance, and whether old-technology inventive performance changes the value of those bridges.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): inventor bridges, technology bridges, hybrid product bridges, and their interactions with analog inventive performance.
- DV(s): digital inventive performance, measured as forward citations to a firm's digital imaging patent portfolio by application year.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: analog inventive performance, interpreted as the strength of the firm's old-technology R&D capability.
- Controls: percent analog patents, digital exploration, number of digital patents, firm size, digital camera models, new digital inventors, firm fixed effects, and year fixed effects.

The paper theorizes intergenerational knowledge bridges as recombination across old and new technological generations. Inventor bridges can carry old capabilities, routines, and cognitive frames into the new domain, lowering inventive performance on average. Technology and hybrid product bridges can also reinforce old knowledge, but when a firm has strong analog inventive performance, old-technology knowledge becomes a usable foundation for learning digital technology rather than only an inertial constraint.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends research on technological transitions by shifting attention from incumbent-versus-entrant comparisons to heterogeneity among incumbents' inventive performance in the new technology. It qualifies inertia-based accounts by showing that old-technology knowledge can be harmful on average but useful when paired with strong old-technology R&D capability. It also links absorptive capacity, recombination, and real options reasoning to explain why technology and hybrid product bridges can function as temporary stepping-stones during transitions.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that firms should not assume all old-technology knowledge is either obsolete or valuable during a transition. Redeploying old-technology inventors into new-technology work may hurt inventive performance, while technology bridges and hybrid products are more likely to help firms with strong existing R&D capability. Managers designing transition units should therefore consider both staffing choices and whether the organization has the capability to identify and leverage relevant old knowledge.

**Limitations**
The authors note that patent data do not directly measure inventors' beliefs or behaviors. The study is also limited to one technological domain, digital photography, in which the new technology ultimately overcame the old. The results should be interpreted as correlational rather than necessarily causal.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research in settings with multiple competing new technologies or where the new technology does not fully displace the old. They also call for work on when bridges are useful, when firms should stop bridging and disengage from old technology, how firms redeploy old expertise into new markets, and how organization design for new-technology development affects inventive performance.
