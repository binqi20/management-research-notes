# Social Valuation Across Multiple Audiences: The Interplay of Ability and Identity Judgments

**Abstract**
How is an evaluating audience influenced by previous evaluations made by another audience? This question is critical to individuals and organizations reaching out to multiple audiences for key resources. While extant work has suggested evaluators are influenced by previous evaluations made by their peers, we develop theory about how evaluators' assessment of a candidate is shaped by previous evaluations made by an external (nonpeer) audience. We argue that the latter represent exogenous indices that affect evaluators in two opposing ways: they positively influence peer valuation by pointing to candidates' unobservable abilities, yet, since they are conferred by an external audience, they are also indicative of candidates' deviation from an expected peer identity. The combination of the two opposite effects suggests an inverted U-shaped relationship between exogenous indices and peer valuation. Further, this effect is moderated by the identity proximity between audiences, and the availability of previous peer evaluations (endogenous indices). We test and find support for our arguments using unique data on the peer valuation of 9,502 academic scientists applying for research grants at a research university. Our work contributes to the understanding of valuation and socially endogenous inferences, and has implications for the management of organizations in multi-audience environments.

**Research Question**
How is the peer evaluation of a candidate influenced by earlier evaluations made by external, nonpeer audiences? The paper studies this question in academic science, where scientists seek grants from peer evaluators while also receiving industry contracts from an external audience.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Industry evaluation, operationalized as the cumulative number of industry contracts acquired by a scientist as principal investigator up to the focal year.
- DV(s): Peer evaluation, operationalized primarily as the count of grants awarded to a scientist as principal investigator in a given year.
- Mediators: Not directly tested as mediators; the theory distinguishes an ability-inference component and an identity-conformity component of exogenous indices.
- Moderators: Identity proximity with industry, quality of publishing record, and irregularity of publishing record.

The mechanism is a countervailing valuation process. External audience evaluations provide peer evaluators with positive information about a candidate's unobservable ability, so moderate industry evaluation can improve peer valuation. At higher levels, the same industry evaluation can signal deviation from the expected peer identity of an academic scientist, producing an inverted U-shaped relationship. This curvilinear effect is weaker where the academic discipline is closer to industry and where strong or regular publication records provide endogenous evidence of academic quality and identity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to social valuation research by separating endogenous indices from exogenous indices and showing why prior positive evaluations do not always reinforce later valuation. It argues that evaluations by external audiences carry both ability information and identity information, making social approval from another audience potentially valuable up to a point and damaging beyond it. It also connects audience-based valuation to institutional logics by showing how peer audiences judge candidates partly through identity expectations embedded in their own valuation framework.

**Practical Implication**
For universities, the findings imply that faculty engagement with external audiences can help scientists obtain peer-valued resources, but excessive industry recognition may create an identity penalty, especially for junior researchers or those without strong publication records. University managers may reduce this penalty by strengthening alternative endogenous indices that reassure peer audiences about academic quality and identity. More broadly, organizations entering new audience domains should manage the trade-off between external audience appreciation and standing with their core audience.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study is situated in public science, raising questions about generalizability to settings where peer and external audiences use more orthogonal valuation criteria. They also state that their empirical approach relies on panel and matched-sample analyses rather than private reviewer scores or randomized assignment, because such scores and experimental designs are unavailable in their setting. External audience evaluations may also reflect unobserved reputation and status attributes beyond the measured industry contracts.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how intangible attributes such as reputation and status shape the relationship between external audience valuation and peer audience valuation. The paper also suggests studying settings where peer and external audiences differ more dramatically, including cases in which external audience appreciation may cease to function as an ability signal. Additional work could test the framework in organizations and markets where candidates are simultaneously evaluated by consumers, business customers, shareholders, clients, regulators, or other audiences.
