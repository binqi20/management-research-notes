# Different Strokes for Different Teams: The Contingent Effects of Positive and Negative Feedback on the Creativity of Informationally Homogeneous and Diverse Teams

**Abstract**
Feedback is a ubiquitous management tool. Employing it to enhance team creativity raises an important question of whether positive or negative feedback is more effective. Unfortunately, prior research on feedback valence and creativity is limited to the individual level, neglecting team creativity’s interdependent and knowledge-intensive nature. We address this issue and advance the team-information-processing perspective on team creativity by integrating two heretofore separate research streams to develop a team-specific model about how negative and positive feedback enhance creativity via two alternative information processing routes, contingent on teams’ informational diversity. Negative feedback fuels teams’ systematic effort and attention to external, novel information. In informationally diverse teams, in which members hold different information and perspectives, these efforts promote team creativity through information elaboration. Conversely, positive feedback propels members to flexibly use their information and contribute the resultant divergent insights to the team. In informationally homogeneous teams, wherein these insights relate to others’ information and perspectives, these divergent insights trigger teams’ generative processing and in turn creativity. Results from a team experiment support the predicted feedback valence by informational diversity interaction on team creativity through elaboration and generative processing.

**Research Question**
How do positive and negative feedback affect team creativity, and when does each form of feedback work better for informationally homogeneous versus informationally diverse teams? The paper specifically asks whether feedback valence interacts with informational diversity to promote team creativity through two team information-processing routes: information elaboration and generative processing.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Feedback valence, manipulated as positive versus negative feedback on teams' initial ideas.
- DV(s): Team creativity, measured as the product of coded novelty and usefulness of the team's final plan.
- Mediators: Information elaboration and generative processing, both coded from post-feedback team discussion videos.
- Moderators: Informational diversity, manipulated as homogeneous versus diverse role-related information and perspectives.

The mechanism is a fit between feedback valence and the team's informational foundation. Negative feedback prompts systematic effort, attention to novel external information, and review of unused informational resources; this benefits informationally diverse teams because members hold different information and perspectives that can be elaborated and integrated. Positive feedback encourages flexible use of existing information and divergent insights; this benefits informationally homogeneous teams because shared knowledge makes members' ideas more likely to trigger one another's generative processing. The experiment supports the interaction on creativity and shows conditional indirect effects through elaboration and generative processing.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to team creativity theory by integrating two previously separate information-processing routes: information elaboration from the team performance and decision-making tradition, and generative processing from the brainstorming tradition. It shows that both routes can enhance team creativity, but they depend on different combinations of feedback valence and informational diversity.

It also shifts feedback-valence theory from an individual-level main-effect account to a team-specific contingency account. Rather than assuming positive feedback is generally better for creativity, the study identifies informational diversity as a team-level moderator with no direct individual-level counterpart.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should match feedback valence to the informational composition of the team when the goal is creativity. Negative feedback can help informationally diverse teams by pushing members to search, discuss, and integrate their distinct informational resources, while positive feedback can help informationally homogeneous teams by encouraging flexible and mutually stimulating use of shared knowledge.

When managers must give feedback that does not fit the team's informational diversity, the paper suggests compensating through process support. Diverse teams receiving positive feedback may need prompts to share and integrate unshared information, while homogeneous teams receiving negative feedback may need novel information or support for generative processing.

**Limitations**
The study is a laboratory experiment with student teams, so the authors caution that follow-up work is needed to test generalizability to field settings. The feedback was randomly assigned and bogus rather than based on teams' actual creativity, although the authors argue this supports internal validity and may make the effects conservative if credibility was doubted.

The feedback occurred during an ongoing task, so its effects may differ from feedback on completed tasks or repeated feedback across future work. The teams were low in authority differentiation and temporal stability, and the informational-diversity manipulation may not capture demographic diversity or diversity that triggers social categorization.

**Future Research**
Future studies could test the model in field settings, with actual performance-based feedback, repeated feedback, and teams that vary in authority differentiation, skill differentiation, and temporal stability. Research could also examine whether external informational resources, such as leaders, knowledge-management systems, or boundary spanning, can substitute for the internal informational resources of diverse teams.

The paper also points to future work on how elaboration and generative processing interact over time, especially whether elaboration can trigger generative processing in diverse teams. Additional research should examine how team affective and motivational states interact dynamically with team information processing, and whether the feedback-diversity fit extends to other team tasks beyond creativity.
