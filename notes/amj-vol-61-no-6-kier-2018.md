# Entrepreneurial Imaginativeness in New Venture Ideation

**Abstract**
Although theories of entrepreneurial action regularly acknowledge the importance of imagination, the ability is rarely defined or measured, and thus effectively treated as uniform in degree and type. Using a creative problem-solving lens, we identify and measure three different cognitive skills—creative, social, and practical imaginativeness—that vary across individuals. Each skill combines the ability of imagination with the knowledge needed to mentally simulate various task-related scenarios used in generating and selecting ideas for new value creation. We then conduct a quasi-experiment to examine each skill’s relative effect on new venture ideation. We find that the three imaginativeness skills vary across individuals and that they predict new venture idea quantity and quality differently over and above the effects of motivation, knowledge, and experience. We conclude with implications for theory development in entrepreneurship and creative problem-solving.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether entrepreneurial imaginativeness can be conceptualized and measured as distinct creative, social, and practical cognitive skills, and whether those skills explain new venture idea quantity and quality. It also asks whether idea quantity mediates the relationship between imaginativeness and idea quality.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Creative imaginativeness, social imaginativeness, and practical imaginativeness.
- DV(s): New venture idea quantity and new venture idea quality.
- Mediators: Idea quantity, tested as a mediator between each form of imaginativeness and idea quality.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is that imaginativeness combines the cognitive ability of imagination with task-relevant entrepreneurial knowledge. Creative imaginativeness helps individuals form novel means-ends connections, social imaginativeness helps them understand others' needs and perspectives, and practical imaginativeness helps them identify problems and evaluate feasible solutions. The study finds that all three forms predict idea quantity, that idea quantity fully mediates the creative imaginativeness-idea quality link, and that idea quantity partially mediates the practical imaginativeness-idea quality link.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to entrepreneurship theory by moving imagination from an assumed latent ability to a measurable set of entrepreneurial imaginativeness skills. It extends creative problem-solving theory by showing that creative, social, and practical imaginativeness explain new venture ideation beyond motivation, knowledge, and experience. It also suggests that opportunity recognition, effectuation, bricolage, narrative/storytelling, and analogical reasoning may depend on unevenly distributed forms of imaginativeness rather than on a uniform imaginative capacity.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that venture teams should not assume that all founders possess the same form of imagination. New venture ideation may benefit from diagnosing or developing creative, social, and practical imaginativeness separately, or from composing teams whose members compensate for one another's imaginative strengths and weaknesses. It also implies that tools such as effectuation, bricolage, design thinking, and lean startup may work differently depending on the imaginativeness of the individuals using them.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study uses a mechanistic, post-positivistic approach that measures imaginativeness as a cognitive skill rather than examining imagination more directly through alternative philosophical worldviews. They also state that the study uses a cross-sectional design, which may be limited because entrepreneurship unfolds over time. The common facial-recognition scenario improves comparability but leaves open whether the findings generalize across phases of entrepreneurship, teams, existing organizations, and other venture contexts.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how entrepreneurial imaginativeness affects other models of entrepreneurial action, including effectuation, bricolage, design thinking, lean startup, improvisation, and analogical or metaphorical reasoning. They suggest work across levels of analysis, especially how individual imaginativeness combines into team-level entrepreneurial imaginativeness. They also recommend longitudinal and interpretive research on the antecedents, development, unconscious, embodied, and sensory aspects of imagination.
