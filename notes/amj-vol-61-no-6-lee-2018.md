# From Protest to Product: Strategic Frame Brokerage in a Commercial Social Movement Organization

**Abstract**
Social movement organizations that engage directly in commercial activities face the particular challenge of mobilizing movement activists and commercial actors simultaneously. In a seven-year case study of a Sicilian anti-racket social movement organization that uses commercial activities to combat Mafia racketeering, we show how strategic framing enables such dual mobilization. Our findings show that original antiracket social movement frames were modified through a process of strategic frame brokerage that incorporated, through interaction, the distributed interpretations of tourists, tourism service providers, and anti-racket activists. As a result of this process, original social movement frames were retained, transformed, or managed through selective referral. We discuss the implications of our findings for research on processes of frame development, social enterprise, and commercialization as a “mobilizing technology” for social movements.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how a social movement organization entering an existing industry can frame its commercial activities so that they mobilize industry actors while retaining support from the social movement whose goals it shares. Empirically, it examines how Addiopizzo Travel reconciled anti-racket movement frames with responsible tourism industry frames while asking how to meet market demands without betraying the anti-racket movement.

**Mechanism Process**
- Initial frame transposition: AT transposed anti-racket movement frames of community, security, dignity, and denunciation into commercial tourism offerings.
- Validation with multiple audiences: AT tested whether these frames resonated with tourists and tourism service providers and whether they remained consistent with anti-racket movement ideology.
- Search through interaction: When some frames failed to resonate, AT interacted with tourists, tourism service providers, and activists to surface alternative interpretations of its activities.
- Strategic lamination and transformation: AT transformed security into sustainability and dignity into beauty by layering newly keyed industry and activist interpretations onto original movement frames.
- Retention and selective referral: AT retained community because it resonated with both audiences, while denunciation was selectively referred to other parts of the anti-racket movement rather than used to frame AT's commercial activities directly.
- Operationalization: Validated frames guided new tours, partnerships, booking services, and referrals that mobilized commercial actors without severing ideological ties to the movement.

The process is strategic frame brokerage: leaders of a commercial social movement organization broker between movement activists and commercial actors by repeatedly validating, revising, retaining, transforming, or selectively referring movement frames. The mechanism is not a statistical causal model; it is an inductive process model showing how interaction supplies distributed interpretations that leaders incorporate into frames capable of dual mobilization. The core tension is that frames strong enough to preserve movement ideology may be confusing or threatening to market actors, while market-resonant frames may be judged by activists as mission drift.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on frame development by integrating strategic framing and interactional framing. It shows that social movement leaders do not only impose frames top-down or wait for frames to emerge bottom-up; they can strategically elicit, broker, and laminate interpretations from multiple supporter groups. It also contributes to social enterprise and hybrid organization research by showing how mission drift concerns can be managed through framing, and to social movement research by theorizing commercialization as a mobilizing technology that can attract supporters who are not already ideological adherents.

**Practical Implication**
For commercial social movement organizations and social enterprises, the paper implies that market entry requires active frame work with both commercial audiences and movement constituencies. Managers should validate whether frames are narratively credible to customers and partners while also checking whether activists see them as ideologically consistent. When a movement frame is too central to transform, selective referral may preserve the movement connection without forcing the commercial activity to carry a frame that undermines market resonance.

**Limitations**
The authors identify as a boundary condition the existence of established interpretive frames in both the social movement and the commercial industry. In settings with nascent market categories or less established movements, a social movement organization may shape the category or the movement rather than merely broker between existing frames. They also note that some movements may not be amenable to commercial transactions, that frame brokerage may not always find common ground, and that the process is costly in time and attention.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on strategic frame brokerage in nascent market categories, newer social movements, and contexts where common ground between activists and industry actors is harder to find. It also suggests studying the limits of multivalent frames, delaminating processes through which frame meanings decay or become more specific, and the skills needed to transition from collective action to commerce. Future work could also examine how commercial social movement organizations coordinate with other movement organizations and whether commercial participation complements or substitutes for traditional activism.
