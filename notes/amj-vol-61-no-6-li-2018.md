# A Network View of Advice-giving and Individual Creativity in Teams: A Brokerage-driven, Socially Perpetuated Phenomenon

**Abstract**
One potential benefit of team contexts is that members can share knowledge and ideas to help one another solve problems (e.g., advice-giving), thereby enhancing members’ creative potential. However, individuals’ advice-giving actions do not guarantee increases in recipients’ creativity and, in some cases, may even be a creative liability for the giver’s own subsequent creativity. We argue that considering advice givers’ brokerage in the team’s advice-giving network (i.e., advice brokerage) may clarify this phenomenon. Specifically, we posit that advice given from a brokerage position contains more nonredundant content that boosts recipients’ creativity. Further, we expect advice brokerage to positively influence a giver’s own creativity, though this effect is at least partially explained via recipients’ proximal creativity gains (e.g., recipients’ creativity change mediates the relationship). Recipients’ perceptions of psychological safety accentuate these relationships. Longitudinal field data support the main hypotheses, but also suggest that advice brokerage has more immediate proximal effects on givers’ creativity; an additional qualitative study elucidates several mechanisms by which brokered advice boosts creativity, including two associated with generating diverse ideas (idea integration, perspective-taking), two associated with domain-specific knowledge accumulation (knowledge accumulation, reflection), collective creativity, and creative feedback. The study yields several important implications.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when advice-giving in teams improves creativity for advice recipients and advice givers, rather than simply consuming the giver's time or reinforcing existing ideas. It focuses on whether a giver's brokerage position in the team's advice-giving network makes advice more creatively useful, whether recipients' creativity gains socially perpetuate gains for givers, and whether recipients' psychological safety strengthens these effects.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Advice givers' brokerage in the team advice-giving network, measured with betweenness centrality.
- DV(s): Advice recipients' creativity enhancement and advice givers' creativity enhancement over time.
- Mediators: Advice recipients' creativity enhancement mediating the relationship between a giver's advice brokerage and the giver's later creativity enhancement.
- Moderators: Advice recipients' perceived psychological safety.

The mechanism is that brokers give advice from positions that connect otherwise unconnected teammates, so their advice is more likely to carry nonredundant knowledge, perspectives, and integrative ideas. Recipients who feel psychologically safe are more willing to use this brokered advice in creative problem solving, increasing their own creativity. Those recipient gains then feed back into the team context and the giver's subsequent creativity. The qualitative study extends the model by showing additional processes: idea integration, perspective taking, knowledge accumulation, reflection, collective creativity, and creative feedback.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to social views of creativity by showing that advice-giving is not uniformly helpful; its creative value depends on the giver's brokerage position in the team advice network. It extends brokerage theory into within-team networks by showing that brokers can act as catalysts for others' creativity rather than hoarding information for private advantage. It also connects network structure with psychological safety and dynamic creativity change, showing that creativity in teams can be socially perpetuated across givers and recipients over time.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that managers should encourage employees to provide novel and helpful advice across relatively separate camps or subgroups within teams. It also implies that managers can strengthen creativity by creating psychologically safe environments, publicly supporting creative behavior, discouraging ridicule of failed attempts, and making members' areas of expertise more visible so that cross-subgroup advice exchange becomes easier.

**Limitations**
The authors state that Study 1 could not capture the specific underlying processes linking brokered advice to creativity, which motivated the qualitative follow-up. They also note that the indirect effect through recipients' creativity was relatively small and that other mediators, such as creative feedback and knowledge reflection, may explain why giving advice enhances givers' creativity. Additional limitations are that Study 2 focused only on brokers rather than comparing brokers and nonbrokers, several qualitative processes overlapped, psychological safety had a counterintuitive negative correlation with recipient creativity, and both studies were conducted in China despite some theory being developed in Western contexts.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on other mediators of the advice brokerage-creativity relationship, especially creative feedback and knowledge reflection. They also encourage broker versus nonbroker comparisons, replication and extension of the qualitative themes, and work on when psychological safety becomes an asset or liability for creativity. Because the empirical setting was China, they further call for validation in other cultures.
