# No Employee an Island: Workplace Loneliness and Job Performance

**Abstract**
This research investigates the link between workplace loneliness and job performance. Integrating the regulatory loop model of loneliness and the affect theory of social exchange, we develop a model of workplace loneliness. We focus on the central role of affiliation in explaining the loneliness–performance relationship, predicting that despite lonelier employees’ desire to connect with others, being lonelier is associated with lower job performance because of a lack of affiliation at work. Through a time-lagged field study of 672 employees and their 114 supervisors in two organizations, we find support that greater workplace loneliness is related to lower job performance; the mediators of this relationship are lonelier employees’ lower approachability and lesser affective commitment to their organizations. We also examine the moderating roles of the emotional cultures of companionate love and anger, as well as of the loneliness of other coworkers in the work group. Features of this affective affiliative context moderate some of the relationships between loneliness and the mediating variables; we also find support for the full moderated mediation model. This study highlights the importance of recognizing the pernicious power of workplace loneliness over both lonelier employees and their organizations. We offer implications for future research and practice.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how workplace loneliness relates to employee job performance and why that relationship occurs through affiliation at work. It also asks whether the affective affiliative context of the work group, including emotional cultures of companionate love and anger and coworker loneliness, moderates loneliness' effects on affiliative outcomes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee workplace loneliness.
- DV(s): Supervisor-rated employee job performance.
- Mediators: Coworker-rated employee approachability and employee self-reported affective commitment to the organization.
- Moderators: Work group emotional culture of companionate love, work group emotional culture of anger, and aggregate loneliness of other coworkers in the employee's work group.

The central mechanism is that workplace loneliness weakens affiliation. Drawing on the regulatory loop model of loneliness, the paper argues that lonelier employees become more socially vigilant, defensive, and withdrawn, which makes them appear less approachable to coworkers. Drawing on affect theory of social exchange, it argues that negative social exchanges are attributed beyond immediate coworkers to the organization, reducing affective commitment. Lower approachability and lower affective commitment then relate to poorer supervisor-rated job performance, with the broader affective affiliative context shaping some of the loneliness-affiliation links.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends loneliness research into the workplace by showing that loneliness is not only an individual psychological state but also a relational workplace phenomenon associated with job performance. It contributes to organizational affect research by examining a discrete affective experience rather than generalized positive or negative affect. It also integrates the regulatory loop model of loneliness with affect theory of social exchange to explain both the psychological withdrawal of lonely employees and the interpersonal/contextual dynamics through which coworkers and organizations become implicated.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that managers should treat workplace loneliness as an organizational problem rather than merely as a private employee issue. Organizations can reduce the performance costs of loneliness by providing timely support, improving opportunities for social connection, and addressing maladaptive social cognitions rather than only bringing lonely employees together. The authors also caution that interventions require sustained effort because loneliness itself can inhibit employees' motivation and skill in reaching out.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the time-lagged design and theory suggest the modeled direction, but the study cannot confirm causality. They note that poorer performance could isolate employees and increase loneliness, and that reciprocal cycles may operate. They also identify limitations from using managerial performance ratings rather than objective performance measures and from studying workplace loneliness only within the United States.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how organizations and employees can alleviate workplace loneliness, how often and how quickly workplace loneliness changes, and which antecedents prevent loneliness from arising. They also suggest examining cognitive attributions, other moderators of approachability, objective performance outcomes such as sales volume or output, and cross-cultural variation in workplace loneliness.
