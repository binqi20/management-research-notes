# Not Too Tired to be Proactive: Daily Empowering Leadership Spurs Next-morning Employee Proactivity as Moderated by Nightly Sleep Quality

**Abstract**
Building on the Model of Proactive Motivation, we hypothesize that a leader’s daily empowering leadership behaviors function as a motivator for employees to display a greater amount of proactive goal-striving behavior, by means of enhanced daily proactive goal setting. Expanding the Model of Proactive Motivation, we incorporate the largely neglected self-regulatory resource perspective and hypothesize that employee sleep quality operates as a qualifying influence on employees’ ability to set and strive for proactive goals that allow employees to take advantage of empowerment opportunities. Using a time-lagged experience sampling methodology, our hypotheses were tested in a sample of 98 professionals who completed twice-daily surveys over a two-week period. Study results confirm our hypotheses that experiencing daily empowering leadership enhances employees’ next morning proactive behaviors of risk-taking and voice behavior due to more proactive goal setting at the start of the workday. Finally, employee next-day goal-setting and goal-striving behavior is especially enhanced for those employees who experienced higher levels of sleep quality. Theoretical and practical implications derived from our research are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do daily empowering leadership behaviors shape employees' next-morning proactive goal setting and next-day proactive goal-striving behaviors? The paper asks whether nightly sleep quality qualifies employees' ability to convert daily empowerment opportunities into proactive goals, risk-taking, and voice behavior.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Daily empowering leadership behavior.
- DV(s): Daily risk-taking behavior and daily voice behavior.
- Mediators: Next-morning proactive goal setting.
- Moderators: Nightly sleep quality, modeled as a first-stage and second-stage moderator of the empowering leadership to proactive goals path and the proactive goals to proactive behavior paths.

The mechanism is a daily carryover process. Empowering leadership creates accountability, ambiguity, and autonomy, which motivate employees to set proactive goals the next morning. Those proactive goals then guide goal-striving behaviors during the workday, expressed as informed risk-taking to improve one's own work and voice behavior to improve work situations. The self-regulatory resource argument is that high-quality sleep restores the resources employees need to set and enact those proactive goals, whereas poor sleep weakens both the goal-setting response to empowerment and the translation of goals into risk-taking and voice.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to workplace proactivity theory by showing that proactivity is not only a stable individual difference but also a within-person, goal-driven process that fluctuates across days. It extends Parker et al.'s Model of Proactive Motivation by integrating a self-regulatory resource perspective and identifying sleep quality as a daily resource condition that strengthens or weakens proactive goal setting and goal striving.

The paper also contributes to empowering leadership research by treating empowering leadership as a daily behavior rather than only as a stable leadership style. This dynamic view explains why the same employee may respond differently to empowerment opportunities across days depending on nightly recovery.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can promote daily risk-taking and voice by sharing power and creating daily empowerment opportunities, but those opportunities are more likely to translate into proactivity when employees are well rested. The authors suggest assigning well-rested employees to novel and challenging work requiring initiative, while assigning employees with poorer sleep to more routine tasks that are less vulnerable to sleep-related cognitive deficits.

The paper also implies that organizations should treat sleep quality as relevant to proactive performance rather than as a purely private issue. The authors suggest that allowing naps may help employees capitalize on empowerment opportunities.

**Limitations**
All study variables were self-reported by participants, so common method concerns remain despite temporal separation of morning and afternoon surveys, interaction tests, and structural equation modeling. The study also measured sleep subjectively rather than with objective sleep measures such as actigraphy.

The authors note that the study did not examine possible negative consequences of employee proactivity, such as proactive goals being viewed as self-serving or ineffective by others. They also did not include a lengthy list of daily or stable control variables, including chronic sleep deprivation.

**Future Research**
Future research could use nonself-report measures, such as supervisor ratings of daily risk-taking and voice, and objective sleep measures such as actigraphy. It could also study the possible costs of proactivity to provide a more balanced account of employee proactive behavior at work.

The paper encourages future studies to incorporate theoretically relevant controls such as chronic sleep deprivation. It also points to nutrition as another resource-restorative influence that could be integrated into models of proactive and overall employee performance.
