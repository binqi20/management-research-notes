# When Equity Seems Unfair: The Role of Justice Enforceability in Temporary Team Coordination

**Abstract**
Temporary teams can accomplish tightly coupled complex work even without the shared experience that enables coordination in longstanding teams. To advance understanding of this process, I conducted an inductive study of temporary teams in four hospital emergency departments (EDs), and found that the teams in two EDs coordinated effectively, but the teams in the other two EDs did not. To theorize an explanation, I draw on the organizational justice literature and introduce the idea of justice enforceability, defined as the perception that authorities can act fairly, given the potential for other people to cheat. The team members' perceptions of justice enforceability were focused on whether the distribution of work within and between teams was fair or could be cheated. When team members perceived that justice was enforceable, they were willing to engage in the extrarole behaviors that wove together their individual responsibilities. But when they perceived that cheating was possible, they avoided the extra-role behaviors that would have made them suckers for working hard while others cheated. Justice enforceability thus resolves a common tension in temporary teams, namely that the potential for uneven effort can undermine team coordination. In these ED teams, members only trusted that authorities could act fairly when the distribution of work was exactly equal; then team members felt they were all "in it together," and each put in the work to coordinate as a team.

**Research Question**
The paper asks under what conditions members of temporary groups engage in the extra-role behaviors that are core to effective group coordination. It examines why temporary teams with similar pod structures in emergency departments varied in whether they developed tightly coupled coordination.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key context: Temporary ED teams had shared role structures and tightly interdependent patient-care work, but team membership was fluid and short-lived.
- Assignment processes: Alpine and Belleview used exactly equal round-robin patient assignment, while Carter and Dyer used Charge Nurse judgments of pod capacity.
- Justice enforceability: Equal assignment made cheating difficult and supported perceptions that fair work distribution was enforceable; equitable assignment was more flexible but gameable.
- Team response: High justice enforceability fostered solidarity, updating, monitoring, helping, and synchrony; low justice enforceability fostered self-protection, individual focus, limited helping, and weak team coordination.

The process centers on whether temporary team members believe authorities can enforce fair work distribution when others may cheat. When fairness is enforceable, members see themselves as "in it together" and invest in extra-role behaviors that knit together their interdependent responsibilities. When fairness is unenforceable, members fear becoming suckers for others' slack effort and retreat into individual responsibilities, which weakens team coordination.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on temporary group coordination by showing that the same formal role structure can be enacted either as coordinated collective work or as individually bounded role performance. It introduces justice enforceability as an organizational justice construct: perceptions that authorities can act fairly given the possibility that others may cheat decisions or procedures. This extends justice theory beyond whether authorities follow justice rules to whether fair treatment can be protected in high-turnover, gameable settings.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations using temporary teams should attend to whether work assignment systems make fair contribution enforceable, not only whether leaders intend to allocate work equitably. The ED comparison suggests that team members may accept stressful equal assignment when it provides visible assurance that others cannot cheat workload distribution. Leaders should therefore examine whether accountability and assignment safeguards give temporary team members reason to trust that extra-role effort will not simply let others free ride.

**Limitations**
The study is based on four hospital emergency departments matched on several characteristics and centered on pod-based doctor-nurse teams, so the findings may depend on contexts where work is countable and roles are relatively clear. The paper notes that the ED setting is distinctive because patients can be counted and physician and nurse roles are clearly defined. It does not claim that exactly equal assignment is universally preferable; it shows a tradeoff between stressful enforceability and flexible but gameable equity.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on how temporary teams interface with larger systems, including boundary-spanning relationships beyond the immediate team. It also suggests studying change and learning in temporary teams, where constant membership change may alter expectations about "the way it's done here." Future work should examine justice enforceability when tasks are less divisible, roles are more ambiguous, or the dynamics of justice and coordination differ from emergency departments.
