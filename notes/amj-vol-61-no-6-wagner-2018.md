# Knowing Me, Knowing You: Inventor Mobility and the Formation of Technology-oriented Alliances

**Abstract**
We link the hiring of research and development scientists from industry competitors to the subsequent formation of collaborative agreements, namely technology-oriented alliances. By transferring technological knowledge as well as cognitive elements to the hiring firm, mobile inventors foster the alignment of decision frames applied by potential alliance partners in the process of alliance formation, thereby making collaboration more likely. Using data on inventor mobility and alliance formation among 42 global pharmaceutical firms over 16 years, we show that inventor mobility is positively associated with the likelihood of alliance formation in periods following inventor movements. This relationship becomes more pronounced if mobile employees bring additional knowledge about their prior firm’s technological capabilities and for alliances aimed at technology development rather than for agreements related to technology transfer. It is weakened, however, if a firm is already familiar with the competitor’s technological capabilities. By revealing these relationships, our study contributes to research on alliance formation, employee mobility, and organizational frames.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether hiring R&D scientists from a competitor increases the likelihood that the hiring firm later forms a technology-oriented alliance with that competitor. It further asks when this mobility-alliance relationship is strengthened or weakened by inventor firm-specific knowledge, the hiring firm's familiarity with the competitor's technology, and the distinction between technology-development and technology-transfer alliances.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Number of inventors moving from competitor j to focal firm i during the preceding five-year window.
- DV(s): Formation of technology-oriented alliances between the firm dyad, separated into joint R&D alliances, pooled technology-development alliances, and technology-transfer/licensing agreements.
- Mediators: The paper theorizes reduced informational uncertainty, transfer of technological and cognitive knowledge, hybrid decision frames, and interfirm frame alignment, but it does not estimate these as formal mediators.
- Moderators: Mobile inventors' firm-specific knowledge, the hiring firm's technological familiarity with the competitor, and alliance type.
- Controls / identification checks: Prior alliances, technological convergence, technological distance, geographical distance, firm patents, patent citations, R&D intensity, employees, year dummies, three-way clustered standard errors, lagged mobility windows, and instrumental-variable probit regressions.

The mechanism is cross-level: mobile inventors carry technological knowledge and cognitive elements from a prior employer into the hiring firm, helping the hiring firm understand the competitor and align decision frames during alliance formation. This makes collaboration more likely for technology-development alliances, where uncertainty, joint coordination, and interpretive alignment are more important than in technology-transfer agreements. The effect is stronger when inventors know more about the prior firm and weaker when the hiring firm is already technologically familiar with the potential partner.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to alliance formation research by showing how nonexecutive mobile inventors can shape interfirm collaboration, not only CEOs, board ties, prior alliances, or resource complementarities. It extends employee mobility and learning-by-hiring research by arguing that mobility transfers cognitive elements and decision frames, not only technical knowledge, and that mobility and alliances can be complementary rather than substitutes. It also brings an organizational-frames lens into technology-oriented alliance formation by specifying how frame alignment helps reduce interpretive uncertainty between potential partners.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that managers should involve R&D scientists in identifying and evaluating collaboration partners rather than treating them only as implementation resources after alliances are formed. It also reframes employee mobility as more than a threat of knowledge leakage: mobile inventors may create contact points, reduce uncertainty, and enable future R&D collaboration with competitors. For firms managing mobility restrictions, the findings suggest that limiting inventor movement may also reduce indirect knowledge spillovers through alliances.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study is rooted in a knowledge-intensive pharmaceutical context and relies heavily on patent data, so the conclusions may not generalize to industries where knowledge is more explicit or embedded in organizational routines, objects, or procedures rather than individuals. They also acknowledge that patents are imperfect for identifying employee mobility, despite their manual verification of mobility events. Finally, without a natural experiment, the analysis cannot fully rule out remaining unobserved heterogeneity, and the authors cannot observe which firm initiates alliance negotiations.

**Future Research**
The authors call for future research that identifies exogenous changes in mobility or alliance formation to examine the relationship more causally. They suggest studying whether alliances related to prior inventor mobility are more effective or less likely to fail because organizational frames are bridged by mobile employees. They also propose case studies or ethnographies of frame alignment processes, and research on directional events such as acquisitions or reverse mobility from the focal firm to competitors.
