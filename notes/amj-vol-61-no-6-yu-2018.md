# Consequences of Downward Envy: A Model of Self-esteem Threat, Abusive Supervision, and Supervisory Leader Self-improvement

**Abstract**
We invoke theory and research on the social functional view of envy to propose a novel theoretical framework of supervisory leader envy of direct reports. Findings from two multi-source, multi-wave studies of supervisor–subordinate dyads provide support for the theses that (1) downward envy of subordinates threatens supervisors’ self-esteem and triggers adaptive strategies in the form of abusive supervision and supervisory self-improvement; and (2) supervisors are more likely to respond to downward envy induced self-esteem threat with abuse when they perceive envied subordinates to be cold and competent and they are more likely to respond with self-improvement when they perceive envied subordinates to be warm and competent.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how supervisory leaders express and manage envy of direct reports, and whether downward envy leads to either abusive supervision or supervisory leader self-improvement through self-esteem threat. It also asks when supervisors choose each response, focusing on supervisors' perceptions of subordinates' warmth and competence.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Supervisory leaders' downward envy of a direct report.
- DV(s): Abusive supervision and supervisory leader self-improvement.
- Mediators: Self-esteem threat, modeled by Time 2 state self-esteem while controlling for baseline self-esteem.
- Moderators: Supervisors' perceptions of the envied subordinate's warmth and competence.

The mechanism begins with downward envy as a painful feeling of inferiority toward a subordinate who possesses something the supervisor desires but lacks. From the social functional view of envy, this emotion threatens the supervisor's self-esteem and motivates gap-reduction behavior. When the envied subordinate is perceived as cold and competent, self-esteem threat is more likely to translate into abusive supervision as a leveling-down response. When the envied subordinate is perceived as warm and competent, self-esteem threat is more likely to translate into supervisory self-improvement as a leveling-up response.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends leadership theory by showing that supervisory leaders can experience downward envy toward direct reports, despite the formal privileges of leadership roles. It contributes to envy theory by specifying self-esteem threat as the mediator linking downward envy to two different adaptive responses, and by using warmth and competence from social cognition theory to explain why one response becomes abusive while the other becomes developmental. It also reframes abusive supervision as potentially strategic and goal-directed, not only as an impulsive hostile reaction to frustration.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that organizations should treat downward envy as a possible risk factor for abusive supervision, while recognizing that abuse is not inevitable. Training can help supervisors recognize envy and abusive triggers, manage those reactions, and channel envy into formal or informal self-improvement. The authors also suggest that employees should not hide competence, but that pairing competence with warmth may reduce the likelihood that supervisory envy turns into abuse.

**Limitations**
The authors note that they did not test whether abusive supervision or self-improvement actually reduced the perceived gap and self-esteem threat that motivated those behaviors. They also did not examine the precursors of downward envy, how direct reports respond to being envied, or fully rule out common method bias for the relationship between downward envy and self-esteem threat because both were measured from supervisors at the same time.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether leveling-down through abusive supervision or leveling-up through self-improvement repairs envy-induced self-esteem threat, and whether abusive responses may worsen rather than repair supervisors' emotional states. The authors also call for work on antecedents of downward envy, including supervisors' relative standing on warmth and competence compared with direct reports. Additional research should study how envied subordinates respond to downward envy and which reactions are more or less effective.
