# Gender, Brokerage, and Performance: A Construal Approach

**Abstract**
We present a new theory that seeks to explain differences in the performance of men and women friendship network brokers—individuals who bridge disconnected friends. In contrast to previous audience-centered explanations, our phenomenological theory emphasizes how brokers construe (i.e., perceive and interpret) their networks. We contend that when women perceive themselves as brokers in friendship networks they experience threat, rooted in negative stereotypes about women brokers, which undermines their performance. Using data from a cohort of MBA students, Study 1 finds that women (but not men) exhibit lower performance when they perceive themselves as brokers in small-group friendship networks. Using data from a larger group of MBA students, Study 2 replicates this finding and rules out the possibility that underlying differences in the propensity to connect those whom one bridges may explain the observed gender-based difference in broker performance. Using an experimental design, Study 3 finds that elevated anxiety about task performance and negative social evaluations mediate the relationship between brokerage and performance for women but not for men. Women and men differ in how they psychologically construe brokerage in friendship networks, and this difference helps to account for gender differences in the performance of network brokers.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why women and men obtain different performance returns from friendship network brokerage. It specifically examines whether the difference comes from brokers' own construal of brokerage, rather than only from audience reactions to brokers.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived friendship network brokerage in Studies 1 and 2; assigned broker versus closed friendship network condition in Study 3.
- DV(s): Individual performance, measured through course exam performance, final paper performance, and expert-rated quality of advice on a workplace problem.
- Mediators: Anxiety about task performance and negative social evaluations in Study 3.
- Moderators: Gender; the predicted negative brokerage-performance process applies to women but not men.

The mechanism is that brokerage in friendship networks carries masculine associations and a negative stereotype about women's broker performance. When women perceive themselves as friendship network brokers, this activates stereotype threat, increasing anxiety about performance and negative social evaluation. That anxiety reduces their ability to capitalize on informational advantages from brokerage, lowering performance relative to comparable men or women in closed networks.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a construal approach to brokerage that shifts attention from network structure alone to how individuals perceive and interpret the network positions they occupy. It contributes to research on gender and social networks by showing that women brokers may be disadvantaged not only because others penalize them, but also because gendered stereotypes alter their own experience of brokerage. It also extends network cognition research by showing that social identity and stereotype threat shape how network structures affect performance.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should not assume that encouraging brokerage will generate equal returns for all employees. The authors suggest raising awareness of stereotype threat, explicitly challenging the legitimacy of gendered broker stereotypes, and helping women reappraise threat in brokerage roles as part of career training and professional education.

**Limitations**
The authors identify the business-school and business-context setting as a possible boundary condition because gender stereotypes about competence and leadership are especially salient in business. The studies focus on friendship relations, so the argument may not apply in the same way to instrumental or mandated workflow networks. The paper also notes that ego-centric construal may interact with others' reactions, but it does not directly test that combined process.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether stereotype threat is weaker when brokerage occurs in instrumental rather than friendship networks. Scholars could also test how contextual factors such as gender composition, cultural artifacts, nationality, ethnicity, rank, or role affect the salience of gender in brokerage construal. The authors further call for research on whether prior brokerage success, women in formal authority positions, withdrawal, turnover, and network change alter or follow from network-based stereotype threat.
