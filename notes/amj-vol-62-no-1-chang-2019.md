# Diversity Thresholds: How Social Norms, Visibility, and Scrutiny Relate to Group Composition

**Abstract**
Across a field study and four experiments, we examine how social norms and scrutiny affect decisions about adding members of underrepresented populations (e.g., women, racial minorities) to groups. When groups are scrutinized, we theorize that decision makers strive to match the diversity observed in peer groups due to impression management concerns, thereby conforming to the descriptive social norm. We examine this first in the context of U.S. corporate boards, where firms face pressure to increase gender diversity. Analyses of S&P 1500 boards reveal that significantly more boards include exactly two women (the descriptive social norm) than would be expected by chance. This overrepresentation of two-women boards—a phenomenon we call “twokenism”—is more pronounced among more visible companies, consistent with our theorizing around impression management and scrutiny. Experimental data corroborate these findings and provide support for our theoretical mechanism: decision makers are discontinuously less likely to add a woman to a board once it includes two women (the social norm), and decision makers’ likelihood of adding a woman or minority to a group is influenced by the descriptive social norms and scrutiny faced. Together, these findings provide a new perspective on the persistent underrepresentation of women and minorities in organizations.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how social norms and scrutiny affect decisions about adding members of underrepresented populations to groups. It specifically examines whether decision makers use descriptive social norms as diversity thresholds and whether visibility and scrutiny strengthen this tendency.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Descriptive social norms for diversity, scrutiny of diversity, and group or firm visibility.
- DV(s): Group diversity composition and the likelihood of selecting women or racial minority candidates for open group positions.
- Mediators: Impression management concerns and goal-threshold behavior around matching peer diversity levels.
- Moderators: Scrutiny and visibility.

The proposed mechanism is that decision makers facing scrutiny seek safety in numbers by matching the diversity levels observed in peer groups. Once the descriptive social norm is reached, they relax efforts to add additional underrepresented members, producing clustering at salient thresholds such as two women on a board. Visibility increases the expected scrutiny of group composition, making conformity to descriptive social norms more likely.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a threshold-based account of persistent underrepresentation by showing how descriptive social norms can limit, rather than simply encourage, diversity gains. It links social norm theory, impression management, and goal-setting logic to explain why scrutinized groups may cluster at minimally acceptable levels of representation.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that scrutiny can increase diversity, but only up to the descriptive norm that decision makers perceive as sufficient. Policymakers and advocates may need to shift salient norms toward more ambitious standards, such as population parity or explicitly endorsed ideals, rather than allowing peer averages to define adequacy.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Studies 1A and 1B are correlational and cannot fully rule out reverse causality or omitted confounds such as firm performance. They also acknowledge that the experiments examine individual decisions in simplified settings, while real group-member selection processes are more complex and often involve multiple decision makers.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on how descriptive social norms around diversity shift, including whether interventions can make more ambitious norms salient. It also recommends examining other organizational contexts, group sizes, and psychological mechanisms through which descriptive social norms and scrutiny create diversity-threshold effects.
