# Something in Common: Competitive Dissimilarity and Performance of Rivals with Common Shareholders

**Abstract**
Economists have long held that bringing firms under the umbrella of a common ownership structure creates monopolistic conditions that reduce competition. We challenge this view by investigating competition between firms in a nuanced manner. We examine the competitive and performance implications of “common institutional ownership,” which occurs when an institutional investor owns a sizeable number of shares in two publicly traded firms. We argue that rival firms with common ownership structures will engage in dissimilar competitive action repertoires to avoid direct competition with each other. Competing aggressively, but with dissimilar action repertoires, allows rivals to maintain high levels of performance. Competing with dissimilar action repertoires also helps ensure that performance disparity between the two firms remains low. In other words, competitive aggressiveness with dissimilar action repertoires yields an optimal competitive solution for rival firms and their common owners.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how common institutional ownership shapes competition between rival firms and whether rival firms with common owners achieve better joint performance and lower performance disparity when they compete with dissimilar competitive action repertoires.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Common institutional ownership; competitive aggressiveness as the volume of competitive actions in a dyad.
- DV(s): Competitive action repertoire dissimilarity, joint ROA, and disparity ROA.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Dissimilarity of rival firms' competitive action repertoires moderates the relationship between competitive aggressiveness and both joint performance and performance disparity.

The mechanism is that common institutional owners function as shared principals across rival firms. Instead of simply reducing competition, these owners can pressure managers to avoid direct rivalry by competing in different action spheres. When rivals are both competitively aggressive and dissimilar in their action repertoires, they can increase joint performance while keeping performance disparity low.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to competitive dynamics by identifying common institutional ownership as an antecedent of competitive action repertoire dissimilarity in rival dyads. It also extends agency theory from a within-firm principal-agent framing to an interfirm governance mechanism in which common principals influence multiple managerial agents across rival firms.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should attend not only to who owns their firm but also to where those owners are invested among rivals. The findings also caution regulators against assuming that common ownership necessarily suppresses competition, because the study finds that common ownership can be associated with more competitive actions when those actions are differentiated across rivals.

**Limitations**
The study measures whether rivals use different action types but does not observe whether different actions are used to pursue the same underlying strategic objective. It also does not distinguish among types of shareholders, shareholder time horizons, or the magnitude and nature of specific competitive moves.

**Future Research**
Future research could unpack finer-grained action types, the pace at which firms move into dissimilar action repertoires, and boundary conditions that prevent firms from reaching the optimal competitive solution. Additional work could examine industry-level common ownership, more complex rivalry sets beyond dyads, and survey evidence on how common owners motivate managers to take or refrain from competitive actions.
