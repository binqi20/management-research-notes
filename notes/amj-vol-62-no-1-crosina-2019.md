# Toward a Model of Organizational Mourning: The Case of Former Lehman Brothers Bankers

**Abstract**
Focusing on the post-bankruptcy reactions of former Lehman Brothers’ bankers, we build a model of organizational mourning that depicts the thoughts, feelings, and actions of individual members dealing with the loss of their organization. We argue that organizational mourning is a process comprised of five interrelated phases, namely: (1) “experiencing the death event,” (2) “remembering the organization,” (3) “assessing loss,” (4) “salvaging: evaluate and restore,” and (5) “creating continuity and detaching.” Our empirical case suggests that at its core, organizational mourning involves both holding on and letting go of a defunct organization. Understanding how former members mourn is crucial to appreciate how they may ultimately find continuity after an organizational death, including how they enact their subsequent career paths. We conclude by discussing implications for theory of our research—notably, for literatures on post-death organizing, and personal mourning—as well as implications for practice.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how organizational mourning unfolds for former members after organizational death and what effects mourning has on those members. It also examines why different expressions of mourning emerge among individuals mourning the same organization and how those expressions relate to subsequent career paths.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / phases: Experiencing the death event, remembering the organization, assessing loss, salvaging through evaluation and restoration, and creating continuity and detaching.
- Pathways: Recreators mourn by salvaging relationships and recreating Lehman-like teams in similar finance work; Repurposers mourn by salvaging entrepreneurial culture and skills for new entrepreneurial ventures.
- Triggering conditions: The unexpected bankruptcy and organizational death of Lehman Brothers created shock, sadness, uncertainty, and perceived loss among former members.
- Emergent outcomes: Former bankers created continuing bonds with Lehman in different ways, shaping whether they stayed in similar jobs with former colleagues or moved into entrepreneurial careers.
- Boundary conditions: The authors present an inductive model from former Lehman Brothers bankers and do not claim statistical generalizability to all organizational deaths.

The process begins with the shock and sadness of Lehman's sudden death, then moves through remembering what Lehman was, assessing what was lost, and salvaging what could still be kept alive. Recreators' familial memories made the loss feel like losing a dear person, so they restored continuity through former colleagues and similar banking work. Repurposers' learning-oriented memories made the loss feel like losing a valued job, so they restored continuity through entrepreneurial skills and ventures. Organizational mourning therefore involves both holding on and letting go, with different forms of continuity shaping later detachment and career trajectories.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a theoretical model of organizational mourning as a post-death process linking organizational attachment, loss, salvaging, continuity, detachment, and career action. It extends research on organizational death and post-death organizing by explaining what happens between organizational closure and later responses, rather than focusing only on death announcements or legacy organizations. It also contributes to personal mourning research by showing how the quality of remembering a lost entity can shape what continuing bonds people seek to preserve.

**Practical Implication**
Dying organizations and those supporting displaced employees should attend to what former members believe they have lost and what they may want to carry forward. Employees who mainly miss colleagues may benefit from alumni clubs, team-based hiring, or other ways to preserve relationships, whereas those who miss the work or skills may benefit from support in recombining those skills into new career paths. The paper also warns that spreading former members into new organizations can transfer both valuable skills and potentially problematic practices.

**Limitations**
The study is an inductive single-case analysis of former Lehman Brothers bankers, so the authors claim analytic rather than statistical generalizability. The authors captured mourning at one point in time several years after Lehman's organizational death; while they defend retrospective inquiry as legitimate for mourning, they note mourning may look different closer to or further from the death event and call for other-timepoint or longitudinal designs. The sample also includes informants who landed in new jobs or ventures, so it may not capture more dysfunctional mourning trajectories among those who could not move on.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the model across multiple cases of organizational death and examine whether similar mourning processes occur after mergers, acquisitions, or broad organizational change. Scholars could study whether different attachments to organizations predict different mourning paths and whether attributions for organizational death affect the quality of mourning. Longitudinal work could also examine mourning closer to the death event, later after the event, and among individuals whose mourning trajectories are less functional.
