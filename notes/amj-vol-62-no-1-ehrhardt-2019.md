# Relational Attachment at Work: A Complementary Fit Perspective on the Role of Relationships in Organizational Life

**Abstract**
Organizational scholars concur that supportive work relationships can attach employees to their workplace and improve the quality of organizational life, but they lack insights into how or why this occurs. Moreover, models of social support focus on what relationships provide without considering the fit between what people need and receive from their work relationships. Addressing these limitations, we developed and tested a theoretical model of relational attachment at work using a complementary fit perspective. After validating measures, we tested, extended, and replicated our model in three field studies. Results revealed that employees became more attached to their organization and experienced more beneficial outcomes when they had stronger psychological attachment to others at work. This relational attachment was not driven by general social support, but, rather, by “relational needs fit,” which reflects employees’ experience of having their needs met by their work relationships. However, receiving more personal support than what was wanted or needed intruded into employees’ privacy boundaries and created a “too close for comfort” reaction whereby they pulled back from their work relationships. This “too much of a good thing” finding indicates that, while positive relationships at work are effective, social support should be tailored to individuals’ needs.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why and how employees become attached to others at work, and how this relational attachment affects organizational attachment, desirable work attitudes, positive psychological states, turnover, absenteeism, and privacy-boundary reactions. It specifically examines whether relational needs fit, rather than general social support, explains when work relationships produce attachment and when too much personal support becomes intrusive.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Relational needs fit for instrumental support and personal support, modeled as the fit between employees' desired support and perceived received support.
- DV(s): Relational attachment, organizational commitment, turnover, absenteeism, work engagement, interpersonal citizenship behaviors, thriving at work, flourishing, life satisfaction, general happiness, and perceived intrusion.
- Mediators: Relational attachment mediates effects of relational needs fit on work and psychological outcomes; perceived intrusion mediates the effect of excess personal support on relational attachment.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The proposed mechanism is that employees become attached to others at work when their work relationships meet their instrumental and personal support needs. This relational attachment then generalizes into stronger organizational commitment and other positive work and well-being outcomes. The model also specifies a threshold process: when personal support exceeds what employees want or need, it can violate privacy boundaries, increase perceived intrusion, and reduce relational attachment.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Kahn's needs-based model of meaningful connections by integrating a person-environment complementary fit perspective, shifting attention from whether support is available to whether received support fits individual relational needs. It contributes to positive relationships at work by theorizing and testing relational attachment as a cumulative attachment to one's constellation of work relationships. It also brings the social support paradox into organizational research by showing that personal support can become "too close for comfort" when it exceeds employees' desired level.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat work relationships as a source of organizational attachment and well-being, but should not assume that more support is always better. Support needs to be tailored to individual preferences, especially when support concerns personal matters. The findings also imply that communication and relationship-building training should help employees and managers recognize needs, boundaries, and nonverbal cues of privacy violation.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the studies used self-report data, creating potential same-source bias, although they used multiple time points, behavioral outcomes in Study 1, and common-method checks to reduce this concern. They also note limits to generalizability because samples were more educated and more non-Hispanic White than the U.S. working-adult population, all respondents were employed in the United States, and women were overrepresented in Studies 2 and 3.

**Future Research**
Future research could extend the complementary fit model to career needs, developmental relationships, relationships outside the workplace, and outcomes such as career efficacy and satisfaction. The authors also call for work on privacy thresholds, including how relationship roles, power, cultural norms, social media, generation, diversity, and changing needs affect when closeness becomes intrusive. Longitudinal studies could examine how privacy thresholds and work relationships evolve over time.
