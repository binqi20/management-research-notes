# Public Governance, Corporate Governance, and Firm Innovation: An Examination of State-Owned Enterprises

**Abstract**
Innovation activities create substantial firm value, but they are difficult to manage owing to agency risk, which is commonly thought to result in shirking, and hence underinvestment in innovation. However, agency risk can also create inefficient allocation of resources among innovation activities, on which the literature has provided limited understanding. We examine an important outcome created by agency risk—that agents pursue quantity of innovation at the expense of novelty—and investigate how it is influenced by corporate and public governance. We theorize that improved corporate governance tools, including better alignment of agents’ private incentives and stronger monitoring, and high-quality public governance reduce such agency risk in state-owned enterprises (SOEs). Furthermore, higher-quality public governance enhances the functioning of corporate governance tools in further reducing such agency risk in innovation. We test our theory by examining SOEs in China that responded to the state’s pro-innovation policies relying disproportionately on quantifiable outcomes (e.g., patent counts) for assessing innovation performance. Our difference-in-differences estimates provide overall support for our hypotheses. These findings provide new insights on how agency risk affects innovation by distinguishing the consequences for quantity and novelty of innovation and for how conventional corporate governance tools shaping innovation depend on public governance.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how agency risk affects the allocation of effort across innovation activities when principals rely heavily on quantifiable innovation metrics. It examines whether corporate governance and public governance reduce SOE agents' tendency to pursue patent quantity at the expense of patent novelty.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Post-policy exposure to China's 2006 innovation procurement policy; proportion of board share; proportion of state share; provincial government quality; interactions among policy exposure, corporate governance, and public governance.
- DV(s): Number of patents; proportion of novel patents among all patents produced by the firm-year.
- Mediators: Agency risk is the theorized mechanism, but it is not directly measured as a mediator.
- Moderators: Corporate governance tools and public governance quality moderate the post-policy change in innovation quantity and novelty.

The mechanism is grounded in agency theory and the multitasking model: when principals evaluate innovation through patent counts, agents can satisfy measurable targets by producing more general patents while underinvesting in more novel patents whose value is harder to measure. Better incentive alignment and stronger monitoring reduce this moral hazard by making agents more attentive to firm value rather than only to quantifiable policy targets. Higher-quality public governance makes state principals more accountable and better able to collect information, which strengthens the effect of corporate governance on innovation novelty.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends agency research on innovation by shifting attention from underinvestment in innovation to inefficient deployment of innovation resources. Its core contribution is to distinguish innovation quantity from innovation novelty and show that agency risk can increase observable innovation while reducing the proportion of more novel innovation.

It also links corporate governance to public governance in state capitalism. The study argues that the accountability and quality of state principals shape whether conventional governance tools, such as incentive alignment and monitoring, reduce agency risk in SOEs.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that patent-count targets can increase visible innovation output while still weakening the novelty mix of firm innovation if governance is weak. For SOEs and policymakers, promoting innovation requires governance systems that reward novelty and value creation, not only measurable patent volume.

The paper also suggests that corporate governance reforms work better when public governance is stronger. State owners seeking innovation should therefore attend both to firm-level governance tools and to the quality of the public governance environment in which SOE principals operate.

**Limitations**
The authors use granted invention patents only, which is a conservative choice and may underestimate a broader post-policy increase in lower-quality patenting. They also acknowledge that they cannot entirely rule out the alternative explanation that the Chinese state intentionally prioritized patent quantity, although the interaction results are more consistent with the agency-based explanation.

The empirical context is Chinese SOEs responding to a specific state innovation procurement policy, so the findings may not generalize directly to privately owned firms or to other political and institutional settings. The paper also notes that it lacks a theory for why corporate governance appears to function differently in low-quality public governance environments.

**Future Research**
The authors identify privately owned enterprises as a fruitful setting for future work on the same moral hazard because incentive alignment and monitoring also address agency risk outside SOEs. Future research could examine how the governance of principals in private firms, including different ownership types, shapes innovation outcomes.

They also suggest connecting innovation research to work on financial control and multidivisional firms. Future studies could test whether financial control affects not only the overall amount of R&D or innovation but also the composition of innovation between incremental and novel outputs.
