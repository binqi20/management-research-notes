# The Coercive Isomorphism Ripple Effect: An Investigation of Nonprofit Interlocks on Corporate Boards

**Abstract**
Institutional theorists have long recognized that board interlocks serve as conduits of information, which can facilitate mimetic isomorphism under conditions of uncertainty. In contrast, the potential for coercive isomorphism—which is a response to direct coercion rather than uncertainty—to spread through board interlocks remains unknown. Drawing on the forced compliance literature from social psychology, we propose that coercive isomorphism exhibits a ripple effect, whereby conforming to coercive pressures leads directors to internalize their acquiescence and apply it to the other boards on which they sit. We test this relationship in the context of nonprofit interlocks on the boards of 209 publicly traded U.S. firms between 2010 and 2014, predicting that coercive pressure to minimize overhead in the nonprofit sphere—a phenomenon known as the nonprofit starvation cycle—spreads to corporate boards through nonprofit interlocks, leading the corporate boards to reduce firm investment in intangible assets. We find that this relationship manifests across our sample, but is strongest via interlocks held by powerful directors and in industries characterized by high investment in intangible assets. Finally, we also find that the coercive isomorphism ripple effect ultimately decreases firm value.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether coercive isomorphism can spread indirectly through board interlocks rather than only through direct pressure inside the organizational field where the pressure originates. It examines whether nonprofit board interlocks transmit overhead aversion to corporate boards and thereby reduce corporate investment in intangible assets.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Nonprofit interlocks on corporate boards, separated in some models into high-power and low-power nonprofit interlocks.
- DV(s): Firm intangible asset investment, with Tobin's Q examined post hoc as a firm-value outcome.
- Mediators: The proposed psychological process is directors' internalization of coerced overhead aversion through the forced compliance effect; the post-hoc firm-value analysis tests intangible asset investment as a partial mediator.
- Moderators: Director power on the focal corporate board and industry intangible asset investment as a measure of contrasting mimetic pressure.

The mechanism is that directors who acquiesce to nonprofit-sector coercive pressures to minimize overhead internalize overhead aversion and carry that attitude to corporate boards on which they also serve. When those directors influence corporate board decision making, especially if they are powerful, the firm reduces investment in intangible assets such as R&D and advertising. The ripple effect is stronger when industry mimetic pressures favor high intangible asset investment because the imported overhead aversion runs against the receiving field's prevailing norm.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends institutional theory by arguing that coercive isomorphism can ripple across organizational fields through interlocking directors, not only through direct dependence or pressure. It contributes to board interlock research by treating directors as psychologically affected actors rather than impersonal information conduits. It also identifies director power and conflicting mimetic pressure as contingencies that shape when the coercive isomorphism ripple effect is most consequential.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that CEOs and boards should evaluate nonprofit interlocks carefully because such ties may bring legitimacy benefits while also importing overhead aversion. For large public firms, the risk is that directors' nonprofit-board experience may unintentionally discourage investment in intangible assets that support future growth and firm value.

**Limitations**
The authors state that their measured variables are distal from the proposed psychological mechanisms, so the evidence depends on assumptions about directors' exposure to coercive isomorphism and internalization of overhead aversion. They also note that the study does not empirically distinguish attitude-congruent from attitude-discrepant forced compliance. The sample is limited to the largest U.S. firms, and the firm-value analysis using Tobin's Q is post hoc rather than a formal test of theory.

**Future Research**
The authors call for finer-grained measurement of the psychological assumptions linking nonprofit board exposure to corporate board behavior. They encourage research on attitude-congruent versus attitude-discrepant forced compliance, other types of cross-field linkages beyond board interlocks, distinctions among nonprofit organizations, other contexts such as healthcare administration or social enterprises, and a wider array of performance outcomes.
