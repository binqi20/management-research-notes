# When Do Ethnic Communities Affect Foreign Location Choice? Dual Entry Strategies of Korean Banks in China

**Abstract**
Research has shown that firms typically expand into foreign locations with sizeable coethnic communities. However, in many cases the ethnic community influences the investment choice of the same firm in one location but not another. We offer an institutional lens to explain this heterogeneity. Ethnic groups function like informal institutions that facilitate transactions between foreign firms and customers, suppliers, and information providers through interpersonal exchange. Relying on ethnic communities to mediate transactions in foreign markets is valuable but limited by the relatively small scale of these communities. In contrast, relying on formal institutions allows firms to expand more broadly into foreign markets because the impersonal exchange inherent in formal governance is more scalable. This is manifested in “dual entry strategies” wherein ethnic communities have a significantly stronger influence on location choice in places with unreliable (weak and unstable) formal institutions than in places with reliable formal institutions. We found support for these ideas using a unique dataset of South Korean banks’ investments in Chinese provinces during 1992–2013. To mitigate endogeneity concerns, we exploited a historical migration that created a quasirandom distribution of ethnic Koreans across provinces. Our work contributes to research on international expansion, ethnic communities, and institutional theory.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when ethnic communities affect foreign location choice by coethnic firms. It examines why the same foreign firm may rely on a coethnic community in one location but not in another, focusing on the reliability of formal institutions as the boundary condition.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Size of the Chaoxian ethnic Korean community in a Chinese province.
- DV(s): Whether a South Korean bank invests in a Chinese province in a given year.
- Mediators: The institutional role of ethnic communities is theorized as the mechanism, but not directly estimated as a mediator.
- Moderators: Strength of formal institutions measured by property rights laws; stability of formal institutions measured by macroeconomic uncertainty and lending uncertainty.

The mechanism is that coethnic communities act as informal institutions that reduce exchange hazards through shared language, norms, solidarity, and social enforcement. These community-based mechanisms help foreign banks transact with borrowers, workers, and information providers when formal institutions are weak or unstable. Because interpersonal exchange within an ethnic community is not scalable beyond the community's social sphere, the community's effect on location choice weakens when formal institutions are reliable enough to support broader impersonal exchange.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on ethnic communities and international expansion by explaining heterogeneity in firms' use of coethnic communities across locations. It reframes ethnic communities as informal institutions and identifies formal institutional reliability as a boundary condition for their influence on foreign location choice.

It also contributes to institutional theory by shifting attention from whether formal and informal institutions substitute or complement each other to the scalability of governance mechanisms. The argument is that formal institutions are "first best" for growth-oriented foreign entry because they can support more numerous and broader transactions than community-based informal governance.

**Practical Implication**
Foreign firms should not treat the presence of coethnic communities as uniformly valuable across all locations. The value of such communities is higher where property rights are weak or uncertainty makes formal governance less reliable.

For banks and other growth-oriented entrants, the findings imply that coethnic communities can help manage demand, labor, and information hazards, but they may also limit market scale if firms rely on them as the primary exchange infrastructure. Location strategy should therefore weigh both ethnic community size and the reliability of formal institutions.

**Limitations**
The authors could not examine performance consequences because nearly all Korean bank subsidiaries established in China since 1992 survived through 2016, making survival uninformative. Subsidiary-level financial and operational performance data were unavailable.

The study also does not separately observe the demand, supply, and knowledge benefits of ethnic community reliance. Instead, it relies on prior research for those mechanisms and tests institutional reliability as the boundary condition for the community-location choice relationship.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether ethnic communities matter differently for foreign investments motivated by goals other than growth, such as research and development. Scholars could also compare similar dual entry strategies in developed markets where institutional quality varies across regions within a country.

The authors call for more work from the ethnic community's perspective, including whether the entry of coethnic firms affects jobs, wages, and entrepreneurship for ethnic individuals. Future studies could also investigate whether formal versus informal institutional arrangements shape the complexity of acquisitions, alliances, and other cross-border exchanges.
