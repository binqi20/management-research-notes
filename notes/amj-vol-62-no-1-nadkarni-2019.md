# Only Timeline Will Tell: Temporal Framing of Competitive Announcements and Rivals’ Responses

**Abstract**
Research has focused predominantly on the influence of the language firms use in their announcements on the reactions of constituents such as shareholders and the media. We extend this research by examining a new form of linguistic construction––temporal framing—in the context of competitive interactions. Building on intertemporal choice theory, we theorized and tested how a focal firm’s temporal framing of its competitive action announcements affects rivals’ response speed. We examined three dimensions of temporal framing: temporal vagueness (lack of clarity and completeness regarding timelines), temporal distance (length of the action timeline: proximal versus distal), and frequency (repetition of vagueness and distance cues). Based on analysis of 2,130 competitive action press releases of 28 duopoly firms from 14 industries, we found that the temporal framing dimensions, both individually and interactively, were related to the response speed of rivals. Specifically, temporally vague and distally framed timelines in action announcements delayed rivals’ response speed, and increased frequency of vagueness and distance cues strengthened these effects. This study explicates the linguistic underpinnings of competitive interactions and highlights the importance of temporal framing in the competitive context.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether variation in focal firms' temporal framing of competitive action announcements influences rivals' response speed. It examines whether temporal vagueness, temporal distance, and the frequency of these cues shape how quickly rivals retaliate.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Temporal vagueness, temporal distance, temporal vagueness frequency, and temporal distance frequency in focal firms' competitive action press releases.
- DV(s): Rivals' response speed, modeled as the hazard of a rival response after a focal firm's announcement.
- Mediators: Rivals' perceived uncertainty, perceived urgency, and temporal discounting are theorized mechanisms, but they are not directly estimated as mediators.
- Moderators: Temporal distance moderates the effect of temporal vagueness; temporal vagueness frequency and temporal distance frequency amplify the respective framing effects.

The mechanism is that rivals rely on focal firms' press-release timelines because they lack access to the focal firms' actual action timelines. Vague timelines increase uncertainty about when and how the action will unfold, encouraging rivals to discount the action and delay response. Distal timelines reduce perceived imminence, and repeated vagueness or distance cues increase salience, further slowing rivals' response speed.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends competitive dynamics research by adding linguistic temporal framing to action-based explanations of rivalry. It shows that rivals respond not only to observable action characteristics, announcement timing, and competitive context, but also to how firms linguistically present action timelines.

The paper also contributes to organizational communication research by showing a competitive downside of language that may be favorable to constituents. Clear and proximal framing can please shareholders or other constituents but also invite faster rival retaliation, creating a trade-off in how firms communicate competitive actions.

**Practical Implication**
Managers communicating competitive actions face a trade-off between mobilizing constituents and deterring rivals. Clear, proximal timelines may generate favorable reactions from shareholders, suppliers, and complementors, but they can also accelerate competitor retaliation.

For firms in intense rivalry, vague or distal temporal framing can delay rival responses, but it may weaken constituent confidence. Communication strategy should therefore consider both public-relations audiences and competitors that monitor the same announcements.

**Limitations**
The sample is restricted to duopoly industries, where rivalry is unusually intense and the top two firms have explicit competitive interdependence. The authors caution that the framing effects may not generalize to less concentrated industries with more varied rivals and competitive positions.

The study focuses only on temporal framing of competitive action announcements and on competitors' responses. It does not test other forms of framing, such as positive versus negative framing or attribution framing, and it does not directly examine reactions from other audiences such as shareholders.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine temporal framing in less concentrated industries and test whether the effects differ when firms face many rivals with heterogeneous stakes. Studies could also investigate other framing forms, including valence framing and attribution framing, to clarify how language more broadly shapes interfirm rivalry.

The authors also point to research on other audiences, especially shareholders. Future studies could examine how firms balance constituent reactions against competitive deterrence when deciding whether to frame action timelines clearly, vaguely, proximally, or distally.
