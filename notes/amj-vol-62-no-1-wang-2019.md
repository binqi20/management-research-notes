# Human Capital Acquisition and Organizational Innovation: A Temporal Perspective

**Abstract**
Newcomers contribute to organizational innovation by bringing in new knowledge and ideas, on the one hand, and by collaborating and exchanging with incumbents, on the other. We propose that an organization’s ability to use these contributions is influenced by hiring rate, hiring rate change, and hiring rate dispersion, which affect both the flow of new ideas into the organization and the level of collaboration between newcomers and incumbents. Using four years of data from a large, multi-industry sample, we find that hiring rate and hiring rate dispersion increase organizational innovation. We also find that increases in hiring rates from year to year are positively related to innovation for organizations with more collaborative work practices, while the relationship between hiring rate dispersion and innovation is less positive when organizations have more collaborative work practices. This study highlights how temporal patterns of hiring influence human capital acquisition and development.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how temporal patterns of managerial and professional hiring influence organizational innovation. It specifically examines whether hiring rate, hiring rate change, and hiring rate dispersion affect product and process innovation, and whether collaborative work practices condition those relationships.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Managerial and professional hiring rate, hiring rate change, and hiring rate dispersion.
- DV(s): Organizational innovation, measured as an ordinal index of improved and new products, services, and processes.
- Mediators: Knowledge acquisition, knowledge combination, and knowledge exchange are theorized mechanisms, but they are not directly measured as mediators.
- Moderators: Collaborative work practices moderate the effects of hiring rate, hiring rate change, and hiring rate dispersion.

The mechanism is that managerial and professional newcomers bring novel knowledge, skills, abilities, and perspectives that can support product and process innovation. Higher hiring rates and more time-dispersed hiring increase the flow of new ideas while also shaping how easily newcomers and incumbents collaborate. Collaborative work practices support communication, participation, information sharing, and trust, which can help organizations integrate newcomers when hiring increases, but can also substitute partly for the integration benefits of evenly dispersed hiring.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances strategic human capital and learning-by-hiring research by shifting attention from who organizations hire to when they hire. It separates hiring rate, hiring rate change, and hiring rate dispersion to show that temporal hiring patterns have distinct implications for organizational innovation.

It also extends human capital research by treating hiring as more than replacement for employee turnover. The study argues that hiring has strategic value because continuous or increasing inflows of managerial and professional newcomers shape both new knowledge acquisition and the development of collaborative human capital.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that organizations seeking innovation should manage hiring as a temporal process, not only as a headcount or talent-quality decision. Regular hiring of managers and professionals can maintain a continuous inflow of new knowledge and perspectives.

The study also suggests that collaborative work practices are especially useful when hiring rates increase or fluctuate, because they help integrate newcomers with incumbents. Organizations with fewer such practices may need more continuous and even hiring to reduce integration problems.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the WES data do not include specific measures of knowledge combination and exchange, so the proposed mechanisms are inferred rather than directly tested. The data also focus on managerial and professional newcomers and lack finer-grained measures of newcomer quality, exact within-year hiring timing, and whether new hires are returnees.

The innovation outcome is an additive self-reported index of improved and new products and processes, without information on ultimate economic benefits. The authors also note that industry contexts may differ because some organizations hire specifically for innovation tasks while service and retail businesses may hire mainly to meet operational demand.

**Future Research**
Future research could directly measure how different kinds of knowledge, skills, abilities, and other characteristics diffuse after hiring and how long that diffusion takes. Researchers could also examine finer-grained hiring timing, returnees, newcomer quality, and other strategic or cultural conditions that shape whether new hires contribute to innovation.

The authors also call for research using alternative innovation measures, such as sales and profits from new products and services, to test the economic consequences of hiring patterns. Additional studies could examine whether temporal hiring patterns influence broader outcomes beyond innovation, including productivity and profitability, across industries with different hiring motives.
