# Dominant Coalitions Directing Acquisitions: Different Decision Makers, Different Decisions

**Abstract**
Coalitions are important in organizational decision making, but the question of how coalitions are built and make decisions in response to firm performance is still not sufficiently explored. In this study, we develop and test theory on how potential coalitions are built through shared experience and recruitment of allies. When organizations respond to performance relative to aspiration levels, either as problemistic search following low performance or opportunity exploration following high performance, members form coalitions to influence decisions. We develop theory of coalition formation that builds on upper echelons theory and the theory of dominant coalitions to predict how past experience of decision makers leads to preferred actions by each member and subsequent coalition formation. We use this theory to make new measures of potential coalitions and apply it to acquisitions made by firms in China. We find evidence that the experience of members of the key decision-making group—the board of directors—affects the potential coalition building, and hence the type of acquisition target, as predicted.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how potential dominant coalitions are built within boards and how those coalitions shape acquisition decisions when firm performance differs from aspiration levels. It focuses on whether board members' state versus market experience changes the type of acquisition target, and whether similar coalition logic appears in state-bank loan choices.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Board potential state coalition, measured with stateness factor, stateness ASW, and stateness proportion; firm performance relative to historical and social aspiration levels.
- DV(s): Market orientation of acquisition target type, nature of market-oriented acquisition, and whether a loan comes from a state-owned or policy bank.
- Mediators: Coalition building is theorized as the process linking decision-maker experience to strategic choices, but it is not directly estimated as a mediator.
- Moderators: Performance above or below historical and social aspiration levels conditions when coalition preferences shape decisions.

The mechanism is that board members use prior state or market experience to evaluate which strategic actions feel familiar, feasible, and proximate. Performance below or above aspiration levels triggers a decision occasion, after which experience-based subgroups try to assemble dominant coalitions by relying on member number, commitment, status, and recruitable allies. In the acquisition tests, stronger state coalitions reduce responsiveness toward market-oriented acquisitions, whereas weaker state coalitions leave more scope for market-oriented acquisition choices. The separate state-bank loan analysis is treated more cautiously: its results run contrary to Hypothesis 2 and suggest a boundary condition because loan-source decisions mix firm and bank decision making.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to dominant coalition theory by turning coalition building into measurable potential coalitions based on board members' experience, commitment, status, and recruitable allies. It also extends upper echelons theory by replacing simple average-composition logic with a coalition-based account of how decision-making groups reach strategic choices.

It contributes to performance feedback theory by making upper-echelons effects contingent on performance relative to aspiration levels. The resulting account links the trigger for organizational change with the content of the decision selected by the board.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that boards' experience composition can shape consequential acquisition choices, not merely the propensity to change. For Chinese listed firms, state-experienced boards may be more likely to favor internal or state-bridged actions, while market-experienced coalitions support more market-oriented acquisitions when performance feedback creates a problem or opportunity.

The paper also implies that governance reform does not erase legacy decision logics simply by formalizing board structures. Directors' prior state and market experience remain relevant to how boards interpret performance feedback and choose strategic responses.

**Limitations**
The study predicts potential rather than realized coalitions, so the authors cannot observe the actual bargaining, persuasion, agenda control, or alliance recruitment inside each board decision. They also note that features of each specific choice could make the realized coalition differ from the potential coalition inferred from board composition.

The state-bank loan test is a boundary condition because loan-source choices mix firm and bank decision making: banks assess loan risk using profitability relative to other firms, and high-stateness firms do not respond to performance by changing loan source in the same way the acquisition theory predicts.

**Future Research**
Future research could apply the coalition-building approach to other kinds of decision-maker experience, decision-making groups, organizational behaviors, and high-stakes decisions. The authors specifically suggest studying financial, technological, and market affiliations in top management teams and boards.

Further work could also examine the actual processes of coalition influence, including agenda control, information release, order of speech, and other procedural interventions. Such studies would extend the paper's potential-coalition measures toward more direct evidence on how coalitions are built and retained across decisions.
