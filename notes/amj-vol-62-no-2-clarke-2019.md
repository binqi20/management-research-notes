# Actions Speak Louder than Words: How Figurative Language and Gesturing in Entrepreneurial Pitches Influences Investment Judgments

**Abstract**
A key challenge for entrepreneurs is to convince investors of their business ideas via a pitch. While scholars have started to explore how entrepreneurs convey their passion and preparedness in a pitch, they have overlooked the possible variation that exists in entrepreneurs’ verbal and nonverbal expressions. We build on research in cognitive science and entrepreneurship to examine the nature and influence of specific forms of speech and gesturing used by entrepreneurs when pitching. In an initial qualitative field study we identify distinct pitching strategies entrepreneurs use, involving different combinations of verbal tactics (i.e., using literal and figurative language to frame a venture) and gesture (i.e., using different types of hand gestures to emphasize parts of their pitch and convey product and venture ideas). In a subsequent experimental study, we examine the impact of these strategies on investors’ propensity to invest. We find that, although variation in the type of language used by an entrepreneur has limited effects, using gestures to depict and symbolize business ideas has strong positive effects. Our findings indicate that the skilled use of gestures by entrepreneurs helps potential investors imagine aspects of a new venture for themselves, thereby enhancing perception of its investment potential.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how entrepreneurs combine verbal tactics and expressive nonverbal behaviors when pitching to investors, and how those channels separately and jointly influence potential investors' judgments. It specifically examines whether figurative language, gesturing, and mental imagery shape investors' propensity to invest.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / pitching strategies: Literal, rhetorical, demonstrative, and integrative pitching approaches, distinguished by high or low use of figurative language and high or low use of gesturing.
- Study 1 process: Inductive coding of naturalistic technology-entrepreneur pitches identifies variation in literal versus figurative language and beat, iconic, deictic, and metaphoric gestures.
- Study 2 experimental factors: High versus low figurative language and high versus low gesturing in video pitches.
- Outcome: Propensity to invest, measured through investment intentions and investment likelihood.
- Mediators: Mental imagery is the focal mediator; passion and preparedness are comparative mechanisms.
- Boundary condition: The indirect effect of gesture through mental imagery is stronger when gestures are coupled with figurative language.

The central process is that ideational gestures do more than signal passion: they help viewers mentally simulate the product, venture, and market. Figurative language alone has limited direct impact, but gestures that depict and symbolize the business idea increase propensity to invest by evoking mental imagery. The integrative value of figurative language appears mainly in strengthening the gesture-to-imagery pathway rather than directly persuading investors.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends entrepreneurship research on pitching by shifting attention from generic passion displays to the communicative functions of specific verbal and nonverbal behaviors. It shows that gestures can carry meaning, not only affect, and that beat and ideational gestures should not be collapsed into a simple frequency-based indicator of passion.

It also qualifies rhetoric-and-framing arguments in entrepreneurship. Figurative language does not directly persuade investors in this setting, but it matters when aligned with gestures that enable mental imagery, thereby offering a more integrated account of entrepreneurial communication.

**Practical Implication**
For entrepreneurs, the findings imply that effective pitching depends not only on what is said but on how product and venture ideas are embodied through gestures. Entrepreneurs should use gestures to depict concrete product features and abstract venture trajectories, rather than relying only on metaphors, anecdotes, or enthusiastic delivery.

For investors, the study suggests that nonverbal cues can shape investment judgments through cognitive imagery as well as emotional impressions. The authors argue that distinguishing meaningful gestural depiction from generalized energetic body language can support more evidence-based recommendations for entrepreneurs and investors in practice.

**Limitations**
The experimental materials used a male actor, so the authors note that future work should test whether the effects hold for female entrepreneurs. The experiment jointly manipulated beat and ideational gestures, which means the separate effects of these gesture types remain unresolved.

The experimental venture involved a basic technological product that experienced and novice investors could understand, so the findings may differ for more technically or conceptually complex ventures. The mediators and dependent variable were also measured contemporaneously, which may inflate observed relationships.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine other nonverbal modalities in entrepreneurial communication, including intonation, pitch, prosody, posture, facial expressions, gaze, and the development of entrepreneur-investor relationships over time. It should also compare interpersonal pitching with mediated forms of entrepreneurial communication such as business plans and IPO prospectuses.

The authors also call for research separating beat gestures from ideational gestures and testing whether the same mechanisms operate across entrepreneur gender and venture complexity. Additional studies could measure mediators and investment judgments at different times to strengthen causal evidence about the communication process.
