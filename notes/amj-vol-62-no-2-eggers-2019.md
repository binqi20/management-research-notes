# Experience and Behavior: How Negative Feedback in New Versus Experienced Domains Affects Firm Action and Subsequent Performance

**Abstract**
How organizations respond to failure (negative feedback) has important implications for subsequent organizational performance. This study focuses on theoretical and empirical distinctions between the different potential organizational reactions to failure (in this case around new product development) and how those feedback-driven choices aggregate to affect firm performance. To understand when and why negative feedback is organizationally beneficial, we focus on how possession of domain-specific experience affects the way in which firms interpret and respond to negative feedback, offering both organizationand mechanism-level theories. In particular, we distinguish between conditions that should lead to different behavioral responses to negative feedback: retreat, local search, or distant search. We find support for our theory using data on all U.S. mutual fund companies from 1962 to 2002, and testing both aggregated and mechanism-level theories. Our results show that negative feedback on new funds launched in new domains is particularly damaging to subsequent organizational performance, as firms retreat from the unknown in favor of already exploited domains. In contrast, negative feedback in experienced domains can produce positive outcomes by encouraging action to search for both local solutions to correct the problem and distant solutions to expand the firm's opportunities.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and why negative feedback improves versus harms subsequent organizational performance. It specifically examines how domain-specific experience shapes firms' interpretation of poor performance on new products and their subsequent choices among retreat, local search, and distant search.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Negative versus positive feedback on newly launched funds; whether the fund domain is new or experienced for the firm.
- DV(s): Firm-level net new cash inflow; subsequent new fund creation in same, new, or experienced categories; third-year new fund cash inflow; existing fund cash inflow.
- Mediators: Behavioral responses to feedback, especially retreat from new domains, local search to repair experienced-domain failures, and distant search into new domains.
- Moderators: Domain-specific experience is the central contingency shaping how negative feedback is interpreted and acted upon.

The mechanism is grounded in the behavioral theory of the firm. Negative feedback in a new domain is interpreted as evidence that the unfamiliar domain itself is risky, leading firms to retreat toward familiar domains and thereby incur opportunity costs and cannibalization. Negative feedback in an experienced domain is interpreted as a fixable product-level problem, leading firms to allocate resources locally and, when needed, search more distantly for new opportunities.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends behavioral theory of the firm research by showing that negative feedback does not have uniform performance implications. Domain-specific experience determines whether poor performance triggers damaging retreat from exploration or productive problem-driven search.

It also moves beyond a simple change-versus-persistence account of performance feedback. By distinguishing retreat, local search, and distant search, the study provides a mechanism-level explanation of how firms choose among different behavioral responses to failure and how those responses aggregate into firm performance.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that managers should not treat failure in unfamiliar domains as straightforward evidence that exploration is unattractive. Premature retreat from new domains can leave growth opportunities unpursued and redirect resources into familiar domains where new products cannibalize existing ones.

For failures in experienced domains, the study suggests that firms can benefit from allocating resources to correct the problem because relevant experience makes the feedback more interpretable and actionable. The practical lesson is to diagnose whether poor performance reflects a fixable product-level issue or uncertainty in a genuinely new domain before reallocating resources.

**Limitations**
The authors note that they directly test only a subset of the full theoretical model, leaving some mechanisms supported indirectly rather than through direct empirical tests. The mutual fund industry is also a relatively stable context in which retreat to familiar domains is viable, so the performance implications may differ in high-technology industries where continued exploration is necessary for survival.

The study treats experience as binary, distinguishing whether the firm has any prior experience in a domain. The authors acknowledge that depth of experience may further shape how managers interpret feedback, but incorporating continuous experience would add conceptual and empirical complexity.

**Future Research**
Future research could test the behavioral mechanisms in other industries, especially high-technology contexts where firms cannot easily retreat from new domains. Such work would clarify whether negative feedback in new domains is always damaging or only damaging when retreat is strategically feasible.

Future studies could also examine how the depth of prior experience changes feedback interpretation. More direct evidence on when local search is judged to have failed would help explain the timing of shifts from local problem solving to distant search.
