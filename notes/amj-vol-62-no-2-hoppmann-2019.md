# Boards as a Source of Inertia: Examining the Internal Challenges and Dynamics of Boards of Directors in Times of Environmental Discontinuities

**Abstract**
The literature on corporate governance indicates that, by engaging in strategy-related activities, boards of directors can help firms adapt to environmental discontinuities. So far, however, studies shed limited light on board-internal challenges and dynamics in such difficult times, thus providing few insights into the conditions under which boards may contribute to organizational inertia. We use a comparative case study of 10 major Swiss electric utility companies during the energy transition to show in detail how environmental and strategic change impair boards' ability to judge strategic issues, and how boards use self-evaluation and self-reconfiguration to renew this ability. Moreover, we offer original insights into the board-internal antecedents of board renewal, and show that environmental discontinuities pose a dilemma for boards, since self-evaluation and self-reconfiguration are critical for preventing organizational inertia yet may run counter to board members' self-interest. By showing that board members, like managers, experience conflicts of interest that can harm firm performance, our study contributes to agency theory and an emerging micro-perspective on boards. Moreover, by highlighting boards as a source of organizational inertia, our study challenges existing findings in the field of strategic management and makes several more specific contributions to important debates in the corporate governance literature.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how boards of directors contribute to organizational inertia during environmental discontinuities. More specifically, it examines how environmental and strategic change affect boards' ability to govern strategic change, how boards renew that ability, and what board-internal factors explain variation in board adjustment.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / challenges: Environmental discontinuity, strategic change, board ability to judge strategic issues, strategy evaluation, strategy reconfiguration, self-evaluation, self-reconfiguration, board members' self-interest, and board chair actions and experience.
- Process / phases: Environmental discontinuities and strategic change reduce boards' ability to judge strategic issues; boards must identify this gap through self-evaluation and renew the ability through self-reconfiguration.
- Board activities: Strategy evaluation monitors the environment and reviews strategy; strategy reconfiguration approves, stimulates, or limits strategic change initiatives.
- Renewal activities: Self-reconfiguration occurs through adaptation of composition, adaptation of size, learning and education, and adjustment of decision-making.
- Boundary conditions: The empirical setting is 10 large Swiss electric utilities exposed to the energy transition between 2009 and 2015.

The process begins when environmental discontinuities make existing board knowledge, routines, and ties less useful for judging strategic issues. If a board recognizes this loss of ability through self-evaluation, it can renew itself by changing composition, reducing size, improving learning, or changing decision-making routines. If board members' self-interest blocks self-evaluation and self-reconfiguration, the board may limit or rubber-stamp strategic change in ways that prolong organizational inertia. Experienced board chairs can partly overcome this barrier by initiating board renewal in a top-down manner.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to agency theory by showing that board members themselves may face conflicts of interest that harm firm performance, rather than only acting as monitors of managerial self-interest. It extends strategic management work on organizational inertia by identifying boards as a potential source of inertia when they fail to renew their ability to judge strategic issues. It also contributes to corporate governance research by developing a dynamic view of board capital, board evolution, and the role of the board chair in sustaining board power vis-a-vis management.

**Practical Implication**
Firms facing environmental discontinuities should not assume that existing board expertise and routines remain adequate as strategic conditions change. Boards need explicit self-evaluation and self-reconfiguration practices, including competency reviews, education, size adjustment, and decision-making reforms. The findings also imply that board chairs are critical governance actors because they can initiate renewal when ordinary board members have incentives to avoid exposing weaknesses or losing valued mandates.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on the Swiss electricity industry, a highly regulated setting with country-specific board responsibilities, so the authors call for caution in generalizing to other sectors or national governance systems. The study relies on retrospective interviews rather than direct real-time observation of boardroom processes, although the authors triangulate interviews with multiple archival and informant sources. The paper also provides initial evidence on antecedents of self-evaluation and self-reconfiguration rather than a complete account of why some boards engage in these practices more than others.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine board mechanisms during environmental change in other national and industrial contexts. The authors call for ethnographic studies of boardroom processes over extended periods and for micro-level work on how board members' motivations and interests shape board dynamics and organizational outcomes. They also suggest studying how board chairs are selected or changed and how board self-evaluation and self-reconfiguration relate to firm history, board member backgrounds, ownership structures, and the relative power of owners, management, and the board.
