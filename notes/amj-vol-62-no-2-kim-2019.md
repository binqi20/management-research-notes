# No Time Like the Present: How a Present Time Perspective Can Foster Sustainable Development

**Abstract**
Sustainable development research has assumed that organizations must make intertemporal trade-offs between benefits now versus benefits later. However, under extreme resource constraints, organizations are unable to sacrifice resources now for benefits later without risking their survival. In these conditions, prior theory has suggested that organizations would be present focused, making sustainable development elusive. Through an ethnographic study, we investigated how tea producer organizations in eight communities in East Africa confronting severe resource constraints acted for sustainable development. We discovered that a “present” time perspective is richer than has been described previously. Prior time research has described the present as a “moment” in time, which allows managers to juxtapose the present against the future to make the intertemporal trade-offs for sustainable development. However, our tea producers did not see the future as a trade-off with the present. We discovered that they see duration in the present—what we call a “long present.” Because the present is long, they see connections among processes such as resource flows, which inspired incremental actions that continuously ease extreme resource shortages. We therefore offer an alternative to the trade-off thinking that currently dominates sustainable development discourse.

**Research Question**
How are organizations under severe resource constraints able to act for sustainable development? The paper asks this inductively in order to contribute to theories of time and sustainable development.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key tension: Fairtrade's future-focused interventions assumed that producers should sacrifice some present benefits to build a better future, while producers faced acute resource constraints that made such sacrifice risky.
- Time perspectives: Fairtrade treated the present as a short, discrete moment; some tea producer organizations enacted a long-present perspective in which the present had duration and contained connected processes.
- Process visibility: The long-present perspective made seasonal production, school fees, bonus payments, loans, rainfall, bees, tea, and other resource rhythms visible as connected flows rather than isolated stocks.
- Producer actions: Producers redirected Fairtrade resources into immediate and incremental initiatives such as beekeeping, concrete benches, savings schemes, and school-fee timing.
- Emergent outcome: These actions eased immediate resource shortages while cumulatively building resources over time, enabling sustainable development through temporal connections rather than intertemporal trade-offs.

The central process is not that resource-poor organizations become future-focused in the conventional sense. Rather, interaction with Fairtrade supplied limited slack, governance forums, and pressure to comply with standards, while producers' long-present perspective helped them see how small present actions could connect resource flows across seasons and cycles. Beekeeping, concrete benches, and related initiatives looked short-term from Fairtrade's short-present lens, but they created immediate resource relief that accumulated into longer-term development.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances time-perspective theory by separating a present focus from short-termism and theorizing short-present and long-present perspectives. It contributes to sustainable development research by showing that intertemporal trade-offs are not the only pathway: organizations under severe resource constraints may build resources through temporal connections. It also brings process thinking into a concrete empirical account of how actors perceive the present as an extended duration rather than as a discrete point.

**Practical Implication**
Development organizations should not assume that local producers' present focus reflects ignorance of the future or simple short-termism. Interventions are more likely to help when they understand local social and biophysical rhythms, provide enough initial resources for incremental improvement, and work with local organizations' own temporal knowledge. Fairtrade's premium, committee structures, meetings, and training mattered, but they were most useful when producers could redirect them toward locally connected resource flows.

**Limitations**
The study is based on an extreme case: resource-poor tea producer organizations deeply embedded in rural East African biophysical systems. The authors argue that short- and long-present perspectives may travel beyond this context, but they state that broader generalization to developed and developing economies requires further empirical investigation. The analysis also foregrounds temporality, while power, control, standardization, certification, and auditing are acknowledged as relevant mechanisms outside the study's main scope.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine short- and long-present perspectives in different cultural and geographical spaces, especially developed-country market economies where everyday life may be less directly connected to biophysical systems. Researchers could also investigate how power and control interact with time perspectives in certification and development interventions. More broadly, the paper calls for research on how organizations transition toward sustainable development through temporal connections rather than trade-off thinking.
