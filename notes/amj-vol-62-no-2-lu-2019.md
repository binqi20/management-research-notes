# Pitching Novel Ideas to the Boss: The Interactive Effects of Employees’ Idea Enactment and Influence Tactics on Creativity Assessment and Implementation

**Abstract**
Employees’ creative ideas often do not receive positive assessments from managers and, therefore, lose the opportunity to be implemented. We draw on Dutton and Ashford’s (1993) issue-selling framework to test a dual-approach model predicting that employees’ use of both high levels of idea enactment behaviors (characterized by use of demos, prototypes, or other physical objects when presenting ideas) and high levels of upward influence tactics will have an interactive effect on the extent to which their creative ideas are positively assessed by their supervisors and, in turn, implemented. We found support for this interactive effect of idea enactment and influence tactics in two studies: a field study of 192 employees and 54 supervisors in a video game and animation company and an experimental study with 264 participants. In the experimental study, we also demonstrated that the benefits of using this dual approach are more likely to accrue when selling a more novel idea rather than a less novel, more mundane one. Both studies highlight the mediating role of supervisors’ creativity assessment in implementing employees’ creative ideas.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how employees can increase supervisors' positive assessment and implementation of their creative ideas. It specifically examines whether idea enactment and upward influence tactics interact to improve creativity assessment, and whether this dual approach works especially well for more novel ideas.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employees' idea enactment and upward influence tactics; in Study 2, experimentally manipulated idea enactment, influence tactics, and idea novelty.
- DV(s): Supervisors' creativity assessment and implementation in Study 1; participants' creativity assessment, willingness to implement, and intended investment amount in Study 2.
- Mediators: Supervisors' or participants' creativity assessment mediates the link from idea enactment and influence tactics to implementation.
- Moderators: Upward influence tactics moderate the effect of idea enactment; idea novelty further conditions the enactment-by-influence interaction in Study 2.

The mechanism is that idea enactment makes abstract novel ideas more tangible through demos, prototypes, drawings, or similar objects, while upward influence tactics persuade supervisors to attend to and support the idea. Together, these tactics help supervisors recognize novelty, usefulness, and feasibility, which improves creativity assessment. That assessment then increases the likelihood that the idea receives resources, approval, or implementation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Dutton and Ashford's issue-selling framework into the creativity-assessment domain by showing that issue packaging and selling tactics interact when employees pitch creative ideas upward. It shifts creativity research from idea generation alone toward the social process through which supervisors recognize and implement employee creativity. It also shows that the dual approach is contingent on idea novelty: enactment and influence help novel ideas but may expose the mundanity of less novel ones.

**Practical Implication**
Employees pitching novel ideas should combine tangible enactment, such as prototypes or visual demonstrations, with strong upward influence tactics rather than relying on either approach alone. Supervisors and organizations should create environments that make idea enactment possible and should be receptive to employees' proactive selling efforts when evaluating creative ideas.

**Limitations**
The field study could not support strong causal inference and did not verify actual implementation of a particular project. The laboratory study measured creativity assessment and implementation intention from the same respondents at the same time, creating possible common source bias. The authors also note that they did not directly measure the cognitive and behavioral microprocesses through which enactment and influence affect supervisors' assessments.

**Future Research**
Future research could trace ideas from proposal through actual project-level implementation, using separate sources or objective implementation measures. Scholars could study how idea enactment, influence tactics, and supervisor assessment co-evolve across iterative innovation processes. Further work could examine other packaging devices, other selling-process choices, coalition building with peers, and whether too-frequent use of enactment and influence creates decreasing returns.
