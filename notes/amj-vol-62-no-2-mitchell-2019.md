# Can You Handle the Pressure? The Effect of Performance Pressure on Stress Appraisals, Self-regulation, and Behavior

**Abstract**
Performance pressure focuses employee efforts toward enhanced performance. It is unclear, however, whether performance pressure serves as a productive or unproductive strategy for producing beneficial work behavior. Our research provides clarity on the dynamic nature of performance pressure. We theorize that reactions to performance pressure are influenced by daily fluctuations in how the pressure is appraised, and these fluctuations explain why performance pressure can be a double-edged sword, producing bright and dark side effects for organizations. We predict that, on a daily basis, performance pressure may be appraised as a threat, which promotes self-regulation depletion that explains dysfunctional behavior (i.e., incivility); daily performance pressure may also be appraised as a challenge, which elicits engagement that explains enhanced task proficiency and citizenship. Trait resilience is predicted to moderate these effects, promoting performance pressure to be appraised as a challenge rather than a threat, which then mitigates the depleting effects that produce dysfunctional behavior and enhances the engaging effects that produce functional behavior. Results from an experience sampling study support our predictions. Implications for theory and research are provided.

**Research Question**
How do daily appraisals of performance pressure explain why the same stressor can produce both dysfunctional behavior and functional behavior, and why are some employees better able to handle this pressure than others?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Daily performance pressure.
- DV(s): Incivility, task proficiency, and citizenship.
- Mediators: Threat appraisal and self-regulation depletion for the dark-side path; challenge appraisal and engagement for the bright-side path.
- Moderators: Trait resilience moderates the daily effects of performance pressure on threat and challenge appraisals, shaping downstream self-regulatory states and behavior.

The paper theorizes performance pressure as a dynamic work stressor rather than a uniformly harmful or beneficial demand. On days when employees appraise performance pressure as a threat, they focus on potential harm and negative consequences, experience self-regulation depletion, and become more likely to act uncivilly. On days when employees appraise performance pressure as a challenge, they focus on opportunities for growth and rewards, experience engagement, and become more likely to show task proficiency and citizenship. Trait resilience shifts this process by making challenge appraisals more likely and threat appraisals less likely.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to the performance-pressure literature by explaining its paradoxical effects through within-person fluctuations in threat and challenge appraisals. It extends organizational stress theory by showing that a single stressor can be appraised in flux rather than categorized as only a threat or only a challenge. It also contributes to self-regulation theory by showing that performance pressure can both deplete and energize self-regulatory resources, depending on daily appraisal and trait resilience.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should take care in how they communicate performance expectations, because the same pressure can be construed as threatening or challenging. The paper suggests emphasizing the benefits and opportunities attached to performance pressure rather than only the negative consequences of underperformance. It also notes possible value in assessing trait resilience for roles involving strong performance pressure, while cautioning that any such measures should be psychometrically sound and paired with other screening techniques.

**Limitations**
The study relies on self-reported daily survey data, creating possible common method variance concerns, although the authors separate predictor and criterion variables in time and test alternative explanations. Stress appraisals and self-regulatory states are measured at the same point in time, which limits causal certainty about the ordering of mediators. The study also does not examine specific work-environment or person-level triggers that make performance pressure more likely to be appraised as threat versus challenge.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine precipitating conditions, such as negative work events, positive work events, social support, or nonwork demands, that shift performance pressure toward threat or challenge appraisals. Scholars could also test whether challenge and threat appraisals occur simultaneously or ebb and flow within a day. Additional work could examine other moderators, such as emotional stability, perfectionism, social structures, or error-management culture, and apply the same within-person lens to other inconsistent organizational findings.
