# Too Busy to Be Fair? The Effect of Workload and Rewards on Managers’ Justice Rule Adherence

**Abstract**
Why do managers act unfairly even when they recognize the significant organizational benefits of treating employees fairly? Prior research has explained this puzzling phenomenon predominantly through an “actor-centric” perspective, proposing that managers’ just behavior is an outcome of their own individual differences. This paper shifts the locus of theorizing away from managers to their organizational context by arguing that managers’ adherence to principles of justice is often influenced by two key contextual features—workload and rewards. Drawing on the multiple-task pursuit framework, we propose that under high workloads, just treatment of employees often competes with other managerial tasks. We further argue that organizations implicitly and explicitly signal to managers that acting justly is not as important as their other technical tasks. Hence, managers who face high workloads (i.e., high amounts of work and time pressures) tend to prioritize other technical responsibilities at the expense of acting justly, unless explicitly rewarded otherwise. We find support for our theorizing across three studies (an experience sampling study, a field study, and an experiment). We discuss the implications of our theory and findings for future research on managerial fairness and offer novel practical implications for organizations attempting to ensure employee fair treatment.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why managers sometimes fail to treat employees fairly even when they recognize the value of fairness. It examines whether managerial workload reduces justice rule adherence by pushing managers to prioritize technical tasks, and whether organizational rewards for justice weaken that process.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Managerial workload, operationalized as daily workload, perceived three-month workload, and experimentally manipulated time pressure.
- DV(s): Managers' justice rule adherence toward employees; Studies 2 and 3 also examine technical task performance.
- Mediators: Prioritization of technical tasks.
- Moderators: Justice rewards, measured as organizational rewards for fair treatment and experimentally manipulated through balanced versus low justice reward systems.

The mechanism is that high workload creates competition among managerial tasks for limited time, attention, and effort. Because organizations often signal that technical responsibilities are more important and more rewarded than justice tasks, overloaded managers prioritize technical deliverables over distributive, procedural, informational, and interpersonal justice rule adherence. Justice rewards alter this prioritization calculus by making fair treatment more salient and valued, reducing the negative indirect effect of workload on justice behavior.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends organizational justice research by shifting from a primarily actor-centric explanation of managerial unfairness toward a context-centric account grounded in workload, rewards, and task prioritization. It conceptualizes justice rule adherence as an effortful managerial task that competes with other responsibilities rather than as an isolated expression of disposition or motive. It also contributes to multiple-task pursuit theory by showing that reward systems can shape prioritization between technical and relational tasks and that justice rewards need not reduce technical performance.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations seeking fairer managerial behavior should not rely only on selecting or training fair-minded managers. They should manage avoidable workload pressures and explicitly include justice rule adherence in formal and informal evaluation and reward systems. The paper also suggests scheduling practices, such as regular employee meetings or protected time for explanations and listening, to make justice-related work harder to crowd out under high workload.

**Limitations**
Study 1 separated workload from later outcomes during the day but measured prioritization and justice rule adherence at the same time, limiting causal inference. Study 2 used multisource data but remained cross-sectional and relied on manager nominations of employee raters. Study 3 established causality through a simulation, but its student sample and laboratory task limit generalizability to practicing managers.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine technical performance tradeoffs using stronger field measures, such as top-management ratings or objective performance indicators. Scholars could also test whether workload and justice rewards affect distributive, procedural, interpersonal, and informational justice differently. The authors further encourage research on justice climates, manager time-management skills, long-term orientation, and synergistic strategies that allow managers to meet technical and justice demands together.
