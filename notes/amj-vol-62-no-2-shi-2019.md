# Examination of CEO–CFO Social Interaction through Language Style Matching: Outcomes for the CFO and the Organization

**Abstract**
This study proposes that CEO–CFO “language style matching”—a form of unconscious verbal mimicry based on function words—can provide insights into social interaction processes between CEOs and CFOs. We argue and empirically verify that high CEO– CFO language style matching reflects CFOs’ strong attempts to ingratiate themselves with CEOs. Because ingratiation with superiors can lead to the superiors’ positive evaluations of subordinates, CFOs who exhibit higher language style matching with CEOs will receive higher compensation and are more likely to become board members of the associated firms. In addition, the proposed relationships will be stronger when CEOs are more powerful. Yet, in the presence of high CEO–CFO language style matching, CFOs are less likely to voice different viewpoints and challenge CEOs in strategic decision processes. As a result, firms tend to undertake more mergers and acquisitions, and such mergers and acquisitions will be paid with a low percentage of cash (vs. stock) and realize lower announcement returns. Using a sample of over 2,000 U.S. firms from the period 2002–2013, we find empirical support for these predictions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether CEO-CFO language style matching reveals otherwise hard-to-observe social interaction processes between top executives and whether those interactions affect both CFO personal outcomes and firm strategic outcomes. It specifically examines whether higher CEO-CFO LSM predicts CFO compensation, CFO board appointment, M&A intensity, M&A payment method, and investor reactions to M&A announcements.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO-CFO language style matching in Q&A sections of conference calls, measured from function-word use.
- DV(s): CFO total compensation, likelihood that the CFO becomes a board member, number and value of M&As, percentage of cash payment, M&A announcement returns, and ROA change.
- Mediators: Percentage of cash payment partially mediates the relationship between CEO-CFO LSM and M&A announcement returns.
- Moderators: CEO power strengthens the positive relationships between CEO-CFO LSM and CFO compensation and directorship.

The mechanism is that high CEO-CFO LSM reflects the CFO's attempt to affiliate with and ingratiate themselves with the CEO. That ingratiation can lead CEOs to evaluate CFOs more favorably, supporting higher compensation and greater likelihood of board appointment, especially when CEOs have more power over pay and nomination processes. The same social dynamic weakens CFO dissent and bottom-up monitoring in strategic decisions, allowing CEOs to undertake more M&As, use less cash in deals, and generate lower announcement returns.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to upper echelons research by replacing demographic proxies with language style matching as a behavioral indicator of social interaction between top executives. It also shifts attention from the whole TMT or CEO alone to the CEO-CFO dyad as a consequential subteam for strategic decisions.

The paper extends corporate governance and executive compensation research by showing that subordinate executives' interactions with the CEO can affect both their own career outcomes and firm-level governance quality. It also contributes to M&A research by theorizing the CFO's role as a potential source of constructive dissent whose absence can reduce acquisition quality.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that boards and other stakeholders can use unusually high language style matching between senior executives as a signal that their relationship may lack constructive challenge. Boards should be concerned when the CFO appears too linguistically and behaviorally aligned with the CEO, because this may reduce independent judgment in major strategic decisions.

The paper also implies that CEOs and boards should encourage CFOs to voice dissent and introduce structured disagreement in decision processes. When CFOs do not play the devil's advocate role, other executives or governance mechanisms may need to compensate for the loss of bottom-up monitoring.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that they infer CFO ingratiation from CEO-CFO LSM, although their evidence cannot fully separate ingratiation from social cohesion. The study focuses on unconscious verbal mimicry and does not examine other forms of behavioral mimicry, such as facial mimicry.

The evidence comes from conference-call language, so the authors call for work on other social-interaction indicators, such as surveys, facial mimicry, and language content matching. The analysis also focuses on M&A decisions, leaving other strategic decisions for future testing.

**Future Research**
Future research could collect CEO-CFO behavioral data through surveys or other direct measures to disentangle social cohesion from CFO ingratiation more fully. Scholars could also study whether facial mimicry, language content matching, or experimental evidence changes third-party evaluations of CEO-CFO dyads.

Future work could extend the LSM approach to other executive dyads, whole TMTs, non-CEO executive relationships, and CEO-board chair relationships. These extensions would test whether language style matching predicts the quality of other strategic decisions and board monitoring.
