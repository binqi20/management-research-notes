# Bored by Interest: How Intrinsic Motivation in One Task Can Reduce Performance on Other Tasks

**Abstract**
While existing research has demonstrated that intrinsic motivation can increase task performance, jobs are composed of multiple tasks, and it remains to be seen how intrinsic motivation in one task affects performance on other tasks. Drawing on theories of psychological contrast, we hypothesize that high intrinsic motivation in one task reduces performance on less intrinsically motivating tasks. In a field study at a Korean department store, employees with the highest maximum intrinsic motivation in one task had lower average and minimum performance across their other tasks as well as more performance variance across their tasks. In a laboratory experiment in the United States, working on a highly intrinsically motivating initial task led participants to perform worse in a subsequent task if it was uninteresting, but not if it was interesting. This effect was mediated by boredom, but not by a range of other psychological processes. Across both studies, moderate intrinsic motivation in one task was associated with better performance in less interesting tasks than high intrinsic motivation, revealing a curvilinear cross-task effect of intrinsic motivation. Our research advances knowledge about the dark side of intrinsic motivation, the design of work, and the drivers of task performance.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how intrinsic motivation in one task affects performance on other tasks within multi-task work. It focuses on whether very high intrinsic motivation in a focal task can reduce performance on less interesting tasks, and whether boredom explains that cross-task performance cost.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Intrinsic motivation in one task, operationalized in Study 1 as maximum task-level intrinsic motivation and in Study 2 as experimentally manipulated low, moderate, or high intrinsic motivation in an initial YouTube-search task.
- DV(s): Cross-task performance, including average performance in less intrinsically motivating tasks, minimum performance, performance variance, and objective performance on a subsequent phone-book or Little Alchemy task.
- Mediators: Boredom during the subsequent task.
- Moderators: Intrinsic motivation in the other task; the negative effect appears for less interesting subsequent tasks but not for highly interesting subsequent tasks.

The mechanism is psychological contrast. A highly intrinsically motivating task raises the experiential standard against which other tasks are judged, making less interesting tasks feel more boring. Boredom then weakens focus and performance on those other tasks. Across the field and laboratory studies, moderate intrinsic motivation produced better cross-task performance than either low or high intrinsic motivation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends motivation theory by shifting attention from within-task benefits of intrinsic motivation to cross-task tradeoffs in multi-task jobs. It shows that intrinsic motivation can be a double-edged sword: beneficial for the focal task but harmful for less interesting tasks through boredom. It also contributes to work design research by explaining why enriching one task may unintentionally depress performance on other tasks.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that employees who are intensely interested in one task may be at risk of underperforming on necessary but less exciting tasks. Work scheduling may matter: the authors suggest sequencing moderately interesting tasks before tedious tasks, rather than placing highly interesting tasks immediately before them. Employees can also use this awareness to manage attention, energy, and task order.

**Limitations**
The field study was correlational and could not establish whether intrinsic motivation caused lower performance in other tasks. It also lacked task-sequencing data, relied on supervisor performance ratings, and came from one Korean department-store context. The laboratory study improved causal inference but used student participants, simplified task settings, and did not test simultaneous task choice under real workplace time pressure.

**Future Research**
Future work could use diary or field-study designs to examine task sequencing, multitasking, and time allocation in real jobs. Researchers could test whether the same cross-task pattern appears for work with greater complexity and creative requirements rather than accuracy and proficiency outcomes. Further studies could examine cross-task effects of extrinsic, achievement, or prosocial motivation and the role of job crafting in making dull tasks less boring.
