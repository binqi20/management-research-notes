# The Cognitive and Behavioral Impact of Promotion and Prevention Contracts on Trust in Repeated Exchanges

**Abstract**
Although contracts certainly facilitate exchange, scholars have debated whether contracts and trust are complements or substitutes. Recent theoretical work has suggested that contract frames influence the relationship between contracts and trust. We test and extend this theorizing by examining the effects of prevention and promotion contract frames on trust and some potential cognitive and emotional mechanisms responsible for them. We also explore how unexpected negative events affect trust developed under different contract frames. Experiment 1 found that promotion contracts fostered stronger attributions of benevolence than prevention contracts, but emotional experiences of the exchanges did not differ. Additionally, trusting intentions were higher following positive exchange experience under promotion than prevention contracts. Experiment 2 found that people were more willing to engage in trusting behavior following positive exchange experience under promotion than prevention contracts. However, violations of exchange expectations were more damaging to trust developed under promotion than prevention contracts. Together, the studies indicate that contract frames and whether exchange experiences are positive or negative affect the relationship between contracts and trust, likely because contract frames influence attributions of benevolence.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether prevention and promotion contract frames lead contracts to function differently as substitutes for or complements to trust in repeated exchanges. It also asks which cognitive or emotional mechanisms explain those effects and how trust formed under different contract frames responds to unexpected negative exchange events.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Contract frame, comparing promotion contracts, prevention contracts, and in Study 2 a no-contract baseline; exchange history, including repeated positive exchanges and an unexpected negative event.
- DV(s): Trusting intentions in Study 1 and trusting behavior, operationalized as joint-venture investment without a contract, in Study 2.
- Mediators: Attributions of exchange partner benevolence are theorized and supported as a cognitive mechanism; emotional experiences are theorized but not empirically supported.
- Moderators: The valence and timing of exchange experience, especially whether an unexpected negative event follows prior positive exchange experience.

The mechanism is that promotion contracts frame exchange safeguards as rewards and opportunities, making positive partner behavior more likely to be attributed to benevolence. Prevention contracts frame safeguards as penalties and obligations, making contractual constraints more salient and reducing benevolence attributions. As a result, promotion contracts foster stronger trust after positive exchanges, but that trust is more fragile when subsequent exchange behavior violates expectations; prevention contracts develop less trust but preserve it somewhat better after negative events.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the contract-trust complement-substitute debate by showing that the effect of contracts on trust depends on contract framing rather than on the mere presence of a contract. It extends Weber and Mayer's contract-frame theory by providing experimental evidence that cognitive attributions of benevolence, rather than emotional experiences, help explain why promotion and prevention contracts affect trust differently. It also brings unexpected negative events into contracting research, showing that contract frames differ in their capacity to develop versus preserve trust over time.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat contract wording as a strategic governance tool, not only as a legal or economic instrument. Promotion frames may be useful when rapid trust development is important, but managers should recognize that trust developed under promotion contracts can be especially vulnerable to unexpected negative events. Prevention frames may generate less trust during positive exchanges, yet they may better preserve expectations when exchange outcomes deteriorate.

**Limitations**
The studies use experimental exchange tasks, so they provide precision and behavioral measurement but do not fully reproduce the complexity of real interfirm contracts. The authors note that the studies examine boundary spanners' trust development but do not determine whether trust resides at the interpersonal or organizational level. They also examine trust developed from exchange outcomes, not trust built from deeper knowledge, identification, information sharing, or long-term partner history.

**Future Research**
Future experiments could distinguish interpersonal from organizational trust by varying whether the representative for one organization changes during an ongoing exchange. Researchers could adapt the paradigm to study trust developed through information sharing, firm identity, or decision-maker identity under different contract frames. The authors also call for work on how trust can be repaired after unexpected negative events, including the roles of explanations, apologies, and new contracts.
