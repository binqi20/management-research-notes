# Friends, Gifts, and Cliques: Social Proximity and Recognition in Peer-Based Tournament Rituals

**Abstract**
Two main accounts of the effect of social proximity between candidates competing for recognition and members of the evaluating audience can be extrapolated from extant literature on peer-based tournament rituals and cultural fields. Following a Bourdieusian tradition, one account—which we label “self-reproduction”—points to the catalyzing effect of social proximity in shaping recognition along relational lines. Drawing from recent scholarship on social evaluation, a second account—which we call “intellectual distance”—suggests that social proximity deters recognition. We probe the influence of different articulations of social proximity (i.e., direct ties, cliquishness, and reciprocity) on recognition by studying awarding decisions within the context of the Norwegian advertising industry. Interviews with key informants as well as econometric results suggest that, while self-reproduction tends to prevail over intellectual distance, these effects coexist and their relative influence varies across levels of recognition. We gauge the relative saliency of the two accounts by using a mixed methods approach. Important implications for research on social evaluation and recognition in peer-based tournament rituals are drawn.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how the social structure cutting across the audience-producer interface affects the allocation of recognition among competing candidates in peer-based tournament rituals. It focuses on whether social proximity between jurors and project members catalyzes recognition through self-reproduction, deters recognition through intellectual distance, or produces different effects across weaker and stronger forms of recognition.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Social proximity between project members and jurors, operationalized as direct ties, cliquishness, and reciprocity.
- DV(s): Allocation of accolades in the Silver Tag contest, ordered as no placement, honorable mention, and award won.
- Mediators: Not modeled as formal mediators; the theoretical process contrasts self-reproduction through relationally patterned consecration with intellectual distance through reputational concern and disinterestedness.
- Moderators: Level of recognition functions as the key distinction, separating entry into the audience's attention space from entry into the consecration space.

The mechanism is ambivalent rather than one-directional. Self-reproduction implies that socially proximate candidates are more likely to receive recognition because peer evaluators share tastes, loyalties, and symbolic commitments with them. Intellectual distance implies that proximity can become a liability when jurors worry that favoring close peers will expose them to reputational criticism or accusations of biased judgment. The findings show that direct ties and cliquishness help projects enter the attention space, while cliquishness lowers the likelihood of moving to award victory and reciprocity remains positive across both levels.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on social evaluation by showing that recognition in peer-based tournament rituals is patterned by audience-candidate relationships, not only by producer characteristics, audience characteristics, status, or quality proxies. It extends Bourdieusian accounts of symbolic fields by showing that self-reproduction coexists with intellectual distance, so relational proximity can both catalyze and inhibit recognition depending on the level of recognition. It also contributes to tournament theory by using network constructs to place award contests more explicitly in their social context.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that award organizers and peer evaluators should treat relational embeddedness as a consequential feature of evaluation, even when formal procedures are designed to ensure fairness. Because direct ties, cliques, and reciprocity can influence who receives attention and consecration, peer-evaluation settings should recognize both favoritism risks and intellectual-distance responses among evaluators. For peripheral producers, the findings suggest that weak embeddedness in field networks may limit access to symbolic and economic opportunities.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that peer-based tournament rituals have features that overlap with but differ from promotion processes, funding committees, auditions, and online user communities, so generalization should be careful. The study analyzes project-level award outcomes while the theoretical arguments are mainly at the individual level, creating a level-of-analysis limitation. The data do not directly observe jury deliberation processes, so the paper cannot fully show how conflicting juror opinions are reconciled into collective award decisions.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine how relational proximity shapes recognition in other peer-based evaluative settings, including labor-market promotion, funding committees, R&D selection panels, orchestra auditions, and online communities. The authors call for more work on how peripheral actors and novel claims gain traction when social structures favor insiders. They also suggest experimental or more processual studies that can observe micro-level deliberation and better control the quality of the project under evaluation.
