# Regulatory Uncertainty, Corporate Structure, and Strategic Agendas: Evidence from the U.S. Renewable Electricity Industry

**Abstract**
Prior research has demonstrated that although organizations generally avoid uncertainties, this behavior is not universally true. To better understand the contingencies around organizations' responses to regulatory uncertainty, we consider the influence of corporate structure on attentional processing. Our theory and findings suggest that because corporate structure differentiates their problem-solving and learning activities, headquarters and subsidiaries differ in attention allocation on their strategic agendas. Our results show that headquarters display lower levels of uncertainty aversion than do subsidiaries, but all organizations generally make greater shifts in attention toward alternative courses of action if they possess complementary experience and access shared experience. We test these ideas using a sample of U.S. electric utility companies attending to renewable technologies from 2000 to 2010—a market greatly affected by regulatory uncertainty. By linking models of decision making under uncertainty and learning with the attention-based view, we develop a more comprehensive understanding of the ways uncertainty influences organizational attention.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how organizations allocate attention to alternative courses of action under regulatory uncertainty, and why headquarters and subsidiaries within the same corporate hierarchy differ in that response. It further asks whether complementary experience and shared experience help units direct more attention toward renewable electricity technologies on their strategic agendas.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Regulatory uncertainty, measured primarily as years remaining until compliance with a state renewable portfolio standard; corporate structure, distinguishing headquarters from subsidiaries; complementary operating/regulatory experience; shared operating, renewable operating, and regulatory experience.
- DV(s): Renewable agenda, measured as counts of renewable electricity technology words in organizations' 10-K reports.
- Mediators: Not explicitly modeled.
- Moderators: Headquarters versus subsidiary status in the regulatory uncertainty and experience relationships; complementary and shared experience as contingencies that shape attention to renewable alternatives.

The mechanism links regulatory uncertainty to organizational attention through the attention-based view. Because headquarters and subsidiaries have different problem-solving roles, stakeholder exposure, and information access, the same regulatory uncertainty is processed differently across the corporate hierarchy. Subsidiaries show stronger uncertainty aversion, reducing attention to renewable alternatives as uncertainty increases, while headquarters are less likely to withdraw attention. Experience changes this pattern: units with complementary regulatory and operating experience, and units that share relevant experience with connected units, are better able to interpret uncertainty and place renewable technologies on their agendas.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on organizational responses to uncertainty by showing that responses are not homogeneous within firms: corporate structure creates systematic variation between headquarters and subsidiaries. It extends the attention-based view by identifying regulatory uncertainty as an external condition that shapes strategic agendas through internal attention structures. It also contributes to organizational learning and nonmarket strategy research by showing how complementary and shared experience condition attention to environmental regulation and renewable technologies.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not assume that headquarters and subsidiaries will interpret regulatory uncertainty in the same way. Corporate managers can support better attention to renewable alternatives by improving knowledge flows from subsidiaries, especially when headquarters lack direct operational experience. Policy makers should recognize that legally related headquarters and subsidiary units may respond differently to the same regulation, so reducing uncertainty and communicating regulatory information to subsidiary managers may improve consistency in organizational responses.

**Limitations**
The study examines a relatively homogeneous empirical context: U.S. investor-owned electric utilities subject to renewable portfolio standards. This helps isolate regulatory uncertainty but limits generalizability to less regulated or more heterogeneous settings. The authors also note that the structure of the data cannot fully separate headquarters' influence on subsidiary agendas from top management team preferences, and that the regulatory uncertainty measure may also capture broader environmental change.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether the headquarters-subsidiary attention pattern holds in other regulated industries, such as pharmaceuticals, telecoms, and information and communications technology. Further work could distinguish regulatory uncertainty from more general environmental uncertainty and use data that more directly identify headquarters' control over subsidiary agendas and the role of top management preferences. Research could also study how formal corporate structure and cognitive divisions of labor jointly shape attention under different environmental contingencies.
