# Collective Emotions in Institutional Creation Work

**Abstract**
In this paper, we explain how and why collective emotions enable institutional creation work. Based on an ethnography in Limonade, a Haitian community affected by the 2010 earthquake, we identify social practices that elicit collective emotions through the creation of new institutions across the three disaster recovery phases. Our study’s key insight is that new institutions converge collective emotions such that they in turn justify ongoing, as well as motivate engagement in new, institutional creation work practices. Theorizing from our findings, we develop a generative model that describes the justifying and motivating function of collective emotions in the establishment of embedded institutions. In conclusion, our paper advances theory on collective emotions in institutional work and generates implications for post-disaster management practice.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how collective emotions enable the creation of institutions in a disaster-affected local community. It examines how social practices by local agents in Limonade, Haiti, elicited shared emotional responses that sustained and expanded institutional creation work after the 2010 earthquake.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Collective emotions, institutional creation work, social practices, new institutional arrangements, post-disaster community recovery.
- Process / phases: Disaster response, disaster recovery, and disaster reconstruction.
- Practices: Improvising, assessing needs, advocating emergent roles, leading, collaborating, valorizing, reflecting, educating, mobilizing engagement, and synergizing.
- Emergent arrangements: Collective awareness, collective trust, collective intentions, temporary material solutions, collective vision, collective alertness, and enduring physical infrastructure.
- Emotional dynamics: Feeling of security, compassionate empathy, affective solidarity, collective hope, collective confidence, and harmonious passion.

The process is that local agents create temporary, symbolic, and material institutional arrangements through situated practices. These arrangements elicit and converge collective emotions among community members. Those emotions first justify the continuation of current practices and then motivate engagement in new practices, allowing temporary post-disaster arrangements to become more embedded institutions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional work theory by specifying how collective emotions operate inside the relationship between social practices and institutional arrangements. It argues that emotions are not only individual experiences of institutional actors but also collective affective responses that become constitutive of institutional creation work.

The paper also contributes to theory on collective emotions by showing two functions in institutional creation: a justifying function that legitimizes ongoing practices and a motivating function that generates new joint action. This model extends institutional work research from fields and discursive spaces to physically proximate local communities where shared emotions are more visible.

**Practical Implication**
Post-disaster management should give stronger attention to locally embedded actors who can mobilize trust, hope, solidarity, and confidence over time. The study suggests that external aid organizations may meet emergency needs but often lack the proximity and long-term ties needed to build emotionally grounded local institutions.

For policy makers and international actors, the implication is to resource local organizations and partnerships beyond the immediate response phase. Locally trusted organizations can help communities move from temporary relief toward durable education, health, food-production, and infrastructure arrangements.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the ethnography is idiographic and trades theoretical generality for contextual particularity. Its strongest boundary condition is physical proximity: the model is most directly applicable where actors are co-present and jointly attend to shared local objects of emotion.

The study also focuses primarily on positive collective emotions and therefore underemphasizes negative or traumatogenic collective emotions. It does not fully examine strategic emotion work by institutional workers or how actors may intentionally create specific emotions to influence others.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine how negative collective emotions such as despair interact with hope, solidarity, confidence, and passion in institutional work. Scholars could also investigate how community agents purposefully create or manage emotions, including differences in emotional competence, emotional investments, and agentic orientation.

The authors also call for research that links people-centered, strategic, and structural-institutional perspectives on emotions. Such work could clarify how particular institutions shape particular collective emotions and how collective emotions, in turn, shape institutions beyond disaster-affected settings.
