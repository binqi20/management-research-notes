# Responding to Bad Press: How CEO Temporal Focus Influences the Sensitivity to Negative Media Coverage of Acquisitions

**Abstract**
Management scholars have demonstrated that CEOs look to cues provided from external stakeholders when determining the direction and timing of strategic action. Research has focused on “hard” forms of external performance feedback, primarily in the form of stock market reactions. To understand both whether and to what degree “soft” external performance feedback of strategic actions influences the subsequent strategic decisions of firms, we build our arguments from and contribute to upper echelons theory. We argue that negative media reactions to the announcement of a major acquisition will shape the degree to which the firm will engage in subsequent acquisition activity. However, our theory suggests that an important individual attribute, CEO temporal focus, will influence how sensitive CEOs are to media coverage. Using a sample of 747 large acquisitions made between 2006 and 2011, we find strong support for our hypotheses. Additionally, our supplemental analysis demonstrates an important difference between the influence of stock market and media reactions following an acquisition. While CEO temporal focus shapes which CEOs will be influenced by media reactions, it appears that CEOs’ propensity to be influenced by stock market reactions is not moderated by their temporal focus.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether soft external performance feedback from the media influences subsequent strategic action after a major acquisition announcement. It also asks whether CEO temporal focus shapes how strongly negative media reactions affect later acquisition spending.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Negative media reactions to the focal acquisition announcement.
- DV(s): Subsequent acquisition spending during the 365 days after the focal acquisition.
- Mediators: No measured mediator; the theorized process is CEO attention to, interpretation of, and aversive learning from negative media feedback.
- Moderators: CEO past focus and CEO future focus; CEO present focus is examined in supplemental analysis and is not significant.

The mechanism is that negative media coverage acts as soft external performance feedback that can make CEOs more cautious about repeating acquisition activity. CEOs high in past focus are expected to attend more to negative prior feedback and carry it into later decisions, strengthening the negative media reaction-subsequent acquisition spending relationship. CEOs high in future focus are expected to emphasize future opportunities and long-term possibilities, weakening the influence of negative media reactions on later acquisition spending.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends upper echelons theory by showing that CEOs respond not only to hard, quantifiable external feedback such as stock market reactions, but also to softer stakeholder evaluations in media coverage. It specifies CEO temporal focus as a cognitive attribute that shapes which executives attend to and act on negative media feedback.

It also contributes to acquisition research by showing that subsequent acquisition behavior is influenced by subjective media evaluations of prior acquisitions, not only by economic indicators. The supplemental analyses further distinguish media reactions from stock market reactions because temporal focus moderates the former but not the latter.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that CEOs' responses to media evaluations can shape subsequent strategic action, not only reactions to stock-market feedback. Negative media coverage is associated with lower subsequent acquisition activity, especially when the CEO is strongly past focused, whereas CEOs high in future focus appear less influenced by negative media reactions.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that an omitted variable could influence both negative media reaction and subsequent acquisition spending, although their confounding-threshold analysis suggests this would need to be a relatively strong omitted correlate. The study also does not show whether CEOs' responsiveness to negative media coverage benefits the firm or moves it away from better strategic actions.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other forms of soft performance feedback and whether they fit within an aspirational framework of CEO decision making. The authors also call for more research on CEO temporal focus as a moderator of stakeholder feedback, including feedback from institutional investors and market analysts.

Future studies could extend the acquisition logic beyond subsequent deal activity to post-acquisition integration, such as whether negative media reactions lead CEOs to invest more effort in proving the acquisition successful or to pursue faster cost reductions. They also call for work on whether attending to media coverage improves firms' future actions or distracts CEOs from the best strategic course.
