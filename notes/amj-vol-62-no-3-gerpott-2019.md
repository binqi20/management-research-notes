# It’s Not Just What is Said, but When it’s Said: A Temporal Account of Verbal Behaviors and Emergent Leadership in Self-Managed Teams

**Abstract**
“Emergent leadership”—the ascription of informal leadership responsibilities among team members—is a dynamic phenomenon that comes into place through social interactions. Yet, theory remains sparse about the importance of verbal behaviors for emergent leadership in self-managed teams over a team’s lifecycle. Adopting a functional perspective on leadership, we develop a temporal account that links changes in task-, change-, and relations-oriented communication to emergent leadership in early, middle, and late team phases. We test the hypothesized relationships in 42 teams that provided round-robin emergent leadership ratings and videotapes of their first, midterm, and final meetings. Team members’ verbal behaviors were captured using finegrained empirical interaction coding. Multilevel modeling showed that task-oriented communication was a stable positive predictor of emergent leadership at all time points. Change-oriented communication predicted emergent leadership at the start of a project and diminished in relevance at the midterm and final meetings. Relations-oriented communication gained importance, such that an increase in relations-oriented behaviors toward the project end predicted emergent leadership. We discuss theoretical implications for conceptualizing the behavioral antecedents of emergent leadership from a timeand context-sensitive perspective.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when changes in task-, change-, and relations-oriented verbal behaviors predict emergent leadership at different points in a self-managed team’s lifecycle. It focuses on whether the behavioral antecedents of informal leadership vary across the first, midterm, and final project meetings.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Team members' task-oriented, change-oriented, and relations-oriented verbal behaviors, coded from videotaped meetings.
- DV(s): Emergent leadership ratings assigned by fellow team members.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Meeting time point across the team lifecycle, operationalized as first, midterm, and final meetings.

The mechanism is temporal fit between a member's communication and the team's changing functional needs. Task-oriented statements help teams accomplish work and remain positively associated with emergent leadership throughout the lifecycle. Change-oriented communication signals direction and initiative early, when the team is establishing its project approach, but loses relevance later. Relations-oriented communication becomes more important near project completion, when social cohesion and support help teams manage final task pressure.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends emergent leadership research by shifting behavioral antecedents from a static account to a time-sensitive account of social influence in self-managed teams. It links functional leadership theory, discursive leadership, team development, and interaction coding to show that leadership ascription depends not only on what members say but also on when they say it. The findings refine prior debates by showing that task-oriented behavior remains consistently relevant, change-oriented behavior matters early, and relations-oriented behavior matters most at the end.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations and leadership-development programs should train members of self-managed teams to read the temporal context of team interaction and adapt their verbal behavior flexibly. The authors caution against teaching employees to use one behavior mechanically at one point in time; instead, they emphasize situational awareness, behavioral flexibility, feedback from videotaped meetings, coaching, and reflective practice.

**Limitations**
The conceptual model assumes a particular lifecycle pattern in which teams align goals early, focus on task execution, and concentrate on goal attainment near the final meeting; other group-development perspectives could imply different timing. The study used consulting project teams embedded in a university setting, so the findings may not generalize to other organizational contexts where relational communication is more central from the start. The a priori coding scheme also captures each statement's primary function but cannot fully represent the simultaneous relational meanings of communication.

**Future Research**
Future research could experimentally vary team activities, goal clarity, social conflict, or time pressure across lifecycle phases to test whether different temporal demands change which behaviors predict emergent leadership. Scholars could examine project teams in other settings, such as caregiving work, where relations-oriented communication may matter earlier. Future work could also use finer-grained within-meeting analyses or relational-control coding to capture both content and interpersonal meaning in ongoing communication.
