# Crossed Wires: Endorsement Signals and the Effects of IPO Firm Delistings on Venture Capitalists’ Reputations

**Abstract**
Signaling theorists have paid a great deal of attention to the costs of acquiring characteristics that can serve as signals, such as endorsements from reputable third parties. However, limited attention has been devoted to the penalty costs associated with providing inaccurate signals and the factors that can exacerbate or attenuate the penalties. We examine the effect of negative feedback loops on venture capital (VC) firms’ reputations that result from the failures (delistings) of the newly public firms they once endorsed. Drawing on signaling and attribution theories, we argue that endorsements by reputable VC firms create high expectations that, when violated, cause stakeholders to look for scapegoats, resulting in reputational damage to the endorsing VCs. We find empirical support for this argument, and for the attenuating effect of both post-IPO market performance and survival. Our study contributes to the conversation about endorsements as signals, and empirically tests the implicit assumption that endorsements place the reputation of the endorser at risk.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether endorsers suffer reputational penalties when the firms they endorsed later fail to meet expectations. Specifically, it examines whether negative delistings by newly public VC-backed firms damage lead VCs' subsequent reputations, and whether IPO underpricing, post-IPO performance, and post-IPO survival duration change the size of that penalty.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Negative delisting of a newly public VC-backed portfolio firm within five years of IPO.
- DV(s): Lead VC reputation at t+1, measured with a modified LPJ VC Reputation Index.
- Mediators: Stakeholder disappointment, negative expectancy violation, and scapegoating are theorized attribution processes but are not directly measured as mediators.
- Moderators: IPO underpricing, post-IPO industry-adjusted ROA, post-IPO market-to-book ratio, and post-IPO survival years.

The mechanism combines signaling and attribution logics. Reputable lead VCs endorse startups by financing and taking them public, which raises stakeholder expectations about the startups' quality and prospects. When those firms later delist for negative reasons, stakeholders search for a blameworthy actor and attribute part of the failure back to the salient lead VC, damaging its reputation. The penalty is attenuated when post-IPO market performance is stronger and when the firm survives longer after IPO, because these conditions cognitively decouple the portfolio firm from its VC; IPO underpricing does not significantly strengthen the penalty.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends signaling theory by empirically testing the often-assumed penalty cost of inaccurate endorsement signals. It shows that negative feedback can travel from a failed signal recipient back to the signal sender, even when the sender has limited ongoing control over the recipient.

It also contributes to research on organizational reputation by showing that reputation can be damaged by distant events involving affiliated firms, not only by a focal firm's own misconduct or proximal crises. The robustness tests further refine endorsement theory by showing that lead VCs, rather than non-lead syndicate members, bear the reputational penalty.

**Practical Implication**
For venture capital firms and other professional service firms, the findings imply that endorsements create long-tail reputational exposure. Firms that raise client or portfolio-company expectations can be penalized later if those expectations are violated, even when the subsequent failure is only partly under their control.

The study also suggests that endorsers should manage expectations and attend to portfolio firms' post-endorsement viability, not only the short-term gains from successful IPO exits. Stronger post-IPO market performance and longer survival help reduce, but do not eliminate, reputational damage from later failure.

**Limitations**
The study is limited to delistings of VC-backed firms in the United States, so institutional, regulatory, and VC-market differences may affect generalizability to other countries. The reputation measure is objective and formative rather than a direct longitudinal measure of stakeholder perceptions, making the test conservative but leaving the perceptual process indirectly observed.

The design also cannot fully determine whether particular delistings were caused by inadequate VC due diligence, premature VC exit pressure, or external forces beyond both the VC's and portfolio firm's control. The authors note that such distinctions could matter for whether stakeholder punishment is reasonable.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether and how VCs repair reputational damage after portfolio-firm delistings, including which repair mechanisms work and how long recovery takes. Studies could also test whether current successes offset past failures in stakeholders' evaluations.

The authors also call for research on the nature of negative events behind delistings. Future work could distinguish failures attributable to VC actions from failures driven by external shocks, and test whether stakeholders respond differently across these causal conditions and national contexts.
