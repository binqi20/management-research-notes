# The Voice Bystander Effect: How Information Redundancy Inhibits Employee Voice

**Abstract**
Employees often remain silent rather than speak up to managers with work-related ideas, concerns, and opinions. As a result, managers can remain in the dark about issues that are otherwise well known to, or universally understood by, frontline employees. We propose a previously unexplored explanation for this phenomenon: Voice is prone to "bystander effects," such that, the more certain information is shared among employees, the less any particular employee feels individually responsible for bringing up that information with managers. We theorize that such bystander effects are especially likely to occur when peers of focal employees, on average, enjoy high-quality relationships with managers and thereby have adequate relational access to voice up the hierarchy. Using a correlational study involving managers and employees working in teams in a Fortune 500 company, as well as two experimental studies (a laboratory study involving undergraduate students working in a hierarchical setting, and a scenario study with a sample of U.S.-based workers), we provide evidence for our conceptual model. We also discuss the theoretical and managerial implications of our findings.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why employees may remain silent about work-related ideas, concerns, and opinions that are widely known among peers. Specifically, it examines whether information redundancy reduces employee voice by diffusing responsibility, and whether this effect is stronger when peers have high-quality relationships with the manager.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Information redundancy, operationalized in Study 1 through workflow structural equivalence and manipulated directly in Studies 2 and 3.
- DV(s): Employee voice to managers or higher-level decision makers.
- Mediators: Diffusion of responsibility.
- Moderators: Average peer-LMX, meaning the average quality of peers' relationships with the manager.

The mechanism is a voice bystander effect. When employees believe peers possess the same work-related information, they feel less personally responsible for raising that information to managers. This diffusion of responsibility lowers voice, especially when those peers appear capable of speaking up because they have strong relational access to the manager. Across the three studies, the authors distinguish this account from psychological safety, efficacy, and instrumentality explanations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends employee voice research by showing that shared information can inhibit, rather than enable, speaking up. It challenges the assumption that common knowledge creates safety or confidence in numbers, arguing instead that information redundancy can create diffusion of responsibility.

It also extends bystander-effect theory by moving the phenomenon into workgroups with ongoing relationships. The paper shows that the relational capacity of other observers, captured through average peer-LMX, shapes whether a focal employee passes responsibility for action to peers.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not assume that widely shared awareness of a problem makes voice more likely. Practices that spread information broadly and build strong manager-employee ties can still leave each employee waiting for someone else to speak up.

The authors suggest that managers reduce this risk by emphasizing each employee's unique contribution and making individual voice feel important rather than redundant. This is especially relevant for issues that many employees know about but no one escalates.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Study 1 did not measure diffusion of responsibility directly, even though Studies 2 and 3 supported that mechanism experimentally. Study 1 also used a relatively small field sample of 132 employees nested in 25 teams and lacked gender diversity because all participants were male.

The paper further limits its tested moderator to average peer-LMX. The authors identify other possible moderators, including personality traits such as conscientiousness, peers' voice-relevant competencies, and whether the issue involves challenging voice or a clear and unambiguous threat to the team.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the mediation process in field settings and examine the model in larger and more gender-diverse team samples. Such work would clarify whether diffusion of responsibility operates similarly in naturally occurring organizational contexts.

The authors also call for research on additional boundary conditions of the voice bystander phenomenon. Future studies could examine personality, peer competence, and issue type to determine when shared information decreases voice and when employees remain personally responsible for speaking up.
