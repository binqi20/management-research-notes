# The Social Practice of Coevolving Strategy and Structure to Realize Mandated Radical Change

**Abstract**
This paper draws on a longitudinal interpretive case study to develop a theoretical model of how actions by people across an organization coevolve strategy and structure in order to realize a mandated radical change. Mandated change, imposed by a powerful external actor, extends understanding of the dynamics of radical change. While other studies have examined how unintended consequences shape the way radical change is realized, under mandated change, actors focus on bringing about the change in ways that will be, collectively, considered to realize the intended mandate. Our study, grounded in a practice approach concerning how actions bring about radical change, identifies three different action cycles: performing action cycles, reinforcing action cycles, and reflecting action cycles. Shifts in cycles are triggered by unintended consequences that escalate into breakdowns. Cumulative cycles, arising from the escalation of unintended consequences that lead to breakdowns, are necessary for bringing about a mandated change in strategy and structure. Following breakdown, actors switch to reflecting actions in which they consider the underlying intent of the mandate and how to modify the espoused strategy and structure to bring about that intent.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how people's actions bring about a mandated radical change of strategy and structure. It focuses on how senior, middle, and frontline actors at Telco enacted, reinforced, reflected on, and modified an espoused strategy and structure under a legally binding regulatory mandate.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Mandated radical change, espoused strategy and structure, realized radical change, performing action cycles, reinforcing action cycles, reflecting action cycles, unintended consequences, breakdowns, and the "spirit" of the mandate.
- Process / cycles: Actors perform the initial espoused strategy and structure; unintended consequences emerge; actors reinforce existing actions because the mandate is legally binding; unintended consequences escalate into breakdowns; actors shift into reflecting action cycles; modified strategy and structure are enacted through new performing cycles.
- Strategic and structural elements: Independence, industry supplier, equivalence, and products, each carrying intertwined strategic objectives and structural arrangements.
- Managerial levels: Frontline employees, middle managers, and top managers all participate in action cycles, with problems escalating upward and revised understandings cascading back through the organization.
- Boundary conditions: The process is theorized for a detailed, externally imposed, legally binding mandate where failure carries financial penalties and possible breakup.

The central process is cumulative rather than linear. Under mandated change, actors initially treat unintended consequences as signs that they should work harder to enact the espoused strategy and structure, not as permission to reinterpret the change. Only when these reinforced actions produce breakdowns do actors step back from everyday performance, reflect on the mandate's intent, and modify the espoused strategy and structure in ways that still comply with the mandate. Strategy and structure therefore coevolve through situated action across organizational levels.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends process theories of radical change by showing that breakdowns can be necessary triggers for reflective action under mandated change. It contributes to practice-based strategy and organization theory by explaining how performing, reinforcing, and reflecting action cycles produce the coevolution of strategy and structure. It also expands accounts of top managers in change processes by showing them not only as designers of change but also as active participants in working out how the mandate should be realized.

**Practical Implication**
The study implies that managers implementing mandated change should treat breakdowns as diagnostic moments for examining whether current actions realize the intent of the mandate, not only as execution failures. It also suggests that top managers need to stay actively engaged with middle managers, frontline employees, and regulators, because the meaning of compliance may need to be worked out through practice. For organizations under regulatory mandates, faithful implementation may require modifying the espoused strategy and structure while preserving the mandate's underlying intent.

**Limitations**
The authors present Telco as an extreme case of externally imposed, detailed, legally binding mandated change, so the process may differ when mandates are weaker, less specific, internally imposed, or easier to abandon. The study is a qualitative single-case process analysis, designed for theory development rather than statistical generalization. The paper also notes that the case involved top managers who had helped negotiate the mandate, which may shape the reflecting cycles observed.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether less detailed or less binding mandates generate the same reinforcing action cycles and breakdowns, or whether actors adapt more flexibly before breakdown occurs. Studies could also compare externally imposed mandates with internally imposed mandates from powerful corporate actors or parent companies. The authors further suggest examining how different degrees of constraint, legal consequence, and top-manager responsibility shape managerial engagement with radical change.
