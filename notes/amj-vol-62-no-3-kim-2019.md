# Stuck in the Past? The Influence of a Leader’s Past Cultural Experience on Group Culture and Positive and Negative Group Deviance

**Abstract**
Extant research on the antecedents of cultures has posited that cultures result from the internal and external changes experienced by the group (the functionality perspective) and from a group leader’s personal values and personality traits (the leader–trait perspective). The current study proposes another important but neglected antecedent of cultures: a leader’s past cultural experience. Specifically, we theorize that group leaders enact cultures based on their past cultural experiences, essentially transferring cultures from their former groups to their current groups. Two studies, one in the field (Study 1) and another in the laboratory (Study 2), reveal that the levels of cultural tightness in current groups are predicted by group leaders’ past experience with cultural tightness in their former groups in which they were followers. This relationship becomes stronger when the group leaders identified with or had longer tenure in their former groups. In addition, the cultural tightness that leaders transferred from their former groups to their current groups influences negative (counterproductive work behavior) and positive (promotive and prohibitive voice) forms of group deviance. The theoretical and managerial implications of our findings for leadership and culture are discussed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks where group cultures come from and whether leaders transfer cultural tightness from groups in which they previously participated as followers to groups they later lead. It also asks when this transfer is stronger and whether transferred cultural tightness reduces both negative group deviance and constructive forms of voice.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader’s past experience with cultural tightness in a former group.
- DV(s): Current group cultural tightness; counterproductive work behavior; promotive voice; prohibitive voice; and, in Study 2, number of rules set by groups.
- Mediators: Current group cultural tightness mediates the relationship between leader former-group cultural tightness and group deviance outcomes.
- Moderators: Leader identification with the former group and leader tenure in the former group strengthen cultural transfer and the indirect relationships through current group tightness.

The proposed mechanism is cultural transfer under bounded rationality and career-history imprinting. Leaders responsible for group functioning do not simply optimize culture from current contingencies; they rely on familiar cultural solutions learned in prior groups, especially when those prior cultures are internalized through identification or tenure. Transferred tightness then creates strong norms and conformity pressure, reducing counterproductive work behavior but also suppressing promotive and prohibitive voice.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends culture-formation theory by introducing leaders’ past cultural experience as an antecedent distinct from both functionality and leader-trait explanations. It argues that leaders shape cultures through remembered cultural solutions, not only through current environmental adaptation or stable personal values and traits.

It also contributes to cultural tightness-looseness research by moving the construct to the group level in organizational settings and linking it to both antecedents and consequences. The evidence frames cultural tightness as a double-edged sword: it can reduce harmful deviance while also discouraging constructive challenge and voice.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations hiring or assigning group leaders should consider the cultures those leaders may import from prior groups, especially when leaders strongly identified with or spent longer time in those groups. Explicit cultural expectations and incentives may be needed when the desired culture differs from the leader’s prior cultural template.

The findings also imply that tight and loose cultures should be matched to task demands. Tight cultures may fit work requiring protocol compliance and stability, whereas looser cultures may be better for work that depends on constructive deviance, dissent, and new ideas.

**Limitations**
The authors focus on cultural tightness rather than cultural values such as individualism, collectivism, or power distance, so it remains unclear whether the same transfer logic applies to other cultural dimensions. Study 1 and Study 2 also examine newly formed groups, leaving open how cultural transfer operates in ongoing groups where past and present cultural experience are harder to separate.

The laboratory study uses students and brief group interaction, so it cannot capture the full complexity of workplace culture formation over time. The counterculture manipulation in Study 2 also gives participants indirect information about effectiveness rather than repeated direct experience with cultural success or failure.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether cultural values interact with cultural tightness and whether voice norms can make tight cultures increase, rather than suppress, employee voice. The authors also call for work on cultural transfer in ongoing groups, particularly the role of early-career cultural experience and career imprinting.

Additional studies should examine culture formation in self-managing groups, where no formal leader holds disproportionate authority. Future work could also test whether groups adjust transferred cultures after repeated direct feedback that an imported cultural solution is ineffective.
