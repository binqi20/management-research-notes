# Visions of Change as Visions of Continuity

**Abstract**
Despite wide consensus that leader vision is a key vehicle for leaders to motivate followers to support organizational change, it remains far from clear what characterizes an effective vision of change. Research on organizational change that has used a social identity perspective has asserted that one important reason why followers resist change is because change may pose a threat to their subjective sense of continuity of organizational identity. Accordingly, we hypothesize that leaders that communicate visions of change can address this resistance by assuring followers that the essence of the organizational identity will remain unchanged—making their vision of change also a vision of continuity. In line with our proposition that collective continuity is valued because it serves an uncertainty-reduction function, such visions should be more predictive of vision effectiveness the higher follower work-related uncertainty. Across a field study and an experimental study we find support for these predictions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what characterizes an effective leader vision of organizational change. It examines whether visions that assure followers of organizational identity continuity generate greater support for change, especially when followers experience high work-related uncertainty.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader communication of a vision of change that emphasizes collective continuity.
- DV(s): Follower support for change, measured through supervisor ratings in Study 1 and behavioral support in Study 2.
- Mediators: Perceived collective continuity.
- Moderators: Follower work-related uncertainty or environmental uncertainty.

The mechanism is that change can threaten employees' subjective continuity of organizational identity. A vision of continuity frames the future as preserving the organization's defining features, increasing perceived collective continuity; this matters most under uncertainty because social identity provides an uncertainty-reduction function.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to visionary leadership research by specifying a content feature of effective change visions: assuring continuity rather than only emphasizing dissatisfaction with the status quo or an attractive future. It also contributes to organizational change and social identity research by showing that leader vision communication can shape perceived collective continuity, which helps explain support for change under uncertainty.

**Practical Implication**
Managers leading change should not only rationalize why change is needed or portray the future positively. They should also identify what employees understand as central to the organization's identity and frame planned change as preserving, reaffirming, or continuing those defining features.

**Limitations**
Study 1 raises possible common method and common source concerns for the self-reported antecedent and mediator, although the authors argue these concerns are offset by Study 2 and by supervisor-rated support in Study 1. The paper also calls for tests of whether the findings generalize to other change outcomes, such as satisfaction, trust in management, and turnover intentions, and to other change contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine the framing strategies leaders use to create perceived continuity, including linking past and future, framing change as preserving identity, and using organizational symbols or work design. The authors also suggest examining leader-member exchange, value congruence, change size perceptions, industry turbulence, and cases where change itself targets identity.
