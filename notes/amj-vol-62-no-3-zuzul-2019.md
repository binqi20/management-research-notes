# “Matter Battles”: Cognitive Representations, Boundary Objects, and the Failure of Collaboration in Two Smart Cities

**Abstract**
In this paper, I present a longitudinal study of two smart city projects that brought together experts from diverse knowledge domains. Both projects structured collaboration around the development of boundary objects that could integrate actors’ expertise. In both projects, however, the objects sparked conflicts that exacerbated rather than attenuated differences. I develop a process model exploring how and why the development of boundary objects can manifest as divisive conflict that derails collaboration. In both projects, extreme novelty gave rise to concept ambiguity, a lack of shared ideas about what smart cities were, and process ambiguity, a lack of shared ideas about how smart cities should be developed. Ambiguity led actors from diverse domains to form divergent cognitive representations about smart cities. As they developed boundary objects, actors made decisions that violated some cognitive representations, while reifying others into material outcomes. Their efforts to develop the objects manifest as matter battles: high-stakes conflicts about material outcomes that, over time, set the stage for collaboration failure. In advancing these ideas, I provide an alternative perspective to the literature on collaboration across boundaries, which has primarily treated boundary objects as tools of integration rather than weapons of division.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why and how attempts to develop boundary objects, usually treated as tools of integration, can become divisive conflicts that derail collaboration. It examines this question in two smart city projects where actors from different knowledge domains tried to transform ambiguous ideas into material urban-development outcomes.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Extreme novelty, concept ambiguity, process ambiguity, divergent cognitive representations, boundary objects, materiality, matter battles, negative emotions, stereotyping, and collaboration failure.
- Process / phases: Extreme novelty creates ambiguity; actors form divergent smart and city representations; boundary object development forces concrete choices; choices reify some representations while violating others; repeated matter battles produce negative emotional and cognitive dynamics; actors withdraw from collaboration.
- Boundary objects: SmartBlock contract, SmartBlock master plan, SmartBlock pro forma, SmartTown financial deck, and SmartTown master plan.
- Emergent outcomes: Conflict over material decisions, simplified us-versus-them stereotypes, reduced interaction, disengagement, project withdrawal, and abandonment of collaboration.
- Boundary conditions: The process is theorized for extremely novel, cross-boundary innovation projects that require diverse experts to create material outcomes without settled templates or accepted authority structures.

The process begins when novelty leaves actors without shared definitions of what smart cities are or how they should be built. Actors from technology, design, urban engineering, architecture, real estate, and consulting domains develop different cognitive representations of the same projects. When boundary objects require concrete decisions about materials, financial assumptions, or development plans, those choices become consequential because the resulting city is physical, durable, and difficult to reverse. The conflicts therefore become matter battles: high-stakes disputes over material outcomes that intensify emotions, stereotyping, and withdrawal rather than integration.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on cross-boundary collaboration by showing that boundary objects can become weapons of division, not only tools of integration. It links cognitive representations and boundary objects by explaining how ambiguous settings allow divergent mental maps to form and then become reified through material choices. It also develops the construct of matter battles as a form of intractable conflict that can emerge in novel organizational projects even without long-standing group divisions.

**Practical Implication**
The study implies that leaders of novel cross-boundary innovation projects should not assume that boundary objects will automatically integrate diverse expertise. They need to surface and compare actors’ cognitive representations before object development forces irreversible choices. The paper suggests that pre-prototypes or other low-stakes draft objects may help reveal divergent representations before decisions become embedded in high-stakes material outcomes.

**Limitations**
The study was designed to generate rather than test theory, so the process model should be treated as analytically grounded but not statistically tested. The evidence comes from two deviant smart city projects that both failed, which supports process theorizing but limits claims about prevalence or comparative success conditions. The author also notes that the cases did not allow systematic comparison of which kinds of boundary objects are more or less likely to generate matter battles.

**Future Research**
Future research can test whether the matter-battle process appears in settings with clearer leadership, more established power dynamics, or less expert collaborators. Studies can also examine which kinds of boundary objects are especially likely to produce matter battles, including whether flexibility, reversibility, or optionality reduce conflict. The paper further calls for work on interventions, such as practices that reveal or integrate cognitive representations before ambiguity is transformed into materiality.
