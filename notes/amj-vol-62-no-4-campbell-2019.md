# Born to Take Risk? The Effect of CEO Birth Order on Strategic Risk Taking

**Abstract**
The importance of birth order has been the subject of debate for centuries, and has captured the attention of the general public and researchers alike. Despite this interest, scholars have little understanding of the impact birth order has on CEOs and their strategic decisions. With this in mind, we develop theory that explains how CEO birth order may be associated with strategic risk taking. Drawing from evolutionary theory arguments related to birth order, we theorize that CEO birth order is positively associated with strategic risk taking; that is, earlier-born CEOs will take less risk than later-born CEOs. As evolutionary theory proposes that birth order effects are driven by sibling rivalry, we also argue that this relationship is moderated by three factors related to sibling rivalry: age gap between a CEO and the closest born sibling, CEO age, and the presence of a sibling CEO. Our results provide support for our theorizing and suggest that birth order may have important implications for organizations. We believe this study helps advance strategic management research, the broader multidisciplinary “family science” literature, and the much-needed cross-pollination of ideas between the two.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether CEO birth order is associated with strategic risk taking, and whether this relationship is strengthened or weakened by conditions tied to sibling rivalry. It specifically examines whether closest-sibling age gap, CEO age, and the presence of a sibling CEO condition the birth order-risk taking relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO birth order, coded as the order in which CEOs were born to their parents.
- DV(s): Strategic risk taking, measured as the logged aggregate of capital expenditures, R&D spending, and acquisitions.
- Mediators: No direct mediator is tested; sibling rivalry is the proposed theoretical mechanism.
- Moderators: Closest sibling age gap, CEO age, and presence of a sibling CEO.

The theorized mechanism is that birth order shapes childhood sibling rivalry and niche differentiation, which in turn forms durable risk-taking tendencies that later carry into CEO strategic decision making. Later-born CEOs are expected to take more strategic risk because later-born siblings historically faced stronger incentives to use riskier behavior to attract parental investment. The results support the main positive association, show stronger effects when sibling age gaps are smaller and when a sibling is also a CEO, and do not support CEO age as a weakening moderator.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends upper echelons research by shifting attention from work-related executive experiences to early-life family-domain experiences. It contributes to family science and birth order research by theorizing boundary conditions around sibling rivalry, and it advances strategic risk-taking research by identifying CEO birth order as an observable ex ante correlate of strategic risk preferences.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that CEO birth order may be one observable input when organizations think about CEO strategic risk-taking tendencies. In family business groups, the presence of sibling CEOs may make sibling rivalry especially salient, so early-life family-domain experience can be relevant when interpreting executives' risk preferences.

**Limitations**
The authors note endogeneity and sample-selection concerns, although they use a Heckman two-stage correction and lagged predictors to mitigate them. The sibling rivalry mechanism is not directly measured, the primary sample is male CEOs in South Korean Chaebols, and the U.S. supplemental sample is small and constrained by historical census data. They also could not control for other executives' birth order or CEO personality traits such as the Big Five.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether CEO birth order influences other strategic decisions and could test more directly how family experiences shape executive psychological properties such as risk-taking propensity. The authors also call for studies in other countries, larger datasets using sources such as census and Ancestry.com records, samples with enough female CEOs to test gender differences, and designs that can control for other top-management-team and personality factors.
