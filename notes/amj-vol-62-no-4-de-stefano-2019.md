# Does Losing Temporary Workers Matter? The Effects of Planned Turnover on Replacements and Unit Performance

**Abstract**
This study examines the performance consequences of planned turnover. In particular, we investigate the departure of temporary workers due to the expiration of their contracts. We reconcile the contradictory predictions of collective turnover research—that any type of worker’s exit impairs organizational performance—and of contingent work research—that temporary workers’ exits improve organizational performance because they provide flexibility. We argue that the planned turnover of temporary workers has an inverted U-shaped relationship with unit performance because it combines flexibility benefits and disruption costs. We also argue that the costs of planned temporary worker turnover are moderated by the proportion and the firm-specific experience of their replacements. We test these arguments using longitudinal monthly data from a leading multinational company in the food and beverage industry, and find support for our hypotheses. The study suggests that managers hiring temporary workers should consider the cost of losing them and challenges the widespread scholarly and managerial assumption that temporary workers are disposable resources.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether the planned departure of temporary workers due to contract expiration improves unit performance through numerical flexibility or impairs performance through operational disruption. It also asks how the proportion of replacements and replacements' firm-specific experience shape that planned turnover-performance relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Planned temporary worker turnover, measured as temporary workers leaving because of contract expiration divided by average temporary workers in the store in the prior month.
- DV(s): Unit financial performance, measured as the logarithm of store net controllable profit.
- Mediators: Not directly measured; the proposed mechanisms are flexibility benefits from adjusting workforce size and disruption costs from lost firm-specific human capital, changed routines, and training demands.
- Moderators: Proportion of replacements among temporary workers; experienced replacements; novice replacements.

Planned temporary worker turnover can initially improve performance by reducing slack and labor costs when units adjust staffing to demand. Beyond an optimal level, exits deplete collective human capital and disrupt routines, so the performance effect turns negative. Replacements can restore productive capacity at low levels of planned turnover, but high replacement intensity, especially with novice replacements, increases disruption when turnover is already high.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconciles collective turnover research and contingent work research by showing that planned temporary worker turnover is neither uniformly harmful nor costless. It develops and tests an inverted U-shaped account in which numerical flexibility benefits coexist with operational disruption costs. It also extends context-emergent turnover and capacity theory by distinguishing the quantity of replacements from their firm-specific human capital.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat temporary workers as resources that can accumulate firm-specific human capital rather than as disposable plug-in labor. The paper suggests that staffing flexibility has hidden costs when high planned turnover forces remaining workers to absorb departures and train replacements. Managers can reduce disruption by avoiding excessive waves of novice replacements after high temporary-worker turnover.

**Limitations**
The evidence comes from one company in Italy, a context with high employment protection for permanent workers and unusually low non-temporary turnover. The jobs studied are low-skill food and beverage service roles, so generalizability to more complex knowledge work remains uncertain. The archival design does not directly measure operational disruption or remaining workers' reactions, and the study is not experimental.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine settings with less employment protection, more simultaneous forms of turnover, and different uses of temporary work. Studies could test whether unit-level HR practices, climate, and managerial discretion moderate the effects of planned temporary worker turnover. Additional work could directly measure disruption, overwork, training burdens, and replacement learning after specific turnover events.
