# Bringing the Firms (and Forms) Back In: Organizational Form Identity and Individual Turnover

**Abstract**
Ideas about the role of the organization in models of voluntary turnover remain scant. We posit that the social audience in general, and people working in organizations in particular, sort organizations into categories (“organizational forms”) and rely on knowledge of the taken-for-granted external identities of firms in these categories to interpret their experience as employees. Although many of these interpretations revolve around size, categorical distinctions between “small” and “large” firms pack concepts and ideas that extend significantly beyond a simple distinction based on size, and would be better understood as an organizational form distinction between specialist and generalist firms. Focusing on the organizational form of a specialist, we claim that two features of its form identity work against each other to influence turnover: employees of specialists realize that it is hard to develop careers internally (a push factor) but value the more relaxed environment, meaningful work, and flexibility of their jobs (a pull effect). We propose a theory to parse out the two effects, find empirical support for it in analyses of managerial turnover in the Korean TV advertising industry, and conclude that the construct of organizational form offers an opportunity to extend models of career mobility and individual turnover.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how organizational form identity, especially the distinction between specialist and generalist firms, shapes individual voluntary turnover beyond the structural effect of firm size. It examines whether specialist employment increases turnover through perceived weak internal career prospects while also reducing turnover for employees whose experience, rank, or educational prestige makes specialist work more appealing.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Specialist organizational form identity; promotion waiting time; employee industry experience; employee rank; educational prestige.
- DV(s): Individual voluntary turnover, defined as external mobility from one advertising agency to another within the Korean TV advertising industry.
- Mediators: Not modeled as formal mediators; the theorized process runs through employees' interpretations of specialist firms as offering limited internal career progression but greater flexibility, meaningful work, and intrinsic appeal.
- Moderators: Promotion clock, high industry tenure, high rank, and educational prestige moderate the specialist employment effect.

The mechanism is cross-level: employees interpret their own career experience through the social identity attached to the employing organizational form. Specialist firms create a push toward turnover because employees expect weaker internal labor markets and slower advancement; long promotion waiting times intensify that push. At the same time, specialists have identity-based appeal because they are viewed as flexible, less bureaucratic, and more meaningful workplaces, so experienced, high-ranking, or high-status employees are less likely to leave specialists than employees without those career buffers.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to turnover research by bringing organizational form identity into models that usually emphasize individual-level psychological, demographic, or career variables. It extends resource partitioning and organizational form theory by showing how macro-level distinctions between specialist and generalist firms shape individual career mobility. It also clarifies why firm size alone is theoretically underspecified: size becomes consequential partly because it is encoded in categorical identities that employees use to interpret work and career prospects.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that retention practices should be tailored by organizational form. Specialist firms may retain employees more effectively by clarifying promotion schedules while emphasizing autonomy, creativity, purpose, flexibility, and intrinsic rewards. They may also gain more from disproportionate investment in higher-echelon, experienced, or high-status employees, who are most likely to fit specialist employment.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that the single-industry Korean advertising context limits generalizability, even though similar patterns may occur in professional service industries such as investment banking or management consulting. They also note that the data lack fine-grained information on formal and informal organizational structures and employment policies inside each agency. The study lacks gender data, which could meaningfully affect observed career patterns.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether the observed specialist-generalist turnover dynamics generalize to other industries, countries, and cultural settings. The paper also points to work that can directly observe organizational employment policies, internal structures, and individual perceptions of organizational form identities. More broadly, future turnover models should examine how organizational forms shape attachment, commitment, satisfaction, and other psychological mechanisms usually studied at the individual level.
