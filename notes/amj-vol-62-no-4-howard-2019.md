# The Spawning of Ecosystems: How Cohort Effects Benefit New Ventures

**Abstract**
Research has examined the process of “entrepreneurial migration,” whereby employees from successful industry incumbents move to new ventures. This phenomenon has been linked to direct benefits to entrepreneurial firms, offering valuable knowledge and routines obtained by employees during their tenure at incumbent firms. We propose a theoretical framework in which shared experience—a common background in beneficial knowledge management practices—creates a “cohort effect,” facilitating direct ties and innovation benefits to new ventures receiving inventors from the same incumbent firms. Analyzing longitudinal data on inventor migration to 658 new biotech ventures tracked from 1990 to 2013, we find shared migration ties increase knowledge and market overlap between firms, enhancing their likelihood of direct engagement through alliances and employee hiring as well as the quality of knowledge they develop. Our core theoretical contribution is the identification of a migration cohort effect, in which inventors who share contemporaneous experiences at incumbent firms and migrate to new ventures create unique relationships between those ventures. Where prior research on spawning and migration has focused primarily on the direct benefit of human capital transfer from incumbents to new ventures, we explore the broader network implications of similarities and interactions between firms receiving this human capital.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how the spawning of knowledge from incumbent firms, represented by inventors moving from incumbents to new ventures, creates a cohort-based ecosystem of firms that can share knowledge, engage in direct exchange, and create higher-quality knowledge. It focuses on whether shared migration cohort ties shape knowledge overlap, market overlap, alliances, inventor hiring, and knowledge quality among new biotech ventures.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Shared ecosystem migration ties between new ventures; number of shared ecosystem migration ties in firm-year knowledge-quality models.
- DV(s): Knowledge overlap, market overlap, alliances, inventor migration, and knowledge quality.
- Mediators: Not modeled as formal mediators; the theorized process runs through shared knowledge management practices, common frames of reference, broader knowledge search, and direct exchange through alliances and inventor movement.
- Moderators: Employee incumbent collaboration, hypothesized to weaken the shared-tie effect on knowledge quality; team migration from incumbent, hypothesized to strengthen it but not supported.

The mechanism is that inventors who share contemporaneous experience at the same incumbent carry similar knowledge management practices, technological understandings, and social connections into different new ventures. These shared migration cohort ties make ventures more likely to recognize similar technologies and markets, form alliances, and hire from one another. Cohort ecosystem membership also broadens a new venture's knowledge search by making other ecosystem firms' knowledge developments more observable and usable. However, prior direct collaboration among the migrating inventors can reduce novelty because firms may be building from overly similar prior knowledge.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes the concept of a migration cohort effect: inventors with shared contemporaneous incumbent experience create unique relationships between the new ventures they join. It extends employee migration and organizational spawning research beyond dyadic parent-to-progeny knowledge transfer by showing that multiple progeny firms can form an intermediate ecosystem structure within an industry. It also contributes to innovation research by linking migration-based affiliations to knowledge overlap, market overlap, direct exchange, and knowledge quality.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that entrepreneurial ventures may benefit not only from hiring knowledge workers from successful incumbents, but also from understanding the broader cohort ecosystem created by those workers' shared histories. For managers in technology-intensive fields, migration cohort ties can identify likely alliance partners, talent pools, and firms pursuing related knowledge trajectories. For policymakers, the results suggest that regulations governing hiring practices and labor mobility can affect interorganizational knowledge flows and innovation dynamics, not just individual employment moves.

**Limitations**
The authors note that founders and migrating knowledge workers may leave incumbents precisely to pursue different technologies, products, or markets, which could limit or negate shared incumbent imprinting. They also acknowledge that larger incumbents may expose employees to only partly overlapping knowledge management practices, and that cohort ecosystem effects may diminish as ventures grow, diversify, hire new personnel, change alliance portfolios, or move into other technological areas. The robustness tests address several alternatives, but the instrumental-variable analysis could not fully rule out endogeneity for direct inventor migration.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how migration cohort ecosystems affect the evolution and pace of technological advancement across industries, including whether strong early incumbents eventually create societal benefits or stifle field-level development. Scholars could study technological complexity, modularity, coordinated action among ecosystem firms, comparisons across parent-firm ecosystems, and parent-level conditions that make some incumbents better spawners of successful generational spin-offs. The authors also call for work on additional outcomes such as survival, growth, overall performance, product launches, successful IPOs, firm characteristics, capabilities, and power imbalances between firms.
