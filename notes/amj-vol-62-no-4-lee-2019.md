# A Functional Model of Workplace Envy and Job Performance: When do Employees Capitalize on Envy by Learning from Envied Targets?

**Abstract**
We integrate the social functional view of emotions with recent developments in workplace envy research to develop and test a novel theoretical framework showing that envious employees can use their envy to promote self-enhancing actions rather than other-diminishing behaviors. We theorize that enviers’ core self-evaluations and friendship ties will attenuate the extent to which enviers undermine envied targets and promote the extent to which enviers actively learn from their envied targets through observational learning and advice seeking. Using two data sets from round-robin surveys of employees in the cosmetic (Study 1) and the financial (Study 2) industries, we show that, while enviers undermine envied targets, they also capitalize on their envy by seeking advice from the envied targets, and enviers who have higher core self-evaluations and friendship with the envied targets are more likely to seek advice from those targets; higher core self-evaluation also decreases the likelihood of undermining the envied targets. We found limited support for the role of envy in triggering enviers’ observational learning. Furthermore, enviers who learn from envied targets perform better and advice seeking is more influential for enviers’ performance than observational learning.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how and when workplace envy can improve job performance rather than only producing destructive behavior. It specifically examines whether envious employees learn from envied targets through observational learning and advice seeking, and whether core self-evaluations and workplace friendship make constructive responses more likely than social undermining.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): A's envy of B.
- DV(s): A's social undermining of B, A's observational learning from B, A's advice seeking from B, and individual job performance.
- Mediators: Learning from envied targets, operationalized as observational learning and advice seeking, is theorized as the route from envy to job performance.
- Moderators: Core self-evaluations and friendship between the envier and the envied target.

The mechanism is that envy signals an unfavorable status comparison and creates pressure to reduce the gap between the envier and the envied target. Employees may respond destructively by undermining the target, but they may also use the target as a source of performance-relevant information. Higher core self-evaluations and friendship make it more likely that envy is handled through advice seeking, and friendship also helps in Study 2 for observational learning. Advice seeking is the more robust learning channel and remains a stronger predictor of performance when observational learning is considered at the same time.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a functional model of workplace envy that treats envy as capable of both destructive and constructive transmutation. It extends workplace envy research by identifying peer-based learning, especially advice seeking from the envied target, as a mechanism through which envy can improve performance. It also refines social functional emotion theory by showing that personal and relational resources shape whether envy serves competitive and coordinating functions or turns into social undermining.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should not treat envy only as a toxic emotion to suppress. Managers can help employees convert upward comparisons into development by encouraging learning from high performers and by using appraisal conversations to identify relative weaknesses constructively. The findings also imply that building positive self-regard and workplace friendships can make advice seeking from envied colleagues more likely.

**Limitations**
The authors did not test whether learning and performance benefits eventually reduce envy, so the feedback loop from constructive action back to emotional experience remains unclear. Several non-performance constructs were self-reported, although the authors used temporal separation, supervisor ratings, archival sales data, and marker-variable checks to reduce common-method concerns. Study 1 also relied on single-item roster measures, and both studies were conducted in South Korea, which may limit generalizability to more individualistic cultural settings.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether successful learning from envied targets reduces envy over longer repeated episodes, or whether persistent comparison gaps shift envy toward admiration or other targets. Scholars could also test curvilinear envy effects, since very high envy may stop increasing constructive advice seeking or undermining. Further studies should examine whether the model generalizes across cultures, especially individualistic cultural settings.
