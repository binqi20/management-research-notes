# Career Resourcing and the Process of Professional Emergence

**Abstract**
We theorize a career resourcing process that explains how individuals can create a new profession. Using historical archives, we trace the emergence of health services research as a new research profession through the career actions of early practitioners. We find that career resourcing can lead to the institutionalization of a new profession by (a) a process of accretion, wherein people pursuing fulfilling careers generate resources that contribute to institutionalization, or (b) institutional work to deliberately build the professional community and infrastructure. We contribute to research on institutional change by specifying career actions that can lead to the institutionalization of a new profession, and by developing theory that accounts for the motivations and the means of individuals to act in ways that result in the institutionalization of a new profession.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what process links individuals' career actions to the emergence of a new profession. Empirically, it examines how early health services researchers' job moves, research projects, mentoring, funding work, and institution-building actions helped create and institutionalize health services research.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / process elements: Career resourcing, career actions, assets, resources, personal career schemas, profession-building schemas, accretion, institutional work, professional community, professional infrastructure, and professional emergence.
- Career actions: Individuals moved into jobs, produced knowledge, mentored students and colleagues, obtained funding, created research centers, formed professional associations, and built tools or programs that later supported further career and profession-building actions.
- Assets / resources: Career actions generated stature, knowledge, material support, and community ties; these assets became resources when actors used them to enable subsequent actions for themselves and others.
- Schemas: Personal career schemas motivated actors to pursue interesting, fulfilling, or impactful work, while profession-building schemas oriented some later actions toward deliberately creating HSR infrastructure and community.
- Emergence pathways: Professional emergence occurred through accretion across individuals and generations, and through deliberate institutional work that built the professional community and infrastructure.

The process is recursive and temporal. Career actions generated assets that actors later converted into resources, and those resources enabled further career actions, including actions by other people. Over time, dispersed career-motivated actions accumulated into a professional community, body of knowledge, and infrastructure; in some cases, those accumulated resources also made deliberate profession-building institutional work possible.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional change and professional emergence theory by explaining a bottom-up process through which individual careers can shape institutions. It extends institutional work theory by showing that institutional consequences can arise not only from purposive institution-building but also from dispersed, personally motivated career actions that accumulate over time. It also brings resourcing theory into a multilevel institutional setting, showing how assets become resources across careers, generations, and professional fields.

**Practical Implication**
The study implies that new professions can be built by supporting career pathways, research infrastructure, mentoring relationships, funding opportunities, and jobs that allow dispersed actors to generate and reuse resources. For actors trying to develop an emerging profession, the findings suggest that personal career moves and institution-building actions are not separate domains: career development can gradually create the stature, knowledge, material support, and community needed for professional infrastructure.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the retrospective interviews at the heart of the data create limitations because respondents may imperfectly recall their career actions and may present themselves as more heroic or coherent than they were at the time.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on how career resourcing interacts with other professional-emergence mechanisms, including ethos, identity, discourse, work practices, and institutional work. It also suggests examining the coevolution of new professions, careers, jobs, and work in organizations, and comparing career resourcing processes across existing professions, different institutional events, and complex multilevel systems.
