# Social Identification in Multiteam Systems: The Role of Depletion and Task Complexity

**Abstract**
Organizations construct multiteam systems to address complex challenges that require the joint efforts of multiple teams. Taking an uncertainty perspective and integrating social identity theory with depletion research, we theoretically and empirically examine the role of social identification in multiteam system performance. In contrast to general assumptions in the literature regarding the need to develop identity at the highest level of a system, we argue that within a multiteam system, identification with that system negatively relates to multiteam system performance, whereas identification with the component team positively relates to multiteam system performance. Our uncertainty perspective suggests that identification with the multiteam system introduces uncertainty regarding the appropriate norms and interdependencies in the system, which leads to more depletion, and consequently lower system performance. Conversely, identification with the component team offers less uncertainty, resulting in less depletion and higher multiteam system performance. Thus, our integrated theoretical framework suggests that depletion mediates the negative effects of multiteam system identification and the positive effects of component team identification on multiteam system performance. Moreover, consistent with our uncertainty perspective, the indirect effect of identification on multiteam system performance via depletion is stronger when task complexity is high and weaker when task complexity is low.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how identification with a component team versus identification with the larger multiteam system affects multiteam system performance. It further asks whether depletion mediates those effects and whether task complexity strengthens the indirect relationships.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Multiteam system identification and component team identification.
- DV(s): Multiteam system performance, measured as standardized points earned in the 10-round Leadership Development Exercise simulation.
- Mediators: Post-session depletion, controlling for pre-session depletion.
- Moderators: Task complexity, manipulated through low-complexity and high-complexity simulation grids.

The proposed mechanism is that multiteam system identification directs members toward an abstract higher-order identity that increases uncertainty about norms, interdependencies, and appropriate action, thereby depleting cognitive resources and reducing performance. Component team identification instead gives members a more concrete identity prototype tied to specialized team tasks, reducing uncertainty and depletion. Task complexity intensifies both pathways because complex multiteam-system tasks make information cues, interdependence, and environmental change harder to manage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the assumption that higher-order system identification should always improve performance in multiteam systems. It extends social identity theory by showing that nested identities can have opposite performance implications when the higher-order target is abstract and uncertainty-inducing. It also links multiteam-system theory with depletion and task-complexity research by specifying depletion as the resource mechanism connecting identification to system performance.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of complex multiteam systems should not assume that cultivating identification with the largest collective is always beneficial. When task complexity is high, the paper suggests that performance may improve when members identify strongly with their component teams and trust leadership teams to coordinate the broader system. Leaders should reduce uncertainty by clarifying component-team roles, interdependencies, and the system-level coordination picture.

**Limitations**
The study uses undergraduate students in a laboratory simulation, so generalizability to experienced employees and field multiteam systems remains uncertain. The level-1 sample of 66 performance episodes is relatively small, although the authors note that this is sizable for multiteam-system research and use a within-subject design to improve precision. The laboratory task offers psychological realism, but the authors note that how its goal hierarchy maps to real-world goal hierarchies is debatable.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether the findings generalize to field settings, older and more experienced working adults, larger samples, and multiteam systems with different levels of task complexity. Additional studies could examine how different component-team roles, especially boundary-spanning leadership teams, alter the costs and benefits of social identification. The paper also points to future work on how multiple identities combine, conflict, overlap, or shift over time in multiteam systems.
