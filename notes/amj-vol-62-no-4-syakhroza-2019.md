# Holier than Thou? Identity Buffers and Adoption of Controversial Practices in the Islamic Banking Category

**Abstract**
Existing scholarship on categories has frequently highlighted how some category members violate codes that others diligently abide by. In this paper, we consider differences in identity across category members, and ask how these relative differences determine their response to a code-violating change. Taking a case where category members are identified as “insiders” and “outsiders,” we argue that insiders’ reaction to a code violation depends upon the extent to which they believe their identity is distinct from the code violator’s, who might be an insider or an outsider. We suggest that it is the presence or absence of an “identity buffer”—i.e., a relative identity advantage—that determines insiders’ reaction. We hypothesize that when a fellow category insider introduces a code violation, the focal insider will be more likely to refrain from the practice. When an outsider introduces the violation, insiders will be more likely to adopt the violation provided they can retain an identity buffer. We posit that when outsiders adopt code-preserving behavior, thus narrowing the identity buffer between insiders and outsiders, it will mitigate insiders’ likelihood of code violation adoption. We find support for our hypotheses using data on the Islamic banking industry in 12 countries (2003–2014).

**Research Question**
The paper asks how the relative difference between a code violator's identity and an insider's identity affects the focal insider's decision to adopt a new code-violating practice. Empirically, it asks when full-fledged Islamic banks adopt derivatives, and whether that likelihood differs when derivatives are first adopted by peer insiders versus CB-owned outsider banks.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Peer insider derivative use; outsider derivative use.
- DV(s): Focal full-fledged Islamic bank derivative use.
- Mediators: Not modeled as formal mediators.
- Moderators: Outsider zakat payment as outsider code-preserving behavior that narrows the identity buffer.

The mechanism is an identity-buffer process. When a peer insider violates a code, other insiders avoid imitation because endorsing a fellow insider's deviance threatens the collective insider identity. When an outsider violates a code, insiders can adopt the profitable controversial practice while retaining their relative identity advantage over outsiders. When outsiders simultaneously preserve another salient code through zakat payment, that advantage narrows, so insiders become less willing to follow the outsider's code violation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to organizational category research by showing that insiders' responses to code violation depend on relative identity differences between the violator and potential adopter, not simply on whether a practice violates a category code. It develops the concept of an identity buffer to explain why outsider-initiated violations can diffuse to insiders while insider-initiated violations are resisted. It also extends social identity theory by showing that in-group loyalty can make insiders stricter toward in-group deviants rather than uniformly favoring them.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations in identity-sensitive categories, controversial practice adoption depends on who introduces the practice and whether identity differences remain visible to audiences. Incumbents may have more room to copy outsider practices when their insider identity remains distinct, but that room narrows when outsiders credibly display code-preserving behavior. Outsider entrants can therefore shape insider behavior not only by violating codes but also by selectively preserving other salient codes.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their measures of code violation and code preservation are binary, even though identity and code adoption are multidimensional. They were also unable to fully examine how outsiders' social position in their original conventional banking category shaped their entry into Islamic banking. A further boundary condition is that derivatives are profitable financial products, so the findings may differ for code violations whose economic payoff is neutral or uncertain.

**Future Research**
Future work could measure gradations in code violation and preservation, including how a firm's grade of category membership affects the type and extent of code violation it can undertake. Researchers could also examine how outsiders' prior-category identities shape whether they enter a new category as code violators, code preservers, or both. Another avenue is to study code violations with uncertain economic value, such as emerging technological standards, to see whether identity-buffer dynamics operate similarly when adoption is risky rather than clearly profitable.
