# Defending Mao’s Dream: How Politicians’ Ideological Imprinting Affects Firms’ Political Appointment in China

**Abstract**
Prior studies on corporate political strategies have taken an exchange view to examine these strategies’ benefits and costs for firms and politicians, but have paid less attention to how politicians’ political values shape their perceptions of, and willingness to engage in, these exchanges. We investigate how politicians’ imprinted political ideologies affect the likelihood of firms’ political appointments. Examining 760 city mayors across 242 Chinese cities from 2001 to 2013, we find that cities have fewer private firms appointed to local councils if the mayor—the key decision maker for such appointments—is more strongly imprinted with an orthodox communist ideology that opposes capitalism. The intensity and evolution of such an ideological imprint are influenced by contextual factors. The imprint’s strength is shaped by the mayors’ prior exposure to intense ideological experiences, such as experiencing the Cultural Revolution at a young age. Working in an environment consistent with the ideology (e.g., a province with a greater communist legacy) sustains and even strengthens the imprint, whereas working in an environment inconsistent with the ideology (e.g., a city with greater economic development) attenuates it. We discuss the implications of these findings for political strategy research, imprinting theory, and nascent research on political ideology.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how city mayors' imprinted political ideologies affect the likelihood that private firms in their jurisdictions receive political appointments in China. It further examines when that ideological imprint is intensified by prior Cultural Revolution exposure and when it persists or decays depending on communist legacy and economic development in the mayor's working environment.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Mayor's communist ideology, coded by whether the mayor joined the Communist Party before 1978.
- DV(s): Ratio of political appointments held by private firms, measured from executives' deputyship in the PC or PPCC at the city level or above.
- Mediators: Not directly modeled as measured mediators; the theory specifies ideological values, anti-capitalist perceptions, and party identity as the process logic.
- Moderators: Mayor's Cultural Revolution cohort, regional communist legacy, and city GDP.

The mechanism is that joining the Communist Party during the Mao era imprints mayors with orthodox communist ideology and party identity that make private entrepreneurs appear less legitimate as political insiders. Mayors with stronger imprints therefore endorse fewer private-firm executives for PC or PPCC deputyship. The imprint is stronger when mayors experienced the Cultural Revolution at a young age, persists or strengthens in provinces with stronger communist legacies, and attenuates in economically developed cities where market-oriented demands make the old ideology less compatible with current governance tasks.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to corporate political strategy research by shifting attention from firms' demand for political ties to politicians' receptivity to those ties. It shows that politicians' ideological values are antecedents of firms' political participation, not merely background context for instrumental exchange.

It also contributes to individual-level imprinting theory by showing that imprint strength and persistence vary with prior ideological experiences and later imprint-environment fit. The paper extends political ideology research beyond the U.S. liberal-conservative framing by examining Chinese communist ideology and party affiliation as both a value system and an in-group identity.

**Practical Implication**
For firms, political appointment depends not only on their own resources, contribution, or willingness to pursue political ties, but also on the ideological background of local political decision makers. Private firms operating in authoritarian or transitional settings may face different political-access prospects across jurisdictions because officials interpret the legitimacy of business participation differently.

For policymakers and analysts, the findings imply that state-firm interactions should be evaluated through the attributes of state agents, not only through formal institutions or aggregate state interests. The same political appointment channel can carry different consequences depending on the decision maker's ideological imprint.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the study remains theoretically silent on firm-level dynamics, even though supplementary analyses suggest firms benefit less from appointments when mayors have strong communist ideology. They also note that the paper does not theorize interaction patterns between mayors and party secretaries in China's dual power structure.

The study's China setting is powerful for examining communist ideological imprinting, but the authors caution that its institutional arrangements limit generalizability. The communist ideology imprint is most applicable to transitional economies such as those in Eastern Europe, Russia, and Asia.

**Future Research**
Future research could theorize how firms respond strategically to politicians' biased attitudes and how different types of firms are affected by politicians' ideology. Further work could examine power sharing between mayors and party secretaries and how leader experience or discretion changes firms' political participation.

The authors also encourage research on how city-level economic and institutional conditions shape mayor turnover and the antecedents of mayor ideology. Comparative work could examine other political ideologies and how they affect firms' strategies in different institutional contexts.
