# Deviant versus Aspirational Risk Taking: The Effects of Performance Feedback on Bribery Expenditure and R&D Intensity

**Abstract**
Combining the theses of “problemistic search” and “slack search,” past research in the behavioral theory of the firm suggests that both lowand high-performing firms may engage in the same type of risk-taking activity. We counter this view with a consistent, motivation-based logic in the theory: low-performing firms are fixated on finding shortterm solutions to immediate problems, so they have an increased probability of exhibiting deviant risk-taking behavior such as bribery, whereas high-performing firms are concerned about sustaining their competitive advantage in the long run and will more likely engage in aspirational risk taking such as research and development (R&D). Using a sample of 9,633 firm-year observations covering 2,224 listed companies in China, we find that, as a firm’s performance falls further below its aspiration level, it has larger abnormal entertainment spending, an implicit measure of bribery expenditure, but not higher R&D intensity. However, as a firm’s performance rises further above its aspiration level, it has greater R&D intensity, but not more bribery expenses. Legal development and industry competition moderate the relationship between performance feedback and risk-taking behavior.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether low- and high-performing firms differ in their risk-taking behavior when performance feedback places them below or above aspiration levels. It examines whether negative feedback motivates deviant risk taking through bribery expenditure while positive feedback motivates aspirational risk taking through R&D intensity, and whether legal development and industry competition condition those relationships.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Performance below and above social aspiration; performance below and above historical aspiration.
- DV(s): Bribery expenditure, measured through abnormal entertainment and travel expenses; R&D intensity, measured as industry-adjusted R&D expenses scaled by sales.
- Mediators: Not directly modeled as measured mediators; the theory specifies motivation-based problemistic search and aspirational search as the process logic.
- Moderators: Provincial legal development and industry competition.

The mechanism is that performance below aspirations creates pressure for quick remedies, making low-performing firms more willing to use myopic and deviant actions such as bribery. Performance above aspirations reduces immediate strain and directs firms toward future-oriented capability building, making high-performing firms more likely to invest in R&D. Strong legal development weakens the bribery path and strengthens the R&D path, while high competition intensifies both the bribery response among low performers and the R&D response among high performers.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the behavioral theory of the firm by using a consistent motivation-based logic for both below- and above-aspiration performance rather than combining problemistic search for poor performance with slack-capacity logic for strong performance. It distinguishes deviant risk taking from aspirational risk taking and shows that firms at different points relative to aspirations are not simply more or less risk seeking, but tend toward different types of risky action. It also extends BTOF by theorizing legal development and industry competition as environmental boundary conditions.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that regulators and investors should watch low-performing firms more closely for bribery risk, especially where legal development is weak or industry competition is high. They also suggest that strong legal institutions can both deter corruption among troubled firms and encourage R&D among high-performing firms. Competition is more ambiguous as a policy lever because it may stimulate innovation among strong performers while also increasing bribery pressure among weak performers.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the bribery expenditure measure is a proxy based on abnormal entertainment and travel expenses, and that it cannot perfectly separate bribes from conventional lobbying or relationship-building expenses. They also state that the study cannot concurrently examine morality or ethics as a moderator of deviant risk taking. The China setting provides useful institutional variation, but the authors caution that bribery, guanxi, and gift-giving may operate differently from developed-economy contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research should develop more objective and accurate measures of bribery. It should examine moral and ethical factors as additional boundary conditions for the relationship between performance feedback and bribery. The authors also call for studies across wider national contexts, including both developed and emerging economies, to test whether the same contrasting risk-taking patterns generalize beyond China.
