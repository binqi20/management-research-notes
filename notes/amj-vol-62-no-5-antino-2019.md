# Structuring Reality Through the Faultlines Lens: The Effects of Structure, Fairness, and Status Conflict on the Activated Faultlines–Performance Relationship

**Abstract**
We investigate how activated team faultlines represent an informal sensemaking structure through which teammates interpret their social reality. Constructed from intersubgroup comparisons, activated team faultlines likely result in ambiguous or illegitimate status perceptions. Thus, activated faultlines threaten team justice climate, which drives status conflict, impairing team performance. We explore the effects of team structure clarity in providing certainty or legitimacy around status and structure, ameliorating the negative effect of activated faultlines on team justice climate. We test our model using a multi-source (three sources), multi-wave cross-lagged design (four waves) on a sample of 271 employees and 41 leaders in 41 teams. We find that the negative relationship between activated faultlines and team performance is mediated by the team justice climate–status conflict causal chain. We also find that team structure clarity reduces activated faultlines’ negative effect on team justice climate. Results highlight the value of using team faultlines, the social identity approach, and justice theories to understand how diverse teams interpret their social reality, which influences their performance. Furthermore, our research provides practical guidance to managers in building clear structures that minimize harmful effects of activated faultlines on justice perceptions and team performance.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how activated team faultlines affect team performance when team members use faultline-based subgroups to interpret their social reality. It specifically examines whether activated faultlines reduce team performance through lower team justice climate and higher status conflict, and whether team structure clarity weakens this process.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Activated faultlines.
- DV(s): Team performance.
- Mediators: Team justice climate and status conflict.
- Moderators: Team structure clarity moderates the activated faultlines-team justice climate-status conflict relationship.

The argument is that activated faultlines operate as an informal sensemaking structure that makes intersubgroup status relations ambiguous or illegitimate. Those perceptions reduce team justice climate, which increases status conflict as subgroups defend or elevate perceived status. Status conflict then draws attention away from common team tasks and impairs team performance. Clear team structures provide an alternative, legitimating structure around roles, procedures, and priorities, reducing the negative effect of activated faultlines on team justice climate.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconceptualizes activated faultlines as a sensemaking structure rather than only as a more refined diversity measure. It extends faultlines research by identifying team justice climate and status conflict as mechanisms linking activated faultlines to performance, and by showing how team structure clarity can attenuate that pathway. It also contributes a measurement approach that combines dormant faultline strength with perceived faultline activation.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not treat all possible demographic splits as equally threatening; the study suggests diagnosing whether team members actually perceive dormant faultlines as activated. For teams with activated faultlines, managers can reduce ambiguity and perceived illegitimacy by clarifying roles, procedures, priorities, and authority relations. The paper also suggests that leaders should attend to status expectations in diverse teams and use structure to support fairness perceptions before status conflict undermines performance.

**Limitations**
The authors note that most measures were perceptual, except demographic information used in the activated faultline measure. The field design did not manipulate independent variables, so the cross-lagged and multisource design does not establish causality. The results come from a specific organizational and cultural context, a Spanish health care organization, which limits generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future research could use nonobtrusive process measures and objective performance indicators to test activated faultlines and status conflict. The authors call for research designs that can test causal relationships and for replication across cultures. They also suggest longitudinal work on how activated faultlines change over time, how different diversity attributes become weighted, whether activated faultlines affect psychological safety, and whether leadership styles provide the clarity that helps manage faultline-based teams.
