# The Ideator’s Bias: How Identity-Induced Self-Efficacy Drives Overestimation in Employee-Driven Process Innovation

**Abstract**
Grasping the true value of ideas is essential for corporate innovation success. When it comes to forecasting the value of one’s own innovation ideas, however, people may err systematically. In this paper, we shed light on this ideator’s bias, and examine when and why certain ideas are more prone to biased evaluations. Specifically, we argue that biased idea evaluations depend on the self-efficacy that ideators may derive from their specific role and social identity in the firm when generating a specific idea. We test our theoretical predictions of such a situation-specific perspective on overconfidence by using a corporate dataset on process innovations and their valuations. Furthermore, we triangulate our predicted mechanism and rule out alternative explanations through a series of additional interviews and four scenario-based experiments. Consistent with our situation-specific, identity-based account, we find that ideas from employees at a higher (vs. lower) organizational level, and from employees generating ideas in groups (vs. individually) are more prone to an ideator’s bias. In doing so, our study helps explain when the ideator’s bias prevails in a real-life organizational context, raises caution about some current proxies to identify high-potential ideas, and provides fresh insights to established theories around overconfidence and self-efficacy.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and why employees overestimate the economic value of their own process innovation ideas. It focuses on whether organizational position and collaborative ideation activate role and social identities that increase self-efficacy and, in turn, intensify the ideator’s bias.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Organizational position of the ideator and collaborative versus individual ideation.
- DV(s): Ideator’s bias, measured as the relative difference between the ideator’s initial value forecast and accounting-verified idea value.
- Mediators: Self-efficacy is theorized as the mechanism and is tested in the scenario experiments.
- Moderators: Group membership is theorized to moderate the organizational-position effect; the paper predicts the lowest overvaluation for noncollaborating lower-level employees.

The proposed mechanism is that salient role identities and social identities shape situation-specific self-efficacy. Higher-level employees draw more optimistic self-efficacy from managerial role identity, while lower-level employees gain additional collective self-efficacy when ideating in groups. The field data support higher overvaluation among higher-level and collaborative ideators, and the interaction shows that collaboration mainly raises overvaluation among lower-level employees rather than adding to already elevated higher-level self-efficacy. The interviews and experiments triangulate the identity-induced self-efficacy account and help rule out alternative explanations such as power, endowment effects, groupthink, and shared mental models.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes an organizationally embedded, situational micro-account of ideator’s bias based on identity-induced self-efficacy, moving beyond trait-like accounts of overconfidence. It also cautions innovation research against treating team-generated ideas as inherently higher potential without accounting for team members’ overvaluation. Finally, it links self-efficacy and overconfidence by showing how personal and collective self-efficacy can become a source of biased idea valuation in organizational settings.

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest separating idea generation from idea evaluation so ideators can gain distance from identities salient during ideation. When ideator input is necessary, managers should apply additional scrutiny to valuations from groups and higher-level employees because those contexts are especially prone to overvaluation. The paper also suggests priming accurate forecasting during evaluation rather than only encouraging inventive confidence.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge the peculiarities of a process-innovation setting where accounting provides standardized evaluations and decision makers rely comparatively little on ideators’ own estimates. They also note that employees in the focal setting are encouraged to generate ideas, so overestimation could operate differently in organizations with greater inertia. Cultural orientation and organizational socialization may also affect whether role identity, group identity, or hybrid identities become salient.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether the findings generalize to non-Western countries, more hierarchical or more egalitarian societies, and organizations with different cultures or onboarding practices. The authors also call for work on when collective self-efficacy contributes to groupthink or escalation of commitment, and on whether identity cues can sometimes evoke moderate self-efficacy that reduces biased forecasts.
