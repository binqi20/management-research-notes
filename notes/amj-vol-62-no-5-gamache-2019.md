# Impression Offsetting as an Early Warning Signal of Low CEO Confidence in Acquisitions

**Abstract**
Researchers have long been interested in understanding the motives behind CEOs’ actions. On the one hand, CEOs may pursue strategic actions because they are confident they will enhance firm value. Alternatively, CEOs may take actions even when they have low confidence in the value of those actions, perhaps driven by self-interest or social pressures. Although research suggests that CEO option exercises following an acquisition announcement are an ex post behavioral outcome of low CEO confidence in the acquisition’s value-creation potential, prior research has not identified any ex ante signals shareholders can look for to assess acquiring CEOs’ confidence when the acquisition is announced. We address this concern by exploring a potential early warning signal of low CEO confidence: impression offsetting. We theorize that impression offsetting—measured as other unrelated positive announcements made by the firm in the days immediately surrounding the acquisition announcement—may serve as an ex ante signal of low CEO confidence in the acquisition’s value-creation potential, and, as such, will be positively associated with CEO option exercises, an ex post behavioral outcome of low confidence. We test our theory with a sample of 491 large acquisitions and find consistent support for our hypotheses.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether investors can gauge a CEO's confidence in an acquisition when it is announced. It also asks why CEOs use anticipatory impression management, theorizing that impression offsetting around acquisition announcements is an ex ante signal that CEOs have low confidence in the acquisition's long-term value-creation potential.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Impression offsetting, measured as the count of material, positive firm-authored press releases in the three-day window centered on the acquisition announcement.
- DV(s): Subsequent CEO stock option exercises, measured as options exercised by the CEO in the quarter following the focal acquisition divided by exercisable options held at the end of the prior year.
- Mediators: No mediator is directly tested; low acquisition-related CEO confidence is inferred through the association between impression offsetting and later option exercises.
- Moderators: CEO age, high firm reputation, and industry dynamism.

The theorized mechanism is that CEOs with low acquisition-related confidence release unrelated positive information around the acquisition announcement to reduce scrutiny and buffer negative market reactions. If the acquisition later appears unlikely to create value, the CEO can protect accumulated equity value by exercising options after the announcement. The positive impression-offsetting-to-option-exercise relationship is expected to be stronger when downside risk is more salient: for older CEOs, CEOs of high-reputation firms, and CEOs operating in more dynamic industries.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to impression management research by identifying low action-specific CEO confidence as a motivation for anticipatory impression offsetting. It extends signaling theory by showing that impression offsetting can function as an unintended signal, rather than only an intentional signal, of executive confidence. It also contributes to behavioral and governance research by offering an observable way to study CEO confidence in strategic actions that may be driven by self-interest or social pressures.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that investors should treat unrelated positive announcements surrounding an acquisition as a warning signal that the CEO may lack confidence in the deal's value-creation potential. When an acquisition is accompanied by impression offsetting, investors should scrutinize the acquisition's value logic rather than interpreting the additional positive announcements in isolation.

**Limitations**
The authors cannot directly observe CEO confidence and rely on subsequent stock option exercises as an established behavioral proxy. The design is observational and cannot fully rule out omitted-variable explanations, although the authors use extensive controls, two-stage residual inclusion Tobit models, and impact-threshold analysis. The sample is limited to large completed acquisitions by public U.S.-based S&P 500 firms with public U.S.-based targets.

**Future Research**
The authors call for future research to examine alternative unobtrusive measures of CEO confidence and compare them with option-exercise signals. They also suggest extending impression offsetting research beyond acquisitions to other strategic and governance actions, and examining additional CEO-level moderators such as narcissism, prevention focus, or construal level.
