# The Goldilocks Effect of Strategic Human Resource Management? Optimizing the Benefits of a High-Performance Work System Through the Dual Alignment of Vertical and Horizontal Fit

**Abstract**
Although vertical and horizontal fit in strategic human resource management are foundational to the links between a high-performance work system (HPWS) and organizational performance, little is known about how these two fits interact to affect organizational performance. We address this shortcoming while also advancing knowledge on each type of fit. We offer a more nuanced examination of vertical fit (which has typically been assessed with respect to organizations’ broad strategic types) by focusing on the alignment of an HPWS with an organization’s market entry timing mode—a key element of strategy. We propose that among organizations pursuing new product development, the effect of an HPWS on organizational performance is most positive under a fast-follower entry timing, followed by a first-mover and finally a fence-sitter entry timing. We then hypothesize that the benefit of vertical fit is magnified when the complementary human resources practices comprising an HPWS are implemented with greater internal consistency (or with similar intensities) across the ability, motivation, and opportunity domains—reflecting a positive interaction between vertical and horizontal fit in predicting the effectiveness of an HPWS. Analyses of four-wave nationally representative panel data yield strong support for our dual-alignment model of SHRM.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how vertical fit and horizontal fit jointly shape the relationship between a high-performance work system and organizational performance. Specifically, it examines whether HPWS effects differ across first-mover, fast-follower, and fence-sitter market entry timing modes, and whether those effects are strengthened when HPWS practices are internally consistent across ability, motivation, and opportunity domains.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Use of a high-performance work system.
- DV(s): Subsequent product sales and subsequent financial performance.
- Mediators: Product sales mediates the conditional indirect effect of HPWS on subsequent financial performance.
- Moderators: Market entry timing mode and HPWS internal consistency.
- Controls: Current product sales or financial performance, establishment size, total assets, capital-to-labor ratio, other entry timing, establishment fixed effects, and year fixed effects.

The mechanism is a dual-alignment argument. Vertical fit occurs when an HPWS supports the knowledge requirements of a firm's market entry timing mode; the authors argue that this fit is strongest for fast followers, weaker for first movers, and weakest for fence sitters. Horizontal fit occurs when the ability-, motivation-, and opportunity-enhancing domains of the HPWS are implemented with similar intensity, reducing bottlenecks in employees' knowledge search, sharing, and application. Product sales then transmits the dual-alignment effect to financial performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to SHRM by theorizing and testing the interplay between vertical and horizontal fit rather than treating them as separate fit concepts. It sharpens vertical-fit theory by focusing on market entry timing as a specific element of strategy instead of broad strategic types. It also contributes to horizontal-fit research by operationalizing internal consistency across AMO domains and showing that consistent implementation conditions when HPWS practices produce stronger performance benefits.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that managers should not treat HPWS investments as universally beneficial. An HPWS is most useful when the knowledge needed for strategy execution is within employees' reach for absorption and leveraging, as in a fast-follower strategy, and less useful when knowledge requirements demand broad external experimentation or are already routine. The paper also implies that managers should balance investments across ability-, motivation-, and opportunity-enhancing HR practices rather than overemphasizing one domain.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the HPWS-entry-timing relationship may depend on contextual factors such as technological and market change that were not directly examined. They also did not observe the intervening knowledge-based mechanisms, leaving absorptive capacity and knowledge behaviors theoretically supported but not directly measured. Additional limitations include single-rater measurement for HPWS and entry timing, many binary HR-practice measures, and the focus on one HR system type.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the dual-alignment argument under different environmental conditions, especially where first-mover advantages vary by technological and market change. The authors call for studies that directly measure knowledge-based mechanisms and organizational flexibility as pathways linking HR systems to entry timing requirements. They also suggest examining more objective HR and strategy measures, richer response scales, and alternative HR systems that may fit other market entry timing modes.
