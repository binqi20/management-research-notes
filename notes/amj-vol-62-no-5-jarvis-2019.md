# Where the Heart Functions Best: Reactive–Affective Conflict and the Disruptive Work of Animal Rights Organizations

**Abstract**
We study the emotive aspect of institutional work performed by U.S. animal rights organizations (AROs) attempting to disrupt industrial practices in modern factory farming operations perceived to be abusive to animals. Drawing on an inductive, qualitative analysis of interviews with ARO advocates, as well as textual and visual archival data collected from AROs’ websites, we argue that the suppression of emotion plays a critical role in AROs’ disruptive work. We find that advocates are motivated to suppress their emotions by a perceived incompatibility between their reactive emotional displays and their affective commitment to institutional work, or what we label reactive–affective conflict. We show how two triggers of reactive–affective conflict—potential supporters’ investment in the status quo and emotive norms governing institutional work—encourage ARO advocates to suppress their emotions in face-to-face interactions with audiences while attempting to elicit emotions via visuals as their strategy of disruptive work. We contribute to the literature on the strategic use of emotion in institutional work by highlighting important relationships between the characteristics of potential supporters, the nature of institutional work, and institutional workers’ management of their own emotions to further their institutional projects. In doing so, we add needed nuance to extant conceptualizations of how emotion is strategically deployed as part of purposeful efforts to create, maintain, and disrupt institutions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what motivates actors to suppress their emotions in support of institutional work, and how strategies of institutional work can incorporate emotional suppression while still eliciting emotion in potential supporters. It studies this problem through the disruptive institutional work of U.S. animal rights organizations seeking to challenge factory farming practices.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Reactive emotions of moral shock, anger, and sorrow; affective commitment to animal wellbeing and AROs' disruptive work; reactive–affective conflict.
- Triggering conditions: Potential supporters' cognitive and emotional investment in the status quo, and emotive norms governing the settings where institutional work is performed.
- Process / tactics: Suppressing emotion to plant seeds in face-to-face advocacy, eliciting emotion through visuals, and suppressing emotion to gather evidence in undercover investigations.
- Emergent outcomes: ARO advocates decouple emotion elicitation from their own emotional display, reducing the risk that potential supporters dismiss them as irrational or fanatical.
- Boundary conditions: The process is developed in a setting where the target practices are highly institutionalized, negative externalities are often hidden, and change agents work from the margins.

The process centers on reactive–affective conflict: advocates' reactive emotional displays may undermine the affective commitment that motivates their disruptive work. In street-side advocacy, suppressing emotion helps advocates appear calm enough to make potential supporters receptive to seeds of information, while visuals carry the emotional force. In undercover investigations, suppressing emotion allows advocates to maintain cover long enough to gather visual evidence of factory farming practices.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional work research by showing that emotion matters not only through workers' emotional displays but also through their strategic suppression of emotion. It theorizes reactive–affective conflict as an experiential mechanism linking workers' reactive emotions, affective commitments, audience characteristics, and emotive norms. It also extends research on visuality in organizations by showing how visuals can elicit emotion while decoupling that elicitation from stigmatized human advocates.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that organizations trying to mobilize support for disruptive institutional projects may need to manage advocates' emotional displays as carefully as their messages. For AROs, the findings suggest that calm interpersonal engagement, emotionally resonant visuals, and disciplined evidence-gathering can be mutually reinforcing tactics when audiences are defensive or heavily invested in the status quo.

**Limitations**
The authors state that their data collection focused on AROs and their advocates, which produces a single-sided view of the moral worth of factory farming operations and their practices. They also note that the study was not designed to test the success or failure of different emotive tactics, and that gender differences in the perception of emotional display were not the primary purpose of the research.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine both sides of institutional battles, including how targets of disruption counter advocates' emotive tactics. Researchers could also study audience reception of emotional displays and visuals, gendered perceptions of emotion in institutional work, and how victims' emotional experiences are marshaled by third parties in work on societal grand challenges.
