# Between Legitimacy and Efficiency: An Institutional Theory of Corporate Giving

**Abstract**
This study explores the cost of legitimacy management in the context of corporate giving. Specifically, how much does an organization spend in order to manage external legitimacy? We view corporate giving as a means by which firms may seek external legitimacy, and propose that, in response to evaluations of external audiences, organizations strategically manage such costs under a tension between legitimacy and efficiency concerns. We examine this argument by developing an institutional theory of corporate giving and testing our hypotheses using the corporate giving behaviors of publicly listed Korean companies from 2003 to 2011. Consistent with our proposition concerning the role of external audiences in shaping legitimacy management costs, we show that strategic decisions regarding how much a firm spends on corporate giving are related to the extent that the firm receives positive or negative media attention. Furthermore, we find that the relationship between media evaluations and corporate giving varies with a firm’s performance, which suggests that its internal efficiency condition can be an important contingency factor in determining its legitimacy management cost. We discuss the implications of these findings for the study of legitimacy management processes and the role of external audiences in such processes.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how much organizations spend to manage external legitimacy in the context of corporate giving. It examines how positive media coverage of giving, negative media coverage of lawsuits against family owners, and firm performance jointly shape the ratio of corporate giving to sales.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Positive media coverage of a firm's corporate giving; negative media coverage of lawsuits against a firm's family owners.
- DV(s): Corporate giving, measured as the ratio of corporate giving to sales in a given year.
- Mediators: No mediator is directly tested.
- Moderators: Firm performance, measured as return on assets.

The mechanism is a tension between social legitimacy and economic efficiency. Positive media attention raises external audience expectations, so firms spend more on corporate giving to maintain legitimacy; this effect is stronger among high-performing firms because they have more room to bear the cost. Negative media attention to lawsuits against family owners creates legitimacy pressure, but it also creates immediate efficiency pressure; firms therefore reduce giving, and the reduction is unexpectedly stronger among high performers, suggesting that they rely more heavily on economic efficiency logic under severe conflict.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends institutional theory by treating legitimacy management cost as a variable rather than an assumed or negligible condition. It argues that conformity to institutional expectations should be analyzed not only through legitimacy benefits and illegitimacy penalties, but also through the costs organizations pay to manage external legitimacy.

It also contributes to research on external audiences by showing that positive and negative media evaluations have different dynamics in corporate giving. Positive media feedback reinforces giving, whereas negative media attention to family-owner lawsuits is associated with less giving, especially under high firm performance.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that managers should treat corporate giving as a recurring legitimacy-management decision under resource constraints, not simply as an ad hoc philanthropic act. Positive media attention can raise future audience expectations, while legal conflicts involving central owners can shift attention and resources toward immediate efficiency concerns.

For external audiences, the study implies that visibility and evaluation matter: media coverage helps shape how much firms spend on corporate giving. For firms in governance contexts where family owners are central to organizational identity, legal disputes involving those owners may materially affect social spending decisions.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that not all institutional conformity is costly, because some organizations may symbolically adopt practices without full implementation. The study's Korean KOSPI context and the prevalence of family-controlled firms also limit generalizability to other countries and unlisted private firms.

The data measure aggregate annual corporate giving and do not observe the portfolio of charitable activities or how spending is distributed across causes and stakeholders. The study also does not jointly observe legitimacy management costs, illegitimacy penalties, and legitimacy benefits in one integrated empirical design.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how legitimacy management cost shapes not only adoption of institutionalized practices but also the scope and degree of implementation. Studies in other countries, markets, and unlisted private firms could test whether the same legitimacy-efficiency dynamics hold outside the Korean listed-firm context.

Future work could also collect portfolio-level corporate giving data and integrate legitimacy management costs, illegitimacy penalties, and legitimacy benefits in the same design. Such research would clarify how organizations decide whether legitimacy management improves performance or survival.
