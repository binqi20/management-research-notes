# The Dark Side of Transformational Leader Behaviors for Leaders Themselves: A Conservation of Resources Perspective

**Abstract**
The vast majority of theory and research to date on transformational leadership has focused on how transformational leader behaviors influence followers, portraying those behaviors as unequivocally beneficial. We pivot from this predominant focus to a focus on the detriments of transformational leader behaviors for leaders themselves. Drawing from conservation of resources theory, we propose that although transformational leader behaviors produce gains for both leaders and followers, those behaviors also trigger losses for leaders themselves. The results of two experience-sampling studies, whereby leaders and their followers completed weekly surveys for six weeks, revealed that transformational leader behaviors were associated with increases in leader emotional exhaustion and subsequent leader turnover intentions, and these detrimental consequences occurred over and above benefits to followers (Study 1) and benefits to leaders themselves (Study 2). Furthermore, the extent to which transformational leader behaviors were associated with increases in emotional exhaustion depended upon attributes of followers, such that leaders experienced greater increases in emotional exhaustion when their transformational leader behaviors were directed toward followers low in conscientiousness (Study 1) and competence (Study 2). Overall, our work provides answers to both why and under what conditions the dark side of transformational leader behaviors is likely to appear.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether transformational leader behaviors have detrimental intrapersonal consequences for leaders even while producing follower and leader benefits. It also asks when those detriments are more likely to emerge, focusing on follower conscientiousness and follower competence as boundary conditions.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Transformational leader behaviors.
- DV(s): Leader turnover intentions.
- Mediators: Leader emotional exhaustion.
- Moderators: Follower conscientiousness in Study 1; follower conscientiousness and follower competence in Study 2.

Drawing on conservation of resources theory, the paper argues that transformational leader behaviors require leaders to expend time, energy, emotion regulation, and attention. In the short term, those resource expenditures increase leader emotional exhaustion, and emotional exhaustion increases leader turnover intentions as leaders seek to protect remaining resources. The boundary-condition evidence differs across studies: follower conscientiousness moderated the indirect effect in Study 1, while follower competence, rather than broad conscientiousness, emerged as the significant moderator in Study 2.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts transformational leadership research from its predominant follower-centered focus to an actor-centric account of consequences for leaders themselves. It uses conservation of resources theory to challenge the assumption that transformational leadership is uniformly positive by showing that the same behaviors can create leader benefits and leader resource losses. It also extends within-person leadership theory by showing that follower characteristics shape the intrapersonal consequences of leader behavior.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest caution in encouraging managers to display transformational leader behaviors consistently without attention to recovery and context. Leaders may need breaks, recovery activities, and more strategic deployment of transformational behaviors when they are depleted or when followers are less likely to benefit from those efforts. Organizations should recognize that behaviors often treated as desirable can still carry resource costs for leaders.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the studies measured focal variables at the same time, so the experience-sampling and lagged controls do not establish causality. Study 1 relied on one follower report, raising concerns about selection bias and representativeness, although Study 2 included multiple followers. The paper also focuses on short-term proximal outcomes, transformational leader behaviors only, and follower characteristics rather than directly measuring whether followers waste leaders' transformational efforts.

**Future Research**
Future research could use designs that better establish causality and test long-term consequences of transformational leader behaviors for burnout, turnover, and career mobility. The authors also call for work on other positive and negative leader behaviors, leader characteristics as moderators, and direct assessment of whether followers actually waste leaders' transformational efforts or whether leaders merely perceive those efforts as wasted.
