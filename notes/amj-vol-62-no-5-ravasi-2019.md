# The Stuff of Legend: History, Memory, and the Temporality of Organizational Identity Construction

**Abstract**
A growing body of research on how organizations engage with their histories has shown that organizational members revisit history in the light of present-day concerns to inspire or legitimize future courses of action. Studies of the processes through which organizational history is brought to bear on the present and future, however, remain rare. To uncover the processes and practices through which organizational members systematically engage with history, we investigate uses of material memory in four corporate museums. Our analysis uncovers three distinct modes of engagement, reflecting different temporal perspectives on organizational identity, involving different cross-temporal interpretative processes, and influencing action in different ways. Our theoretical insights have significant implications not only for understanding the use of history in organizations, but also for research on organizational identity and organizational memory.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how organizational members systematically engage with history and material memory to construct task-relevant understandings of organizational identity. It focuses on how the past is brought to bear on present identity understandings and future-oriented organizational action.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / modes: Identity stewardship, identity evangelizing, heritage mining, material memory, historicized identity understandings, mnemonic communities, and cross-temporal sensemaking.
- Process / phases: Members use corporate museums and archives as material memory, prioritize different identity definitional standards according to task audiences, construct historicized identity understandings, and translate those understandings into action.
- Identity stewardship: Members prioritize enduringness, treat artifacts as identity markers, search for intertemporal patterns, and use the past to maintain legitimacy with core members of the mnemonic community.
- Identity evangelizing: Members prioritize centrality, treat artifacts as organizational relics, abstract essential qualities, and use the past to stimulate identification among new or potential members.
- Heritage mining: Members prioritize distinctiveness, treat artifacts as symbolic resources, recontextualize historical cues, and use the past to enhance the meaning and appeal of new offerings.
- Boundary conditions: The empirical setting consists of four corporate museums with rich historical artifacts and audiences that vary in knowledge of and attachment to the organizations' histories.

The process begins when members face identity-related tasks, such as product innovation, brand communication, HR induction, or merchandising, that require them to explain what distinguishes the organization. Depending on whether their primary audience consists of core, new, or peripheral members of the mnemonic community, they emphasize enduringness, centrality, or distinctiveness. These priorities shape how they interpret artifacts and connect past, present, and future. The result is not a single organizational identity narrative, but situated, task-relevant identity understandings that guide action in different domains.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to organizational identity research by showing how material memory supports the construction of situated, task-relevant identity understandings rather than only broad, shared narratives of "who we are." It specifies three cross-temporal sensemaking processes through which members use the past: searching for intertemporal patterns, holistic abstraction of essential features, and intuitive recontextualization. It also contributes to research on organizational history and memory by theorizing mnemonic communities and historical imperatives as conditions that constrain and enable how organizations use history.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that corporate museums and archives can shape action when members use them as interpretive resources, not simply as repositories of historical information. Organizations with meaningful historical artifacts can support innovation, brand work, training, and communication by helping different role holders connect the past to present tasks in audience-sensitive ways. The study also suggests that managers should recognize tensions between historically precise continuity for core audiences and more flexible symbolic reuse for broader audiences.

**Limitations**
The authors identify mnemonic communities as an important boundary condition and note that the four cases involve organizations with iconic products, celebrated brands, and rich historical artifacts. They also state that the inductive interview protocol was not designed to measure antecedents of engagement modes because these constructs emerged later in the analysis. The transferability of the model to less glamorous organizations, mundane objects, or settings without museum facilities remains an empirical question.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether similar processes unfold in less celebrated organizations and around historical objects outside formal museum facilities. The authors also point to opportunities to study how professional training, tenure, identification, and career-related factors influence members' modes of engagement with history and identity. They further encourage research on the compatibility of different situated identity understandings and on how these understandings affect coordination across functions and audiences.
