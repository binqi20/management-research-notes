# Bringing “Together”: Emotions and Power in Organizational Responses to Institutional Complexity

**Abstract**
The theoretical puzzle of how organizations deal with contradicting logics has been extensively investigated during the past two decades. This stream of research has focused on the cognitive, but has overlooked emotions and power, which are fundamental to the lived experience of logics, and to their constitution. Drawing on a 15-month ethnography of “Together,” a Jewish–Palestinian organization in a mixed city in Israel, we explore how the organization succeeds in challenging societal dominant notions of ethno-nationalism by stimulating universalistic notions, and how it occasionally fails to do so, as ethno-nationalism creeps in. Our findings indicate that emotional control allows organizations to deal with contradictory logics and achieve their desired constellation, while unbidden emotions disrupt local efforts and enable changes in desired constellations of logics. Further, systemic power mediates the very experience of logics and the prospects of changing their constellation: Social asymmetry necessitates harder emotion work on behalf of the underprivileged, differentiating their experience of logics, and limiting the extent to which emotional eruption is a viable option. Thus, our study highlights the intersection of logics, emotions, and power, and how logics are managed and failed to be managed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what role emotions play in stabilizing or destabilizing constellations of logics inside organizations facing institutional complexity. It also asks how systemic power shapes the emotional generation of institutional stability and change.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Universalistic logic, ethno-national logic, emotional control, emotional eruption, emotion rules, emotion work, and systemic power.
- Process / routine stabilization: Material objects, texts, and interactions generate emotion rules that cultivate hope, closeness, care, love, safety, and avoidance, helping Together sustain universalism during routine periods.
- Triggering conditions: External political or violent events, especially Memorial and Independence Days and the 2012 Israel-Gaza War, activate ethno-national emotions such as fear, suspicion, anger, tension, and splitting.
- Emergent outcomes: Emotional eruptions temporarily make ethno-nationalism more salient inside the organization, even though the universalistic constellation is restored after events pass.
- Power dynamics / boundary conditions: Jewish and Palestinian members face unequal emotion work; Palestinian members must work harder to bracket discrimination and movement restrictions, while emotional eruption is more organizationally available around events salient to Jewish society.

The process is not a simple causal variable model. Together maintains a desired constellation of logics by controlling emotional content and emotional process through material, textual, and interactional arrangements. When external events pierce this routine control, unbidden emotions make the rejected ethno-national logic salient. Systemic power mediates both sides of the process by shaping which events count as disruptive and how much emotion work privileged and underprivileged members must perform.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional complexity research by moving beyond cognitive mechanisms such as sensemaking and negotiation to show how emotions and power constitute the lived experience of logics. It theorizes emotional control as a mechanism through which organizations stabilize a desired constellation of logics, and emotional eruption as a mechanism through which rejected logics re-enter organizational life. It also extends institutional theory by showing that emotion rules are not neutral: systemic power differentiates members' experience of logics and the organizational consequences of emotional eruption.

**Practical Implication**
The paper implies that organizations pursuing social change against dominant societal logics cannot rely only on formal goals, inclusive symbols, or avoidance of conflict. They need to recognize that emotional control may preserve cooperation in routine periods while also suppressing the sensemaking needed when disruptive events occur. Leaders in conflict-laden or hybrid organizations should attend to unequal emotional burdens across groups, because apparently shared emotion rules can require much heavier work from underprivileged members.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the ethnographic design provides depth but is based on one organization, so analytic generalization is bound by the study context. The case involves an organization committed to a logic that challenges a dominant societal logic, and it involves highly incompatible logics, which may limit transferability to organizations where logics are more compatible or where emotion rules align with dominant institutions. The authors also discuss limits linked to the first author's Jewish identity and limited Arabic, which constrained access to some Palestinian members' experiences.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine how emotional dynamics influence organizational responses to institutional complexity under different degrees of dominance between logics and in contexts where logics are more compatible. The paper also calls for work on when collective sensemaking moderates the effect of emotional eruptions on constellations of logics. More broadly, future studies should connect affective, political, and cognitive processes in institutional complexity.
