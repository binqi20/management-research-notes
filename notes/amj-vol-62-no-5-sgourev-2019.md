# From Montagues to Capulets: Analyzing the Systemic Nature of Rivalry in Career Mobility

**Abstract**
The paper proposes a systemic theory of rivalry that addresses a key limitation of past research by articulating how rivalry spreads across levels of analysis and between social domains. Shifting the perspective from what rivalry does to what it prevents from doing, we conceptualize rivalry as a system of relational constraints that extends beyond the level of direct rivals. Using qualitative and quantitative data on the mobility of jockeys in the Palio di Siena (1743–2011), the famous horse race in Siena, Italy, we document relational gaps in the mobility network—career moves that do not occur, even if expected on economic grounds. The analysis shows that rivalry constrains not only the moves to rivals but also to allies of rivals and rivals of allies. The effect becomes stronger over time, attesting that relational constraints persist in developed markets. Our fieldwork reveals that the constraining ability of rivalry is based on conformity to expectations of loyalty and adherence to group identities. The observed relational gaps are pertinent not only to scholarship on rivalry but also to mobility and network research.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how rivalry operates as a systemic form of relational constraint rather than only as a dyadic psychological relationship. It examines whether rivalries among Siena's contrade constrain jockey career mobility not only between direct rivals, but also through allies of rivals, rivals of allies, and increasingly professionalized market conditions.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / relational structures: Rivalry, alliances, allies of rivals, rivals of allies, professionalization, and relational gaps in mobility networks.
- Process / mechanisms: Rivalry creates expectations of loyalty and purity of affiliation; jockeys who move to a rival or adjacent rival camp risk stigmatization, damaged relationships, and possible retaliation.
- Empirical outcomes: Career moves between contrade, modeled as dyad-year mobility ties.
- Boundary / system conditions: The constraining effect is tested in a competitive labor market for Palio jockeys and examined as the market professionalizes over time.

The paper's process argument is that rivalry organizes social structure by creating categorical boundaries around friend and foe. Those boundaries make some otherwise plausible career moves socially costly, even when competitive labor-market logic would predict mobility toward attractive employers. Alliances extend the effect beyond direct rival dyads, so jockeys avoid not only rivals but also allies of rivals and rivals of allies. Professionalization does not erase these relational constraints; in the authors' account, it reinforces adherence to informal norms because reputation and professional standing depend on respecting the rivalry system.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to rivalry scholarship by shifting attention from dyadic rivalry effects to the extended relational system in which rivalry is embedded. It links rivalry, alliance networks, and career mobility by identifying relational gaps: expected but unrealized mobility ties that arise from antagonistic network structure. It also contributes to network and mobility research by theorizing missing ties as the product of systemic relational constraints rather than only lack of opportunity, information, or economic interest.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that rivalry can spill across groups and domains, so managers should not assume rivalry remains confined to direct competitors or symbolic conflict. In organizational settings, rivalry may constrain information exchange, staffing, collaboration, or mobility through adjacent relationships as well as direct rival ties. The paper suggests managerial attention is needed to prevent rivalry from diffusing into work-related tasks and adjacent social relationships.

**Limitations**
The authors identify the lack of direct data on jockey remuneration as the main limitation, because contracts between captains and jockeys are secret. They use proxies, dyad fixed effects, and year fixed effects to address resource-endowment and market-opportunity differences, but the paper cannot directly observe compensation incentives. They also note that the study's bounded historical setting raises scope conditions for contexts where crossing organizational boundaries does not carry comparable identity or professional implications.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine the interplay between relational constraints and market incentives in labor markets with more direct compensation data. The authors also call for work on individual-level factors explaining why some actors move to rivals while others do not, other forms of market exchange beyond mobility, and relational gaps in email exchanges, casual talk, co-attendance at third-party events, and other business contexts. Additional work could test whether the consequences of rivalry extend beyond direct rivals through collaborative ties.
