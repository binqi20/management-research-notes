# Microfoundations of Corporate Social Responsibility and Irresponsibility

**Abstract**
This study examines the importance of social perception of corporate social responsibility (CSR) and irresponsibility (CSI). Drawing from social psychology literature on stereotypes, we argue that two fundamental dimensions of social perception—warmth and competence—help explain the underlying processes and conditions under which CSR leads to specific outcomes. We propose that firms engaging in CSR are perceived as higher in warmth and, by default, competence; moreover, different perceptions of the organization’s warmth and competence can moderate CSR rewards and CSI penalties. To demonstrate this, we conduct three experiments. Experiment 1 links CSR with perceptions of warmth and competence, showing that warmth perceptions mediate the relationship between CSR and important outcomes, such as purchase intentions and reputation. Experiment 2 adds information on firms’ countries of origin, revealing that CSR rewards and CSI penalties differ depending on the (mis)alignment of CSR strategy with country stereotypes. Experiment 3 replicates these findings using behavioral paradigms. We find that firms from high-warmth countries (the United States, Sweden, Portugal) receive lower CSR rewards and pay higher CSI penalties than firms from low-warmth countries (Germany, Pakistan) but this effect is moderated by competence. Our micro–macro study advances social evaluation, strategic CSR, and international management literatures.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how individuals perceive CSR and CSI, and whether warmth and competence explain when these practices translate into outcomes such as reputation, purchase intentions, subjective quality, helping behavior, and purchase price. It also asks whether country-of-origin stereotypes change CSR rewards and CSI penalties by providing independent cues about organizational warmth and competence.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CSR, CSI, or control information about a firm or product manufacturer; in Studies 2 and 3, country-of-origin warmth and competence cues.
- DV(s): Warmth, competence, reputation, purchase intentions, subjective product quality, helping behavior measured as feedback word count, and purchase price.
- Mediators: Warmth perceptions, with competence included in mediation models but not consistently mediating CSR/CSI effects.
- Moderators: Country-of-origin warmth and competence stereotypes, represented by the United States or Sweden, Germany, Portugal, and Pakistan.

The mechanism is that CSR acts as a warmth signal and CSI as a negative warmth signal in initial social judgments of organizations. Warmth perceptions mediate the effects of CSR and CSI on immediate evaluations such as reputation and purchase intentions, while country-of-origin stereotypes create assimilation or contrast effects. Low-warmth firms can receive larger relative CSR rewards because CSR violates expectations positively, whereas high-warmth firms can face harsher CSI penalties because irresponsibility violates expectations negatively; competence sometimes buffers or amplifies these effects.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a microfoundational account of CSR and CSI by linking corporate social strategy to individual-level social perception. It extends CSR research beyond macro-level performance links by showing warmth and competence as psychological mechanisms and boundary conditions for CSR/CSI effects. It also connects the stereotype content model and BIAS map to strategy and international management by showing how country-of-origin stereotypes shape evaluations of firms' social behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should account for how stakeholders perceive both the firm's CSR/CSI behavior and other warmth-competence cues, including country of origin. CSR may be especially useful for firms initially perceived as low in warmth, including new multinationals or firms from low-warmth country contexts, because it can improve warmth and competence perceptions. The paper also warns that high-warmth firms may be penalized more sharply for CSI because irresponsibility conflicts with the warmth expectations attached to them.

**Limitations**
The authors note that controlled experiments provide causal leverage but limit ecological scope; future work should test other scenarios, industries, stakeholders, CSR/CSI activities, outcomes, and methods. The country manipulations use actual countries, which improves realism but reduces experimental precision and limits the number of warmth-competence combinations. The study was tested in technology and manufacturing contexts, and it could not directly address decoupling or greenwashing dynamics.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine stakeholder-specific judgments, organizational-level outcomes, and additional firm or country attributes such as size, status, and industry stereotypes. The authors suggest testing these ideas with real-world data, including country-level social evaluations and cross-border mergers and acquisitions. They also call for work on how different CSR actions generate warmth or competence perceptions and how stakeholders interpret symbolic versus substantive CSR.
