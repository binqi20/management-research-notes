# Why Do Extreme Work Hours Persist? Temporal Uncoupling as a New Way of Seeing

**Abstract**
This paper develops temporal uncoupling as a new way of seeing the puzzling persistence of extreme work hours, as well as the temporal relations of organizations and their environments. Drawing on a historical case study, we trace and analyze the genesis, reinforcement, and maintenance of extreme work hours in an elite consulting firm over a period of 40 years. We find that a small shift in temporal structuring mobilized two positive feedback processes. These processes consolidated a temporal order that increasingly uncoupled from the traditional workweek. Grounded in these findings, we make two contributions. First, we challenge the orthodox view of entrainment as an ideal synchronous relation between organizations and their environments. Instead, we offer temporal uncoupling as an alternative lens. It enables us to see how both synchrony and asynchrony are potentially viable options, which coexist and sometimes coconstitute each other. Second, we shed new light on temporality as a constitutive force that underpins extreme work hours and offer a novel explanation of their persistence as a case of systemic temporal lock-in. We develop positive feedback as a mechanism that explains how small temporal shifts can become consolidated into hardly reversible temporal lock-ins.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why and how extreme work hours persist in a state of asynchrony relative to dominant societal work time rhythms. It investigates this puzzle through the historical emergence, reinforcement, and maintenance of extreme work hours at an elite strategy consulting firm called Consult.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: temporal structuring, temporal uncoupling, synchrony with clients, asynchrony from societal work time rhythms, positive feedback, temporal lock-in.
- Process / phases: outcome-oriented temporal structuring in office projects; process-oriented temporal structuring in presence projects; temporal lock-in after hiring and retention pressures made extreme work hours increasingly problematic.
- Reinforcing processes: daytime synchronization with clients increased client expectations for presence, speed, and availability; desynchronization from societal work time norms aligned hiring, rewards, scheduling, and project calculation around long hours.
- Emergent outcomes: extreme work hours became embedded in Consult's internal activity system and in client-consultant expectations, making later work-life initiatives difficult to sustain.
- Boundary conditions: the explanation is grounded in an elite consulting context where client visibility, implementation-oriented projects, and price competition made availability and speed central to perceived value.

The process begins with a shift from weekly alternation between client interaction and desk work to presence projects in which consultants worked on-site with clients during the day. This increased synchrony with clients, but pushed desk work into evenings and weekends, thereby increasing asynchrony from regular work time. Positive feedback then made both sides of the pattern self-reinforcing: clients came to expect continuous availability, while Consult reorganized internal practices around extreme hours. Over time, this produced temporal lock-in, where the firm maintained the temporal structure despite employee burnout, attrition, and repeated change efforts.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes temporal uncoupling as an alternative to the dominant entrainment lens in organizational temporality research. Rather than treating organizations as time takers that adapt to external Zeitgebers, the authors theorize organizations as time makers that select which rhythms to synchronize with and which to desynchronize from.

The paper also explains the persistence of extreme work hours as temporal lock-in. This shifts attention from only cultural, technological, or power-based pressures toward endogenous temporal dynamics in which small temporal shifts become reinforced through positive feedback and eventually hard to reverse.

**Practical Implication**
The paper argues that piecemeal HR initiatives, work-life rules, and cultural change programs are unlikely to change extreme work hours when the underlying temporal arrangement remains intact. Sustainable change would require a systemic redesign of the business model, value proposition, project scoping, staffing, scheduling, and client expectations that currently make extreme availability valuable.

For consulting firms, this may require experimenting with business units or service models that are not premised on extreme speed and 24-7 availability. It may also require collective field-level action if single firms face competitive penalties for reducing extreme work hours alone.

**Limitations**
The authors identify retrospective data as a limitation, even though it was useful for studying long-term organizational dynamics. They also note that the analysis foregrounds synchronization with clients and societal work time rhythms while backgrounding broader industry-level dynamics.

The paper's focus on temporality also leaves other relevant factors, such as digital technologies, organizational control, and power, outside the main analysis. These factors may interact with temporal uncoupling and lock-in but are not fully examined in this study.

**Future Research**
Future research could use ethnographic, time-use, or digital trace data to examine the micro-dynamics of temporal uncoupling and temporal lock-in. Studies could also analyze how multiple temporalities across families, professional organizations, universities, government agencies, competitors, and industries interact.

The authors also call for research on how digital technologies shape synchronization, desynchronization, and temporal uncoupling. Further work could link temporal lock-in to organizational control and power, and investigate innovative temporal arrangements that help organizations manage extreme work hours more sustainably.
