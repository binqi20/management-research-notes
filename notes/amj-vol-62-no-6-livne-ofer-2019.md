# Eyes Wide Open: Perceived Exploitation and Its Consequences

**Abstract**
Drawing on the array of literature on exploitation from several social science disciplines, we propose a new way of seeing employer–employee relationships by introducing the concept of perceived exploitative employee–organization relationships, distinguish it from related concepts, and conduct five studies to develop a scale and test our theoretical model of the effects of such employee perceptions. Contributing to the employee– organization relationships and workplace emotions literatures, perceived exploitation is defined as employees’ perceptions that they have been purposefully taken advantage of in their relationship with the organization, to the benefit of the organization itself. We propose and find that such perceptions are associated with both outward-focused emotions of anger and hostility toward the organization and inward-focused ones of shame and guilt at remaining in an exploitative job. In two studies including construction workers and a time-lagged study of medical residents, we find that the emotions of anger and hostility partially mediate the effects of perceived exploitation on employee engagement, revenge against the organization, organizational commitment, and turnover intentions, whereas the emotions of shame and guilt partially mediate the effects of perceived exploitation on employee burnout, silence, and psychological withdrawal.

**Research Question**
How can organizational behavior theorize and measure employees' perceptions that their organizations exploit them, and what emotional, attitudinal, and behavioral consequences follow from those perceived exploitative employee-organization relationships?

**Mechanism Process**
- Focal construct: Employees' perceived exploitative employee-organization relationships.
- IV(s): Perceived exploitation by the organization.
- DV(s): Revenge, work engagement, turnover intentions, organizational commitment, burnout, employee silence, and psychological withdrawal.
- Mediators: Outward-focused emotions of anger and hostility; inward-focused emotions of shame and guilt.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The paper argues that perceived exploitation is an overall appraisal of an employee-organization relationship in which the employee sees the organization as purposefully taking advantage of them for organizational benefit. That appraisal can trigger outward-focused anger and hostility toward the organization, which helps explain revenge, lower engagement, higher turnover intentions, and lower organizational commitment. It can also trigger inward-focused shame and guilt, which helps explain burnout, silence, and psychological withdrawal. The two field studies support most of the predicted partial mediation paths, with revenge nonsignificant in Study 1 but supported in Study 2.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces and validates perceived exploitative employee-organization relationships as a distinct construct in organizational behavior, differentiating it from psychological contract breach, low perceived organizational support, distributive injustice, perceived organizational obstruction, and organizational cruelty. It extends exploitation research beyond structural accounts in sociology and industrial relations by focusing on individual-level perceptions and consequences. It also contributes to workplace emotions research by showing that outward-focused and inward-focused negative emotions explain different employee responses to negative organization relationships.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should not assume that employees simply leave when they experience the employment relationship as exploitative. The paper argues that organizations need to ascertain whether employees hold such perceptions, understand why those perceptions arise, and address the underlying practices or attributions. It also suggests that perceived exploitation can affect outcomes relevant to both employee well-being and organizational performance, including turnover intentions, commitment, burnout, silence, and withdrawal.

**Limitations**
The authors identify common method bias as a primary limitation, although they attempted to mitigate it through counterbalancing, time separation in Study 2, cross-over tests, and common latent factor checks. They also note limits to generalizability despite using distinct studies across different occupations and industries. A further limitation is the three-month separation in Study 2, which was chosen to manage attrition among residents likely to move from residencies to fellowships or permanent posts.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on how perceived exploitative relationships develop, including whether they emerge gradually or through abrupt threshold events. It encourages studies of contractors, gig-economy workers, temporary workers, and migrant workers, as well as supervisor roles in shaping perceptions of organizational exploitation. It also suggests examining buffers such as calling or attraction to the work, alternative mechanisms such as resource depletion and thwarted psychological needs, actual behavior beyond intentions, adverse health outcomes, and links to mobilization, social movements, and collective action.
