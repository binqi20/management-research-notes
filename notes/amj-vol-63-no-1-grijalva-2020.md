# Examining the “I” in Team: A Longitudinal Investigation of the Influence of Team Narcissism Composition on Team Outcomes in the NBA

**Abstract**
Previous research has shown that narcissism is unrelated to job performance, yet this individual-level effect may be underestimating narcissism’s wider influence on organizational performance. To assess this possibility, we draw on social exchange theory and the agency model of narcissism to investigate how team narcissism composition affects team coordination and performance. Our model was tested using game-level longitudinal data from National Basketball Association teams. Teams with higher mean and maximum levels of narcissism as well as higher narcissism members in core roles (i.e., central and influential roles) had poorer coordination and in turn performance than teams with lower levels. In addition, having higher team familiarity amplified the effects of narcissism for team mean and core role narcissism. The nature of the observed interaction, however, was surprising. Originally, we hypothesized that narcissism would lead to greater decrements in coordination as familiarity increased. Instead, we found that teams with higher mean and core role narcissism maintained the same levels of coordination over time, whereas teams with lower narcissism experienced improvements in coordination. Thus, team-level narcissism appears to prevent teams from capitalizing on normative coordination gains that occur as familiarity increases. These results underscore the importance of considering narcissism when forming teams.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether the composition of narcissism within teams undermines coordination and, through coordination, team performance. It further asks whether these effects differ across mean, maximum, and core-role operationalizations of team narcissism and whether team familiarity amplifies the effects over time.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Team-level narcissism composition, operationalized as team mean narcissism, team maximum narcissism, and core role narcissism.
- DV(s): Team coordination measured as assists; team performance measured as winning probability and victory margin.
- Mediators: Team coordination mediates the association between team-level narcissism and team performance.
- Moderators: Team familiarity moderates the narcissism-coordination relationship for mean and core role narcissism; the hypothesized maximum narcissism interaction is not supported.

The mechanism combines the agency model of narcissism with social exchange theory. Because narcissists are high in agency and low in communion, their self-interested behavior is expected to trigger reciprocal selfishness and weaken the exchange relationships needed for coordination. In high-familiarity teams, lower-narcissism teams gain coordination as members learn to work together, whereas higher-narcissism teams fail to capture these normative coordination gains.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The article shifts narcissism research from individual job performance to team-level outcomes, showing that narcissism can matter for organizational performance through interdependent team processes even when individual-level performance relationships are weak. It also clarifies team personality composition by comparing mean, maximum, and core-role narcissism rather than assuming one aggregation approach.

The paper contributes a temporal perspective by showing that team familiarity changes the observed narcissism-coordination relationship. Its surprising result is that narcissism does not mainly make coordination deteriorate over time; rather, higher narcissism prevents the improvements in coordination that lower-narcissism teams realize as familiarity accumulates.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should consider narcissism when forming teams, especially for highly interdependent teams that must coordinate over extended periods. The findings suggest avoiding highly narcissistic members in central or influential team roles when those roles shape workflow and interpersonal norms.

The paper also implies that managers should not rely only on individual talent when composing teams. When narcissistic team members cannot be avoided, compensation and incentives that tie rewards to team performance may help align narcissists' goals with collective outcomes.

**Limitations**
The narcissism measure uses unobtrusive Twitter indicators rather than direct questionnaire measures, although the authors report validation evidence for the composite measure. The NBA context may limit generalizability because it is a visible, competitive, highly interdependent action-team setting with immediate performance feedback and strong individual incentives.

The authors also note that NBA teams have rotating membership and a seasonal fresh-start structure that may affect how familiarity accumulates. The study does not specify a precise time frame for when familiarity should become more negative in narcissistic teams, and core-role findings apply most directly to teams with differentiated central roles.

**Future Research**
Future research could test more fine-grained mechanisms, such as norms, trust, and selfish behavior, to explain how team narcissism affects coordination. Studies could also examine whether core-role and high-status narcissism operate similarly across other forms of team structure.

The authors encourage research in other industries, countries, and team types to test the boundary conditions of the findings. Additional longitudinal work could examine the timing of familiarity effects across contexts where team members do or do not experience a clear seasonal reset.
