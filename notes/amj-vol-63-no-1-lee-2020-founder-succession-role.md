# Founder CEO Succession: The Role of CEO Organizational Identification

**Abstract**
Drawing from the organizational identification literature, we theorize that founder CEO succession decisions are dependent on the strength of these CEOs’ organizational identification with their firms. Our theoretical premise is that factors that decrease founder CEOs’ organizational identification—namely, prior entrepreneurial experience and the number of cofounders—will positively affect their voluntary succession. Conversely, factors that increase founder CEOs’ organizational identification—specifically, the length of time that their organization was a private firm and core founder status— will negatively affect their voluntary succession. We find strong support for our arguments in the context of firms that undertook an initial public offering during the period from 2000 to 2013. Our findings enable us to illustrate that founder CEOs’ succession decisions may be better understood by considering factors that might influence their psychological traits rather than focusing only on their performance and control.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how founder CEOs’ organizational identification with their firms affects the timing of voluntary founder CEO succession after an IPO. It specifically examines whether prior entrepreneurial experience, number of cofounders, core founder status, and time as a private firm predict faster or slower voluntary departure from the CEO role.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Prior entrepreneurial experience, number of cofounders, core founder status, and length of time as a private firm.
- DV(s): Time to voluntary founder CEO succession after IPO.
- Mediators: Founder CEO organizational identification is theorized as the psychological mechanism but is proxied rather than directly measured.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is that IPOs disrupt founders’ ownership, control, and identification with the organization, making post-IPO founder CEO succession a setting where differences in organizational identification should matter. Prior entrepreneurial experience and more cofounders are expected to weaken the founder CEO’s identification with the focal firm, increasing the hazard of voluntary succession. Core founder status and a longer private-firm period are expected to strengthen identification, decreasing the hazard of voluntary succession.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends organizational identification theory by applying it to founder CEOs, a group expected to have unusually strong baseline identification with the organizations they created. It contributes to founder CEO succession research by shifting attention from shareholder control, performance, and human capital toward founders’ psychological attachment to the firm. It also adds to corporate governance and upper echelons research by showing that CEO organizational identification can shape leadership-transition outcomes, not only agency costs or firm performance.

**Practical Implication**
Boards and investors should treat founder CEO succession as partly shaped by the founder’s psychological attachment to the firm, not only by performance or governance constraints. The findings imply that succession planning may need to account for heterogeneity among founder CEOs, because strong identification can help reduce agency problems but may also make leadership transitions more politically difficult.

**Limitations**
The sample is limited to U.S. IPO firms whose founder CEOs survived until the IPO, so the findings may not generalize to less successful ventures or other corporate life-cycle stages. The study uses archival proxies for organizational identification rather than direct measures, which limits the ability to test the proposed mechanism explicitly. The authors also acknowledge possible measurement error in classifying voluntary succession and cannot fully rule out alternative explanations.

**Future Research**
Future research could compare the role of organizational identification in CEO succession across different stages of the corporate life cycle, such as venture-capital investment, first product development, IPO, and mature-stage operations. Studies using simulations, experiments, surveys, or other methods could directly test whether the proposed archival indicators accurately capture founder CEO organizational identification. Future work could also examine whether similar identification mechanisms apply to hired managers and other organizational actors in different contexts.
