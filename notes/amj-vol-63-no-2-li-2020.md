# The Boon and Bane of Creative “Stars”: A Social Network Exploration of How and When Team Creativity Is (and Is Not) Driven by a Star Teammate

**Abstract**
Although previous research has highlighted the disproportional contributions of star employees to their teams, an emerging line of research has begun to examine the potentially negative consequences of team stars. Drawing on a social model of team creativity, we develop a dualistic model of the influences of team creative stars on team creativity. Specifically, we explore the roles of two types of team members—star and nonstar employees—in driving team creativity. Across two multiwave and multisource field samples, we find that a creative star who occupies a central position in the team workflow network has both a positive direct effect on team creativity and a negative indirect effect on team creativity via reducing nonstars’ learning (i.e., exploratory and exploitative activities). Our study also reveals that team coordination can mitigate the detrimental effect of a star’s centrality on nonstars’ learning behavior, and subsequently buffer the indirect effect on team creativity.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how a team creative star's centrality in the workflow network affects team creativity. It specifically examines whether star centrality has simultaneous positive direct effects and negative indirect effects through nonstars' learning, and whether team coordination changes these effects.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Creative star centrality in the team workflow network.
- DV(s): Team creativity.
- Mediators: Nonstars' learning activities, combining exploratory and exploitative learning.
- Moderators: Team coordination moderates the star centrality to nonstars' learning path and the conditional indirect effect; the hypothesized amplification of the direct positive effect is not supported.

The mechanism is dualistic. Centrally located creative stars can improve team creativity directly because they access diverse inputs, understand team workflow, and can spread novel ideas through the team. At the same time, teammates' reliance on a central star can reduce nonstars' own exploratory and exploitative learning, which indirectly weakens team creativity. Team coordination buffers the negative learning pathway by helping members integrate actions and maintain participation rather than overdepending on the star.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The article extends the social model of team creativity by showing that team creativity depends on interaction patterns between creative stars and nonstars, not simply on aggregating individual creativity. It also complicates star-performer theory by showing that a central star can be both a boon and a bane for team-level creative outcomes.

The paper further contributes to team microdynamics research by placing star centrality and team coordination in the same model. Its evidence shows that team coordination weakens the negative star-centrality effect on nonstars' learning, while the predicted strengthening of the direct positive effect on creativity is unsupported and, in the R&D sample, reversed.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should avoid assuming that placing creative stars in central workflow positions is unambiguously beneficial. The study suggests that central creative stars can lift team creativity directly but can also suppress the learning behavior of other members.

The paper recommends supporting nonstars and promoting effective team coordination. Coordination can reduce overreliance on stars, create shared language and common goals, and encourage broader participation in the team's creative process.

**Limitations**
The authors note that star identification differs across the two samples and that the R&D sample's highest-rated member may not always be meaningfully superior to other members. They also caution that the China context may limit generalizability because collectivism and power distance could accentuate dependence on central creative stars.

The study focuses on how star network position affects nonstars' learning, but nonstars may also shape star performance and contribution. The direct beneficial effect of star centrality may also depend on contextual factors such as team size and network density.

**Future Research**
Future research could test other measures of creative stars, including objective indicators such as patents or awards, and replicate the model in other cultural contexts. Studies could also examine how network connections between stars and nonstars influence the stars' own performance.

The authors encourage work on contextual factors that shape team creative processes and on how stars and nonstars contribute differently to exploration, exploitation, radical creativity, incremental creativity, and different phases from creativity to innovation.
