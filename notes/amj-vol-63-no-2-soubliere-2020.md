# The Legitimacy Threshold Revisited: How Prior Successes and Failures Spill Over to Other Endeavors on Kickstarter

**Abstract**
How does the legitimacy conferred on entrepreneurial endeavors affect the legitimacy of subsequent ones? We extend the notion of a “legitimacy threshold” to develop and test a recursive model of legitimacy. Whereas extant research has focused on whether entrepreneurial endeavors garner sufficient support from key audiences to cross this threshold, we argue that the order of magnitude by which they succeed or fail is also consequential for later entrants. Distinguishing “blockbuster” from “unsung” successes, and “path breaking” from “broken path” failures, we contend that recent successes and failures affect related subsequent endeavors in predictable, though sometimes counterintuitive ways. We test our hypotheses by examining 182,358 entrepreneurial endeavors pitched within 165 categories over a six-year period on Kickstarter, one of the most important crowdfunding platforms. We show that individual outcomes, taken collectively, generate legitimacy spillovers, either by encouraging audiences to repeatedly support other related endeavors or by discouraging them from doing so. Our research contributes to understanding the recursive nature of legitimacy, the competitive dynamics of entrepreneurial efforts, and crowdfunding platforms.

**Research Question**
How does the legitimacy bestowed upon prior entrepreneurial endeavors affect the legitimacy of subsequent related endeavors? The paper asks whether the magnitude and type of prior success or failure on Kickstarter change how audiences support later campaigns in the same category.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Recent category-level counts of blockbuster successes, unsung successes, path breaking failures, and broken path failures in the 90 days before a focal campaign.
- DV(s): Amount pledged to the focal campaign, logged, as a resource-acquisition proxy for legitimacy.
- Mediators: Not directly estimated; the theorized process is legitimacy spillover through new backers and established backers.
- Moderators: Attraction of new backers, category maturity, and concurrent launches are tested in additional analyses.

The paper argues that prior campaign outcomes prime audiences' legitimacy assessments of related later campaigns. Blockbuster successes and path breaking failures expand the carrying capacity for subsequent campaigns by encouraging audience support, whereas unsung successes and broken path failures reduce later support. The mechanism is recursive because individual legitimation outcomes collectively flow back into the category and shape later resource acquisition.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to legitimacy theory by showing that legitimacy is not only a threshold crossed by individual endeavors but also a recursive category-level process. It qualifies the usual positive view of legitimacy by showing that prior successes can be detrimental and prior failures can be beneficial, depending on their magnitude. It also reframes entrepreneurial categories as simultaneously cooperative and competitive spaces rather than as moving cleanly from cooperation to competition as they mature.

**Practical Implication**
Entrepreneurs using crowdfunding platforms should attend to recent outcomes in their category because those outcomes can alter audience receptivity before a focal campaign launches. The findings also imply that entrepreneurs need to cultivate new audiences rather than rely only on established category backers, since established backers are especially exposed to spillover effects from prior successes and failures.

**Limitations**
The analysis is observational and uses resource acquisition as a proxy for legitimacy, so related social evaluation constructs such as reputation, celebrity, certification, or authenticity may partly overlap with the measured effects. The empirical setting is a transparent crowdfunding platform, which may amplify spillovers relative to settings where successes and failures are less visible. The paper also focuses on campaigns at an early conception-stage legitimacy threshold, leaving later market performance outside the analysis.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine backers' decision-making processes more directly and separate legitimacy from other social evaluations. Scholars could test whether similar success and failure spillovers appear in other settings, such as record labels, studio pitches, academic publishing, or other entrepreneurial platforms. The authors also call for work on transparency and secrecy, cross-platform spillovers, later marketplace outcomes, and textual or visual cultural spillovers in crowdfunding pitches.
