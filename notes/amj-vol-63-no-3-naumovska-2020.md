# Last to Come and Last to Go? The Complex Role of Gender and Ethnicity in the Reputational Penalties for Directors Linked to Corporate Fraud

**Abstract**
Scholars have found consistent evidence that directors who served on boards of firms accused of misconduct face reputational penalties in the director labor market. While commonly interpreted in terms of an “ex post settling-up” process that penalizes directors for failing in their role as monitors of management, the fundamentally social basis of the director labor market suggests that the ex post settling-up process may also incorporate a resource-provisioning role for directors as conferrers of legitimacy. We analyze how growing socioeconomic pressures that aim to redress the longstanding underrepresentation of female and ethnic minority directors may lessen, for these sought-after directors, the penalties typically imposed by the labor market in the aftermath of corporate misconduct. Using a rich proprietary data set on financial misconduct and directors’ demographic characteristics, we find strong support for our hypotheses regarding a possible “reputational immunity” effect. We also provide supplementary analyses demonstrating the specific mechanisms underlying our predictions, and establishing the robustness of the results to a variety of alternative explanations. We discuss the implications of our theoretical perspective and empirical findings for future research on corporate governance, corporate misconduct, and the duality of minority status as it relates to discriminatory outcomes in modern labor markets.

**Research Question**
How does a director's demographic minority status affect the ex post settling-up process in the director labor market after corporate fraud? The paper asks whether socioeconomic pressures for board diversity can lessen or reverse the reputational penalties usually imposed on fraud-tainted directors, and whether media discourse and the diversity level of nonfraudulent boards amplify or attenuate that effect.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Minority director status; media discourse on board diversity; number of minority directors at the nonfraudulent firm.
- DV(s): Net loss of outside directorships at nonfraudulent firms; loss of a specific outside directorship at a nonfraudulent firm.
- Mediators: No mediator is directly estimated.
- Moderators: Media discourse on board diversity strengthens minority directors' reputational immunity; the number of minority directors already on a nonfraudulent board lowers a fraud-tainted minority director's marginal diversity value.

The mechanism is a cross-level reputational calculus in which director labor-market evaluators weigh fraud-tainted directors' apparent monitoring failure against their resource-provisioning and legitimacy-conferring value. Because women and ethnic minority directors are underrepresented and increasingly sought after, socioeconomic pressures for board diversity can counterbalance penalties attached to corporate fraud. This reputational immunity is stronger when media discourse heightens the value of board diversity and weaker when a nonfraudulent firm already has more minority directors, reducing the focal director's marginal diversity contribution.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends ex post settling-up research by showing that director labor-market penalties are not only agency-theoretic assessments of directors as monitors. They also reflect the socially embedded value of directors as resource providers and conferrers of legitimacy under evolving norms for board diversity.

It contributes to corporate governance, corporate misconduct, and labor-market discrimination research by theorizing and testing how minority status can produce reputational immunity in a specific upper-echelons market. The contribution is bounded: the authors do not claim broad advantage for women or ethnic minority directors, but identify conditions under which demand and perceived scarcity can partially reverse expected penalties.

**Practical Implication**
For boards and nominating committees, the findings imply that diversity pressures can shape retention and appointment decisions even after serious corporate misconduct. Firms may keep or appoint fraud-tainted minority directors because those directors contribute legitimacy and diversity value, especially on less diverse boards.

The authors also imply that expanding the supply of minority directors through broader search processes and less traditional routes could reduce uneven ex post settling-up across demographic groups. As the minority-director pipeline deepens, the imbalance in reputational penalties may diminish.

**Limitations**
The study is limited to boards of directors in the United States, where diversity quotas are implicit; the authors explicitly ask whether similar or stronger results would appear in countries with explicit quotas for women directors. The design also interprets director labor-market outcomes as reputational penalties, while acknowledging that some directors may voluntarily exit the director labor market after misconduct.

The paper addresses but cannot exhaust all alternative explanations. Supplementary analyses test perceived lower influence, benevolent sexism, firm status, audit-committee accountability, busyness, and separate female versus ethnic-minority effects, but the authors call for more direct research on perceptions of directors' influence and ethical dispositions.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how reputational immunity decays as boards move from historically low to more moderate diversity levels. The authors also call for work on whether fraud-tainted minority directors who retain seats become more isolated or less influential in boardroom processes, and on backlash, tokenism, and perceptions that minorities are tokens.

Additional research could compare implicit-quota contexts such as the United States with explicit quota regimes, examine voluntary director exit after misconduct, and compare legal sanctions with reputational penalties after regulatory changes such as Sarbanes-Oxley. The paper also encourages more integrative work across economics, sociology, social psychology, organizational studies, and legal studies on corporate misconduct.
