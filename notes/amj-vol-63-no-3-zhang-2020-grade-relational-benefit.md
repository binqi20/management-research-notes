# Not in My Pay Grade: The Relational Benefit of Pay Grade Dissimilarity

**Abstract**
We draw upon relational demography and social identity perspectives to argue that working with teammates who are dissimilar in pay grade can help individual team members reduce social uncertainty in their work relationships within the team, subsequently influencing their identification with the team, performance, and turnover intention. This effect is more pronounced for team members in a lower pay grade because they are more sensitive to social influence owing to their lower status in the team. We test these arguments through a three-part survey of 203 employees from 33 work teams of a telecommunication company in China. Our data show that pay grade dissimilarity is associated with lower relational uncertainty, more for individuals with a lower pay grade, and that lower relational uncertainty, in turn, is associated with higher task performance, higher identification with the team, and lower intent to leave the team. This paper contributes to the literature on pay dispersion by highlighting the importance of relative pay, especially for individuals who are lower paid than others, and uncovering the social identity-related process underlying its effects. Post hoc analysis suggests that these effects are present especially when the pay system is perceived to be legitimate.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how individual team members respond to pay grade differences within their teams. Specifically, it examines whether pay grade dissimilarity reduces relational uncertainty, whether this effect is stronger for lower pay grade members, and whether lower relational uncertainty is associated with team identification, task performance, and turnover intention.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Pay grade dissimilarity from teammates and individual pay grade.
- DV(s): Team identification, task performance, and turnover intention.
- Mediators: Relational uncertainty.
- Moderators: Individual pay grade moderates the pay grade dissimilarity-relational uncertainty relationship and the indirect effects; post hoc analysis examines perceived legitimacy of pay grade difference as an additional boundary condition.

The mechanism is an uncertainty-reduction account grounded in relational demography and the social identity perspective. Pay grade differences create a legitimate and stable status hierarchy that gives team members cues about expected interaction patterns, reducing relational uncertainty about how to behave with teammates. Because lower pay grade members are theorized to be more attentive to social influence, pay grade dissimilarity reduces their relational uncertainty more strongly. Lower relational uncertainty is then associated with stronger identification with the team, better leader-rated task performance, and lower intention to leave the team.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The article contributes to pay dispersion research by shifting attention from collective-level pay dispersion to individual pay grade dissimilarity and by specifying a social identity-related process through which pay differences matter. It shows that relative pay can have relational benefits for lower paid team members when pay grade differences are perceived as legitimate.

The paper also extends relational demography theory by identifying a positive effect of dissimilarity on identification-related outcomes. Instead of treating demographic dissimilarity mainly as a source of self-enhancement threats or in-group bias, it shows that dissimilarity on a legitimate and stable status dimension can reduce uncertainty about team interactions.

**Practical Implication**
For managers, the findings suggest that pay differences within teams are not uniformly harmful; when a pay grade system is transparent, stable, and perceived as legitimate, pay grade dissimilarity may clarify interaction expectations and support team attachment and performance. The implication is not simply to widen pay differences, but to attend to whether employees understand and accept the status basis of pay grades.

The results also imply that lower pay grade employees may be especially sensitive to the relational information embedded in pay grade structures. Managers should therefore treat the legitimacy and communication of pay grade differences as part of the social architecture of teams, not only as a compensation issue.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the observed effect sizes are small, explaining about 3-5% of variance, although they argue that restricted variance in pay grade dissimilarity may have attenuated effects and that individual-level effects can accumulate across larger units. The study is also conducted in China, a context with lower uncertainty avoidance and higher power distance, so cultural scope conditions may matter.

The mediator and two dependent variables, team identification and turnover intention, are self-reported, although they were measured at different waves to reduce common method variance. The design is time-lagged but not experimental, so the authors call for stronger causal evidence.

**Future Research**
Future research could extend the argument into a multilevel framework by examining whether aggregated uncertainty reduction links pay differences to performance and turnover at group or organizational levels. It could also examine higher-level moderators, especially cultural uncertainty avoidance and power distance, that may condition the individual-level process.

The authors also suggest that future work could replicate the study experimentally to strengthen causal inference. More broadly, additional research could test whether the positive uncertainty-reduction effects of status differences hold only when status hierarchies are perceived as legitimate and stable.
