# Making Change from Behind a Mask: How Organizations Challenge Guarded Institutions by Sparking Grassroots Activism

**Abstract**
We examine how organizations can challenge institutions that are coercively protected by powerful elites—guarded institutions—when they are unable or unwilling to advocate publicly against them. To do so, we draw on an in-depth qualitative study of efforts to combat child marriage in Indonesia. We explore how an international children’s rights organization worked alongside local nongovernmental organizations and activists to disrupt the institution of child marriage through two discrete strategies: the crafting of an alter ego that takes the appearance of a social movement that has emanated from the grassroots but is actually highly organized, and the use of this alter ego to support the incubation of public dissent by means of a high-stakes event. We contribute to the literature by developing a theorized account of how organizations can challenge guarded institutions when they cannot speak out—an important organizational problem that has received limited attention. We also challenge the theoretical distinction that has been drawn between the organizational mobilization of activists, often referred to as astroturfing, and seemingly organic mobilization that is said to emerge at the grassroots level.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how organizations can challenge guarded institutions when they are unable or unwilling to advocate publicly against them. It examines this question through ICO's covert campaign against child marriage in Indonesia.

**Mechanism Process**
- Contextual problem: Child marriage in Indonesia operated as a guarded institution, morally reinforced and coercively protected by religious and political elites.
- Organizational constraint: ICO wanted to challenge child marriage but depended on government and religious relationships for other child-welfare work, making open dissent risky.
- Strategy 1, crafting an alter ego: ICO and partners built a secretive coalition and feigned a grassroots social movement through StopChildMarriage, with students and local actors as the public face.
- Strategy 2, incubating public dissent: The coalition used the alter ego to create and exploit a high-stakes judicial-review event, provoking public outrage and fueling broader grassroots activism.
- Emergent outcome: Public contestation moved beyond ICO's control as independent activists, organizations, media actors, and local initiatives took up the anti-child-marriage cause.

The process centers on covert institutional disruption rather than direct public advocacy. ICO masked its organizational role, mimicked grassroots mobilization, and used a high-stakes event to shift child marriage from a repressed issue to one that could be publicly contested. The paper argues that what began as strategically organized, quasi-astroturfed activism became increasingly independent grassroots activism.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a process model explaining how organizations can challenge guarded institutions when open advocacy is too risky. It contributes two strategies--crafting an alter ego and incubating public dissent--and specifies their tactics, including secretive coalition building, feigning a social movement, provoking public outrage, and fueling grassroots activism. It also challenges a sharp distinction between astroturfing and grassroots mobilization by showing how organization-led mobilization can stimulate activism that later becomes more organic and bottom-up.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations facing coercively protected social problems may need to assess whether open contestation would endanger their mission or beneficiaries. The study suggests that indirect coalition work, careful protection of local partners, and event-based mobilization can create space for public dissent when direct advocacy is unavailable. It also raises ethical risks because covert mobilization and organizational disguise can be used for emancipatory or oppressive purposes.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that the theory is built from a single-case study and cannot capture the full range and complexity of the dynamics involved. They also note that structuring the analysis around ICO may privilege ICO's perspective and make the change effort appear more strategically choreographed than it was. The case depends partly on digital communication, Indonesia's political and religious context, and guardians who were political and religious leaders, so the model may not transfer unchanged to more tightly surveilled settings or institutions guarded by corporate or military actors.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether crafting an alter ego and incubating public dissent operate similarly in other guarded institutions, including those protected by corporate, military, or state actors. Scholars could also examine functional equivalents to high-stakes events, such as photographs, books, documentaries, or sustained social-media work. The paper further invites deeper study of the ethical implications of foreign organizations covertly mobilizing local activism around contested moral issues.
