# The Underdog Effect: When Low Expectations Increase Performance

**Abstract**
Existing theory and research has documented the benefits of facing high expectations and the perils of encountering low expectations. This paper examines the performance effects of underdog expectations, defined as individuals’ perceptions that others view them as unlikely to succeed. Integrating theory and research on self-enhancement with psychological reactance, I predict that underdog expectations have the potential to boost performance through the desire to prove others wrong when others’ credibility is in question. Studies 1 and 2 provide support for the positive relationship between underdog expectations and performance. Study 3 reveals support for the positive effect of underdog expectations on performance through the desire to prove others wrong. Study 4 demonstrates that these effects depend on the perceived credibility of observers: when observers’ expectations are seen as more credible, underdog expectations undermine performance (consistent with the Golem effect and self-fulfilling prophecy), but when observers’ expectations are viewed as less credible, underdog expectations boost performance (demonstrating the underdog effect). My theory and results challenge the assumption that perceiving low expectations from others is always detrimental, and offer meaningful insights into why and when underdog expectations increase versus inhibit performance.

**Research Question**
When do underdog expectations increase rather than inhibit individual performance? The paper asks whether individuals' perceptions that others view them as unlikely to succeed can motivate performance through the desire to prove others wrong, and whether this effect depends on observers' perceived credibility.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Underdog expectations, compared with high observer expectations or neutral/evenly matched expectations across the experimental studies.
- DV(s): Supervisor-rated job performance, mouse-target task performance, and negotiation performance.
- Mediators: Desire to prove others wrong; task self-efficacy is tested as an alternative mediator in supplementary analyses.
- Moderators: Perceived credibility of observers.

The paper argues that underdog expectations can activate self-enhancement and psychological reactance motives: individuals discount less credible low expectations, seek to protect positive self-appraisals, and work harder to show observers that their judgments are wrong. This desire to prove others wrong explains the performance benefit in Study 3 and partly explains the conditional effect in Study 4. When observers are credible, the same underdog expectations are more likely to be internalized or to make proving others wrong performance-disruptive, aligning the findings with the Golem effect and self-fulfilling prophecy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the assumption that low expectations from others are uniformly detrimental by showing that underdog expectations can motivate performance. It contributes a relational mechanism, the desire to prove others wrong, to expectations and motivation research, moving beyond self-efficacy as the dominant explanation for expectation effects. It also identifies perceived observer credibility as a boundary condition that reconciles the underdog effect with self-fulfilling prophecy theory.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that leaders and organizations may help employees reframe low expectations as an opportunity to prove others wrong. It also implies that managers can reduce the force of low expectations by highlighting why doubting observers may lack credibility. At the same time, organizations should be cautious when people who hold low expectations also have direct authority over employees, because their lack of support may outweigh any motivational benefit.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that its full theoretical model is tested only in the final study, and the mechanisms are measured only in Studies 3 and 4. The conditional indirect effects through proving others wrong are not strongly supported in all analyses, including some low-credibility analyses. Task self-efficacy receives little support as a mechanism, leaving open whether relative inexperience, overconfidence, goal-setting conditions, or boundary-condition effects explain its limited role.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine other mechanisms that may explain the effects of underdog expectations, including proving oneself right, sense of challenge, pride, and feeling that one has nothing to lose. Scholars should also examine settings where credibility and status diverge, and test whether high-status observers produce different responses than low-status observers. Additional research could study how proving others wrong operates in stereotype threat and reactance contexts, including whether it creates performance benefits or costly overexertion.
