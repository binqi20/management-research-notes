# Reinventing the State-Owned Enterprise? Negotiating Change during Profound Environmental Upheaval

**Abstract**
This study builds theory on organizational change through a qualitative study investigating how a small state-run factory in China transformed into a global leader in power equipment manufacturing. Drawing on cross-level longitudinal data from the company’s founding in 1966 to 2016, we unpack a process of organizational change that unfolded during China’s transition away from a centrally planned economy. Our findings draw attention to the role of “values work” in facilitating and mitigating the risks of implementing highly controversial and seemingly immoral changes, which, in the case of the present study, were necessitated when the relevance of prior experience and organizing templates eroded. We present a model illustrating three strategies that, in combination, animate this values work: reconditioning, negotiated obsolescence, and mitigating risks of nonconformity. The model contributes to an understanding of how organizations reconcile the need for radical change with pressures for continuity and coherence. It also shows how managers find a balance between taking risks and minimizing them through efforts to time and align ongoing changes with shifting conceptualizations of appropriateness—both inside and outside the organization.

**Research Question**
How can an organization purposefully encode practices and structures that appear antithetical to its underpinning values during profound environmental upheaval? The paper examines this question through Turbo Company, a Chinese state-owned enterprise that had to reconcile market-oriented change with socialist roots, employee welfare commitments, and state-enterprise expectations.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Organizational values and change; values work; archetypal socialist organizing template; institutional upheaval; value system discrepancies; appropriateness; continuity and radical change.
- Context / triggering condition: China's transition away from a centrally planned economy eroded the relevance of Turbo's prior organizing template and created pressure to adopt market-oriented structures, incentives, and governance arrangements.
- Process / strategies: Priming for change; reconditioning through coercive embedding, targeted persuasion, and normalizing paradoxes; negotiated obsolescence through indifferent substitution and objectifying and replacing; mitigating risks of nonconformity through external entrainment, grassroots monitoring and feedback, and signifying consent.
- Emergent outcome: Turbo reconfigured its organizing template by introducing new value commitments while removing or replacing institutionalized elements tied to old commitments, without fully abandoning the coherence of its socialist value system.
- Boundary conditions: The model is developed from a single, highly embedded state-owned enterprise in China's transition economy, where government mandates, Party authority, employee welfare expectations, and risks of nonconformity were especially salient.

The process starts when environmental upheaval makes the old organizing template less useful but does not automatically dissolve the organization's inherited values. Turbo's leaders first anchored change narratives to legitimate state mandates, then used values work to make controversial changes implementable: they reconditioned members toward market-oriented commitments, negotiated replacements for valued socialist-era arrangements, and continually aligned scope, timing, and pressure with external policy shifts and internal acceptability. The mechanism is therefore not a clean break from the past, but a negotiated reconfiguration in which continuity in implementation helps make value-incongruent change more acceptable.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to organizational change research by showing that values do not only channel what change options are considered or get upended during radical change. In Turbo's case, values regulated how change was implemented, providing procedural continuity while the organization altered its mission, structures, incentives, and employee-enterprise relationships.

The paper also contributes to research on transition economies by theorizing how organizations manage the risks of divergent change when external rules, political expectations, and internal norms are unsettled. Its model of values work explains how managers balance risk taking and risk minimization through external entrainment, grassroots monitoring, and signifying consent.

**Practical Implication**
For managers facing controversial or value-incongruent change, the study suggests that implementation processes matter as much as change content. Change is more likely to gain traction when managers connect new practices to familiar values, negotiate replacements for valued arrangements, and monitor what internal and external audiences consider appropriate.

The paper also cautions that coercive pressure must be selective and calibrated. Turbo's leaders used top-down pressure to move reforms forward, but they also delayed, modified, or abandoned initiatives when employee resistance, policy ambiguity, or risks of nonconformity made the change too dangerous.

**Limitations**
The study draws on a single qualitative case of Turbo Company, so the model is bounded by a state-owned enterprise in China's transition economy. The authors explicitly note that the setting involves unusually high political ambiguity, strong state authority, socialist legacies, and risks such as sanctions, imprisonment, labor unrest, and retrospective accountability.

The paper's evidence is also weighted toward leaders, managers, archival documents, and executive meetings. The authors address this limitation by using staff appeals, meeting minutes, and other documents to understand lower-level experiences, but the account remains more top-down than a study centered on ordinary employees' lived experience.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether changes in normative expectations are more or less likely than technological disruption or regulatory change to dislodge organizational values. The paper also invites comparative work on how different contextual conditions and different levels of commitment to an archetypal template shape the mutability of organizing templates.

Further studies could examine how values work evolves as transition economies mature and how prospective alignment strategies help managers anticipate future shifts in the boundaries of appropriateness. These questions would extend the model beyond Turbo's specific Chinese SOE context and test how organizations manage value-incongruent change in other turbulent environments.
