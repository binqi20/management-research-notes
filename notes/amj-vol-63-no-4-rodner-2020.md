# Making Space for Art: A Spatial Perspective of Disruptive and Defensive Institutional Work in Venezuela’s Art World

**Abstract**
The physical and material aspects of space, such as geographical distance or boundaries, have social and symbolic consequences that impact how people influence and are influenced by institutions. Social actors can, however, contest how space is conceived, perceived, and lived, thus making space a crucial lever in the disruption and defense of institutions. However, we lack understanding of the spatial aspects of such institutional struggles. In exploring how space is leveraged in institutional work, our study foregrounds the sociopolitical nature of space, building on and expanding the theorization of Henri Lefebvre. We draw on an in-depth longitudinal analysis of the material, social, and symbolic aspects of the spatial dimensions of disruptive and defensive institutional work over the past 20 years in Venezuela’s art world. Following the start of the Bolivarian Revolution in the late 1990s, the incoming government transformed the organization of the national cultural landscape, resulting in a prolonged period of institutional disruption and defense. Analyzing data relating to Venezuela’s art world during this time, we demonstrate that actors use the material, social, and symbolic dimensions of space to challenge and maintain their key values and practices—and that these three dimensions are intertwined.

**Research Question**
The paper asks what role space plays in the disruption and defense of institutions, and how the dimensions of space interact in that disruption and defense. It examines these questions in Venezuela’s art world after the Bolivarian Revolution, where government actors and displaced art professionals used different spatial tactics to contest what art should be and who could legitimate it.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Space; disruptive institutional work; defensive institutional work; material, social, and symbolic dimensions of space; government appropriation of official art venues; displaced art actors’ alternative “bubble of freedom.”
- Process / phases: Government centralization and reclamation of official art spaces; exclusion and displacement of established art professionals; clustering of private galleries and alternative venues; social and symbolic reconstruction of value-granting practices.
- Spatial dimensions: Material positioning through proximity, distance, boundaries, and isolation; social embodiment and enactment of space through actors’ practices and networks; symbolic expressiveness through curatorial discourse, rebranding, and value conferral.
- Emergent outcomes: Coexistence of competing institutional prescriptions in different spaces rather than a simple replacement of one institution by another; partial defense of art-world values in a spatially isolated but symbolically meaningful private-sector cluster.
- Boundary conditions: The process is theorized in a mature field marked by a strong power imbalance, where government actors controlled publicly financed cultural spaces and displaced actors retained social and symbolic links to the global art world.

The process begins when the Chávez administration appropriates and reorganizes museums, galleries, art salons, and cultural agencies to align official art space with Bolivarian ideology. Established art professionals respond by distancing themselves from state-run venues and clustering private galleries, foundations, and alternative venues into a “bubble of freedom” where they can continue curatorial and value-conferring practices. Material demarcation enables new social interactions, and those interactions reinforce symbolic legitimacy; at the same time, the bubble protects defensive work while also narrowing access to wider audiences and material resources.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional work by showing that space is not merely a background location for institutional struggle but a strategic lever through which actors disrupt and defend institutions. It extends research on place and materiality by distinguishing material positioning, social embodiment/enactment, and symbolic expressiveness, and by showing how these dimensions mutually reinforce one another during institutional conflict.

The study also refines accounts of institutional change by showing coexistence between competing institutional prescriptions rather than straightforward deinstitutionalization or replacement. Controlling official places such as museums does not automatically produce control over the institution, because displaced actors may retain social and symbolic resources in alternative spaces.

**Practical Implication**
For cultural-sector leaders and policymakers, the study cautions that physical control of museums, galleries, or public venues does not by itself confer legitimacy over artistic practices or audiences. Spatial redesign, rebranding, and centralized governance may disrupt existing institutional roles, but they can also isolate official venues from the social networks and symbolic resources that make those venues meaningful.

For displaced professionals and field actors, the study shows the defensive value of clustering, alternative venues, and international connections. It also warns that protective spatial isolation can become a double-edged strategy when alternative spaces preserve professional practices but reduce public accessibility and dependence on scarce private resources.

**Limitations**
The authors identify the scope of the case across time and space as a limitation, since the model is grounded in the disrupted Venezuelan art sector. The setting involves a mature field, a strong power imbalance, and government control over publicly financed cultural spaces, so the findings may travel most directly to fields where established actors can be forcefully excluded from institutional spaces.

The study also does not observe radical spatial destruction as institutional work. Its account therefore speaks more directly to appropriation, displacement, demarcation, isolation, and symbolic reconfiguration than to cases where actors destroy places or remove all spatial supports for institutional practice.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on the interaction of time and space in institutional struggles, especially because spatial support for institutions can develop over decades or centuries. Future studies could examine how temporal organizing and spatial organizing jointly challenge, maintain, or sediment institutional practices.

The authors also encourage research on power, space, and resources from an institutional perspective, including cases where actors destroy places or deprive others of spatial supports for institutional practice. They further suggest multimodal research on how spatial artifacts such as galleries, museums, biennales, and fairs constrain and enable meaning-making, discourse, and institutionalization.
