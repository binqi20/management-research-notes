# Identity Asymmetries: An Experimental Investigation of Social Identity and Information Exchange in Multiteam Systems

**Abstract**
Many complex organizational tasks are performed by networks of teams, or "multiteam systems." A critical challenge in multiteam systems is how to promote information exchange across teams. In three studies, we investigate how identity "asymmetries"— differences between teams in terms of whether the team or overarching system constitutes their primary focus of identification—affect interteam information sharing and performance. In Study 1, we manipulate teams' foci of identification (team vs. system focused) in a sample of 84 five-member teams working in one of 21 four-team multiteam systems performing a computer strategy simulation. We find that, while system-focused teams shared information equally with all teams, team-focused teams shared less information with system-focused teams than they did with other team-focused teams. Interteam information sharing positively predicted interteam performance. In Study 2, we test the assumptions underlying our theory in a vignette experiment, demonstrating that team-focused individuals adopt instrumental motives toward interteam interaction. Finally, in Study 3, we investigate the implications of system composition in terms of team identity foci by means of a simulation study based on the empirical results of Study 1. The results of the simulation yield novel propositions about the nonlinear effects of social identity in multiteam systems.

**Research Question**
How do identity asymmetries between component teams in a multiteam system affect interteam information sharing and interteam performance? The paper examines whether team-focused and system-focused teams approach interteam interaction with different motives, and whether those motives make some identity configurations more disruptive than others.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Source team's identity focus, seeking team's identity focus, and their interaction as identity asymmetry or symmetry.
- DV(s): Proactive information sharing, reactive information sharing, interteam performance, reciprocity-oriented information sharing, and simulated system-level information sharing and performance.
- Mediators: Proactive information sharing carried the supported Study 1 indirect path; reactive information sharing was hypothesized and positively related to performance but did not support the indirect-effect hypothesis. Study 2 tests instrumentality motive.
- Moderators: Identity configuration of the multiteam system in Study 3; seeker-source identity-focus configuration at the dyadic level.

The mechanism is that team-focused teams treat interteam relations more instrumentally, sharing proactively when they expect direct reciprocal value from another team. System-focused teams treat other component teams as part of the same in-group and therefore share more benevolently and less conditionally. Identity asymmetry is most harmful when a team-focused source interacts with a system-focused seeker, because the source has less reason to invest in proactive anticipatory sharing while the seeker depends on that sharing for interteam performance; the reactive-sharing pathway was theorized but did not provide a supported indirect effect in Study 1. The simulation extends this dyadic mechanism to show that system-level performance is nonlinear: partial shifts toward system focus can create asymmetric dyads before the benefits of broad system focus dominate.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes causal evidence that social identity affects interteam coordination and performance in multiteam systems, but not through a simple linear benefit of superordinate identification. It introduces identity asymmetry as a dyadic and configural property, showing that the effect of one team's identity focus depends on the identity focus of its partner team. It also extends social identity and self-categorization theory by linking identity foci to instrumental versus benevolent motives for interteam information exchange.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations managing multiteam systems should watch for arrangements that make some teams system-focused while others remain team-focused, such as workflow centrality, physical or social isolation, or inconsistent leader rhetoric. Interventions to build system identification may need to be broad and simultaneous, because a staggered or partial shift can temporarily increase identity asymmetries and weaken information sharing. The paper especially emphasizes proactive information sharing as a managerial target, because source teams may know who needs information better than seeking teams know where to request it.

**Limitations**
The authors note several boundary conditions: the theory assumes reciprocal informational dependence between teams, alignment between team goals and system goals, and tasks in which source teams can anticipate who needs what information. They also acknowledge that low variability in reactive information sharing may have underpowered the test of Hypothesis 1b. The laboratory setting simplified field realities such as long-standing relationships, power and status differences, organizational politics, and stronger naturally occurring identities.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine temporal sequences of proactive and reactive information sharing rather than aggregated information-sharing totals. It could also study identity asymmetries in field multiteam systems where identities evolve over longer periods and where status, power, politics, and goal misalignment interact with social identity. The authors further suggest research on the dynamic nature of identity asymmetries, including how initial asymmetries and interaction patterns may shift component teams' identity foci over time.
