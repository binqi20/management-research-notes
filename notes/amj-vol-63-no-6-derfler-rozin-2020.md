# Motivation Purity Bias: Expression of Extrinsic Motivation Undermines Perceived Intrinsic Motivation and Engenders Bias in Selection Decisions

**Abstract**
Organizational selection decisions often involve an exchange of information between candidates and decision makers as to why candidates are motivated to work in the given position. Drawing on popular management myths as our overarching framework, we theorize that candidates' expressions of extrinsic motivation lead decision makers to infer that the candidate is less intrinsically motivated, in turn engendering bias against such candidates. We term this effect motivation purity bias, and argue that it emerges despite ample evidence, which we review, that penalizing expressed extrinsic motivation is not only unfair to candidates but also counterproductive from the standpoint of maximizing future employee performance. Four studies conducted among hiring managers and business school students yield support for our theory. We discuss implications for fairness and efficiency of organizational selection decisions, as well as for prospects of developing a more balanced view of intrinsic and extrinsic motivation in management research and practice.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether decision makers penalize job candidates who express extrinsic motivation because they infer that such candidates are less intrinsically motivated. It focuses on whether this "motivation purity bias" affects selection decisions even though the authors argue that expressed extrinsic motivation should not be treated as a negative signal in salaried employment contexts.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Candidates' expressed extrinsic motivation in cover letters, interview scripts, videos, or transcripts; expressed intrinsic motivation is manipulated or measured as a comparison/control.
- DV(s): Perceived intrinsic motivation, binary selection decisions, and continuous candidate ratings.
- Mediators: Perceived intrinsic motivation is the theorized and tested mediator linking expressed extrinsic motivation to selection outcomes.
- Moderators: Expressed intrinsic motivation and decision-maker work experience are examined, but the paper reports that the bias generally persists regardless of candidates' expressed intrinsic motivation and is no less pronounced among more experienced decision makers.

The mechanism is that decision makers draw on a management myth that treats extrinsic and intrinsic motivation as antagonistic. When a candidate signals interest in salary, benefits, flexibility, or similar extrinsic job features, decision makers infer lower intrinsic motivation, and lower perceived intrinsic motivation then reduces candidate evaluations and selection likelihood. The paper tests this process across naturally written cover letters, experimental interview scripts, and hiring-manager evaluations of video or transcript stimuli.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces motivation purity bias as a selection-bias mechanism rooted in naive beliefs about motivation rather than stereotypes about social categories. It integrates motivation research with organizational decision-making bias research by showing that decision makers' beliefs about candidates' motives can shape consequential hiring judgments. It also contributes to research on the science-practice gap by arguing that outdated or simplified management ideas can persist in managerial judgment even when later evidence points in a different direction.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that organizations should reduce opportunities for decision makers to penalize benign discussion of extrinsic job features, for example by making salary, benefits, and flexibility information available early and limiting unnecessary motive-based interpretation during selection. It also implies that training or selection-process design should correct the assumption that extrinsic interest necessarily signals low intrinsic motivation. For candidates, the authors note the uncomfortable implication that openly volunteering high extrinsic motivation may trigger bias rather than provide useful information to employers.

**Limitations**
The authors describe the investigation as the first test of the phenomenon and state that more research is needed on generalizability, robustness, and actionable boundary conditions. They used multiple methods and samples, but the research was largely experimental and did not include access to real hiring notes or naturally occurring interview records. The paper also leaves open whether motivation purity bias harms some candidates more than others, especially those with stronger needs for money, time flexibility, or other extrinsic job features.

**Future Research**
Future research should test motivation purity bias with real hiring notes, real interviews, and other consequential organizational decisions. The authors call for work on whether the bias disproportionately harms candidates who need or must discuss extrinsic job features, and whether it is stronger for particular candidate groups such as women. They also encourage broader research on naive views of motivation and the possibility that people undervalue extrinsic motivation in domains beyond selection.
