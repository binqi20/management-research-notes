# Discovery as an Abductive Mechanism for Reorienting Habits within Organizational Change

**Abstract**
Process studies of organizational and strategic change have drawn attention to the dynamics that generate such change, but we still know little about how discovery unfolds within change. A field study of efforts to create a new system for delivering inpatient medical care revealed that surprises and discoveries, and constructively oriented responses to them, occurred continuously throughout organizational change, not merely at the outset. Seeking to understand this empirical puzzle, I drew on the concept of abduction from pragmatism and organizational studies. This study makes two contributions to theory about the relation between discovery and change. First, I develop a framework that explicates the central role of discovery as an abductive mechanism that enables participants to reorient prevailing habits. Analyses reveal discovery to operate through what I call “abduction sequences,” or loosely connected and overlapping episodes of creative social activity. Three key motors and their attendant feelings drive discovery via abduction sequences: surprise, doubt, and inquiry. Second, I provide a methodology for use in future research on discovery. Specifically, I propose abduction sequences as a useful analytic means for examining discovery within change and other inquiry processes, such as innovation and learning, that generate fundamentally new ways of working.

**Research Question**
How did discovery unfold within the organizational change process? The paper studies how DeltaCare participants reoriented prevailing habits of belief and action while creating a new inpatient care delivery model.

**Mechanism Process**
- Indeterminate situation: Participants first encountered surprising difficulty mapping and walking care flows, which exposed fragmented care delivery and patient hardship.
- Abduction sequences: Discovery unfolded through five interdependent sequences: engaging the indeterminate situation, deepening its significance, constructing an imagined configuration, trying on new habits, and negotiating new habits.
- Motors of discovery: Surprise, doubt, and inquiry moved participants from interrupted prevailing beliefs toward new actionable beliefs.
- Constructively oriented responses: Participants sustained discovery by halting activity, reflecting on what they did not yet know, involving patients and clinicians, experimenting with Trio and tollgates, and revising contested practices.
- Habit reorientation: Discovery helped participants move away from independent professional habits toward collaborative bedside care and negotiated interdependence.

The process is abductive rather than a linear implementation mechanism. Surprises disrupted settled beliefs about care delivery; doubt made those beliefs problematic; inquiry enabled participants to imagine, test, and negotiate new ways of working. Across the five sequences, discovery operated continuously through organizational change rather than only at the outset.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to organizational change theory by explaining discovery as an abductive mechanism through which participants reorient prevailing habits of belief and action. It theorizes abduction sequences as loosely connected and overlapping episodes of creative social activity driven by surprise, doubt, and inquiry. It also contributes a methodology for future process research by proposing abduction sequences as an analytic means for examining discovery in change, innovation, learning, and other inquiry processes.

**Practical Implication**
For managers and change participants, the study implies that surprise and doubt should not be treated only as obstacles to implementation. Change work can benefit when participants pause to examine puzzling situations, involve those affected by the work, and use constructive inquiry to test and negotiate new practices. The DeltaCare case also suggests that leaders, OD support, and lean tools may help discovery when they are used to surface and explore problems rather than merely enforce predetermined solutions.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a single medium-sized health system and a system-level change initiative in an organization that did not face immediate survival pressure. DeltaCare also had a credible OD function, senior leadership involvement, and recently introduced lean management, all of which may have shaped members' ability to sustain discovery. The author notes that these contextual features may limit transferability to more time-pressured settings or organizations without similar support for inquiry.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine discovery in time-pressured environments, including crisis or urgent radical-change settings. The paper also calls for research on leaders who are disposed to foster rather than derail discovery, and on whether intentionally incorporating discovery into lean management or other prescriptive methods changes their effects. Additional studies could analyze how political, cultural, and emotional dynamics foster or derail discovery processes within change.
