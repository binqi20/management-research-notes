# Too Hot to Handle and Too Valuable to Drop: An Expanded Conceptualization of Firms’ Reactions to Exchange Partner Misconduct

**Abstract**
Multiple theories addressing firms’ reactions to exchange partner misconduct coalesce to depict a trade-off. On the one hand, maintaining commitments to transgressors poses negative spillover risks, so theories posit firms are more likely to avoid such risks by ending commitments as negative spillover rises. On the other hand, exchange relationships often create embedded value, so theorizing also posits firms are more likely to avoid risking loss by maintaining commitments as relationships are more embedded. We argue this “maintain or end” choice oversimplifies a complex situation in which there are mixed gambles (i.e., choices offering both positive and negative outcomes). We integrate theorizing on mixed gambles and negative spillover to develop a broader conceptualization of alternative strategies for firms to reconcile these risk trade-offs beyond a binary “maintain or end” reaction, theorizing firms may: (a) increase or decrease commitments to transgressors, (b) hedge against risks by adding new partners while maintaining existing ones, or (c) “boomerang” by restarting exchange after previously ending commitments. Using firms’ relationships with politicians accused of misconduct to test our arguments, findings support our theorizing. Collectively, we offer a more complete understanding of firms’ reactions to exchange partner misconduct, extending theory and practical knowledge in multiple ways.

**Research Question**
How do firms react to exchange partner misconduct when maintaining the relationship creates association risks but ending it creates detachment risks? The paper asks whether firms' reactions extend beyond a binary maintain-or-end choice to include altering commitment levels, hedging by adding partners, and boomeranging back to a partner after temporarily ending commitment.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Relational embeddedness with the transgressing exchange partner and relational uncertainty after misconduct.
- DV(s): Ending commitment, partner commitment amount, hedging with a competitor, and boomerang commitment after first ending commitment.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Relational embeddedness is theorized to moderate the associations between relational uncertainty and partner commitment, hedging, and boomerang behavior.

The mechanism is a mixed-gamble response to exchange partner misconduct. Relational embeddedness increases the upside of preserving a valuable relationship, while relational uncertainty increases concern that the partner may not be able or willing to deliver future benefits and may expose the firm to negative spillover. Firms therefore manage the trade-off by changing contribution levels, adding a competitor relationship while maintaining the original one, or temporarily ending and later restarting the relationship. The evidence supports positive effects of embeddedness on partner commitment and boomerang behavior and a negative effect of uncertainty on partner commitment; it also supports uncertainty increasing hedging, while several hypothesized moderation effects are not supported.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends negative spillover research by moving beyond the dominant maintain-or-end framing of reactions to exchange partner misconduct. It integrates mixed gambles with negative spillover, relational embeddedness, and relational uncertainty to theorize a broader set of strategic options: altering commitments, hedging, and boomeranging. It also adds temporality to misconduct-response theory by showing that ending a relationship need not be permanent.

**Practical Implication**
The paper suggests that firms facing exchange partner misconduct should consider a broader menu of responses than simply staying or exiting. Managers can reduce exposure while preserving potential value by decreasing commitment, maintaining a relationship while adding alternatives, or temporarily pausing and later restarting exchange when conditions change. The authors' post-hoc evidence also suggests that political-relationship portfolios may shape firms' exposure to spillover from any single misconduct event.

**Limitations**
The empirical context is corporate political activity, so the authors note that generalizability should be tested in other exchange settings such as buyer-supplier relationships or industry supply chains. The study observes publicly visible campaign-contribution relationships, which may miss covert or less observable exchange relationships. The theory focuses on relational uncertainty and does not theorize stigma anxiety, partly because of measurement difficulties.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other strategies firms use to manage negative spillover, including partner substitution and impression-management responses after associations with transgressors become public. It could test the model in other misconduct contexts, such as supplier misconduct in petroleum or automotive supply chains. The authors also call for research on when relational uncertainty is more influential than relational embeddedness, why hedging behaves differently from other responses, and how stigma anxiety relates to alternative risk-management strategies.
