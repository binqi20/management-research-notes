# Undertrusted, Overtrusted, or Just Right? The Fairness of (In)Congruence between Trust Wanted and Trust Received

**Abstract**
The nascent literature on feeling trusted has focused on the notion that it is a benefit to employees and their organizations, with several studies demonstrating a link to improved job performance. It is not surprising, therefore, that the prevailing assumption is that employees will react positively when their supervisors trust them more as opposed to less. Recent research, however, has suggested that the benefits of feeling trusted are accompanied by strains, which some employees may be unwilling to bear. Drawing on seminal theorizing on fairness, we propose that employees will perceive their supervisors as being more mindful of their needs—acting more fairly—when there is a fit between trust wanted and trust received, even when the fit is at low levels of trust. By extension, when trust received exceeds or falls short of an employee’s desire for trust, the employee should perceive the supervisor as less fair. We build a model in which the overall fairness perceptions resulting from the trust wanted–received interplay influence employee performance. Our model is supported by a multisource, three-wave field study and an experiment.

**Research Question**
When employees receive more or less supervisor trusting behavior than they want, do they judge the supervisor as less fair, and do those fairness perceptions explain downstream task performance and citizenship behavior? The paper also asks whether congruent high wanted/high received trust is perceived as fairer than congruent low wanted/low received trust.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Congruence and incongruence between trust wanted and trust received; deficient trust, excess trust, high-high fit, and low-low fit.
- DV(s): Overall fairness and need consideration; downstream task performance and citizenship behavior in Study 1.
- Mediators: Overall fairness is the predicted mediator; need consideration is used as a direct operationalization of the active fairness ingredient and in supplemental mediation tests.
- Moderators: None formally tested; the high-high versus low-low comparison is tested as a response-surface feature rather than as a separate moderator.

The mechanism is a need-rule fairness process. Trusting behaviors are treated as socioemotional resources that vary in how much employees want them, so deficiency and excess both signal that the supervisor is not sufficiently mindful of the employee's needs. Congruence between wanted and received trust increases perceived overall fairness, with high-high fit judged especially fair because it requires more active supervisor effort. Those fairness perceptions then support higher supervisor-rated task performance and citizenship behavior through the social-exchange logic linking fair treatment to reciprocation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the prevailing assumption in the feeling-trusted literature that more supervisor trust is uniformly beneficial for employees. It introduces overtrust and undertrust as meaningful forms of trust misfit, showing that low received trust can be fair when low trust is wanted and that excess trust can be unfair when it violates the employee's needs.

The study also foregrounds the neglected need rule in fairness theory and links it to person-environment fit. By shifting from trusting intentions to trusting behaviors, it shows why employee reactions to being trusted depend on the match between supervisor behavior and employee preference, not simply the absolute amount of trust received.

**Practical Implication**
Supervisors should not assume that allocating as much trust as possible will always improve employee reactions or performance. Trusting behaviors are effortful and can impose demands, so supervisors need to attend to whether employees actually want those responsibilities and whether they have enough support to carry them.

The findings suggest that overtrusting employees who do not want additional trusting behaviors may be worse than wasted effort because it can reduce perceived fairness and harm performance. Supervisors may be better served by increasing the support and developmental benefits that make employees more willing to accept higher trust.

**Limitations**
Study 1 measured trust wanted and trust received from employees, so the design cannot show whether employees' perceptions aligned with supervisors' trusting intentions. The field study also did not control for distributive, procedural, interpersonal, and informational justice rule adherence, although Study 2 experimentally addressed this concern with alternative-mechanism checks.

The model is largely agnostic about time beyond the three-wave field design and the scenario experiment. It does not directly examine how employees recalibrate desired trust over time, how trust levels are negotiated between employees and supervisors, or why some employees want less trust.

**Future Research**
Future studies could compare supervisor-rated trusting intentions with employee perceptions of trusting behavior to test whether and when the two diverge. Research could also examine whether other justice rules interact with need-rule adherence, such as whether high informational or procedural justice offsets the unfairness of unwanted trust.

Additional work could study trust recalibration and negotiation over time, antecedents of lower trust wanted, and other outcomes of overtrust or undertrust. The paper specifically points to possibilities such as unwelcome obligations, need for achievement, need for impact, engagement, self-esteem, and spillovers from excess autonomy.
