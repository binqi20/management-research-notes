# Do the Hustle! Empowerment from Side-Hustles and Its Effects on Full-Time Work Performance

**Abstract**
Side-hustles, income-generating work performed alongside full-time jobs, are increasingly common as the gig economy provides opportunities for employees to perform supplementary work. Although scholars have suggested that side-hustles conflict with full-time work performance, we assert that psychological empowerment from side-hustles enriches full-time work performance. We argue that side-hustle complexity—the motivating characteristics of side-hustles—positively relates to empowerment and that side-hustle motives moderate this relationship. A study of 337 employees supports these assertions. We then investigate the spillover of side-hustle empowerment to full-time work performance in a 10-day experience-sampling method study of 80 employee–coworker dyads. We address an affective pathway in which daily side-hustle empowerment enriches full-time work performance through side-hustle engagement and positive affect at work. We also consider a cognitive pathway wherein side-hustle empowerment distracts from full-time work performance through side-hustle engagement and attention residue—persistent cognitions about side-hustles during full-time work. Overall, performance enrichment from side-hustles was stronger than performance conflict. We also consider affective shift from full-time work to side-hustles, finding negative affect from full-time work strengthens the relationship between side-hustle empowerment and engagement. Combined, our two studies examine the source of side-hustle empowerment and how side-hustle empowerment influences affective and cognitive experiences during full-time work.

**Research Question**
What makes side-hustles psychologically empowering, and how does empowerment from side-hustles spill over into full-time work performance? The paper asks whether side-hustle complexity and side-hustle motives shape empowerment, and whether daily empowerment enriches or conflicts with full-time work through affective and cognitive pathways.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Study 1: side-hustle complexity and side-hustle motives. Study 2: daily side-hustle empowerment and full-time work negative affect.
- DV(s): Study 1: side-hustle empowerment. Study 2: daily full-time work performance, with side-hustle engagement, positive affect, and attention residue modeled as intermediate outcomes.
- Mediators: Side-hustle engagement, positive affect within full-time work, and attention residue within full-time work.
- Moderators: Study 1: self-enhancement, self-transcendence, openness to change, and conservation motives were theorized, but only self-enhancement, self-transcendence, and conservation significantly moderated the side-hustle complexity-empowerment relationship. Study 2: negative affect within full-time work earlier in the day.

The mechanism combines enrichment and conflict. More complex side-hustles give workers opportunities for autonomy, skill use, task identity, significance, and feedback, which are expected to support psychological empowerment, especially when some motives make workers more responsive to those opportunities. Daily empowerment then increases side-hustle engagement; the affective component spills into full-time work as positive affect and improves performance, while the cognitive component produces attention residue that can distract from full-time work. The authors find that both pathways operate, but the positive affect pathway is stronger overall, and negative affect from full-time work strengthens later side-hustle engagement through affective shift.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts side-hustle research away from the assumption that side work mainly drains resources and harms full-time work. It uses role enrichment theory to show that side-hustle empowerment can generate resources that carry into full-time work, while still modeling attention residue as a conflicting pathway.

It also extends job characteristics theory to side-hustles by showing that side-hustle complexity and motives jointly explain empowerment. Finally, it extends affective shift theorizing across work domains, not only within a single workday or single job context.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should be cautious about blanket assumptions that side-hustles are necessarily harmful to full-time work performance. The findings suggest that side-hustles can produce empowerment and positive affect that benefit full-time work, although managers still need to account for attention residue and other workplace distractions.

For employees, the results imply that the type of side-hustle and the reason for pursuing it matter. Side-hustles with higher motivating potential may be more likely to generate empowerment, but employees still need to weigh these benefits against other uses of limited nonwork time, including family, leisure, volunteering, and sleep.

**Limitations**
The studies use survey designs, so causal inference remains limited even though the authors use temporal separation, source separation, previous-day controls, and theoretically grounded ordering. Study 1 relies on worker perceptions of side-hustle characteristics rather than objective work-characteristics data.

The message-board recruitment strategy may have attracted people especially interested in side-hustles, and the authors could not know how many eligible workers saw the postings but did not participate. The model also does not provide a clear account of hours worked on side-hustles, because side-hustle hours had small or difficult-to-interpret indirect effects and may be endogenous to engagement.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how employees craft fit between side-hustles and full-time work, including whether side-hustles supplement or complement unmet needs from full-time jobs. Research could also study congruence in knowledge and skills across side-hustles and full-time work, because overlap may provide training benefits but may also reduce detachment from full-time work.

The authors also call for alternative theoretical perspectives, including challenge-hindrance stress, conservation of resources, and boundary theory. Future designs could use objective work-characteristics data, coworker ratings of side-hustle characteristics, experimental ESM, or before-and-after designs following workers as they adopt side-hustles.
