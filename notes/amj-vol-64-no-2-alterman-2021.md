# Best Not to Know: Pay Secrecy, Employee Voluntary Turnover, and the Conditioning Effect of Distributive Justice

**Abstract**
Building on uncertainty management theory, we develop and test a model explicating how and when secrecy in pay communication may affect employee turnover-related outcomes (i.e., employee turnover intentions and firm voluntary turnover rates). Underlying this model is the notion that employees triangulate perceptions of pay secrecy (i.e., a pay-related procedural justice cue that also reflects uncertainty) with their own or others' perceptions of distributive justice as a basis for assessing organizational trustworthiness, with the latter serving as an important driver of voluntary turnover intentions and behavior. Results of two studies (Study 1 at the individual level and Study 2 at the firm level) indicate that, rather than being universal, the relationship between pay secrecy and turnover is contingent upon perceptions of distributive justice, with turnover intentions (at the individual level via organizational trustworthiness) and voluntary turnover rates (at the firm level) differentially affected by pay secrecy under conditions of higher and lower levels of distributive justice. These findings suggest an important extension to organizational justice theories; namely that, when procedural justice cues are confounded with uncertainty (as they are with pay secrecy), the assumed compounding interaction between procedural and distributive justice cues may be replaced by a more antagonistic interaction.

**Research Question**
How and when does pay secrecy affect employee turnover-related outcomes, and does distributive justice condition the relationship between pay secrecy, organizational trustworthiness, turnover intentions, and firm voluntary turnover rates?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Pay secrecy perceptions in Study 1 and pay secrecy practice in Study 2.
- DV(s): Turnover intentions in Study 1 and firm voluntary turnover rate in Study 2.
- Mediators: Organizational trust in Study 1.
- Moderators: Distributive justice perceptions in Study 1 and distributive justice climate in Study 2.

The mechanism is that pay secrecy functions as a procedural justice cue that is also confounded with uncertainty. Under uncertainty management theory, employees triangulate pay secrecy with distributive justice to assess organizational trustworthiness. When distributive justice is high, lower pay secrecy provides clearer evidence of fair pay-related treatment and is associated with higher trust and lower turnover-related outcomes; when distributive justice is low, pay secrecy can preserve trust at the individual level by allowing employees to discount aversive fairness information, although this protective pattern did not become significant for firm voluntary turnover rates in Study 2.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends organizational justice theory by showing that procedural and distributive justice cues do not always compound when the procedural cue is also uncertain. It positions organizational trustworthiness as a global fairness judgment linking pay secrecy and turnover-related outcomes, and it extends pay secrecy research from individual attitudes to firm-level voluntary turnover rates.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations considering pay transparency should attend to employees' distributive justice perceptions rather than assuming that transparency or secrecy has a uniform turnover effect. The findings suggest that higher pay secrecy is especially risky when employees generally perceive pay outcomes as fair, whereas additional pay-range or pay-procedure information may help reduce uncertainty when full transparency is not feasible.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study excludes pay level transparency because of lack of variance, relies partly on self-report measures, and may have limited generalizability beyond Chinese or East Asian contexts. They also acknowledge possible reverse causality, a single manager rating for firm pay secrecy practice, limited firm-level sample size, and the absence of firm-level organizational trust as a mediator.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine pay level transparency, multiple justice dimensions, cross-cultural generalizability, other employee populations, enterprise ownership differences, and additional firm-level controls such as union status, diversity, pay-for-performance policies, and organizational climate. The authors also call for stronger longitudinal designs, multiple manager ratings, firm-level mediating mechanisms, justice climate strength, supervisor-focused justice climate, other outcomes such as performance and counterproductive behavior, and turnover quality among good versus poor performers.
