# How Technological Overlap between Spinouts and Parent Firms Affects Corporate Venture Capital Investments in Spinouts: The Role of Competitive Tension

**Abstract**
Drawing on the literature on entrepreneurship and competitive dynamics, we investigate how technological overlap between spinouts and parent firms influences corporate investors’ decisions regarding investments in spinouts. We suggest that a high level of technological overlap between a spinout and its parent firm deters potential corporate investors from making an investment in the spinout because of competitive tension arising from anticipated hostile actions by the parent firm. We further suggest that these negative effects are contingent on the tradeoffs between competitive risks and benefits. Our findings show that the negative effects are amplified when parent firms have a strong litigiousness in claiming their intellectual property rights. However, we find that the negative effects are mitigated when corporate investors can expect benefits from gaining indirect access to parent firms’ technological knowledge. Using a sample of corporate venture capital investments in the U.S. medical device industry, we find evidence that supports our hypotheses.

**Research Question**
How does technological overlap between a spinout and its parent firm affect a corporate investor's likelihood of investing in the spinout? The paper also asks when this negative relationship is amplified by competitive risks or mitigated by competitive benefits from access to parent-firm knowledge.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Technological overlap between a spinout and its parent firm (TOSP); in the baseline comparison, spinout status with high versus low TOSP.
- DV(s): Corporate venture capital investment in the venture or spinout.
- Mediators: No formal mediator is estimated; competitive tension from anticipated hostile parent-firm actions is the theorized mechanism.
- Moderators: Parent firm litigiousness amplifies the negative TOSP effect; technological overlap between the corporate investor and parent firm (TOCP) and parent firm innovativeness mitigate it.

The proposed mechanism is a competitive-tension process. When a spinout's technology overlaps strongly with its parent firm's knowledge base, corporate investors anticipate that the parent firm may retaliate through legal or market-based actions, making the investment riskier. That risk is stronger when the parent firm is litigious, but it can be offset when the corporate investor expects strategic benefits from indirectly accessing a competitor's knowledge through the spinout.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends employee entrepreneurship research by showing that technological inheritance can be a liability for spinouts, not only a resource endowment or reputational advantage. It identifies a downside of spinout-parent similarity: overlap may trigger competitive tension that reduces external corporate investment.

The paper also contributes to competitive dynamics by theorizing the transitivity of competitive tension through interfirm networks. Rather than treating competitive tension only as a dyadic rival relationship, it shows how tension between a parent firm and a spinout can shape a third party's decision to form an investment relationship.

**Practical Implication**
For corporate investors, the findings indicate that spinout investments involve a balance between access to useful external technologies and competitive risks from parent-firm reactions. Spinout-parent technological overlap, parent-firm litigiousness, and the investor's potential benefit from indirect access to parent-firm knowledge are central conditions in that balance.

For spinouts, the results imply that close technological inheritance from a parent firm may make CVC funding harder to secure when corporate investors anticipate retaliation risks. Parent firms' reputations for enforcing intellectual property rights can shape how attractive their spinouts look to outside corporate investors.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting is the U.S. medical device industry, where intellectual property protection and patent litigation are especially salient, so the risk-benefit balance may differ in industries with weaker formal protection regimes. The paper also cannot provide decisive performance implications for whether corporate investors actually create value by accessing spinout knowledge.

The analysis focuses on patented technological knowledge and CVC investment ties, so it does not capture the full range of competitive actions, tacit knowledge conflicts, or governance choices in other forms of external knowledge acquisition. The dyad-level data also cannot fully represent the network structure of triadic competitive tension.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether the same mechanism operates in industries where parent-firm retaliation has weaker deterrent effects or where corporate investors are less sensitive to intellectual property litigation. It could also test when expected benefits from spinout knowledge translate into better corporate-investor performance.

Additional work could study competitive tension across other governance modes, such as R&D alliances, acquisitions, or other external knowledge acquisition relationships. The paper also points to future research on ongoing post-founding knowledge inheritance through founders' social ties with former colleagues at parent firms.
