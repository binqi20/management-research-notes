# Antecedents and Consequences of Employee and Asset Restructuring

**Abstract**
We examine two forms of employment and asset restructuring: downsizing and upsizing. Guided by institutional theory, we focus on two important questions: (a) are the antecedents of restructuring common across downsizing and upsizing? and (b) are the performance consequences of such restructuring related to prior firm performance? To answer these questions, we use primary data from companies listed on the New York Stock Exchange over a 37-year period (1980–2016). Our results suggest that six key antecedents play a prominent role in both downsizing and upsizing, although they differ in magnitude and direction in how they affect each form of restructuring. However, in both instances they reflect efficiency considerations (company performance and managerial foresight), as well as institutional forces (the economy, political uncertainty, industry, and technology), just as neo-institutional theory suggests. In addition, prior performance plays a central role in restructuring decisions, in both the upsizing and downsizing of assets and employees. The evidence suggests that it is not just current-year performance but also the trend in company performance over the prior two years that forecasts subsequent firm performance; thus, our findings extend and refine institutional theory.

**Research Question**
Are the antecedents of restructuring common across downsizing and upsizing, and are the performance consequences of employee and asset restructuring related to prior firm performance? The paper asks these questions for two restructuring forms (downsizing and upsizing) and two restructuring types (employees and assets).

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Company performance, managerial foresight, performance of the economy, political uncertainty, industry performance/competition, technology, and prior firm-performance trends.
- DV(s): Firm-level decisions to downsize or upsize employees, assets, or both; ex post industry-adjusted stock market returns after restructuring.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper as a separate moderation model. The paper compares hypotheses and regressions across restructuring categories and prior-performance trends to explain ex post performance patterns.

Guided by institutional theory, the authors argue that restructuring is shaped jointly by efficiency considerations and institutional forces. Company performance and managerial foresight capture normative/efficiency pressures; the economy and political uncertainty capture coercive pressures; and industry effects plus technology capture mimetic processes. The evidence links these antecedents to downsizing and upsizing decisions, then links prior performance trends to subsequent industry-adjusted returns: restructuring after difficult performance is associated with stronger ex post performance than restructuring under more benign conditions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends institutional theory by showing that efficiency considerations and institutional forces jointly predict both downsizing and upsizing, not only employee downsizing. It also refines institutional theory by showing that trends in prior company performance, not only contemporaneous performance, forecast subsequent firm performance after restructuring. The design further highlights time as a research-design issue because market responses to restructuring vary across the 37-year sampling period.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should consider company performance, managerial foresight, the economy, political uncertainty, industry competition, and technology before restructuring, with attention to recent and prior performance trends. The paper cautions against treating employee downsizing as an easy lever to restore profitability because it is frequent in the sample but has the lowest payoff among downsizing categories. It also suggests that upsizing does not automatically produce better stock market performance when undertaken from high profitability.

**Limitations**
The study uses archival data rather than an experimental design, so it identifies statistically significant associations rather than causal effects. The sample consists of publicly traded firms, and privately held firms may behave differently. The authors also could not observe different downsizing motives and goals, such as declining demand versus efficiency improvement.

**Future Research**
Future studies should account explicitly for sampling periods because restructuring-performance relationships vary over time. Qualitative research using in-depth case studies and interviews with senior executives could provide richer evidence about unmeasured variables, tacit knowledge, and firm-specific restructuring decisions. Future work could also examine motives and goals for downsizing more directly.
