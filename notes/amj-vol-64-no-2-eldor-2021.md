# Leading by Doing: Does Leading by Example Impact Productivity and Service Quality?

**Abstract**
This paper presents a research model for the creation of organizational value through leading by example. I argue that leading by example serves as a unique value-creation driver by enhancing engagement, productivity, and service quality at the business level. Using a strategy-based resource management framework, I also argue that there are synergies between leading by example and the manifestation of organizational core values. My theory is grounded in the strategy-based notion that combining organizational resources—leading by example (practical element) and organizational core values (conceptual element)—optimizes employee engagement, which in turn enhances productivity and service quality. I examine my model empirically using two-time-point panel data derived from three different sources (employees, financial data, and secret shoppers) in a sample of 233 retail stores. The findings provide evidence that leading by example improves productivity and service quality (after taking into account the potential influence of charismatic leadership). The indirect effect of leading by example on productivity and service quality (through engagement) is even stronger when manifestation of organizational core values is high. Contributions to theory, research, and practice are presented.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether leading by example creates business-level value by improving productivity and service quality at the store level. It also asks whether employee engagement mediates this effect and whether written manifestation of organizational core values strengthens the engagement pathway.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leading by example at the store level.
- DV(s): Store-level productivity and service quality.
- Mediators: Store-level employee engagement.
- Moderators: Core value manifestation, defined as employees' perceptions that written workplace statements communicate the retail chain's core values.

The mechanism combines strategy-based resource management with leadership and social learning arguments. Leading by example is treated as a practical organizational resource: by visibly performing employee activities, leaders clarify desired behavior, support social learning, and encourage employees to invest more fully in their roles. Core value manifestation adds a conceptual resource that explains why the demonstrated behavior matters for the organization's goals, strengthening engagement and, through engagement, productivity and service quality.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops leading by example as a distinct leadership construct rather than treating it as a catchphrase or a subset of charismatic leadership. It extends leadership research by showing that a doing-oriented leadership practice has business-level associations with productivity and service quality beyond a rhetorically oriented leadership style.

The paper also contributes to resource management theory by showing how practical and conceptual resources can work together. Leading by example and core value manifestation jointly enhance engagement, linking micro-level employee motivation to store-level performance outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat leading by example as a substantive leadership practice rather than a peripheral personal style. The findings imply that leaders can support productivity, service quality, and employee engagement by visibly demonstrating expected frontline behaviors.

The paper also suggests that leading by example works better when paired with clear written communication of organizational core values. Leadership training and promotion criteria should therefore value managers who both model desired behaviors and connect those behaviors to organizational purpose.

**Limitations**
The study uses multisource, three-year panel data and a three-month lag, but the author states that this design still does not fully support causal inference. Employees' subjective ratings may also imperfectly capture actual leading by example and may be affected by social desirability or employee-leader relations, although anonymity and confidentiality were used to reduce these concerns.

The study examines leading by example mainly in relation to charismatic leadership, while other leadership characteristics such as ethical leadership and behavioral integrity may also affect business performance. It is also limited to one retail chain of medium-sized stores in Israel, which constrains direct generalization to larger organizations, other industries, and other national contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research could use experimental designs to test the causal effects of leading by example more decisively. Researchers could also examine how leading by example differs from or interacts with ethical leadership, behavioral integrity, and other leadership constructs.

The author calls for work on other ways of manifesting core values, such as leader speeches, conferences, and videos, and for further refinement of the core value manifestation construct. Future studies could also test cascading processes at higher organizational levels and examine leading by example in other contexts, such as high technology or cultures with different authority norms.
