# How Voice Emerges and Develops in Newly Formed Supervisor–Employee Dyads

**Abstract**
We develop and test theory regarding how voice emerges and develops in new supervisor– employee dyads. We propose that employees higher in proactive personality speak up more in early stages of interactions in their dyads. The subsequent path that their voice takes—that is, increasing in a virtuous positive trajectory or decreasing in a vicious negative trajectory—is determined by whether their proactive personality matches their supervisors’. For instance, when paired with supervisors who are not similarly proactive, they confront low congruence on change-related goals in the working relationship; hence, rather than building off their initial voice and developing a positive voice trajectory, frustration might lead their voice to decay in a negative trajectory. This theory is supported in a sample of 403 newly formed dyads. Employees higher in proactive personality were more likely to speak up to their supervisors immediately after beginning to work together. Supervisor–employee congruence in proactive personality predicted how voice changed in the dyads in the ensuing six months. Dyadic (in)congruence in proactive personality drove the linear (negative) positive trend in voice and enabled voice growth to pick up momentum and nonlinearly (decelerate) accelerate over time. We discuss our findings’ implications for theory and practice.

**Research Question**
How does voice emerge and develop over time in newly formed supervisor-employee dyads? The paper asks whether employees' proactive personality predicts initial voice and whether supervisor-employee congruence in proactive personality shapes later linear and nonlinear voice trajectories through perceived change goal congruence.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee proactive personality for initial voice; supervisor-employee congruence and level of congruence in proactive personality for later voice trajectories.
- DV(s): Employee voice at Time 1 and the linear and quadratic slopes of voice across Times 1-3.
- Mediators: Change goal congruence, measured from supervisors after Time 1.
- Moderators: Supervisor proactive personality as the matching or mismatching dyadic trait; high-versus-low congruent levels of proactive personality.

The paper argues that newly formed dyads initially create uncertainty and opportunity, activating proactive employees to speak up before dyadic norms have solidified. Over time, supervisors and employees become social situations for each other: congruent proactive personalities create mutual trait activation and shared change goals, supporting voice growth and acceleration. Incongruence produces frustration, perceived mismatch in change priorities, and self-deflating voice trajectories. Change goal congruence explains why proactive personality fit translates into both positive linear and nonlinear voice development.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts voice research from a snapshot view to a temporal dyadic model, showing that employee traits have stronger independent effects early in a relationship and later operate jointly with supervisor traits. It extends supervisor-employee dyad research beyond LMX by showing that voice can follow self-amplifying or self-deflating trajectories rather than merely stabilizing as affiliative relationships do. It also identifies change goal congruence as a proximal mechanism linking proactive personality fit to voice development.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should attend to the social fit between supervisors and employees, not only the technical fit between employees and job demands, because shared proactivity can support sustained voice. Supervisors cannot assume early voice will persist automatically; the paper suggests they need to cultivate repeated successful voice exchanges and recognize whether their change goals align with employees'. The findings also imply that proactivity at only one hierarchical level may be insufficient to sustain voice when adjacent-level partners do not share similar change orientations.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the China setting may shape the results because higher power distance could make supervisor traits more salient than in lower power-distance contexts. They also state that the study cannot definitively establish causality and that random assignment in quasi-experimental designs would strengthen causal inference. The temporal design modeled linear and nonlinear trends across the first six months, but voice may also shift after critical events or follow cyclical ebb-and-flow patterns not captured here.

**Future Research**
Future research should compare proactive personality fit effects across national contexts that vary in power distance and use designs with random assignment of supervisors and employees where possible. Researchers should study voice over longer horizons to identify when it reaches equilibrium and what events disrupt or reset that equilibrium. The authors also encourage work on how the basis, content, quality, and novelty of voice shift as employees move from outsider-like early perspectives to insider on-the-job experience.
