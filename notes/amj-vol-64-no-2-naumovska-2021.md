# Strength and Weakness in Numbers? Unpacking the Role of Prevalence in the Diffusion of Reverse Mergers

**Abstract**
A common prediction in research on practice diffusion is a “strength in numbers” effect (i.e., that a growing number of past adopters will increase the number of future adopters). We advance and test a theoretical perspective to explain when and how practice prevalence may also generate a “weakness in numbers” effect. Specifically, to explain the diffusion of reverse mergers—a controversial practice that allows a private firm to go public by merging with a publicly listed “shell company”—we suggest that prevalence affects their diffusion in a complex way based on two divergent social influence pathways, creating (a) a direct, positive effect of practice prevalence on potential adopters, who view prevalence as evidence of the practice’s value; and (b) an indirect, negative effect mediated through third-party evaluators (i.e., investors and the media), who view prevalence as a cause for concern and skepticism. We also highlight the utility of this theoretical framework by analyzing how a decline in the status of past adopters exerts a negative effect on diffusion through both social influence pathways. Employing structural equation modeling techniques, we find support for the hypothesized relationships, and we discuss the implications of the study for future research on practice diffusion.

**Research Question**
What does the prevalence of a controversial practice imply for its subsequent diffusion? The paper asks whether reverse merger prevalence creates only a direct "strength in numbers" effect among potential adopters or also a mediated "weakness in numbers" effect through negative third-party evaluations by investors and the media.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Number of past reverse merger adopters; proportion of past lower-status reverse merger adopters.
- DV(s): Weekly number of new reverse mergers.
- Mediators: Stock market returns for reverse merger firms; negative media coverage of reverse mergers.
- Moderators: No formal moderator is estimated in the main model; supplementary analyses test but do not support moderation by third-party evaluations.

The proposed mechanism is a competitive mediation process. Growing prevalence directly informs potential adopters that reverse mergers may be valuable, increasing adoption through informational social influence. At the same time, because reverse mergers are controversial and challenge accepted IPO norms, growing prevalence attracts skeptical attention from investors and the media, lowering stock-market evaluations, increasing negative coverage, and indirectly reducing later adoption through normative social influence.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to practice diffusion research by separating prevalence effects into two pathways that can diverge for controversial practices. It shows that prevalence can simultaneously produce a direct strength-in-numbers effect among potential adopters and an indirect weakness-in-numbers effect through third-party evaluators.

The paper also endogenizes third-party evaluations in diffusion theory. Rather than treating media and investor reactions as exogenous influences on adoption, it theorizes and tests how those reactions arise partly from the practice's own growing prevalence and from the declining status of past adopters.

**Practical Implication**
For potential adopters, the findings imply that widespread use of a controversial practice should not be read only as evidence of value or legitimacy. Reverse merger prevalence also changed how investors and media evaluated the practice, which in turn shaped the practical viability of future adoption.

For regulators, investors, and media evaluators, the study shows why rapid diffusion of a counternormative financial practice can signal both growing market demand and rising legitimacy concerns. Monitoring should therefore consider not only the number of adopters but also the status and quality signals of those adopters.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on reverse mergers in the United States, a visible and controversial financial-market practice where investor and media evaluations are consequential. Its theoretical scope is therefore strongest for practices that contravene established legitimacy pillars, are visible to third-party evaluators, and can plausibly threaten relevant actors in the organizational field.

The empirical design treats regulatory actions as controls rather than fully modeling them as mediators, and the authors note that the analyses did not allow them to adequately model regulatory actions' mediating role. The results also depend on a long enough observation window to see third-party contestation emerge after prevalence increases.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine contingencies that strengthen either the direct or mediated pathway, including differences in practice controversy, prospective adopters' vulnerability to third-party evaluations, and adopters' uncertainty about a practice's economic value. The framework could also be applied to noncontroversial practices, where the authors expect complementary rather than competitive mediation.

The paper points to research on multiwave diffusion, practice adaptation, abandonment, and symbolic abandonment of controversial practices. It also calls for work on how adopters shield controversial practices from opposing third-party evaluators through framing, persuasion, knowledge transfer, or adaptation.
