# Salient Expectations? Incongruence across Capability and Integrity Signals and Investor Reactions to Organizational Misconduct

**Abstract**
Research in signaling theory has recently begun to explore how audiences process signal sets and the incongruence across the signals within. However, prior studies have assumed homogenous compositions of signal sets, and thus unidimensional signal incongruence, although social evaluations tend to involve simultaneous processing of different dimensions. In this study, we examine audiences' responses to the interdimensional incongruence between capability and integrity signals, particularly by focusing on how the salience of positive capability signals aggravates investor reactions to organizational misconduct, a negative integrity signal. Using irregular financial restatements as the negative integrity signals and prior alliance announcements as the positive capability signals, we find that investors react more negatively to restatements by firms whose alliance announcements are more salient—that is, the firms that announce more frequently and firms that create more positive expectations from those announcements. We also find that firm size and level of diversification weaken these negative effects. We contribute to research on signaling theory, social evaluations, organizational misconduct, and alliances.

**Research Question**
How do investors respond when a firm has salient positive capability signals before revealing a negative integrity signal? The paper asks whether the salience of prior alliance announcements aggravates investor reactions to irregular financial restatements, and whether firm size and diversification weaken that reaction.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Salience of prior alliance announcements, measured as frequency of alliance announcements and extremity of investor reactions to those announcements.
- DV(s): Investor reaction to irregular financial restatements, measured as cumulative abnormal returns around the restatement.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Firm size weakens the negative effects of both frequency and extremity of alliance announcements; firm diversification weakens the frequency effect, while the extremity-by-diversification interaction is not statistically significant.

The mechanism is that irregular financial restatements act as negative integrity signals, while prior alliance announcements act as positive capability signals. When alliance announcements are salient, investors process them together with the restatement, producing interdimensional incongruence between capability and integrity. Because integrity judgments have primacy over capability judgments, investors reassess the prior capability signals through the negative integrity signal and impose larger valuation penalties. These penalties are weaker for larger firms. Diversification weakens the frequency-based salience effect, but the paper does not find a significant extremity-by-diversification interaction.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends signaling theory by shifting signal incongruence from intradimensional inconsistency among similar signals to interdimensional inconsistency between capability and integrity signals. It also contributes to social evaluations research by showing that integrity judgments can mar capability judgments, and to organizational misconduct and alliance research by showing when normally positive alliance announcements can intensify negative investor reactions.

**Practical Implication**
Managers cannot assume that making capabilities salient will protect firms when integrity failures emerge. The findings imply that firms with highly visible alliance activity may face sharper investor penalties after irregular financial restatements, especially when they are smaller or less diversified.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the empirical design cannot directly capture the microfoundational mechanism through which audiences resolve incongruence. They also focus on irregular financial restatements, investor reactions, frequency and extremity as forms of salience, and two alliance-intensive industries, which may limit generalizability to other forms of misconduct, other stakeholders, other salience mechanisms, or industries where alliances are less important.

**Future Research**
Future research could compare negative-integrity/positive-capability incongruence with other interdimensional combinations, including negative capability signals with positive integrity signals. The authors also call for experimental or policy-capturing work on audience processing, research on ambiguous misconduct, errors, accidents, non-investor stakeholders, alliance characteristics, other strategic initiatives, external shocks, and industries where alliances are less central.
