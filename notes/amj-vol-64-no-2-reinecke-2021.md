# Microfoundations of Framing: The Interactional Production of Collective Action Frames in the Occupy Movement

**Abstract**
Social movement scholars have typically focused either on how social movements strategically use collective action frames to confront targets and mobilize supporters or on how targets respond to social movements. Few have captured the interactional dynamics between the two. This oversight tends to obscure how an extant collective action frame may shift or how a new one may arise during such interactions. To address this issue, we focus on movement–target interactions and illuminate the microfoundations of framing that produce a new collective action frame. Drawing on real-time participant observations, we examine how an unintended collective action frame emerged and escalated during a year-long interaction between the Occupy London movement and St. Paul’s Cathedral, Church of England. Occupy protesters shifted from a “capitalism is crisis” frame targeting the U.K.’s financial establishment to a “what would Jesus do?” frame targeting the Church of England. We develop a process model based on the interplay of frame laminations and three situational mechanisms—emotional attachment to a frame, frame sacralization, and frame amplification—derived from an analysis of framing in movement–target interactions to explain the emergence and escalation of an unexpected collective action frame.

**Research Question**
How can the microprocesses of framing in movement-target interactions lead to the emergence and escalation of an unanticipated collective action frame? The paper asks this through the case of Occupy London, whose original "capitalism is crisis" frame targeting the financial establishment shifted into a "what would Jesus do?" frame targeting St. Paul's Cathedral and the Church of England.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: collective action frames, interactional frames, frame laminations, keying, frame breaks, misframing, grievance construction, and movement-target interaction.
- Process / phases: emergence, ascendance, and escalation of the WWJD collective action frame across eight interaction episodes from the initial encounter to the anniversary Evensong stunt.
- Situational mechanisms: emotional attachment to the frame, frame sacralization, and frame amplification through media and public audiences.
- Emergent outcome: an initially unintended religious-moral frame displaced the original anti-capitalist frame and turned a potential ally into a target.
- Boundary conditions: the model is grounded in a physically copresent, materially salient, highly public movement-target encounter.

The process begins with an accidental encounter in which St. Paul's became drawn into Occupy's activity frame after protesters were blocked from the London Stock Exchange. Misframing and frame breaks created a new grievance against St. Paul's; protesters then attached emotionally to the WWJD frame, sacralized it through Christian symbolism and the cathedral setting, and amplified it through media-facing performances. These mechanisms reinforced each other over time, making the new frame increasingly salient even though it diverted attention from Occupy's original target.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to social movement and organization theory by shifting attention from strategic deployment of ready-made collective action frames to the interactional production of frames in concrete situations. It theorizes collective action frames as emergent, contested, and endogenous to movement-target interactions rather than as context-free tools that movements simply choose and deploy.

The paper also identifies situational mechanisms that explain how interactional frames become collective action frames. Emotional attachment, sacralization, and amplification show how emotions, moral meaning, material setting, and third-party audiences can transform local interactional dynamics into a movement-level frame trajectory.

**Practical Implication**
For movement actors, targets, and intermediaries, the study shows that seemingly local interactional choices can redirect a movement's grievance, target, and public identity. Actors who treat framing as only a strategic communications problem may miss how emotional reactions, sacred meanings, material settings, and media feedback can bind participants to a frame that no one originally intended.

For organizations facing activism, the findings imply that responses such as symbolic welcome, legal distancing, public statements, or silence can become frame breaks that reshape the conflict. Managing such encounters requires attention to the interaction order, not only to formal stakeholder positions or message content.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that their model is built around a setting marked by physical copresence between a movement and its target. This may limit direct extension to virtual communities and social-media-based movements, where amplification and emotional dynamics may operate through different material and sensory conditions.

The case is also an unusually public, symbolic, and religiously charged encounter involving Occupy London and St. Paul's Cathedral. The resulting model is most directly grounded in situations where movement-target interaction is observable, emotionally intense, materially salient, and open to media amplification.

**Future Research**
Future research can examine how broader cultural meaning systems are reworked at the interactional level and how interactional meanings scale up to affect those systems. The authors specifically invite work on how collective meaning structures and micro-interactional meaning making recursively connect in other movements and contentious settings.

The paper also calls for more research on multimodal framing processes. Scholars could study how verbal, visual, material, sensory, embodied, and digital modes combine in framing, including how online platforms, building design, spatial arrangements, images, gestures, and immersive encounters shape collective action frames.
