# Workplace Interpersonal Capitalization: Employee Reactions to Coworker Positive Event Disclosures

**Abstract**
“Capitalization”—the process of sharing a personal positive event with others— provides disclosers with many advantages such as increased self-esteem, relationship quality, and relationship trust. Existing theory on capitalization has been developed within the context of close relationships where individuals typically maintain aligned outcomes. However, when outcomes are opposed such as when individuals are competing, capitalization may not be an effective relationship behavior as currently conceptualized. In this work, I examine the consequences of capitalization within the workplace, a context that is characteristically competitive. I build a model of workplace interpersonal capitalization, defined as the process of sharing personal work-related positive events with coworkers. Drawing from social comparison theory, I posit that hearing a coworker disclose work-related positive events causes an employee to experience either inspiration or envy as a function of the degree to which the disclosing coworker is viewed as a competitor. Furthermore, I explain how these inspirational and envious reactions motivate interpersonal citizenship behavior and social undermining, respectively. Overall, I establish distinct responder-based reactions and postulate competition as a key boundary condition that alters the effects of capitalization.

**Research Question**
How do employees respond when coworkers disclose personal work-related positive events, and how does perceived competition with the disclosing coworker shape responders' emotional and behavioral reactions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Workplace interpersonal capitalization, defined as coworker disclosure of personal work-related positive events.
- DV(s): Interpersonal citizenship behavior toward the discloser and social undermining of the discloser.
- Mediators: Inspiration and envy.
- Moderators: Competition or rivalry with the disclosing coworker.

The mechanism is a social-comparison process. Positive event disclosures provide upward comparison information; when competition is low, the discloser's success appears more attainable or co-beneficial, eliciting inspiration and motivating interpersonal citizenship behavior. When competition is high, the same disclosure highlights opposed outcomes, eliciting envy and motivating social undermining as a way to reduce the comparison threat.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts capitalization theory from a discloser-centered account to a responder-centered account. It shows that capitalization is not uniformly constructive in workplaces: under competition, disclosure of good fortune can backfire by producing envy and social undermining. The paper also extends workplace social comparison research by showing that privately disclosed, less visible positive events can trigger consequential comparisons.

**Practical Implication**
Employees should be attentive to competitive cues before sharing positive work events with coworkers, because the benefits of capitalization depend on whether outcomes feel aligned or opposed. Managers can reduce the risk of envy and undermining by deemphasizing competition and designing performance metrics or reward structures that support information and resource sharing.

**Limitations**
The author notes selection bias in Study 1 because participants chose which coworker to invite, and Study 1 was correlational rather than causal. Study 2 did not fully test whether personal sharing has unique effects, although the supplementary study addressed self-disclosure versus third-party disclosure. The field studies also reported relatively low levels of competition, limiting what can be inferred about highly competitive workplaces.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine how the content and type of capitalization shape responder reactions, including whether positive events reflect hard work, luck, first news, or information the responder already knew. The paper also calls for work on who does or does not capitalize at work, and what contexts encourage or discourage positive event disclosure.
