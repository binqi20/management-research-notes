# Deploying Human Capital Resources: Accentuating Effects of Situational Alignment and Social Capital Resources

**Abstract**
Researchers and organizational leaders alike have long known that securing human capital (HC) is critical for organizational success. The bulk of research in this domain has addressed issues regarding the accrual of HC stocks. However, there has been a dearth of research exploring the underlying mechanisms through which accrued HC stocks translate to competitive advantage. We expand on Wolfson and Mathieu’s (2018) human capital resource complementarity (HCRC) theory by incorporating the deployment of human capital resources (HCRs) as well as the role of social capital resources (SCRs) in the form of members’ shared team task-specific experience in predicting performance. We empirically test HCRC theory-derived hypotheses using a sample of 448 cyclists from 17 world teams who competed in 26 races during the 2015 season of the Union Cycliste Internationale World Tour. Our results revealed that higher-level HCR stocks indirectly led to deployed HCR performance through deployed HCR alignment with situational characteristics. Furthermore, SCRs acted as a critical boundary condition for the value of aligning deployed HCRs with situational characteristics such that the alignment of deployed HCRs was only advantageous when paired with relatively high levels of SCRs. Implications for future theory building, research, and practice related to HCR deployment are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do accumulated human capital resource stocks translate into performance through deployment decisions across changing task situations? The paper asks whether aligning deployed HCRs with race characteristics improves start-list performance and whether social capital resources, operationalized as shared team task-specific experience, strengthen that alignment-performance relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Team-level HCR stocks; deployed HCRs in mountain and sprint competencies; race-level mountain and sprint characteristics.
- DV(s): Deployed HCR performance, measured as mountain points and sprint points earned by team start lists.
- Mediators: Deployed HCR alignment with situational characteristics is the indirect pathway linking accumulated HCR stocks to performance.
- Moderators: Race characteristics moderate stock-to-deployment and deployment-to-performance relationships; deployed shared team task-specific experience moderates the value of HCR-situation alignment.

The mechanism is a cross-level deployment process. Teams with stronger HCR stocks have more options for composing start lists, but performance gains depend on whether those deployed competencies match the specific race demands. Social capital resources matter because members with greater shared task-specific experience can coordinate interdependent work more effectively, allowing aligned HCRs to become performance advantages rather than merely available talent.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends human capital resource complementarity theory by making deployment a central mechanism between HCR stocks and performance. It shifts attention from the accumulation of valuable human capital to the question of how organizations compose and recompose teams across changing situations.

It also bridges strategic human capital research and team composition research by treating start-list composition as the concrete vehicle through which HCRs are mobilized. The paper further identifies SCRs, especially shared team task-specific experience, as a boundary condition that helps explain when HCR-situation alignment yields superior performance.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should not assume that accumulating high-quality talent is sufficient for competitive advantage. They need deployment systems that match members' competencies to task demands and that preserve enough shared experience for team members to coordinate effectively under pressure.

For managers, the results imply that staffing decisions should consider both competence-task fit and prior working relationships among deployed members. HR information systems and team assignment processes can be useful when they track not only individual skills but also members' prior shared task experience.

**Limitations**
The authors note that professional cycling offers objective measures and dynamic deployment situations, but it may limit generalizability because competencies and situational demands are unusually visible and well defined. Findings may not transfer directly to settings where task demands, individual competencies, or alignment requirements are harder to observe in advance.

The study indexes competencies using prior performance and measures SCRs through shared race experience, so these are proxies rather than direct measures of underlying ability or coordination processes. The models also include relatively few predictors, leaving room for other individual and team factors such as personality, leadership, and training interventions.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine the costs and benefits of HCR deployment over time, including underutilization, development costs, burnout, and whether superstar-focused or team-wide competence strategies perform better in different contexts. The authors also suggest studying multiple team memberships as a mechanism for flexible deployment and as a possible source of role stress.

Longer temporal designs could examine how collective turnover, leavers, replacements, and dynamic team membership reconfigure both HCRs and SCRs. Future studies should also investigate patterns and antecedents of shared team task-specific experience, including whether leaders sometimes need to sacrifice short-term performance to develop future deployment options.
