# Lenient Reactions to Misconduct: Examining the Self-Conscious Process of Being Lenient to Others at Work

**Abstract**
How employees address others' misconduct is a common aspect of organizational life. Although some employees adhere to punishment standards when addressing misconduct, not all do. Instead, some are lenient. We propose that being lenient generates a self-conscious affective process that has countervailing effects for grantors. Drawing from the theory of self-conscious emotions and moral psychology, we propose that leniency elicits feelings of pride that enhance engagement and also elicits feelings of guilt that enhance exhaustion. Taken together, we propose that leniency indirectly influences energy states through self-conscious emotions. A multistudy approach was utilized to test these predictions. The results from Study 1 (a multiwave field study of police officers in the United States) and Study 2 (an experiment) largely supported our predictions. In Study 3 (an experiment), we extended our model to consider a theoretically relevant moderator. Grantors' forgiveness was predicted to impact the extent to which feelings of pride and guilt are experienced from leniency. As predicted, our results showed that the impact of leniency on pride was strengthened by forgiveness and the impact of leniency on guilt was weakened by forgiveness.

**Research Question**
How does being lenient toward others' workplace misconduct affect the leniency grantor's self-conscious emotions and subsequent energy states at work? The paper also asks whether forgiveness changes the extent to which leniency produces pride and guilt.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leniency, defined as lessening or removing a negative consequence for misconduct.
- DV(s): Engagement and exhaustion; Study 1 also examines supervisor-rated task performance in supplemental analysis.
- Mediators: Pride and guilt as self-conscious emotions.
- Moderators: Forgiveness in Study 3.

The mechanism is an affective appraisal process. Leniency aligns with an ideal-self aspiration to be benevolent, which can evoke pride and increase engagement. At the same time, leniency can conflict with an ideal-self aspiration to be rule-abiding and a good organizational steward, which can evoke guilt and increase exhaustion. Forgiveness changes this appraisal by strengthening the pride pathway and weakening the guilt pathway.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reframes leniency as a double-edged experience for the person granting it, not simply as a benevolent alternative to punishment. It integrates moral psychology and punishment perspectives by showing that the same lenient act can be appraised as both self-confirming and self-sanctioning.

The paper also extends research on workplace misconduct by shifting attention from victims, wrongdoers, and observers to the grantor who decides how to respond. It highlights self-conscious emotions as a mechanism through which employees evaluate their own workplace behavior against competing ideal-self aspirations.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should recognize that employees who address misconduct with leniency may experience a complex emotional process rather than a simple relief from punishment decisions. Support systems after misconduct should therefore consider the grantor as well as victims and wrongdoers.

The findings also suggest that forgiveness may help grantors experience the positive side of leniency while reducing its guilt-related costs. The paper cautions, however, that efforts to promote forgiveness should not encourage disregard for organizational standards.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that Study 1's time-lagged field design makes it difficult to link emotions to specific leniency events, and the month-long separation may have allowed guilt to dissipate. Study 1 also did not collect detailed information on the particular misconduct incidents, so some incidents may fall near the boundaries of the paper's misconduct definition.

The results are not fully consistent across studies, especially for the main effects of leniency on pride and guilt. Study 3 may also include selection bias because participants had to recall recent lenient and nonlenient incidents, and recall designs may favor self-serving or emotionally accessible events.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine antecedents of leniency, including close relationships with wrongdoers, unethical climates, self-oriented versus other-oriented motives, and moral identity. It should also compare leniency with punishment decisions to see whether punishers experience parallel self-conscious emotions.

The paper calls for work on how misconduct characteristics, such as severity, intent, and interpersonal versus organizational direction, shape reactions to leniency. It also suggests examining additional outcomes, including citizenship, voice, support, retaliation, avoidance, coordination, workgroup trust, team performance, and climate of hostility.
