# Employer–Employee Matching and Complementary Assets: The Role of Cross-Organization Collaborations

**Abstract**
Building on human capital theory and social capital theory, we theorize that cross-organization collaborations generate a rich and distinct source of relational capital that enhances employer–employee matches when complementary assets are important in the production process. We test our theory in the context of academic scientists where collaborations within and across organizations are common channels to access complementary assets. We find that cross-organizational collaborations are positively related to an individual’s decision to move to a previous coauthor’s organization. Additionally, moving to an organization where an individual had a direct collaboration is positively related to postmobility performance. This suggests that prior collaboration facilitates better employer–employee matches. We unpack this finding and show that the postmobility performance increase is not only driven by increased productivity with prior coauthors, it is also driven by novel collaborations with new colleagues. Together, our findings suggest that cross-organization collaborations facilitate hiring employees who can integrate well with the complementary assets of the entire unit.

**Research Question**
How do cross-organization collaborations shape employer-employee matching when complementary assets are important to productivity? The paper asks whether prior collaborations increase mobility to collaborators' organizations and whether such moves improve postmobility productivity through better matching with prior and new colleagues.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Cross-organization collaborations; mobility to a previous coauthor's organization; current colleague-new coauthors.
- DV(s): Mobility choice, postmobility productivity, postmobility citation productivity, and current colleague-new coauthor counts.
- Mediators: No formal mediator is estimated; the theorized channels are reduced information asymmetry, lower search costs, better assessment of fit with complementary assets, and brokerage into new colleague relationships.
- Moderators: No formal moderator is central to the tested model.

The mechanism is a matching process in which cross-organization collaboration creates relational capital before hiring occurs. Prior collaboration gives both the employee and the receiving organization more direct information about general human capital and fit with complementary assets, reducing uncertainty in mobility decisions. After mobility, prior collaborators also help the mover integrate with new colleagues, so productivity gains are not limited to continued work with old coauthors.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to employer-employee matching theory by showing how collaboration across organizational boundaries can reveal fit with a receiving unit's complementary assets before a move occurs. It extends strategic human capital theory by treating complementary assets as central to both selection into mobility and postmove adaptation.

The paper also refines social capital theory by distinguishing cross-organization collaboration from general social ties. The authors argue that this hybrid form of relational capital combines features of internal and external relational capital, giving richer information about both employee quality and the employee's likely fit with organizational assets.

**Practical Implication**
For managers, the findings imply that cross-organization collaborations can operate as a hiring pipeline when productivity depends on fit with complementary assets. Such collaborations provide information about whether a potential employee can work productively with the receiving organization's people and resources, not just whether the person has high general ability.

The implication is not one-sided: collaborations may also increase outbound mobility by making employees visible to better-fitting employers. The authors therefore frame collaboration investment as potentially beneficial at the collective level, especially for organizations with idiosyncratic complementary assets that can attract better-fitting hires.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the evidence has limited causal interpretation because mobility events are not randomly assigned and omitted variable bias remains possible despite fixed effects, controls, and CV-based individual information. They also note that the academic life-sciences context has unusual transparency, autonomy, and persistent postmove collaboration opportunities, which may limit generalizability.

Additional limitations concern measurement and mechanism visibility. The study can classify within- and cross-organization coauthorships, but it cannot fully distinguish types of new collaborations, organizational onboarding ability, nonhuman complementary assets, or the dynamic evolution of matching after initial hiring.

**Future Research**
Future research should pursue stronger causal designs and richer data that can rule out alternative explanations for mobility and productivity changes. The authors also call for work that parses new collaborations by topic area, seniority, star status, and specialist versus generalist roles.

Further work could examine organizational heterogeneity in onboarding and integration, distinguish selection from treatment when hiring prior collaborators, and study how complementary assets shape matching after initial entry. The paper also suggests examining long-term postmobility publication trajectories, movement into administrative roles, involuntary turnover, and organization-level effects of investing in cross-organization collaborations.
