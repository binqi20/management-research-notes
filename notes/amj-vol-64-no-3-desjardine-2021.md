# Why Activist Hedge Funds Target Socially Responsible Firms: The Reaction Costs of Signaling Corporate Social Responsibility

**Abstract**
We develop and test a theory of how unintended audiences create reaction costs for firms that use corporate social responsibility (CSR) as a signal. We introduce and define reaction costs as costs that signal senders incur when unintended audiences react negatively to a true signal that was intended for another audience. We argue that activist hedge funds—an unintended audience—treat CSR as a signal that firms have wasteful intentions and capabilities, which prevent firms from maximizing shareholder value in the short term. On that basis, we hypothesize that activist hedge funds are more likely to target firms with higher levels of CSR, thus imposing reaction costs on these firms. We further argue that this relationship is weaker when firms operate in industries with high levels of CSR, and stronger when firms’ financial communication is vague. Using data on activist hedge fund campaigns in the U.S. between 2000 and 2016, we find supporting results. Our study shows that CSR signals may be costlier than previously assumed and contributes to research on CSR, signaling, and corporate governance.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why activist hedge funds target socially responsible firms and whether CSR can create reaction costs when unintended audiences interpret CSR signals negatively. It tests whether higher CSR increases the likelihood of activist hedge fund targeting, and whether this relationship depends on industry CSR norms and the vagueness of firms' financial communication.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm CSR score, measured from KLD strengths in environment, community, diversity, employee relations, and human rights.
- DV(s): Hedge fund targeting, measured as whether an activist hedge fund initiated a campaign against the firm in a given year.
- Mediators: Not modeled statistically; the theorized mechanism is activist hedge funds' inference that CSR signals wasteful intentions and capabilities from a short-term shareholder-value perspective.
- Moderators: Industry CSR weakens the CSR-targeting relationship; firm vagueness in earnings-call communication strengthens it.

CSR usually functions as a signal to intended stakeholders that a firm is willing and able to act with a long-term orientation and attend to stakeholder interests. DesJardine, Marti, and Durand argue that activist hedge funds are an unintended audience that can read the same CSR signal as evidence of wasteful intentions and capabilities. This makes socially responsible firms more attractive targets for campaigns that seek short-term shareholder-value reorientation. CSR is less salient when it blends into a high-CSR industry, but more informative when financial communication is vague.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends CSR research by showing that CSR signals need not only generate goodwill; they can also invite costly negative reactions from audiences that interpret stakeholder orientation as poor business character. It contributes to signaling theory by introducing reaction costs as a third signaling cost, distinct from production costs and penalty costs because it arises when unintended audiences react negatively to true signals. It also contributes to corporate governance research by showing that signaling can create, rather than merely reduce, governance conflicts.

**Practical Implication**
The paper warns that socially responsible firms can face activist pressure before activists later suppress CSR activities. For policy-makers, the authors suggest that protecting firms targeted by activist hedge funds could relieve pressures that undermine CSR. For socially responsible firms, the findings highlight the value of attracting socially minded and long-term shareholders who may moderate profit-centered activist interventions.

For sustainability-oriented investors, the paper suggests reassessing whether investments in activist hedge funds align with their values. More broadly, policy-makers and investors need to weigh the potential efficiency gains of activist hedge funds against their negative effects on CSR and the long-term prosperity of targeted firms.

**Limitations**
The authors note that they examine average reaction costs created by hedge fund activism but do not directly measure the reaction costs borne by each firm. They also assume substantial similarity among activist hedge funds, even though hedge funds differ in investment horizons, tactics, and campaign size. Finally, the analysis examines one context: hedge fund targeting based on CSR signals.

**Future Research**
Future research could directly measure the resources firms allocate to responding to activist campaigns and examine whether firms differ in their capacity to absorb reaction costs. It could also study how variation among activist hedge funds moderates the relationship between CSR signaling and targeting. More broadly, future work could examine reaction costs from other unintended audiences, such as social movements, customer watchdogs, or regulators, and explore how firms balance reaction costs against the benefits of true signals.
