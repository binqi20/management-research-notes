# The Use of Agency Workers Hurts Business Performance: An Integrated Indirect Model

**Abstract**
The use of agency temps in the workplace has been the subject of considerable research interest, much of it focused on the effects that using temps have on the job attitudes of regular employees. We advance this stream of research by examining the effects of using agency temps on business performance. We find first that, when otherwise identical workplaces make greater use of temporary help provided by agencies, the identification of the employee or "regular" workforce with their workplace declines because the perceived status of the workplace declines. This, in turn, leads to lower store-level service quality and sales. This effect is independent of the notion suggested in earlier studies that temp workers threaten the job security and opportunities for advancement of regular employees. Finally, we find that these negative effects can be mitigated by workplace-level strategies associated with shared instrumental values and social integration practices. We examine these relationships using a range of employeeand store-level data sources over time from a retail chain.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether greater use of agency temps in a blended workplace hurts store-level business performance by lowering the regular workforce's identification with the workplace. It also asks whether shared instrumental values and social integration practices buffer this indirect negative relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Proportion of agency workers in the workplace.
- DV(s): Store-level service quality and private-label sales share.
- Mediators: Workplace identification of the regular workforce.
- Moderators: Shared instrumental values and social integration practices.

The proposed mechanism is identity-based. When regular employees work alongside lower-status agency temps, the workplace is perceived as lower status, which weakens regular employees' workplace identification. Lower workplace identification then reduces motivation to act on behalf of the store, harming service quality and private-label sales. Shared instrumental values and social integration practices are theorized to reduce this identity threat by explaining the business value of agency workers and blurring the "we-they" boundary between regular and agency workforces.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends research on agency temps from individual job attitudes to store-level business outcomes. It shows that the negative association between agency-worker use and performance operates through workplace identification, not only through prior explanations based on job security or advancement threats.

It also connects social identity theory, self-categorization theory, and diversity research to blended employment arrangements. The paper treats employment status differences as a contemporary workplace distinction that can create identity threat, while also showing how workplace-level practices may mitigate that threat.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not assume that agency temps improve performance simply by increasing flexibility or lowering labor costs. The findings suggest that extensive use of agency temps can carry hidden store-level costs through the regular workforce's reduced identification with the workplace.

The paper also implies that managers can partly offset these costs by communicating why agency workers are needed and by using social integration practices. The authors caution, however, that integration practices operate in a legal and policy context where treating agency workers too similarly to regular employees may create employment-law concerns.

**Limitations**
The authors state that they cannot rule out all confounding factors and that designs where agency-worker use is more clearly exogenous would be useful. The study is also limited to one form of nonstandard employment, agency workers, and different nonstandard arrangements may affect regular employees differently.

External validity is bounded by the empirical context: a retail service chain in Israel with low-wage frontline work. The authors also note that fixed-effects analysis limited the study to factors operating within stores and that they could not compare individual performance outcomes with business-level outcomes.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine other occupations, industries, and nonstandard work arrangements, including contractors and outsourced on-site operations. The authors call for a broader taxonomy of nonstandard employment by type, industry, cost effects, and business outcomes.

They also suggest examining whether regular employees accept buffering practices, whether agency temps affect other attitudes and behaviors such as commitment, citizenship, and prosocial behavior, and whether other organization-level outcomes such as innovation and productivity are affected. Further work should also study the process through which employment externalization and alternative work arrangements are introduced into workplaces.
