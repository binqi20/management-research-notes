# Sizing Up Entrepreneurial Potential: Gender Differences in Communication and Investor Perceptions of Long-Term Growth and Scalability

**Abstract**
Female entrepreneurs have been found to face disadvantages when compared with male entrepreneurs, especially in acquiring the financial resources needed to sustain and grow their ventures. Across three studies, we examine how disparities in funding outcomes may be due to differences in how entrepreneurs communicate their ventures, whereby female entrepreneurs have a tendency to use more concrete language when describing their ventures than their male counterparts. We find that the use of abstract speech affects investors' perceptions of which ventures are oriented toward long-term growth and scalability, which in turn affects the likelihood that a venture will receive investment. We conclude with a discussion of the important role of communication style as a key mediating mechanism in influencing investor cognition.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether gender differences in entrepreneurs' communication style help explain gender disparities in entrepreneurial funding outcomes. Specifically, it examines whether female entrepreneurs use more concrete and less abstract language than male entrepreneurs, whether investors read communicative abstraction as a signal of long-term growth and scalability, and whether that perception affects investment likelihood.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Entrepreneur gender and communicative abstraction versus concreteness in venture descriptions.
- DV(s): Investor perceptions of long-term growth orientation and scalability; investment likelihood, including advancement past VC screening and stated willingness to invest.
- Mediators: Communicative abstraction and perceived long-term growth orientation mediate the gender-investment relationship in Study 2; perceived growth orientation mediates the abstraction-investment relationship in Study 3.
- Moderators: Exploratory analyses test founder gender as a moderator of the abstraction to growth-perception link and the growth-perception to investment-likelihood link.

The mechanism links gendered communication to investor cognition. The authors argue that women entrepreneurs tend to communicate more concretely, while abstract communication is interpreted by investors as a sign that a venture is oriented toward long-term growth and scalability. In the field corpus, female founder gender is associated with lower abstraction, abstraction is associated with stronger growth perceptions, and growth perceptions are associated with greater investment likelihood. In the experiment, manipulating pitch abstraction provides causal evidence that abstract descriptions increase perceived long-term growth orientation and, through that perception, willingness to invest.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on gender and entrepreneurship by identifying communicative style as a behavioral pathway that can partly explain gender disparities in investor funding decisions. It does not reduce the disparity only to investor bias or venture type; instead, it shows how apparently success-oriented investor criteria can still generate gendered outcomes when they reward a communication style more commonly used by men.

It also contributes to entrepreneurial resource acquisition and communication research by showing that the level of linguistic abstraction matters for investment evaluation. The paper extends construal-level arguments into entrepreneurial pitching by linking abstract communication to perceptions of long-term growth, scalability, and investment worthiness.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that entrepreneurs, particularly women entrepreneurs, should attend not only to the factual content of a pitch but also to the level of abstraction used to communicate venture potential. When investors are evaluating long-term growth and scalability, more abstract framing may help signal a broader, future-oriented venture trajectory.

The paper also cautions that investor reliance on communicative abstraction can appear economically reasonable while still producing gendered consequences. Because women entrepreneurs in the studies tended to communicate less abstractly, perceptions of long-term growth and scalability may partly reflect communication style rather than only the venture's underlying potential.

**Limitations**
The authors identify Study 2's correlational design as a key limitation because unobserved differences between ventures or founders could explain associations between abstraction, perceived growth, and funding outcomes. Study 3 addresses part of this problem experimentally, but the overall evidence remains focused on investor perceptions in venture-backed, growth-oriented settings.

The scope is also limited to entrepreneurial investment contexts where venture capital or equity investors seek financial returns from long-term scalable growth. The paper acknowledges that other investors, such as microfinance institutions or social-mission organizations, may value different communication styles and decision criteria.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether the effects of abstract and concrete communication differ for female entrepreneurs, social-mission ventures, or settings where concrete communication may be advantageous. Additional work could also study other resources that early-stage entrepreneurs seek, such as advice, introductions, human capital, and intellectual capital.

The authors also call for research on how entrepreneurs can modify speech abstraction and whether different ways of achieving abstraction have different consequences. Such work could clarify when impression management through abstract communication is beneficial and when concrete communication better supports entrepreneurial outcomes.
