# Political Dynamics in Organizational Identity Breach and Reconstruction: Findings from the Crisis in UNICEF Germany

**Abstract**
This paper elucidates the political dynamics of organizational identity breach and reconstruction. Drawing on the revealing case of UNICEF Germany, we develop a process model of four phases of identity breach and reconstruction: build-up of identity tensions, revelation of identity breach, identity reconstruction, and enactment of the reconstructed identity. Our analysis explains how each phase was characterized by specific political strategies employed by the managerialist and idealist organizational members, the effects of these strategies on the power balance and consensus or conflict over organizational identity, and how particular triggers explain shifts from one phase to another. By so doing, our analysis advances our understanding of organizational identity breach and reconstruction as political processes, and paves the way for new studies of political identity dynamics in other contexts.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how organizational politics shape processes of organizational identity breach and reconstruction. It also asks what political strategies organizational actors employ when wrestling with consensus and conflict over organizational identity.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / orientations: Organizational identity breach, organizational identity reconstruction, managerialist identity beliefs, and idealist identity beliefs.
- Process / phases: Build-up of identity tensions, revelation of identity breach, identity reconstruction, and enactment of the reconstructed identity.
- Political strategies: Normalizing identity beliefs and masking controversial practices; revealing identity violations and defending prevailing identity beliefs; arguing for alternative identity constructions and forging a compromise; enforcing and maintaining the compromise.
- Political effects: Distorted, reversed, restored, and effectuated power balance, paired respectively with disguised conflict, open conflict, orchestrated consensus, and sustained consensus.
- Triggers: Increasing tensions reaching a breaking point, crisis pressure for reconciliation, and the urge to conclude reconstruction and move on.

The process begins when managerialist actors normalize fundraising- and efficiency-oriented identity beliefs while masking practices that idealist actors may contest. Whistleblowing and media attention then reveal perceived identity violations, allowing idealists to reverse the power balance and force open conflict over what UNICEF Germany stands for. Reconstruction occurs through negotiation between activist-educator and professional-fundraiser identity constructions, producing a compromise that is later enforced publicly and maintained backstage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances organizational identity research by theorizing identity breach and reconstruction as political processes rather than merely cognitive or cultural disruptions. It contributes a four-phase process model linking political strategies to changing power balances and forms of consensus or conflict. It also extends research on hybrid identities by showing how idealist actors can use breach and reconstruction to restore a more favorable balance against managerialist dominance.

**Practical Implication**
For hybrid organizations, the case shows that hidden identity tensions and distorted power balances can build into a public identity breach. The UNICEF Germany reconstruction suggests that inclusive mission review, open argument over alternative identity constructions, and compromise formation can help restore consensus after a breach.

The study also shows that reconstructed identity settlements require continued enactment and maintenance. Enforced consensus can conceal unresolved tensions again, so the practical lesson is less a one-time communication response than sustained attention to the political work behind organizational identity.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that the model is based on a revealing but idiosyncratic single case in a German hybrid nonprofit organization. They note that the boundary conditions may differ in organizations with other goal sets, such as profit orientation or public value generation. The analysis also focuses mainly on intraorganizational dynamics, even though media and other external constituencies shaped the breach and reconstruction process.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine organizational identity breach and reconstruction in settings beyond hybrid organizations and beyond the German context. Comparative work could study how external stakeholders perceive and influence breaches, how different hierarchical or professional groups experience breach, and how similar processes unfold across sectors or strategic groups. The authors also call for deeper study of masking, silencing, framing, discursive microprocesses, and the darker side of organizational identity construction.
