# Who Will Board a Sinking Ship? a Firm–Director Interdependence Perspective of Mutual Selection between Declining Firms and Director Candidates

**Abstract**
We develop a firm–director interdependence perspective to theorize director appointments as the outcome of a mutual selection process in which firms and director candidates select each other based on their organizational and personal dependencies. We apply this perspective to address a puzzle regarding director appointment in declining firms: Declining firms are often motivated to bring in director candidates with high resource endowments to help enact turnarounds, but these candidates may not join a declining firm’s board due to the potential uncertainties and reputational loss of doing so. Drawing on the firm–director interdependence perspective, we predict that director candidates’ social capital and human capital have inverted U-shaped effects on their likelihood of joining a declining firm. Moreover, the declining firm’s organizational social capital differently moderates the effects of candidates’ social capital and human capital. Evidence from a sample of public firms under special treatment (*ST) in China supports our theoretical predictions.

**Research Question**
Which director candidates will join declining firms, and why? The paper asks how declining firms' organizational dependence on candidates and candidates' personal dependence on declining firms jointly shape director appointments.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Director candidates' social capital; director candidates' human capital; declining firms' organizational social capital.
- DV(s): Whether a director candidate joins a declining firm as a new outside director.
- Mediators: No formal mediator is estimated; matched organizational and personal dependencies are the theorized selection mechanism.
- Moderators: Declining firm organizational social capital flattens the inverted U-shaped effect of candidate social capital and steepens the inverted U-shaped effect of candidate human capital.

The mechanism is a mutual-selection process. Declining firms want resource-rich directors for turnaround, but director candidates also assess reputational risk, career development, positional returns, and experience-based returns. Candidates with moderate social or human capital are most likely to match with declining firms because they are attractive enough to be invited but still personally dependent enough to accept; candidates with very high endowments face lower personal dependence and greater downside risk.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances board research by moving director appointment theory beyond a firm-centric selection model. It develops a firm–director interdependence perspective in which director appointments depend on the match or mismatch between firms' organizational dependence and director candidates' personal dependence.

It also extends resource dependence theory by theorizing dependencies across levels: firms depend on directors' personal resources, while individual directors depend on firms for positional and experience-based returns. This helps explain why firms may not obtain the directors they most desire.

**Practical Implication**
For declining firms, the findings imply that attracting turnaround directors is not only a question of identifying high-resource candidates. Firms must consider whether those candidates have enough personal dependence on the opportunity to accept the appointment.

For director candidates, the study clarifies why joining a distressed firm can be attractive at moderate levels of social or human capital but risky at high levels. Organizational social capital changes this calculus by altering both the firm's need for candidates' resources and the reputational risks candidates face.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the RDT-based interdependence perspective is not the only possible explanation for mutual selection. Two-sided matching theory, social network theory, status-based matching, and person-organization fit could further explain how firm-director matches form.

The empirical design relies on secondary information from financial reports of public firms. This limits insight into the specific contents of candidates' social and human capital, the candidate pool firms considered, and the firms candidates targeted.

**Future Research**
Future research could compare the RDT-based account with two-sided matching theory, social network theory, and person-organization fit explanations of firm-director mutual selection. It could also examine other candidate-level and firm-level features, such as personal ties with incumbent or outgoing upper echelons and fit between firm culture and candidate values.

The authors also call for primary data collection and qualitative approaches that can more directly capture the interdependence and mutual selection process. Such work could clarify what candidate resources firms consider, what director opportunities candidates target, and how these judgments unfold before appointment.
