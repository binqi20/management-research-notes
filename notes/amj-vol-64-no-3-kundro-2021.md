# Understanding When and Why Cover-Ups Are Punished Less Severely

**Abstract**
Cover-ups of unethical actions are undesirable and often costly. However, existing theory is unclear on when and why some cover-ups are punished less severely by in-group third parties compared to out-group third parties. Drawing on theories of attribution and social identity, we theorize that the punishment of cover-ups by inand out-group third parties depends on the type of cover-up: specifically, whether individuals conceal their own unethical transgressions (personal cover-ups) or the unethical transgressions of another individual (relational cover-ups). By highlighting this distinction, we hypothesize and find across three studies that in-group third parties punish relational—but not personal—cover-ups less severely than out-group third parties. Moreover, we theorize and find support for the mediating role of perceptions of group loyalty. Our theory and results reveal the ways in which different forms of cover-ups can escape severe punishment, and offer important theoretical contributions for research on unethical behavior, social identity, and loyalty.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and why in-group third parties punish cover-ups less severely than out-group third parties. It specifically examines whether punishment depends on cover-up type: concealing one's own unethical act versus concealing another group member's unethical act.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Third-party group membership, distinguishing in-group from out-group third parties; cover-up type, distinguishing personal from relational cover-ups.
- DV(s): Punishment severity, measured through punishment recommendations, bonus withdrawal, and coded language severity.
- Mediators: Perceptions of group loyalty.
- Moderators: Cover-up type moderates the relationship between third-party group membership and punishment.

The mechanism is attributional and identity-based. In-group third parties interpret relational cover-ups as evidence that the transgressor risked personal sanction to protect the group, increasing perceived group loyalty and reducing punishment severity. Personal cover-ups do not receive the same credit because they appear self-serving and harmful to the group. Across the studies, in-group leniency emerges for relational cover-ups but not for personal cover-ups.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to behavioral ethics by showing that cover-ups are not a unitary category of unethical behavior: personal and relational cover-ups elicit different punishment responses. It extends social identity research by identifying a boundary condition for in-group favoritism, showing that in-group third parties are more lenient only when the cover-up can be read as loyal to the group. It also advances work on prosocial unethical behavior by shifting attention from first-person motives to third-party punishment judgments.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should not assume that internal decision makers will punish all cover-ups consistently. Internal review boards or managers may punish relational cover-ups less severely when they view the actor as loyal, which can unintentionally signal that covering for peers is more permissible. The authors suggest involving out-group third-party input and balancing loyalty with fairness and care when evaluating cover-ups.

**Limitations**
The authors note that mechanisms beyond group loyalty may also explain punishment differences, including perceived incompetence or other judgments. They also acknowledge that the baseline unethical actions in Studies 2 and 3 were limited in severity compared with the wider range of incidents recalled in Study 1. The paper does not fully address how rank, leadership responsibility, or punishment of the original wrongdoer may alter responses to cover-ups.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine when in-group third parties punish personal cover-ups more severely, including situations where out-group observers are present. It could also compare cover-ups with other intentional unethical acts to clarify the role of intentionality and initial wrongdoing severity. Additional work could investigate high-ranking transgressors, leaders who oversee cover-ups, and whether the original wrongdoer in a relational cover-up is punished differently from the person who conceals the act.
