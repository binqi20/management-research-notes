# Minimal and Adaptive Coordination: How Hackathons’ Projects Accelerate Innovation without Killing it

**Abstract**
The innovation journey of new product development processes often spans weeks or months. Recently, though, hackathons have turned the journey into an ad hoc sprint of only a couple of days using new tools and technologies. Existing research predicts that such conditions will result in a failure to produce new working products, yet hackathons often lead to functioning innovative products. To investigate this puzzle, we closely studied the product development process of 13 comparable projects in assistive technology hackathons. We found that accelerating innovation created temporal ambiguity, as it was unclear how to coordinate the challenging work within such an extremely limited and ad hoc time frame. Multiple projects worked to reduce this ambiguity, importing temporal structures from organizational innovation processes and compressing them to fit the extremely limited and ad hoc time frame. They worked in full coordination to build a new product. They all failed. Only projects that sustained the temporal ambiguity—by working with a minimal basis for coordination and allowing new temporal structures to emerge—were able to produce functioning new products under the intense time pressure. This study contributes to theories on innovation processes, coordination, and temporality.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how accelerated new product development projects can produce functioning innovations under extreme time pressure when existing innovation and time-pressure research would predict failure. It examines how project teams in assistive technology hackathons deal with temporal ambiguity and how different coordination patterns shape whether they produce fully functioning, basic functioning, or nonfunctioning products.

**Mechanism Process**
- Context / triggering condition: Ad hoc 72-hour assistive technology hackathons create high time pressure and temporal ambiguity because only the beginning, end, and challenge are defined.
- Full coordination path: Seven projects import and compress familiar organizational innovation temporal structures, establish a full basis for coordination around methods, materials, and measurements, and continue rich realignment when problems emerge.
- Adaptive coordination path: Three projects let new temporal structures emerge, begin with only a minimal high-level product design, and gradually increase alignment through swift sensing, brief updates, nudges, and adjustment to one another's work.
- Minimal coordination path: Three projects also begin with a minimal basis for coordination but do not increase alignment enough to integrate separate development efforts.
- Outcomes: Full coordination projects fail to produce working products; adaptive coordination projects produce fully functioning products; minimal coordination projects produce only basic functioning products.

The process mechanism is not that less coordination is always better. Rather, under accelerated innovation conditions, early full coordination can lock teams into a detailed solution path and reduce flexibility when ambiguity re-emerges. A minimal basis for coordination preserves flexibility, but only adaptive coordination adds enough later alignment to integrate components into fully functioning products.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to innovation process theory by showing that acceleration need not mean compressing existing innovation stages into a shorter time frame. In this setting, projects succeeded when they allowed new temporal structures to emerge and sustained ambiguity long enough to adapt to unexpected development hurdles.

It contributes to coordination theory by identifying adaptive coordination as a distinct mode between full coordination and minimal coordination. Adaptive coordination combines an initially minimal basis for coordination with later swift sensing and adjustment, explaining how ad hoc teams can maintain flexibility without losing integration. It also extends temporality research by showing how actors' enactment of temporal structures affects coordination processes and innovation outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations using hackathons, innovation sprints, or crisis-driven accelerated development, the study cautions against simply compressing familiar agile routines into a shorter schedule. Teams may need visible workflows, rapid experimentation, and brief adjustment interactions rather than early closure around a fully specified design.

The findings are especially relevant for urgent health care or assistive technology innovation, where speed matters but premature alignment can prevent adaptation. Managers and organizers should create conditions that let participants see one another's work and adjust quickly without forcing extensive meetings or detailed upfront plans.

**Limitations**
The authors explicitly caveat that the study concerns acceleration under changed temporal conditions without clear temporal structures; the findings do not apply when a new innovation process has become routinized with a stable temporal structure. The setting is also bounded to two comparable U.S. assistive technology hackathons in visible makerspaces, where participants could directly observe one another's work.

The model therefore may travel less directly to settings that lack an equivalent visible workflow for sensing and adjustment. Because the study is qualitative and processual, it explains how the observed projects coordinated under temporal ambiguity rather than estimating population-level effects.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine coordination in other ambiguity-heavy contexts, including innovation and entrepreneurship settings beyond hackathons. The paper also calls for research on whether conceptual closure remains necessary in contemporary innovation processes as digital fabrication, open-source tools, and rapid prototyping reduce experimentation costs.

Further research can study other contemporary ways of organizing for innovation, including remote or virtual accelerated work where visible workflow must be produced through digital technologies. The paper also calls for work on new ways of leveraging human creative capabilities to innovate and solve scientific, technological, and social problems in combination with machines.
