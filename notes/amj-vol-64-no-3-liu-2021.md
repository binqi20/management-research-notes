# Institutional Intermediaries and Firm Choices in Response to Regulations

**Abstract**
This study develops a nuanced framework to unpack the heterogeneous mechanisms of institutional intermediaries’ efforts by aligning their bridging and buffering roles with the information and certification support they offer to clients. I further examine how these mechanisms explain firms’ intermediary choices in response to government regulations. Using a novel dataset of power plants across China, I find that the theorized intermediation mechanisms and the ways in which firms assess them are partially bounded by institutional complexities. Firms’ inexperience with regulations and their adoption of novel technologies are positively related to those firms’ selection of public intermediaries, with the former relationship strengthened when regulatory changes suggest a continuous need for policy updating. Meanwhile, firms’ noncompliance records and a lack of institutional linkage to the government are positively related to their reliance on private intermediaries’ buffering competency, with the latter relationship evident only in settings with an interventionist government. These findings contribute to the literature on the complex and dynamic value of intermediaries and firms’ intermediary choices at the intersection of management and government.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how firms choose between public and private institutional intermediaries when responding to government regulations. It examines how firms match organizational needs to intermediaries' bridging or buffering roles and to the information or certification support those intermediaries provide, and how regulatory and political conditions alter those choices.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm inexperience with the Cleaner Production program, noncompliance record, adoption of novel technology, and institutional linkage to the government.
- DV(s): Selection of a public intermediary rather than a private intermediary for Cleaner Production program auditing.
- Mediators: Not directly modeled in the quantitative analysis.
- Moderators: Regulatory changes, political commitment to environmental protection, and marketization as an indicator of government intervention versus institutional development.

The mechanism is an alignment process between firm needs and intermediary strengths. Public intermediaries are theorized to be more valuable when firms need bridging support: learning policy requirements or lending credibility to novel prosocial technologies. Private intermediaries are theorized to be more valuable when firms need buffering support: selective information disclosure or enough legitimacy to avoid heightened regulatory scrutiny. The results support public-intermediary selection for inexperienced firms and firms with novel technologies, private-intermediary selection for firms with noncompliance records, a stronger inexperience-public intermediary association during regulatory change, and private-intermediary selection by less-connected firms mainly where government intervention is stronger.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional intermediary research by integrating two dimensions that are often treated separately: support content, namely information versus certification, and intermediary role, namely bridging versus buffering. This 2 by 2 framework clarifies why intermediaries do not simply improve connectivity but can also preserve distance between firms and regulators.

It also contributes a dynamic account of firm-intermediary-regulator triads. The findings show that intermediary value depends not only on intermediary type but also on firm needs and institutional contingencies such as regulatory change and government intervention.

**Practical Implication**
For managers, intermediary choice should be treated as a strategic compliance decision rather than a purely administrative contracting decision. Firms that need policy learning or credible endorsement may benefit more from public intermediaries, while firms facing scrutiny or lacking government linkage may seek the buffering functions associated with private intermediaries.

For policy-makers, the study implies that regulation is shaped by more than dyadic firm-regulator interaction. Intermediaries can translate, certify, filter, or buffer regulatory meanings and effects, so policy design and implementation need to account for the incentives and roles of third parties.

**Limitations**
The paper states that its empirical setting concerns situations in which firms must, or typically do, select an intermediary to obtain sociopolitical regulatory legitimacy. Generalizability is therefore bounded, and the author calls for evidence from other industries, societal contexts, and geographic settings.

The findings are also mixed across contingencies: regulatory changes did not strengthen the noncompliance-private intermediary relationship, and political commitment did not strengthen the novel technology-public intermediary relationship. Although the paper runs robustness and reverse-causality checks, the core evidence remains observational and centered on intermediary choice rather than downstream performance.

**Future Research**
Future research could test the framework in similar regulatory contexts such as food safety testing and financial accounting, and in more complex make-or-buy or keep-and-switch decisions where not using an intermediary is also possible. Additional work could examine policy-change sequences and whether responses to earlier reforms create residual structures that affect later responses.

The paper also calls for research on intermediaries' own private interests and evolution over time. Case studies or event-based designs could trace whether intermediaries become more firm allied, regulator allied, or neutral as political turbulence, public sector privatization, or anticorruption campaigns reshape the institutional environment.
