# Performance Benefits of Reciprocal Vicarious Learning in Teams

**Abstract**
Team members' vicarious learning from other members' knowledge and experience is a critical component of learning and performance in interdependent team work contexts. Yet, our understanding of vicarious learning among individuals in teams is still quite limited, as this learning is often oversimplified (as one-way knowledge-sharing) or aggregated (as a collective, team-level property), resulting in incomplete and inconsistent findings. In this paper, I extend these views by exploring the underlying distribution of dyadic vicarious learning relationships in teams, specifically using a network approach to examine the consequences of reciprocity in team members' vicarious learning with one another (i.e., where both individuals in a given dyad learn vicariously from each other's knowledge and experience). Using a novel method for calculating weighted reciprocity in networks in a study of MBA consulting project teams, I demonstrate that greater team vicarious learning reciprocity is associated with greater team performance, and also moderates the performance consequences of teams' external learning efforts, offering a potential reconciliation of conflicting results in prior research. In doing so, this paper advances research on vicarious learning in teams, while also providing conceptual and empirical tools for studying learning and other interpersonal workplace interactions from a network perspective.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how reciprocity in dyadic vicarious learning relationships among team members affects team performance. It also asks whether team vicarious learning reciprocity changes whether external learning helps or harms team performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Team vicarious learning reciprocity; team external learning.
- DV(s): Sponsor-rated team performance, combining team output and team quality items.
- Mediators: Not modeled statistically; the theorized process is richer mutual knowledge exchange, stronger shared mental models, cross-understanding, and more efficient integration of internal and external learning.
- Moderators: Team vicarious learning reciprocity moderates the relationship between team external learning and team performance.

Myers argues that reciprocal vicarious learning gives team members a more robust map of one another's expertise and perspectives than one-way knowledge sharing. In reciprocal learning dyads, members can ask questions, compare experiences, and refine shared lessons, strengthening shared mental models and cross-understanding. These relational and cognitive resources are expected to improve performance directly and to help teams absorb external learning without overtaxing members' time and attention. Empirically, reciprocity positively predicts team performance, and external learning is more beneficial in high-reciprocity teams but more harmful in low-reciprocity teams.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances team learning research by shifting attention from aggregate team-level learning volume to the dyadic network distribution of vicarious learning ties. It challenges the common one-way sharer-recipient assumption in knowledge-sharing and advice-seeking research by theorizing learning as potentially bidirectional within dyads. It also contributes a weighted-network approach for measuring reciprocity in workplace relationships, preserving tie strength rather than reducing reciprocity to binary mutual ties.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not treat learning in teams as only a matter of increasing knowledge sharing or external information search. Teams may gain more from structures and routines that foster reciprocal learning relationships, especially when projects require external learning from clients, experts, or other teams. Mentoring, team meetings, and project routines may be more useful when they encourage bidirectional learning rather than one-way advice transfer.

**Limitations**
The study uses MBA student consulting teams, which offered strong response rates and realistic client work but may have inflated performance ratings and unusually high vicarious learning reciprocity because the project was embedded in an educational program. Team members also had little prior experience working together and no formal assigned hierarchy, limiting direct generalization to long-tenure or hierarchical organizational teams. The study focuses on dyadic reciprocity and does not examine generalized reciprocity across larger networks.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine reciprocity in other team environments where performance, reciprocity, density, and centralization vary more widely. It should study how prior shared experience, team tenure, formal power differences, and hierarchy shape reciprocal vicarious learning. Additional work should directly measure mediating processes, examine antecedents of reciprocity, and explore generalized forms of reciprocity beyond immediate dyadic reciprocation.
