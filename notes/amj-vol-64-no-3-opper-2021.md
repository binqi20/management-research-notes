# Social Network and Temporal Myopia

**Abstract**
This paper examines the link between the social networks surrounding business leaders and temporal myopia in strategic planning. Specifically, we hypothesize that processes characteristic of being embedded in a closed network are associated with a lack of foresight and a tendency to neglect long-run strategic planning. Using a probability sample of 700 CEOs in China, we show that network closure is associated with temporal myopia, which is evidenced in various measures of business planning. We show that managers embedded in closed networks are less experienced in long-run planning and are also less successful in implementing long-run business plans. Our contribution to the literature is twofold: we add a network perspective to the literature on temporal myopia in strategic management and, more significantly, by grounding temporal myopia in the network surrounding a person, we separate temporal myopia from the person. Myopia emerges from the social situations we create or in which we find ourselves.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how the social network surrounding a business leader is associated with temporal myopia in strategic planning. More specifically, it examines whether network closure around CEOs is linked to shorter planning horizons and to weaker performance benefits from long-run planning.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO network closure, measured primarily as network constraint around the CEO.
- DV(s): Planning horizon; long-term management contracts; long-term technical-staff contracts; employee training; perceived price elasticity; business success.
- Mediators: No formal mediator is estimated; the theorized process runs through information redundancy and social pressure within closed networks.
- Moderators: Network closure is theorized and tested as changing the performance implications of medium- and long-run planning horizons.

The mechanism is that closed networks expose managers to redundant information and stronger pressure to adopt majority views, making present-oriented, familiar, and incremental planning more likely. Open networks, by contrast, provide more heterogeneous information and weaker conformity pressure, which the authors argue supports foresight and longer-run strategic planning. The evidence is associational: the authors explicitly treat causality as unresolved because the design is cross-sectional.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper adds a network perspective to research on temporal myopia in strategic management by shifting attention from individual cognitive traits to the social situation surrounding the decision-maker. It argues that myopia can emerge from network structure, especially the information redundancy and conformity pressures of closed networks.

It also extends social network theory by identifying temporal myopia and planning horizon as outcomes associated with network closure and structural holes. The paper reframes the familiar broker "vision advantage" as not only a creativity or opportunity advantage but also a planning advantage.

**Practical Implication**
For managers whose firms need long-run strategic plans, the findings imply value in maintaining relatively open business networks with diverse contacts not tightly connected to one another. For managers embedded in close-knit networks, the paper suggests actively disrupting established advice networks and adding diverse contacts to increase information heterogeneity.

The authors also caution that closed-network managers may be more successful with shorter planning horizons than with poorly designed long-run plans. Short-run planning is not always irrational, but relying on it as a long-run strategy is risky in competitive and changing environments.

**Limitations**
The central limitation is the cross-sectional research design, which cannot determine causality or fully rule out endogeneity. The authors use controls, network-history checks, industry fixed effects, and robustness tests, but they still call causal corroboration unresolved.

The sample is also concentrated in China's extended Yangtze Delta region and consists mainly of medium-sized, owner- or founder-led private manufacturing firms. The authors therefore caution that the association may differ in large public companies, professionally managed firms, and other institutional or cultural contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine contingencies that protect people in closed networks from temporal myopia, including whether external knowledge sources increase diversity inside closed networks or are rejected as unfamiliar. The authors also call for longitudinal employee data, laboratory or field experiments, and comparative studies across cultural and institutional settings.

Additional work should test whether the network-closure association generalizes to larger firms and professional managers who answer to boards, shareholders, and external stakeholders. Such work could refine the boundary conditions before the findings are used to extend theory or guide practice.
