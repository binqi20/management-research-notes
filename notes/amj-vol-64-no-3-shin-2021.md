# When Putting Work Off Pays Off: The Curvilinear Relationship between Procrastination and Creativity

**Abstract**
Although it is widely assumed that procrastination is counterproductive, delaying task progress may have hidden benefits for creativity. Drawing on theories of incubation, we propose that moderate procrastination can foster creativity when employees have the intrinsic motivation and opportunity to generate new ideas. In two experiments in the United States, we tempted participants to engage in varying degrees of procrastination by making different numbers of funny YouTube videos easily accessible while they were supposed to be solving business problems. Participants generated more creative ideas in the moderate rather than low or high procrastination conditions. This curvilinear effect was partially mediated by problem restructuring and the activation of new knowledge. We constructively replicated and extended the curvilinear effect in a field study with Korean employees: procrastination predicted lower task efficiency but had an inverted-U-shaped relationship with creativity. Employees who procrastinated moderately received higher creativity ratings from their supervisors than employees who procrastinated more or less, provided that intrinsic motivation or creative requirement was high. We discuss theoretical and practical implications for time management, creativity, and motivation in organizations.

**Research Question**
Does procrastination have an inverted-U-shaped relationship with creativity, such that moderate procrastination can increase creative output relative to low or high procrastination? The paper also asks whether problem restructuring and activation of new knowledge explain this effect, and whether intrinsic motivation or creative requirement determines when moderate procrastination is beneficial.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Procrastination level, operationalized experimentally as low, moderate, or high temptation to delay task work and, in the field study, as employee-reported procrastination across core tasks.
- DV(s): Creativity of business ideas or work-related ideas; Study 3 also examines task efficiency to test whether the pattern is specific to creativity.
- Mediators: Problem restructuring and activation of new knowledge in Study 2.
- Moderators: Intrinsic motivation and creative requirement in Study 3.

The proposed mechanism is an incubation process. Low procrastination can make people start too quickly and remain tied to obvious initial framings, while high procrastination leaves too little time and too much deadline pressure for creative restructuring. Moderate procrastination creates enough distance from the task introduction and deadline for people to reframe the problem and access remote knowledge. This process is expected to support creativity only when intrinsic motivation or creative requirement keeps the task active in the back of the mind rather than allowing full disengagement.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the dominant treatment of procrastination as uniformly dysfunctional by showing that moderate delay can benefit creativity under bounded conditions. It extends creativity research by identifying procrastination as a behavioral antecedent of incubation, and by distinguishing the creative benefits of moderate delay from the efficiency costs of delay.

The study also contributes to motivation theory by showing that intrinsic motivation need not simply prevent procrastination. Instead, intrinsic motivation shapes how attention is allocated during procrastination, while creative requirement can substitute for intrinsic motivation by giving employees a reason to keep a creative problem mentally active.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not treat all procrastination as equivalent. When novel and useful ideas are needed, a moderate delay after initial task exposure may help employees incubate ideas, especially when they are intrinsically interested in the work or when the job clearly requires creativity.

The findings do not justify open-ended delay. The authors emphasize that procrastination still reduces task efficiency and that useful creative procrastination requires actual work, sufficient task exposure, and limits that prevent last-minute rushing.

**Limitations**
The mediators and moderators were tested in separate studies, so the full moderated mediation model was not examined in one integrated design. The studies also do not directly compare different motives for procrastination or the content of alternative activities undertaken while procrastinating.

Additional limitations include partial mediation in Study 2, limited theory about what time intervals count as moderate versus high procrastination, a cross-sectional field study, and a focus on individual rather than group procrastination and creativity.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the full model in a single design and examine whether different motives for procrastination, such as dissatisfaction with initial ideas versus self-regulatory failure, change its creative consequences. Studies could also test how mindless, moderately interesting, or highly intrinsically motivating alternative activities affect incubation.

The authors call for richer timing theory, experience-sampling designs, research on group-level procrastination and creativity, and studies of organizational antecedents such as deadline design. They also suggest examining additional mechanisms, including selective forgetting, idea selection, and feedback from weak ties.
