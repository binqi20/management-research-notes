# Amplifying Voice in Organizations

**Abstract**
We extend the field’s understanding of voice recognition by examining peer responses to voice. We investigate how employees can help peers get a status boost from voicing, while also raising their ownstatus, by introducing the conceptof amplification—public endorsement of another person’s contribution, with attribution to that person. In two experiments and one field study, we find that amplification enhances status both for voicers and for those who amplify voice. Being amplified was equally beneficial for voicers who framed their ideas promotively and prohibitively (improvement-focused and problem-focused, respectively; Study 1), and for men and women (Study 2). Furthermore, amplified ideas were rated as higher quality than nonamplified ideas. Amplification also helped amplifiers: participants reading experimentally manipulated meeting transcripts rated amplifiers as higher status than those who self-promoted, stayed quiet, or contributed additional ideas (Studies 1 and 2). Finally, in an intervention in a nonprofit organization, select employees trained to use amplification attained higher status in their work groups (Study 3). Overall, these results increase our understanding of how social actors can capitalize on instances of voice to give a status boost to voicers who might otherwise be overlooked, and help organizations realize the potential of employees’ diverse perspectives.

**Research Question**
How do peers' responses to employee voice shape observers' evaluations of the voiced idea, the voicer's status, and the responder's status? The paper asks whether amplification, defined as public endorsement of another person's contribution with attribution, can create status gains for both the person being amplified and the person doing the amplifying.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Peer response to voice, especially amplification, compared with staying quiet, self-promotion, or voicing an additional idea; Study 1 also varies promotive versus prohibitive voice, and Study 2 varies voicer and responder gender.
- DV(s): Perceived quality of the voiced idea, voicer status, responder status, and, in Study 3, team-member status after an amplification intervention.
- Mediators: Perceived idea quality mediates the relationship between amplification and voicer status.
- Moderators: Voice type, voicer gender, responder gender, and initially low-status position in work teams.

The proposed mechanism is social evaluative rather than purely individual: amplification signals that a peer regards the voiced contribution as worth considering, which can raise perceived idea quality and thereby increase the voicer's status. For amplifiers, the mechanism draws on competitive altruism: publicly crediting someone else's contribution appears prosocial and group-oriented, allowing the amplifier to gain status without taking attention away from the voicer. Across studies, the effects held for promotive and prohibitive voice and for both men and women.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends voice research by shifting attention from manager recognition to peer responses during the voice process. It contributes to status theory by showing that voice episodes need not be zero-sum: voicers and amplifiers can both gain status when a peer publicly endorses and attributes a contribution. It also suggests that peer amplification can help ideas and contributors that might otherwise be overlooked, including prohibitive voice and voices from lower-status group members.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations can train employees to amplify peers' contributions in meetings, especially when some employees' ideas are not being heard. The field intervention suggests that low-status employees can learn to use amplification and may gain status in their work groups. The findings also reassure employees that amplifying a colleague need not lower their own standing relative to self-promotion or adding another idea.

**Limitations**
Studies 1 and 2 use experimental vignette methods, which provide control but may not capture the full information and social history present in real meetings. Study 3 improves realism but uses a pre-post design in one small nonprofit organization, with employees selected by a manager rather than randomly assigned. The authors also could train amplifiers but could not control which voicers were amplified.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine when amplification has stronger or weaker effects, including contexts with strong subgroup faultlines, competition, or only one idea under consideration. The authors call for research on whether status gains persist over time and whether explicit attribution helps voicers receive longer-term recognition. They also suggest studying group-level consequences, such as performance, decision quality, psychological safety, future voice, and gendered relationships between idea quality and status.
