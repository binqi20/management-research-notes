# Getting the Picture Too Late: Handoffs and the Effectiveness of Idea Implementation in Creative Work

**Abstract**
Past research on idea implementation has focused on employees trying to win social support for their own ideas, yet employees are often handed ideas to implement that were developed by others. We propose and test hypotheses on such handoffs, focusing on how handing employees relatively mature ideas to implement may lead them to build less creative final products. We tested our hypotheses using two studies: an archival study of 5,676 movies in the U.S. film industry and a complementary experiment. Results suggest that late handoffs yielded less creative final products than no or early handoffs, meaning it was costly to creativity when employees implemented relatively mature ideas without driving at least some of their prior development. However, “serialized late handoffs”—wherein implementers were handed relatively mature ideas after an earlier handoff between two other individuals—were less costly to creativity than late handoffs from one other individual. Mediation results suggest that late handoffs reduced implementers’ creativity by restrictingtheirsenseofpsychologicalownershipandthecoherenceoftheirfinalproducts. This research advances theory on idea implementation, handoffs, and psychological ownership in creative work.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when and from whom creative ideas can be handed to implementers without reducing the creativity of the final product. It specifically tests whether late handoffs of relatively mature ideas reduce implementers' final-product creativity compared with no handoffs, early handoffs, and serialized late handoffs.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Handoff type, comparing late handoffs with no handoffs, early handoffs, serialized late handoffs, and in Study 2 a separate no-handoff condition.
- DV(s): Creativity of the final product, measured with Rotten Tomatoes critic scores in Study 1 and independent consumer ratings of advertisements in Study 2.
- Mediators: Psychological ownership and product coherence in Study 2.
- Moderators: Not modeled statistically.

Berg and Yu argue that implementers receiving late handoffs enter only after an idea has become relatively mature, leaving less opportunity to shape the idea, invest the self in it, or develop a unified product vision. This weakens psychological ownership and product coherence, which in turn reduces the creativity of the finished product. Serialized late handoffs are theorized to be less costly because an earlier handoff makes ownership seem more open and may encourage the implementer to carry forward a coherent trajectory rather than overwrite a single predecessor's mature plan. Empirically, late handoffs predicted lower creativity in both studies; mediation in Study 2 supported psychological ownership for most late-versus-other comparisons and product coherence for all four late-versus-other comparisons.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts idea implementation research from a primarily social-political focus on winning support for one's own ideas toward the production side of building handed-off ideas into tangible creative products. It extends handoff theory in creative work by distinguishing no, early, late, and serialized late handoffs according to idea maturity and prior handoff structure. It also contributes to psychological ownership research by showing how the timing and structure of creative-process handoffs can shape implementers' ownership, product coherence, and downstream creativity.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should avoid giving implementers mature creative ideas only at the implementation stage when those implementers have had no role in earlier development. When possible, implementers should enter before ideas become detailed plans, so they can develop ownership and a coherent vision. If late handoffs are necessary to use specialized skills, making implementers aware of an earlier handoff may reduce some creativity costs by framing their role as the next step in a sequence rather than as taking over a single creator's mature plan.

**Limitations**
The authors note that generalizability may be limited by the film-industry setting in Study 1 and the advertising task in Study 2, both of which involve aesthetic final products. Their framework assumes one principal individual drives each creative stage, while many real projects involve co-led stages, changing groups, repeated entries and exits, or shared ownership. Study 2 measured psychological ownership after the final ads were completed and measured product coherence from the final products, limiting direct evidence on the cognitive and motivational processes during implementation.

**Future Research**
Future research should test whether the handoff effects generalize to other creative and organizational contexts, especially settings where final products are less aesthetic. Researchers could examine shared ownership, co-led stages, varying group size and stability, repeated handoffs, and relationships between handoff givers and receivers. Additional work should directly measure ownership, coherence, and related cognitive processes during implementation and investigate when late handoffs may help because they bring in unique specialized skills.
