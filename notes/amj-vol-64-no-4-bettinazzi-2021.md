# Stakeholder Orientation and Divestiture Activity

**Abstract**
We conceptualize divestitures as a costly alternative to the internal resolution of conflicts among stakeholders, albeit one that avoids the more costly liquidation of the firm. In firms that have lower stakeholder orientation (defined as the extent to which management focuses attention on and integrates the interests of multiple stakeholders in its decisionmaking), divestitures will be less costly than the internal resolution of stakeholder conflicts, while the opposite will be true in firms that have higher stakeholder orientation. Consistent with this argument, we document a negative relationship between stakeholder orientation and divestiture activity, using a unique dataset of 909 U.S.-based, publicly listed firms from 2002 to 2015. This negative relationship is more pronounced for selloffs than for spinoffs, for selloffs of businesses that are unrelated to or located far from the divesting firm, and for selloffs to acquirers that are not alliance partners of divesting firms. The core contribution of this paper is to treat different types of divestitures as increasingly costly responses to conflicts among stakeholders, thereby populating the theoretical middle ground between negotiated adaptations of firms’ governance structures and total firm failure. In so doing, this paper contributes to research at the intersection of stakeholder theory and corporate strategy.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether stakeholder orientation explains firms' divestiture activity and whether this relationship varies across modes and types of divestiture. It examines whether lower stakeholder orientation makes divestitures, especially selloffs, a less costly alternative to internal governance adaptation when stakeholder conflicts arise.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Stakeholder orientation, measured from Asset4 indicators of attention to employees, customers, suppliers, local communities, and shareholders.
- DV(s): Number of divestitures, with additional outcomes for spinoffs, selloffs, unrelated versus related selloffs, distant versus proximate selloffs, and selloffs to alliance partners versus nonpartners.
- Mediators: Not modeled statistically; the theorized mechanism is the relative cost of resolving stakeholder conflicts internally versus divesting businesses or liquidating the firm.
- Moderators: Divestiture mode and selloff characteristics, including selloff versus spinoff, business relatedness, geographic distance, and whether the acquirer is an alliance partner.

The mechanism is a governance-cost comparison. Firms with higher stakeholder orientation have routines, relationships, and reputations that make internal conflict resolution less costly, so they undertake fewer divestitures. Firms with lower stakeholder orientation have weaker capabilities for stakeholder engagement and compromise, making divestiture a more attractive response to unresolved stakeholder conflicts. Selloffs, especially of unrelated or distant businesses and to nonpartner acquirers, are theorized as more costly stakeholder-disrupting responses that should therefore concentrate among lower-stakeholder-orientation firms.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes by framing divestitures as an intermediate governance response between internal adaptation and liquidation. It extends stakeholder theory into corporate strategy by showing how stakeholder orientation can shape firm-boundary decisions, not only stakeholder treatment or performance. It also differentiates divestiture types as progressively costly responses to governance failure.

**Practical Implication**
The analysis implies that managers should treat divestiture decisions as stakeholder-governance decisions, not only portfolio or financial decisions. Firms with stronger stakeholder orientation may be better positioned to resolve conflicts internally or use less disruptive divestiture paths, such as spinoffs or sales to alliance partners. For firms considering selloffs, relatedness, geographic distance, and acquirer relationships matter because they shape the stakeholder costs of separating a business.

**Future Research**
The authors suggest future studies could examine whether divestitures resolve misalignments among stakeholders in divesting firms by creating better alignment within acquiring firms. They also call for work on the interplay between governance and scope, including managerial agency, motivation, and perceptions of stakeholders in divestiture and other scope-changing transactions. They note that in-depth qualitative methods could help study these managerial perceptions and motivations.
