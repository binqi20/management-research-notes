# Unpacking Political Ideology: CEO Social and Economic Ideologies, Strategic Decision-Making Processes, and Corporate Entrepreneurship

**Abstract**
We integrate political psychology and upper echelons research to introduce an alternative conceptualization of executive political ideology by separating the two distinct ideologies: social and economic. We theorize and test how the two ideologies exert distinct effects on a critical strategic outcome: corporate entrepreneurship. We examine this contention in Iran, a political context that sharply deviates from the exclusively studied U.S. context. We find that social and economic conservatism exert opposing effects on corporate entrepreneurship through distinct strategic decision-making processes; CEO social conservatism positively affects corporate entrepreneurship by promoting intuitive strategic decision-making, whereas CEO economic conservatism negatively affects corporate entrepreneurship by impairing cooperative strategic decisionmaking. These results highlight the need to separate social and economic ideologies, especially in non-U.S. contexts, and inform the underlying strategic decision-making processes through which executive ideology shapes strategic behaviors. The promising results also underscore the importance of examiningthestrategicimplicationsofexecutivepoliticalideologyindiversepoliticalcontexts that differ from the U.S. context.

**Research Question**
How do CEO social and economic ideologies independently shape strategic decision-making processes and corporate entrepreneurship? The paper asks whether separating these two ideological dimensions explains firm-level strategic outcomes better than the unitary liberal-conservative view developed mainly in the U.S. context.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO social conservatism; CEO economic conservatism.
- DV(s): Corporate entrepreneurship, measured as innovation, corporate venturing, and strategic renewal.
- Mediators: Intuitive strategic decision-making mediates the CEO social conservatism path; cooperative strategic decision-making mediates the CEO economic conservatism path.
- Moderators: No formal moderator is estimated in the main model; the theoretical context is Iran as a low ideology-constraint political system.

The mechanism is cross-level. CEO social conservatism promotes reliance on experience-based intuition in the TMT, which helps identify and implement corporate entrepreneurship opportunities under uncertainty and time pressure. CEO economic conservatism reduces cooperative strategic decision-making by emphasizing competition and individualized goals, which impairs the TMT's ability to coordinate and implement corporate entrepreneurship. The results show opposing indirect effects: social conservatism increases corporate entrepreneurship through intuition, while economic conservatism decreases it through reduced cooperation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances executive political ideology research by replacing a unitary conservatism-liberalism spectrum with a two-dimensional account of social and economic ideology. This matters especially outside high ideology-constraint contexts such as the United States, because social and economic conservatism may be distinct or negatively correlated.

It also contributes to upper echelons theory by specifying strategic decision-making processes as mechanisms linking CEO ideology to firm outcomes. For corporate entrepreneurship research, it highlights the potential value of intuitive decision-making under uncertainty and connects corporate entrepreneurship to behavioral strategy.

**Practical Implication**
The study implies that CEOs should recognize how their ideological tendencies shape TMT decision processes. Socially liberal CEOs may need to deliberately create room for experience-based intuition when speed and uncertainty are central to corporate entrepreneurship.

Economically conservative CEOs may need to compensate for competitive and individualistic tendencies by designing shared goals and rewards that support cooperative strategic decision-making. This is especially relevant when corporate entrepreneurship requires coordination across units.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study is conducted in Iran to address the prior U.S. focus, but it does not directly compare countries with different degrees of ideology constraint. Therefore, the cross-national scope of the two-dimensional ideology argument remains to be tested more directly.

The paper also does not explain why TMT members follow CEOs' ideological tendencies in strategic decision-making. In addition, the ideology scales were adapted to avoid controversial issues that are illegal or socially unacceptable in some contexts, which improves contextual fit but may limit comparability.

**Future Research**
Future research could compare the strategic implications of social and economic ideology across countries with different ideology constraints, including single-party contexts such as China. It could also examine whether the two ideology dimensions explain other outcomes such as organizational culture, citizenship behavior, turnover intentions, strategic flexibility, diversification, and mergers and acquisitions.

The authors call for more work on the black box of upper echelons theory, including the motivations that lead TMT members to behave consistently with CEO tendencies. Future studies could also examine how combinations of social and economic ideologies within TMTs create different group dynamics and strategic outcomes.
