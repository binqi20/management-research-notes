# From the Editors—Improving the Transparency of Empirical Research Published in AMJ

**Abstract**
Not reported in paper

**Research Question**
Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Contribution and transparency are mutually reinforcing because empirical claims are meaningful only when readers and reviewers can evaluate how the research was conducted, analyzed, and reported.
- Quantitative transparency: Authors can strengthen trust through multiple studies, preregistration, open or reviewer-accessible data, shared materials, code, output, detailed sample-selection explanations, and supplementary robustness checks.
- Qualitative transparency: Authors should explain why, where, and how data were collected, their relation to the field, their ontological stance, and the analytical path through which concepts and mechanisms emerged.
- Editorial responsibility: AMJ editors and reviewers should evaluate transparency in relation to epistemological and methodological diversity rather than equating any single practice, such as open data, with quality.
- Boundary conditions: Transparency should preserve human-subject protections, confidentiality, double-blind review, and the distinct constraints of proprietary organizational data and international research settings.

The editorial argues that AMJ's contribution standard depends on transparent research processes, not polished or perfect results. It positions transparency as a field-wide practice involving authors, reviewers, editors, and readers, with different appropriate forms for quantitative, qualitative, and mixed-methods studies. Its process logic is pragmatic rather than causal: more visible research choices improve trust, enable correction of the scientific record, and support stronger theoretical conversations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial contributes to management-methods discourse by decoupling transparency from any single template and emphasizing fit between transparency practices, methodology, and epistemological assumptions. It argues that transparency supports both trustworthiness and theoretical impact by helping readers inspect evidence, understand research journeys, and build on published work responsibly.

**Practical Implication**
Prospective AMJ authors should make research decisions, materials, analyses, and deviations visible where appropriate, while protecting confidentiality, anonymity, and human subjects. Reviewers and editors should examine supplementary evidence carefully, resist requests to retrofit hypotheses to results, and assess research quality together with transparency rather than treating transparency practices as automatic proof of rigor.

**Limitations**
The editorial explicitly notes that it does not resolve all transparency issues facing management research. It flags unresolved tensions around incentives, tenure pressures, field-site constraints, null-hypothesis significance testing, results-blind reviewing, international human-subject protections, preprint dissemination, and preserving double-blind review when supplementary materials are hosted online.

**Future Research**
The editorial invites continued collective learning about transparency practices across journals, methods, and subfields. It identifies mixed-methods research, cross-national human-subject protections, preregistration evaluation, anonymous sharing infrastructures, and responsible engagement with pre-peer-review dissemination as areas where the management field still needs better practices.
