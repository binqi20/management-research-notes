# Covering in Cover Letters: Gender and Self-Presentation in Job Applications

**Abstract**
Despite decades of research and intervention efforts, gender-based occupational segregation remains a significant problem. An emerging body of research has suggested that one way women overcome gender discrimination when applying for male-dominated jobs is by deliberately managing gender impressions. However, social role theory and research on prescriptive stereotypes have suggestedthat these attemptsto manage gender may backfire. In this research, we theorize that, while women actively respond to anticipated sexism using social identity–based impression-management (SIM) strategies (e.g., attempting to appearlessfeminineincoverletters),theseactionscanactuallybackfirebecause theyclash with prescriptive gender stereotypes. Across three studies, we investigate the motivations, techniques, andoutcomesofmanaging genderinjob applicationsfordifferentkindsofjobs. We find that women, but not men, manage gender when applying for gender-incongruent (i.e., male-dominated) jobs by using less feminine language, and that, paradoxically, they are less likely to be hired when they do so. The current research contributes to our understanding of the consequences of SIM strategies and shows that women’s coping behaviorinresponsetoexisting genderinequalitiesinthe labormarket isa novel and ironic mechanism through which occupational gender-segregation is perpetuated.

**Research Question**
How do job seekers manage gender impressions when applying for gender-incongruent jobs, and what consequences do these strategies have for hiring outcomes? The paper specifically asks whether women applying for male-dominated jobs cover by reducing feminine language, whether anticipated gender discrimination motivates this behavior, and whether evaluators penalize it through lower liking and hireability.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Applicant gender, job gender context, and applicant covering through reduced feminine language in cover letters or self-descriptions.
- DV(s): Job attainment, MBA callback, evaluator-rated hireability, and related evaluations of likeability and competence.
- Mediators: Anticipated gender discrimination links gender-incongruent job context to covering; likeability links reduced feminine language to lower hireability.
- Moderators: Applicant gender and job gender congruence; the theorized backlash is strongest for women who reduce feminine language.

The mechanism begins with devaluation threat: women applying to male-dominated jobs anticipate gender discrimination and attempt to signal fit by downplaying feminine cues. This social identity-based impression-management strategy conflicts with prescriptive gender stereotypes, so evaluators interpret women who use less feminine language as less likeable and less hireable. The result is an ironic reinforcing cycle in which women respond to anticipated discrimination in a way that can reduce their chances of entering male-dominated jobs.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Roberts's professional image construction model by showing that social identity-based impression-management can backfire when the identity being managed is governed by strong prescriptive stereotypes. It contributes to social role theory by specifying a context in which women adopt counterstereotypic self-presentation to signal job fit but are penalized for violating gender prescriptions. It also offers a supply-side and demand-side mechanism for occupational gender segregation: women's coping behavior and evaluators' prescriptive expectations interact to perpetuate underrepresentation.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue against placing responsibility on women to navigate biased hiring systems through careful self-presentation. Organizations should change hiring structures and signals, including anonymized recruitment, changes in how applicants are presented to hiring managers, group-based hiring processes, and job postings that genuinely value historically devalued traits. Employers also need to follow through on such signals by valuing candidates who possess more feminine traits rather than treating inclusive language as symbolic.

**Limitations**
The paper reports that it cannot determine whether the observed gender-management strategies apply across job levels or mainly to relatively entry-level jobs. It also did not test how gendered wording in job advertisements shapes applicant self-presentation, because the experimental postings were tightly controlled. The authors further note that they measured devaluation threat through anticipated gender discrimination but did not run competing tests against legitimacy threat.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether women use similar strategies when applying for more senior jobs and whether the same backlash persists at later career stages. The authors call for work on how gendered job-advertisement language affects applicants' gendered language in application materials, and on the relative roles of devaluation and legitimacy threat. They also suggest studying men's gender-management strategies, noncisgender applicants' experiences, and intersections of gender with age, social class, and race.
