# Seeing the Glass as Half Full or Half Empty: The Role of Affect-Induced Optimistic and Pessimistic States on Justice Perceptions and Outcomes

**Abstract**
In this paper, we develop and test theory to explain how employees’ perceptions of supervisor justice behavior are subjectively influenced by optimistic and pessimistic states. We propose that state affect gives rise to optimistic and pessimistic states, which color justice perceptions and impact performance behaviors (i.e., task performance, citizenship behavior, counterproductive behavior). Results from an experience sampling study and a set of experimental studies showed that state positive affect fosters an optimistic state that promotes perceptions of justice rule adherence, which influences task performance and citizenship behavior, whereas state negative affect promotes a pessimistic state that promotes perceptions of justice rule violation, which influences counterproductive behavior. Interestingly, state affect does not have a direct relationship with justice perceptions, which points to a new perspective on affect and justice.

**Research Question**
How do employees' affect-induced optimistic and pessimistic states shape their perceptions that supervisors adhere to or violate justice rules, and how do those perceptions influence performance behaviors? The paper asks what cognitive lenses explain the subjective "eye of the beholder" in justice perceptions beyond supervisor behavior itself.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): State positive affect and state negative affect; in the experiments, manipulated state affect, optimistic/pessimistic states, and justice rule adherence or violation.
- DV(s): Justice rule adherence perceptions, justice rule violation perceptions, task performance, citizenship behavior, and counterproductive behavior.
- Mediators: Optimistic state mediates the effect of state positive affect on justice rule adherence; pessimistic state mediates the effect of state negative affect on justice rule violation; justice perceptions then mediate performance outcomes.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism is that momentary affect produces valence-congruent optimistic or pessimistic states, which act as perceptual lenses for interpreting supervisor behavior. Optimistic states make employees more likely to see supervisor behavior as adhering to justice rules, supporting task performance and citizenship behavior. Pessimistic states make employees more likely to see supervisor behavior as violating justice rules, supporting counterproductive behavior. The ESM results and causal-chain experiments indicate that affect operates through these states rather than directly overriding justice perceptions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a more precise account of the "eye of the beholder" in organizational justice by specifying optimistic and pessimistic states as the cognitive lenses through which employees interpret supervisor behavior. It extends Carver and Scheier's optimism-pessimism framework into workplace justice research and conceptualizes optimism and pessimism as dynamic states rather than only stable traits. It also shows that justice rule adherence and violation have differentiated behavioral consequences: adherence predicts productive behaviors, whereas violation predicts counterproductive behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Supervisors should not assume that adhering to justice rules is sufficient for employees to perceive them as just, because employees' current affective states can color their interpretations. The findings imply that managers should attend to work conditions that foster positive affect and reduce negative affect, since these states can indirectly shape justice perceptions and performance behaviors. Supervisors may also need to monitor whether their own view of justice-relevant behavior diverges from employees' perceptions.

**Limitations**
The ESM study relies on self-rated independent and mediating variables, so common-method variance remains a concern despite temporal separation and group-mean centering. Study 1 uses coworker-rated performance rather than supervisor-rated performance, and the experiments measure behavioral intentions rather than actual workplace behavior. The paper also focuses on overall justice perceptions, leaving open whether the same affect-induced lens operates similarly across specific justice dimensions and longer-term entity justice perceptions.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether discrete emotions, nonwork recovery, or other perceiver factors produce optimistic and pessimistic states that shape justice perceptions in specific events such as layoffs, pay cuts, or policy changes. Studies could also test whether these states influence other interpersonal processes, including perceived support, ostracism, incivility, leader affect, and team performance. Additional research could compare event-level and entity-level justice perceptions and investigate how employees react when supervisor behavior contradicts their optimistic or pessimistic expectations.
