# Swift Sense of Community: Resourcing Artifacts for Rapid Community Emergence in a Temporary Organization

**Abstract**
Relational sense of community (SOC) research suggests that SOC depends on the depth of relationships cultivated between members over time. The rise of temporary organizations—transient work arrangements with limited expectations for future interactions— implored us to consider how a swift SOC can emerge in temporary organizations, where the cultivation of relationships may be challenging. We introduce a broader relational approach and draw on high-quality connections and resourcing theories to examine how a swift SOC emerges. Utilizing rich data sources, our qualitative analyses present a swift SOC cultivated in five days in a sleepaway summer camp. We find that this swift SOC was built on brief supportive connections that were made durable by resourcing artifacts. Resourcing artifacts creates scaffolds that mobilize actors to create a web of connections, leading to an organization-wide swift SOC. We propose that a swift SOC can emerge through four intertwined resourcing artifact phases: initial resourcing, embracing resourcing, reinterpreting resourcing, and expanding resourcing. During these phases, individuals imbue artifacts with new meaning and resource artifacts for (a) dyadic connection, (b) staff coordination, and (c) membership in a subgroup and (d) an organization-wide community. We demonstrate symbolizing and momentary connections as novel resourcing mechanisms enabling this process.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how a sense of community can be cultivated in transitory, temporary organizational structures where members' interactions are short-lived. It examines how a swift SOC can emerge when there is low familiarity, limited shared work history, and no expectation of future interactions.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Swift sense of community, brief supportive connections, momentary positive regard, widespread influence, resourcing artifacts, and the tension between dyadic care and broader availability.
- Process / phases: Initial resourcing, embracing resourcing, reinterpreting resourcing, and expanding resourcing.
- Practices: Staff and campers imbue bread tags with layered meanings, distribute them, wear them publicly, personalize them, and use them to invite brief supportive connections.
- Emergent outcomes: Positive regard, fulfillment of needs, bottom-up, top-down, and lateral influence, subgroup membership and responsibility, and eventually organization-wide membership and responsibility.
- Boundary conditions: The process is observed in a caring, secluded, five-day temporary organization with high openness to improvisation and broad willingness to disclose distress.

The process begins when a staff member uses a bread tag to make a brief supportive connection with a homesick camper more durable. Staff then embrace the artifact for coordination, using it to signal homesickness and adjust care without repeated probing. Campers reinterpret the artifact as membership in a homesick peer subgroup and later expand it into a symbol of care for many forms of distress across campers and staff. Through these layered acts of symbolizing, the artifact scales dyadic moments into a web of organization-wide supportive connections.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends sense-of-community research by showing that a swift SOC is not merely a faster version of conventional SOC, but a qualitatively different form built on momentary positive regard and widespread influence, including lateral peer influence. It contributes to resourcing theory by identifying symbolizing as a resourcing mechanism through which actors imbue artifacts with layered meanings that can generate both stability and change. It also extends high-quality connections research by explaining how brief dyadic connections can become durable and aggregate into a new social order.

**Practical Implication**
Temporary organizations can cultivate swift community by leaving room for members to improvise, notice distress, and repurpose simple artifacts as visible supports for connection. Leaders should pay attention to organizational openness, because the Camp Magic process depended on staff and campers being able to recognize problems, disclose difficult emotions, and adopt emergent practices quickly. The findings also suggest that community-building in short-lived organizations may depend less on deep prior relationships than on small, emotionally meaningful, and publicly visible moments of support.

**Limitations**
The authors note that Camp Magic is an extreme and caring context, so the process may look different in less care-centered temporary organizations such as movie sets, seasonal festivals, or construction sites. The secluded setting helped reveal the micro-processes of swift SOC emergence, but limited the study's ability to observe spillover effects beyond the camp. The study also focuses on emergence during a five-day period and does not establish whether swift SOC endures after members leave the temporary organization.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine the consequences of swift SOC for relational coordination, voice, word of mouth, and organizational reputation. Researchers could study how vulnerability and present-moment attention enable brief supportive connections, and compare spontaneous versus deliberately engineered swift SOC. Additional work could examine contradictory symbolizing, regeneration of social orders through familiar meanings, spillover beyond the original setting, and whether swift SOC erodes or persists over time.
