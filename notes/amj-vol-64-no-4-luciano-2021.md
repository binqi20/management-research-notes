# The Double-Edged Sword of Leadership Task Transitions in Emergency Response Multiteam Systems

**Abstract**
Multiteam systems (MTSs) operating in complex and dynamic environments often have a formal hierarchical leadership structure. However, it is unclear whether individuals should stick exclusively to performing their designated tasks within the hierarchical leadership structure, or if, instead, they should switch between different types of tasks to align efforts with changes in the environment. We refer to such task switching—an individual shifting to or from tasks designated for a particular leader position—as leadership task transitions. Our qualitative study of six MTSs responding to live-actor mass-casualty incidents revealed that leadership task transitionsare a double-edged sword as they can simultaneously help manage the MTS–environment interface and harm MTS internal functioning. More specifically, leadership task transitions benefit the MTS by rapidly reallocating effort to alleviate the dominant environmental pressure at that time. However, they also harm the MTS by disrupting its internal task-based cycles. Rapidly restoring the disrupted cycles mitigates this harmful effect, but such cycle restoration is not successful when there is a high level of cycle activity or when multiple areas of the MTS are disrupted. Our findings generate new knowledge on how and why leadership task transitions impact MTSs. Implications and future directions are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do leadership task transitions influence multiteam system effectiveness in complex and dynamic environments? The paper further asks how MTSs function in such environments, and how leadership task transitions influence that functioning and, in turn, effectiveness.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / setting: Emergency response multiteam systems with system- and sector-level task-based cycles under a formal hierarchical leadership structure.
- Environmental pressures: Dynamic complexity creates pressure to gain awareness; dynamic challenge creates pressure to make progress.
- Process trigger: Shifts in the dominant environmental pressure prompt leadership task transitions, defined as temporary movement to or from tasks designated for a leader position.
- Beneficial pathway: Leadership task transitions rapidly reallocate effort toward awareness or progress, helping the MTS alleviate the current dominant pressure.
- Harmful pathway: The same transitions disrupt one or more task-based cycles by interrupting updating, awareness, leader tasks, enabling, worker tasks, or progress.
- Boundary conditions: Rapid restoration of the disrupted cycle mitigates harm; restoration is less successful when cycle activity is high or multiple cycles are disrupted.

Leadership task transitions operate as a double-edged process. They help the MTS manage the environment by moving effort to the pressure that most urgently needs attention, but they also pull effort away from the baseline task-based cycles that keep the system internally coordinated. The net effect depends on whether the disrupted cycle can be restored quickly and completely.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper builds process theory on how leadership task transitions affect MTS effectiveness, explaining why fluid leadership can be simultaneously beneficial and harmful inside a formal hierarchy. It reframes the MTS-environment interface from a single external influence into shifting, opposing pressures of dynamic complexity and dynamic challenge. It also advances MTS theory by foregrounding task-based cycles, enabling scholars to examine taskwork alongside the more established focus on teamwork and system-level processes.

**Practical Implication**
Emergency-response MTS members should not treat "stay in your lane" as an absolute rule; brief transitions can be useful when environmental pressures shift. However, members should avoid transitions during high cycle activity, avoid leading outside their zone when they lack awareness, and restore the disrupted cycle quickly by reestablishing updating, awareness, direction, enabling, work, and progress.

**Limitations**
The study is based on one emergency-response training exercise with paramedic students in simulated mass-casualty incidents, so transferability is strongest for similar emergency response MTSs. The MTS had clear formal hierarchy, shared training, low competency separation, and low structural dynamism; systems with flatter structures, high information transparency, different competencies, or more structural change may show different dynamics.

**Future Research**
Future research should test the boundary conditions of the theory in other emergency settings and in non-emergency organizational crises where dynamic complexity and dynamic challenge shift over time. Scholars could examine MTSs with different leadership structures, information transparency, competency separation, and structural dynamism, and could study when repeated task transitions become leader role transitions. The paper also calls for work on individual differences, learning, adaptability, and transferability to teams, matrix organizations, and other organizational forms.
