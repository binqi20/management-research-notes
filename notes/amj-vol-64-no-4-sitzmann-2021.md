# The Hidden Cost of Prayer: Religiosity and the Gender Wage Gap

**Abstract**
Religion is a preeminent social institution that meaningfully shapes cultures. Prevailing theory suggests that it is primarily a benevolent force in business, and differences across world religions preclude examining effects that thread across religions. We develop a theoretical account that fundamentally challenges these assumptions by explaining how and why religiosity—regardless of which religion is prominent—differentiates based on gender, widening the gender wage gap. Guided by an integrated review of the religion literature, we specify three dimensions of gender differentiation—social domains, sexuality, and agency—that explain why religiosity widens the gender wage gap. A series of studies tested our theoretical model. Two studies showcased the predictive power of religiosity on the gender wage gap across 140 countries worldwide and the 50 United States via gender-differentiated social domains, sexuality, and agency, explaining 37% of the variance in the wage gap. U.S. longitudinal data indicated that the gender wage gap is narrowing significantly faster in secular states. Moreover, experiments allowed for causal inference, revealing that gender-egalitarian interventions blocked the effect of religiosity on the gender wage gap. Finally, theoretical and empirical accounts converge to suggest that religiosity's effect on the gender wage gap applies across the major world religions.

**Research Question**
How and why does cultural religiosity, regardless of which religion is predominant, relate to the gender wage gap? The paper asks whether religiosity widens wage inequality through three forms of gender differentiation: social domains, sexuality, and agency.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Cultural or organizational religiosity; in Study 3b, gender-egalitarian organizational policy interventions that target the theorized mechanisms.
- DV(s): Gender wage gap or gender-differentiated wage allocation.
- Mediators: Gender differentiation in social domains, sexuality, and agency.
- Moderators: Religious affiliation was tested and did not carry or significantly moderate the religiosity effect; Study 3b policy interventions blocked the effect of religiosity on wage allocation.

The paper argues that religiosity works as a broad cultural value that legitimizes differentiated expectations for men and women. Religious cultures are theorized to assign men to public and professional domains, regulate women's sexuality more than men's, and grant men greater agency and decision-making authority. These forms of differentiation can shape organizational wage allocation by devaluing women's workplace contributions, reducing women's accumulated human capital, and limiting women's ability to change unequal systems. Field studies support indirect associations through these mechanisms, while the experiments show that explicitly egalitarian policies can interrupt the wage-allocation effect.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges management scholarship's prevailing view of religion as mainly benevolent for organizations by theorizing and testing a systematic inequality-producing effect. It contributes to religion-and-organization research by treating religiosity as a cross-religion cultural value rather than comparing discrete faith traditions. It also extends work on patriarchy, social role theory, and biosocial explanations of gender roles by identifying social domains, sexuality, and agency as cross-cultural mechanisms linking religiosity to wage inequality.

**Practical Implication**
Managers in religious cultures should not assume that religious inclusion is automatically neutral for wage equality. The experiments suggest that policies supporting equitable parental leave, prohibiting sexual harassment, and promoting inclusive leadership development can block gender-differentiated wage allocation. Organizations can also audit pay equity, increase transparency, and strengthen accountability where cultural norms may make unequal valuation of men's and women's work seem natural.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the global sample lacks complete data for every country, and missingness may reflect development stage, censorship, or other external conditions. The theory contrasts religious and secular cultures, but religiosity is measured on a continuum, and some mediator indicators were collected after wage-gap data. The sexuality indicators rely on online searches for rape and pornography, which may miss non-digital behavior, censorship, or differences in whether people seek information online.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether separation between church and state reduces gender inequality and whether religiosity affects other organizational phenomena beyond the gender wage gap. Additional work could study how pornography consumption, rape, and broader sexualization relate to wage inequality. The authors also call for integrating cultural tightness-looseness to test whether religious cultures impose tighter behavioral expectations on women than on men.
