# Networks, Creativity, and Time: Staying Creative through Brokerage and Network Rejuvenation

**Abstract**
In this paper, we adopt a dynamic perspective on networks and creativity to propose that the oft-theorized creative benefits of open networks and heterogeneous content are less likely to be accrued over time if the network is stable. Specifically, we hypothesize that open networks and content heterogeneity will have a more positive effect on creativity when network stability is low. We base our prediction on the fact that, over time, network stability begets cognitive rigidity and social rigidity, thus limiting individuals' ability to make use of the creative advantages provided by open networks and heterogeneous content. On the contrary, new ties bring a positive "shock" that pushes individuals in the network to change the way they organize and process knowledge, as well as the way they interact and collaborate—a shock that enables creators to accrue the creative advantages provided by open network structures and heterogeneous content. We test and find support for our theory in a study on the core artists who worked on the TV series Doctor Who between 1963 and 2014.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when open networks and heterogeneous network content continue to support individual creativity over time. It specifically examines whether network stability weakens the creative benefits of brokerage and content heterogeneity, and whether adding new ties rejuvenates those benefits.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Network openness and content heterogeneity.
- DV(s): Individual creativity in artists' contributions to Doctor Who episodes.
- Mediators: Not directly tested; the theorized process is cognitive rigidity and social rigidity in stable networks versus cognitive and collaborative disruption from new ties.
- Moderators: Network stability, operationalized as the degree to which artists maintain existing ties rather than add new collaborators.

The mechanism is that open networks and heterogeneous content provide nonredundant perspectives and raw material for creative recombination, but stable network membership can make creators cognitively and socially rigid. When artists add new ties, newcomers create a positive shock that disrupts established mental models and collaboration routines, making creators more able to use the advantages of brokerage and heterogeneous content. The panel results show that openness and heterogeneity foster creativity mainly when they are coupled with low network stability.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper adds a dynamic view to network-creativity theory by showing that the benefits of brokerage and content heterogeneity depend on how network composition changes over time. It also separates network structure from network content, arguing that open networks offer a vision advantage beyond access to heterogeneous knowledge. The study further suggests that short-term network rejuvenation can explain how individuals sustain creative performance across longer creative careers.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and creative professionals should not treat a nonredundant network as a static asset. Sustained creativity may require keeping collaboration structures open to newcomers, even when existing teams or products are successful. The implication is to balance brokerage and continuity with periodic renewal that introduces fresh perspectives and disrupts rigid routines.

**Limitations**
The study is set in the Doctor Who production world, a creative, project-based context with fixed roles, so the findings may travel most directly to settings such as consulting, scientific research, and new product design. The analysis focuses on one product category, which helps control product- and company-level confounds but may miss collaboration networks spanning other product domains. Because the data are archival, the study cannot directly measure the cognitive and social mechanisms through which network stability and structural holes shape creativity.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how much network change is too much across different time horizons, since short-term rejuvenation and long-term instability may have different effects. Scholars could also study alter-centric network dynamics, including how changes in collaborators' ties affect a focal creator's creativity. Additional work using laboratory studies or ad hoc surveys could directly test the cognitive rigidity, social rigidity, and newcomer-shock mechanisms proposed here.
