# Too Many Peas in a Pod? How Overlaps in Directors’ Local and Global Status Characteristics Influence Board Turnover in Newly Public Firms

**Abstract**
Drawing on status characteristics theory, we explore how boards’ social structures influence board turnover. We theorize that (a) understanding directors’ relative standing and spheres of influence in the local status hierarchy creates deference structures that reduce conflict and enhance stability, thereby reducing board turnover; and (b) shared performance expectations and attraction based on homophily in the global status hierarchy can also reduce conflict and enhance stability and thus serve as another means of reducing board turnover. Using five years of post-initial public offering data on 218 firms that went public between 2001 and 2005, we find that overlaps in directors’ local status characteristics captured by their shared tenure and expertise and directors’ global status homogeneity increase the likelihood of board turnover. However, the directors’ global status characteristics shape the relative salience of the positive or negative effects of boards’ local status characteristics, leading to different effects on board turnover.

**Research Question**
How do overlaps in directors’ locally valued status characteristics (board tenure and functional expertise) and global status characteristics shape board turnover in newly public firms? The paper asks whether board-level status structures stabilize boards by clarifying deference relationships or destabilize them when too many directors share the same status characteristics.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Board tenure overlap, board expertise overlap, board global status homogeneity, and interactions between global status homogeneity and the two local-status overlap measures.
- DV(s): Board turnover, measured as the count of outside directors exiting the board in a given year.
- Mediators: Not directly estimated; the theorized mechanisms are local deference structures, conflict/process losses, shared performance expectations, and status homophily.
- Moderators: Global status homogeneity moderates the tenure-overlap and expertise-overlap relationships; the tenure interaction is supported, while the expertise interaction is not supported.

The mechanism is conditional rather than uniformly stabilizing. When directors differ in tenure or expertise, those characteristics can create distinct spheres of influence and deference patterns; when overlap is high, those characteristics lose differentiating power and can increase board turnover. Global status homogeneity has mixed effects: it initially appears to reduce turnover, but in the fuller models it increases turnover while attenuating the positive tenure-overlap effect at higher levels of tenure overlap.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends status characteristics theory by shifting attention from whether an individual possesses a valued status characteristic to what happens when multiple group members share the same characteristic. It also theorizes nested local and global status hierarchies, showing that status characteristics can have different implications depending on whether their value is local to the board or recognized in the broader director labor market. For corporate governance research, it moves beyond formal board structure to explain board turnover through informal board social structures.

**Practical Implication**
For newly public firms, board composition should not be treated as a simple exercise in adding high-status or similarly credentialed directors. The findings suggest that maintaining board stability may require enough heterogeneity in tenure, expertise, and status characteristics for deference structures and distinct spheres of influence to form. Firms refreshing boards during transitions should consider how new appointments affect the board’s informal social order, not only whether appointees look prestigious on paper.

**Limitations**
The study uses archival data, so it cannot directly observe boardroom interaction, directors’ perceptions of the local status hierarchy, or the actual decision processes behind director exits. The dependent variable does not distinguish voluntary from involuntary exits because such information is limited for newly public firms. The sample also has limited variance in diffuse status characteristics such as race, gender, and nationality, and it focuses on young U.S. firms during a period that included SOX and the 2008 financial crisis.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine individual director-level responses to board status hierarchies, including whether committee chairs, board chairs, or directors with specialized skills react differently to hierarchy and overlap. Scholars could also study the consequences of board turnover and refreshment, the interaction of diffuse and specific status characteristics, and how board fault lines are created and activated. Additional work using experiments, ethnography, surveys, policy capturing, or qualitative comparative analysis could directly observe board dynamics and test whether these mechanisms travel to older firms, other time periods, and outcomes beyond board turnover.
