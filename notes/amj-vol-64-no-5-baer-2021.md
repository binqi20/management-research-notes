# Zoning Out or Breaking Through? Linking Daydreaming to Creativity in the Workplace

**Abstract**
Much has been written about the liabilities of mind wandering in the workplace. Given its prevalence, however, mind wandering may carry underappreciated benefits—especially with respect to creativity. Examining this possibility, we hypothesize that mind wandering involving imaginative thoughts, also known as “daydreams,” has the potential to spur creativity. We develop a theoretical model in which we examine two facets of daydreams based on their content: problem-oriented daydreams and bizarre daydreams. In addition, we specifyanantecedentconditionthat produces such daydreams(cognitively demanding work; Studies 1 & 2) as well as a boundary condition of the effects of daydreaming on creativity (professional identification; Study 2). Taken together, the studies reported here largely support our theoretical model. Cognitively demanding work reliably elicits both facets of daydreams. However, only problem-oriented daydreams relate to creativity directly; the relationship between bizarre daydreams and creativity is entirely dependent onprofessionalidentification. Inaddition, weobserve negative relationshipsbetween both facets of daydreams and performance more generally when employees lack professional identification (Study 2). Our results indicate that among professionally identified individuals, daydreaming carries noteworthy benefits for creativity but can also impair performance in the absence of identification.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether daydreaming, a form of imaginative mind wandering, can produce creative benefits in workplace settings rather than only performance costs. It examines whether cognitively demanding work elicits problem-oriented and bizarre daydreams, and whether professional identification conditions their effects on creativity and performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Cognitively demanding work.
- DV(s): Creativity, with performance examined for discriminant validity.
- Mediators: Problem-oriented daydreams and bizarre daydreams.
- Moderators: Professional identification moderates the daydreaming-to-creativity links in Study 2.

The proposed mechanism is that cognitively demanding work stimulates or overloads employees' cognition, drawing the mind toward imaginative daydreams. Problem-oriented daydreams can directly generate novel and useful ideas for existing work problems, while bizarre daydreams may become creatively useful when highly professionally identified individuals treat them as material for problem finding or idea generation. Without professional identification, the same daydreaming can undermine broader performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reframes workplace mind wandering by showing that daydreaming can be a source of creativity rather than only a liability. It differentiates problem-oriented and bizarre daydreams and identifies professional identification as a boundary condition shaping whether employees convert daydreaming into creative value.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations that depend on creativity should be cautious about treating daydreaming only as distraction or deviance. The findings suggest that professionally identified employees may benefit from more license to daydream and from reduced stigma around discussing ideas that emerge from such episodes.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the two studies do not establish the causal ordering of cognitively demanding work, daydreaming, and creativity. They also note that the designs measured quotidian and generalized tendencies rather than specific daydreaming episodes.

**Future Research**
Future research could use experimental designs and finer-grained experience sampling to test causal ordering and episode-level dynamics. The authors also call for direct tests of the mechanisms linking cognitively demanding work to different daydream types and of whether problem-oriented and bizarre daydreams map onto different stages or forms of creativity.
