# Institutional Translation Gone Wrong: The Case of Villages for Africa in Rural Tanzania

**Abstract**
Why do ideas that have been successfully moved across highly different contexts subsequently disappear? To answer this question, we use longitudinal data on the organization Villages for Africa that introduced “macrocredit” loans to rural Tanzanians that would enable them to establish their own village enterprises. Several years after the implementation of the idea, it collapsed. Our findings allow us to make two key contributions. First, we provide a process model of high-distance translation that shows how proponents can strategically introduce an idea across highly different contexts by “culturally detaching” it from its institutional origins, leading to the idea being “culturally assimilated” into the recipient context. However, although cultural detachment and cultural assimilation indicate the successful translation of an idea, the means of doing so can later prompt its rejection. We call this the “reactance effect” of translations across highly different contexts. Second, we showcase the role of history for translation theory more generally. History—particularly the historical relationship between the sociocultural categories of the mzungu (Swahili for “foreigner”) and the villagers— influenced the way in which the macrocredit idea could be introduced to villagers and played a key role in its subsequent rejection.

**Research Question**
The paper asks why ideas that appear to have been successfully moved across high institutional distance may later disappear. It examines this question through Villages for Africa's macrocredit initiative in rural Tanzania, focusing on how an initially accepted development idea became rejected after adoption.

**Mechanism Process**
- Contextual conditions: Historical suspicion of the mzungu coexisted with hope and admiration for foreign-backed development initiatives.
- Preadoption translation: VFA culturally detached the macrocredit idea from its foreign origins through local surrogates, political cooptation of elders, localized customization, and ceremonial affirmation.
- Postadoption translation: Villagers culturally assimilated the idea through inclusive-exclusivity, materialization of social norms, and emotive celebration, creating a locally owned interpretation of the village enterprises.
- Collision and aftermath: Requests for repayment and renewed VFA involvement triggered in-group shaming, historically anchored vilification, anger, and reluctance to reengage with foreigners.

The process is not a simple failure of initial adoption. The paper argues that cultural detachment helped VFA gain acceptance, while cultural assimilation made the idea meaningful and locally owned for villagers. The same dual success later produced a reactance effect: when VFA reappeared to advise the enterprises and request repayment, villagers interpreted this as a violation of local ownership and as another instance of mzungu exploitation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a process model of high-distance translation failure that distinguishes preadoption cultural detachment from postadoption cultural assimilation. It shows that the conditions enabling successful translation across very different sociocultural contexts can also heighten later rejection when recipients' locally owned interpretation collides with the proponent's assumptions. The paper also extends institutional translation theory by foregrounding history, sociocultural categories, and emotional registers as forces that shape both translation strategies and translation breakdown.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations moving social or business ideas across high institutional distance should not assume that local acceptance means shared understanding. The study suggests that proponents need to communicate their own sociocultural assumptions, including meanings attached to contracts, ownership, repayment, entrepreneurship, and accountability, while still translating those assumptions into recipient-language and recipient-context terms. It also implies that historically charged relationships can remain latent until a triggering event makes them central.

**Limitations**
The study is a single qualitative case of failure in rural Tanzania, and the authors explicitly note that generalizing from one failed case is difficult. The data predominantly capture local informants' perspectives, and the authors acknowledge that their longitudinal narrative was influenced by the third author's interpretations and field relationships. The empirical setting is also a high-distance development context, so the model should be tested against other forms and degrees of institutional distance.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine whether the strategies and processes identified here characterize all high-distance translation contexts, and whether they are unique to such contexts compared with low-distance translations. Scholars could also study high sociocultural distance within countries, including racial, class, religious, ideological, or migration-related divides. The paper further calls for longer-term studies of translation, including how adapted ideas may be translated back into their context of origin as a form of reverse innovation.
