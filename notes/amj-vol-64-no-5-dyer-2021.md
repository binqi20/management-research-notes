# Who Should Become a Business School Associate Dean? Individual Performance and Taking on Firm-Specific Roles

**Abstract**
The "firm-specific human capital dilemma" suggests that firms generally want employees to make firm-specific investments but that employees prefer not to make them. We suggest that individual performance may moderate this dilemma such that the dilemma increases as individual performance increases—that is, firms may prefer high performers in firm-specific roles while high performers may resist these roles more than their lowerperforming counterparts. We examine our extended firm-specific human capital theory in a context in which the classic firm-specific human capital dilemma likely exists: business academia. Using a unique data set of 4,164 business school professors from 39 of the top 100 U.S. business schools, we examine how research performance affects propensity to become an associate dean and their compensation increases when taking on these roles. Even though associate dean roles come with a significant pay increase, we find that higherperformingindividualsare lesslikelytobecomeassociatedeans. Surprisingly,wefind that lower performers receive higher pay increases when taking on these roles. We conduct exploratory interviews to understand this surprising finding, and discuss implications and opportunities for future research.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how individual research performance affects a business school professor's likelihood of taking on a firm-specific administrative role as associate dean and the compensation increase attached to that role. More broadly, it asks whether individual performance intensifies the firm-specific human capital dilemma by increasing both firm desire for high performers and high performers' resistance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Research performance/general human capital performance, measured primarily as cumulative Financial Times 50 publications.
- DV(s): Serving as an associate dean in a given year; log annual salary/compensation change when serving as associate dean.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Individual performance is theorized as the condition that changes the magnitude of the firm-specific human capital dilemma; performance also interacts with associate dean status in the salary models.

The mechanism is that universities often prefer research-productive faculty for firm-specific administrative roles because performance signals ability, credibility, and capacity to help others. At the same time, high performers face higher opportunity costs and mobility risks because research productivity is portable and externally valued. The authors therefore predict a lower probability of becoming an associate dean as research productivity increases, while also expecting stronger inducements for high performers. The first prediction is supported; the compensation prediction is reversed, with lower-performing associate deans receiving larger salary increases.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends firm-specific human capital theory by making individual performance a moderator of the classic firm-specific human capital dilemma rather than treating employee resistance as uniform. It shows that the dilemma can intensify for high performers because firm demand and employee resistance move in opposite directions as performance rises. The exploratory evidence further refines the theory by showing how salary bands and equity concerns can constrain financial inducements, producing a zone of misaligned incentives and possible boundary conditions around star performers.

**Practical Implication**
For business schools, associate dean selection involves a trade-off: high-performing faculty may bring credibility and capability but sacrifice more research productivity and may resist the role more strongly. Lower-performing faculty may gain more financially from the role and may be easier to attract, but schools need to assess whether they can perform the administrative work effectively. Compensation systems based on salary bands or internal equity may unintentionally make high performers less willing to accept firm-specific administrative roles.

**Limitations**
The setting is business academia, where tenure, visible publication metrics, nonprofit missions, and public salary information may make the human capital dilemma different from other professional service firms. The authors also cannot directly observe resistance in the archival data and therefore use exploratory interviews and supplemental analyses to support that mechanism. Generalizability depends on whether other contexts have similar job security, performance transparency, relative value of general versus firm-specific investments, and organizational mission.

**Future Research**
Future research could test whether the proposed performance-based dilemma appears in consulting, financial services, legal services, health care, and other professional service settings. The authors also call for work on star performers, where firms may stop wanting the highest performers to take firm-specific roles because their continued general human capital production is too valuable. Additional research should examine other factors that moderate individual resistance to firm-specific investments and the conditions under which firms compensate, undercompensate, or avoid assigning such roles.
