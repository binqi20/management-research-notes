# Navigating Space for Personal Agency: Auxiliary Routines as Adaptations in Toxic Organizations

**Abstract**
Many workers experience organization dysfunction stemming from leaders. Yet organization members have limited responses; they can directly or indirectly confront senior leaders, engage individual stress coping strategies, or leave the organization. We offer another response by theorizing auxiliary routines as behavioral sequences through which multiple actors coordinate responses to complex and enduring socioemotional dynamics that threaten to undermine the enactment of standard operating task routines. Through a qualitative, inductive study of a consulting firm, we delimit three auxiliary routines—absorption, dissemination, and differentiation—through which people navigate between the destructiveness of organizational toxicity and the need to perform given roles and tasks. We illustrate how these routines emerged in response to role and psychological diminishment originating from senior leaders, how the routines helped manage and sometimes perpetuate diminishment, and the consequences for individuals’ personal agency and the organization as a whole. In doing so, we contribute to knowledge about coping with toxic organizational conditions and on routines as a facet of emotional capability in organizations.

**Research Question**
How do organization members work with and around toxic conditions that narrow personal agency while still accomplishing necessary work? The paper asks how recurring patterns of coordinated behavior allow managers and subordinates to navigate role and psychological diminishment originating from senior leadership.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: organizational toxicity, CEO centrality, CEO aggression, role diminishment, psychological diminishment, personal agency, focal routines, and auxiliary routines.
- Process / routines: absorption, dissemination, and differentiation.
- Triggering conditions: persistent diminishment by senior leaders, collusion by other Alpha members, and hierarchical boundaries that varied in permeability.
- Emergent outcomes: held, narrowed, or expanded space for personal agency; maintenance, amplification, or separation from diminishment; and reduced organizational growth.
- Boundary conditions: the routines depended on middle leaders' positions between senior leaders and group members, and differentiation depended on a distinct client base and external network.

The process begins with toxic organizational conditions that reduce members' role authority and psychological space. Middle leaders respond by initiating auxiliary routines: absorption buffers subordinates and holds limited space for agency, dissemination aligns middle leaders upward and amplifies diminishment downward, and differentiation creates more separate group space that expands agency. These routines help work continue, but they also stabilize the toxic system and limit firm-wide integration and growth.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends routine theory by theorizing auxiliary routines as emergent, coordinated adaptations to socioemotional complexity, not only as supports for focal task routines. It also contributes to research on toxic organizations by shifting attention from individual coping choices to system-level patterns through which members collectively manage and perpetuate toxicity. Finally, it links auxiliary routines to emotional capability by showing how routines can regulate collective emotional energy, either supporting or handicapping members' availability for work.

**Practical Implication**
The study suggests that organization members facing toxic conditions may develop recurring pathways for protecting themselves and others while still doing their work, but these pathways have costs. Middle leaders need to attend to whether their boundary-management routines buffer others, transmit toxicity, or create healthier independent bases of agency. Leaders and consultants should examine how structures, decision processes, and collusion authorize some people while deauthorizing others, because those patterns shape both personal agency and actual work.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the theorizing is limited by one specific organization with idiosyncratic personality, political, hierarchical, structural, and cultural dynamics. AMP involved a particular form of toxicity centered on CEO centrality and aggression, so other organizations with peer-, partner-, or customer-generated toxicity may develop different auxiliary routines. The small case setting also leaves open whether a larger cohort of middle leaders would produce variations on these routines or different mechanisms for managing collective emotional energy.

**Future Research**
Future research could chart the range, frequency, triggers, and implications of other auxiliary routines across different toxic organizational contexts. Studies could examine whether routines focus on boundary negotiation, coalition building, dissent, or other responses when toxicity has different sources. Additional empirical work could also investigate how auxiliary routines relate to emotional capability in settings marked by emotional exhaustion, emotional labor, dysfunctional decision-making, or unacknowledged organizational disturbance.
