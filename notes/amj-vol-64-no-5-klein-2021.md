# The Dynamics of Framing: Image, Emotion, and the European Migration Crisis

**Abstract**
an inherently dynamic process.

**Research Question**
The paper asks how and why the framing of a contentious societal issue changes over time, with particular attention to emotions in media organizations. Empirically, it examines how the publication of the Alan Kurdi photograph altered U.K. newspaper framing of the European migration crisis.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / frames: migrant frame, refugee frame, frame prominence, framing bricolage, emotional array, iconic photograph, receptive ideology, non-receptive ideology, emotional fatigue.
- Process / phases: pre-image framing of displaced people, publication and circulation of the Alan Kurdi photograph, rapid frame-emphasis shift, emotional-array amplification/degradation, newsroom behavior change, and temporal diminishing of the image effect.
- Practices / analytic moves: historical contextualization, frame identification, frame-prominence coding, emotional-intensity coding, visual analysis, and interview-based reconstruction of newsroom mechanisms.
- Emergent outcomes: the refugee frame gained prominence, the migrant frame lost dehumanizing emotional force, resource allocation and campaigning changed in some newspapers, and the duration of change varied by ideology and emotional fatigue.
- Boundary conditions: the process depends on an emotionally powerful image, media organizations as framing agents, audience expectations, organizational ideology, and the continuing salience of the issue.

The process begins with two historically available frames: migrants as economic or social threat and refugees as vulnerable people needing protection. The Alan Kurdi photograph carried emotional cues that aligned with the refugee frame, intensifying compassion, solidarity, anger, and shame while weakening disgust, fear, anger, and dehumanizing language in the migrant frame. In receptive newspapers, emotional energy and moral justification supported longer-lasting reframing and action; in non-receptive newspapers, perceived reader expectations produced a more instrumental and shorter-lived shift. Emotional fatigue and competing news issues then reduced attention, although the emotional arrays remained altered.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends framing theory by showing that macro-level frames are more plastic and temporally dynamic than static accounts imply. It introduces emotional arrays as the combination of emotions, emotional intensity, and associated language through which frames gain meaning, legitimacy, and behavioral force. It also theorizes distinct mechanisms of framing change in media organizations with receptive versus non-receptive ideologies, showing how iconic images can reshape frame emphasis even when ideology would otherwise resist change.

**Practical Implication**
For actors trying to influence public policy or social debate, the study suggests that iconic photographs can create a window for reframing, but the mechanism and duration of that window vary across media organizations. Advocacy and policy actors should attend not only to message content, but also to the emotional array attached to competing frames and to whether target media organizations are ideologically receptive. The findings also caution that emotional fatigue and new events can quickly redirect media resources even after a powerful reframing moment.

**Limitations**
The authors identify scope conditions around the focal organizations, the medium of the photograph, and the extreme nature of the case. The empirical setting is U.K. newspapers, so the theorizing needs testing in other framing organizations such as social media platforms, television companies, governments, and nongovernmental organizations. The Alan Kurdi photograph was unusually powerful, so less extreme images or other forms of text may not generate the same cross-ideological effect.

**Future Research**
Future research could test the theory across other media, organizational, and policy settings, including social media and multimodal technologies. The authors call for work on how frame plasticity varies over time, whether hybrid frames emerge from conflicting frames, and how different emotional arrays produce different social outcomes. They also highlight emotional fatigue as a promising area for studying why reframing effects decay or persist.
