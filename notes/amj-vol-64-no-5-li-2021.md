# High Risk, Low Return (and Vice Versa): The Effect of Product Innovation on Firm Performance in a Transition Economy

**Abstract**
Common wisdom suggests that high-risk strategies will be associated with high expected returns and vice versa. Focusing on the effect of new product development on firm performance, in this paper we argue that this relationship may reverse in a market undergoing substantial institutional transition. We examine domestic pharmaceutical firms in China during the 1990s and find that in this context, introducing new products was associated with lower average firm profitability but higher variance. In conformity with our predictions, these relationships were stronger in areas where the rate of institutional change was higher and for product types that took longer to develop. Thus, we explain why, for particular strategic actions, high risk may be associated with low returns. A key conceptual corollary of these findings—and for strategic management research in general—is that firms may sometimes be more focused on the potential upside of their actions than on the expected value of those actions.

**Research Question**
The paper asks when a specific strategic action can generate a Bowman-type effect by increasing risk while decreasing expected return. It studies whether product innovation in China's transitioning pharmaceutical industry lowered average firm profitability while increasing variance in firm performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Product innovation, measured as the number of self-developed new drugs introduced by a firm.
- DV(s): Firm performance, measured as annual return on sales, and variance in firm performance.
- Mediators: Not modeled as mediators; the theorized process is value-appropriability uncertainty created by institutional transition and lengthy product development.
- Moderators: Length of the product development process and rate of institutional change in the focal provincial market; post hoc moderators include IPR protection and bribery prosecution.

The mechanism is that long product-development lead times expose innovating firms to unpredictable shifts in institutional rules, drug approval, pricing, and appropriability conditions. In this setting, new products could occasionally create large upside gains, but more often the targeted performance peak had diminished by commercialization, producing lower average returns and higher variance. The authors argue that firms may still innovate because they focus on upside potential and visible top innovators rather than expected value.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts Bowman-paradox research from population-level correlations between firm risk and return to the level of a particular strategic action. It shows that product innovation can be high risk and low return when lengthy lead times meet institutional transition and uncertain value appropriability. It also argues that variance in performance should be studied as a dependent variable in its own right, not only as noise around mean performance.

**Practical Implication**
Managers in turbulent transition economies should not infer that a strategy is generally profitable merely because it is common among top performers. For long-cycle innovation projects, they need to evaluate both expected value and performance variance, especially where pricing, approval, IPR, and corruption risks affect appropriability. Policymakers seeking indigenous innovation should recognize that unstable institutions may make innovation privately unattractive even when the upside cases are visible.

**Limitations**
The authors state that they do not observe the pre-transition period, so they cannot directly test whether product innovation would avoid a Bowman effect without institutional change. They also observe new products that emerge from the invention pipeline and seek production permission, but not abandoned development processes, products withheld from permission requests, R&D expenditures, or each product's inherent promise. The sample consists mostly of small and medium-sized firms, making the influence of a single new product especially salient.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how other strategic actions, such as mergers and acquisitions, international expansion, top management turnover, or adoption of management techniques, affect both mean performance and variance. Studies could also test whether similar mechanisms arise from market or technological uncertainty rather than institutional transition. Additional work observing aborted product-development processes and complementary-resource arrangements could clarify how firms generate and trade off risk and return.
