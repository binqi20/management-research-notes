# The Effects of Racial Diversity Congruence between Upper Management and Lower Management on Firm Productivity

**Abstract**
We examine whether matching levels of racial diversity in upper management and lower management (i.e., racial diversity congruence) impact firm productivity. In a sample of high-tech firms, we found that congruence between upper-management racial diversity and lower-management racial diversity positively impacts firm productivity, supporting knowledge-based view perspectives. Furthermore, organizations with high levels of racial diversity in both upper and lower management (i.e., high–high racial diversity congruence) realized superior productivity compared to organizations with low levels of racial diversity in both upper and lower management (i.e., low–low racial diversity congruence). Results also revealed differences across levels of racial diversity incongruence between upper management and lower management (i.e., asymmetry effects) whereby firmswith amore racially diverse uppermanagement thanlowermanagement outproduced firms with a more racially diverse lower management than upper management. A supplemental sample of Fortune 500 firms enhanced generalizability, as the pattern of findings was very similar to that of the high-tech sample. We discuss the study’s implications and make a call for future research that simultaneously considers the upper echelons and the lower echelons.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether congruence between racial diversity in upper management and lower management is associated with higher firm productivity. It further asks whether high–high racial diversity congruence outperforms low–low congruence, and whether high upper-management/low lower-management incongruence differs from the reverse pattern.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Upper-management racial diversity, lower-management racial diversity, and their congruence or incongruence.
- DV(s): Firm productivity, measured as revenue divided by number of employees and log-transformed.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper; the study models congruence level and direction of asymmetry between upper-management and lower-management racial diversity as focal response-surface configurations rather than as conventional moderators.

The mechanism is that racial diversity functions as a knowledge-based resource, but its productivity value depends on how diversity-based knowledge and absorptive capacity are distributed across managerial tiers. Congruence between upper and lower management supports knowledge symmetry and strategy implementation, while incongruence creates knowledge asymmetry. High–high congruence is theorized to generate the strongest productivity because both strategy formulation and strategy implementation benefit from racial diversity-based knowledge.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends upper echelons theory by arguing that lower management should be incorporated into explanations of firm outcomes, because lower managers implement strategy and transmit knowledge upward. It also contributes to knowledge-based view and diversity research by showing that racial diversity effects depend on cross-tier alignment rather than on diversity within a single managerial group alone.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that firms should avoid large racial-diversity disparities between lower and upper management if they want to convert diversity-based knowledge into productivity gains. Organizations with racially diverse lower management may especially need to improve racial diversity in upper management so that ideas, knowledge, and absorptive capacity are not concentrated in lower tiers with less strategic influence.

**Limitations**
The authors state that the study does not directly measure the mechanisms explaining the relationship between racial diversity congruence and firm productivity. They also do not measure the knowledge of every upper- and lower-level manager, and the EEOC data do not allow them to distinguish subgroups within broad racial categories or identify whether diversity came from internal promotion or external hiring.

**Future Research**
Future research could directly measure the mechanisms linking managerial racial diversity to productivity, including promotion versus external hiring, leadership, empowerment culture, and knowledge exchange across tiers. The authors also call for studies of racial diversity congruence in other industries, nationality congruence across management tiers, and faultline approaches that consider multiple demographic characteristics simultaneously.
