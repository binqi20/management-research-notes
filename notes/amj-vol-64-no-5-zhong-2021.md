# Customer Concentration, Executive Attention, and Firm Search Behavior

**Abstract**
It is unclear why, despite the increasing emphasis on innovation in modern business, firms are still myopic in their search efforts. Although prior studies have examined the role of supply-side factors such as technologies, capabilities, and managerial cognition, they rarely explore how demand-side factors such as customers can shape managerial cognition and firm search behavior. Extending the attention-based view to the demand side, we argue that major customers channel the attention of executives to the existing competitive environment, resources and capabilities, and management practices (i.e., existing-oriented attention), resulting in firms’ increased emphasis on deep search within familiar domains and limiting their search in new fields. Through an empirical examination of the patenting behavior of U.S. publicly listed firms, we show that customer concentration increases search depth and decreases search breadth via executives’ existing-oriented orientation. We also find that both performance feedback above social aspiration levels and product market competition amplify the effects of customer concentration. Our study contributes to the organizational search literature by adding a demand-side explanation. It also extends the cognition view by foregrounding the role of customers in shaping executive attention. Moreover, it explores the interaction between supplyand demandside factors in driving firm search behavior.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether and how a demand-side structural factor, customer concentration, affects firm search behavior. It specifically examines whether major customers shape executives' existing-oriented attention and thereby increase search depth while reducing search breadth.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Customer concentration, measured with an HHI-based measure using sales to major customers.
- DV(s): Search depth and search breadth, measured from firms' patent citation patterns.
- Mediators: Existing-oriented executive attention, derived from textual analysis of top managers' shareholder letters and earnings conference calls.
- Moderators: Performance feedback relative to social and historical aspirations, and product market competition measured primarily with TNIC.

The mechanism is that dependence on a few major customers directs executives' attention toward the existing competitive environment, existing resources and capabilities, and established management practices. This existing-oriented attention encourages firms to deepen search within familiar knowledge domains and to limit broader exploration into new fields. The paper finds partial mediation through existing-oriented attention, and it shows that social performance feedback above aspirations and high product market competition strengthen the customer-concentration relationship with deeper and narrower search.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper adds a demand-side explanation to organizational search research by showing that customer structure can help explain why firms persist in myopic search. It extends the attention-based and cognition literatures by positioning customers, not only technologies, capabilities, or competitors, as sources of executive attention. It also integrates demand-side and supply-side accounts by showing that social performance feedback and product market competition condition the effect of customer concentration.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that firms highly dependent on a few major customers should monitor whether customer demands are narrowing executive attention and search behavior. The results also suggest that positive performance relative to peers and high product market competition can reinforce customer-driven search myopia rather than automatically encouraging broader exploration.

**Limitations**
The authors note that patent data may not capture all forms of search because patenting propensity varies across industries and some firms may avoid patenting critical knowledge. They also state that executive attention measures based on public statements may be affected by social desirability bias and emotional elements. The paper further acknowledges that it does not explain the determinants of customer concentration and focuses on customer structure rather than the content of heterogeneous customer demands.

**Future Research**
Future research could use alternative indicators of search behavior beyond patents and improve executive-attention measurement through field experiments, video coding, or other designs. Scholars could examine why customer concentration emerges, especially in smaller, younger, less profitable, and more R&D-intensive firms. Additional work could also study how different customer-demand characteristics, not just the concentration structure of customers, affect firm search behavior.
