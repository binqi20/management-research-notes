# Distinct but Not Apart? Stigma Reduction and Cross-Industry Evaluative Spillovers: The Case of Medical Marijuana Legalization

**Abstract**
Previous research on stigma reduction has been primarily confined to single industries. However, industry boundaries are porous in the eyes of audience members and can hardly act as artificial confines to moral reevaluations. We contend that evaluative spillovers induced by stigma reduction in a focal industry diffuse to other industries that share the same vilifying label when (a) they possess the morally approved attribute that underlies the reduction of stigma, and (b) the morally approved attribute is embedded in formal rules and procedures that ensure the desired purposes. Empirically, we focus our tests on the legalization of marijuana as a medicine in several U.S. states during the period 2000–2015, which has reduced the “vice” stigma around marijuana by highlighting the “health safety” attribute of marijuana, namely its safe consumption and potential health benefits. We show that legalization generated evaluative spillovers to other “vice” industries sharing the same health safety attribute as marijuana (i.e., alcohol), but not to those that did not share it (i.e., tobacco). Those spillovers were primarily observed when the law was accompanied by a patient registration system that highlighted the health safety attribute of marijuana by limiting its use to medical purposes.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether stigma reduction in one industry can generate positive evaluative spillovers across industry boundaries. It examines when medical marijuana legalization changes customer behavior toward other vice industries, especially alcohol and tobacco, and whether formal patient registration systems strengthen those spillovers.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Medical marijuana legalization and medical marijuana legalization accompanied by a mandatory patient registration system.
- DV(s): State-level alcohol sales and tobacco sales, with supplementary analyses of marijuana consumption, media health-safety discourse, binge drinking, and gambling sales.
- Mediators: The paper theorizes evaluative reassessment of vice industries through the morally approved health safety attribute, but does not estimate a formal mediation model.
- Moderators: Mandatory patient registration systems that limit marijuana use to medical purposes and embed the health safety attribute in formal rules and procedures.

The mechanism begins when legitimate but incongruent information from medical marijuana legalization challenges taken-for-granted vice stigma and shifts audiences from label-based judgment to attribute-based evaluation. Alcohol, unlike tobacco, is theorized to share the health safety attribute with medical marijuana when consumed responsibly, so evaluative spillovers should increase alcohol sales more than tobacco sales. The paper argues that patient registration systems make the health safety claim more procedurally legitimate, which is why the positive spillover to alcohol appears mainly when legalization is paired with such systems.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to stigma research by showing that stigma reduction need not remain confined to the focal industry where reevaluation begins. It reconciles stigma as a vilifying label with stigma as moral evaluation by arguing that legitimate incongruent information can move audiences into an attribute-centered reassessment mode. It also adds counterfactual quantitative evidence that formal rules and procedures shape the credibility and cross-industry reach of stigma reduction.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that policymakers should consider how regulations in one stigmatized industry can alter evaluations and behavior in adjacent industries. Medical marijuana rules accompanied by patient registration systems changed alcohol sales patterns, suggesting that formal procedures can make a stigma-reducing health-safety claim more credible. The paper cautions against assuming that customer preferences remain fixed when new laws redefine the moral meaning of stigmatized products.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their theory accounts for multiple audiences but their empirical analysis focuses mainly on customers and, secondarily, media discourse. They also acknowledge that they infer micro-level reassessment processes from aggregate behavior rather than testing individual-level mechanisms directly. The boundary conditions are limited to settings where industries share a vilifying label and differ in whether they possess the morally approved attribute made salient by a legitimate source.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine other audiences such as social movements, watchdog groups, religious organizations, social media, banks, insurers, and corporations. Studies could test the individual-level mechanisms directly, for example through laboratory experiments. Further work could also generalize the argument beyond vice industries to other contexts where a legitimate source makes a morally approved attribute salient across stigmatized categories.
