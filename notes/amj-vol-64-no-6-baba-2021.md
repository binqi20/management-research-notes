# Increasing Dispositional Legitimacy: Progressive Legitimation Dynamics in a Trajectory of Settlements

**Abstract**
By focusing on individual settlements rather than viewing them as part of a trajectory of change, prior research on institutional settlements has not fully captured the dynamics of longer-term institutional change; yet this would be important to better understand progressive legitimation dynamics—in other words, how successive settlements may increase legitimacy in a long-term trajectory of change. This is a crucial issue, especially for peripheral actors lacking the resources, status, or power of more central ones. Our historical analysis focuses on the long-term struggle of the Cree First Nation in Canada and explains how they succeeded in increasing their dispositional legitimacy in and through the settlements. This required work in three interrelated processes: expansive argumentation, including problematizing of the state of affairs and creating discursive resonance with the prevailing discourses and values; building momentum by generating attention and exerting pressure in networks of actors; and seizing opportunities to negotiate with the other side. Thus, we challenge previous research on settlements by showing how they should not merely be seen as endpoints but rather as stepping-stones in a trajectory of institutional change. More specifically, we develop a process model explaining how progressive legitimation unfolds in a series of settlements.

**Research Question**
How can peripheral actors succeed in progressively increasing their dispositional legitimacy in a series of settlements? The paper examines this question through the Cree First Nation's long-term struggle to legitimate Indigenous rights in and through successive settlements with Quebec, Canada, and Hydro-Quebec.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core phenomenon: progressive legitimation in a trajectory of settlements, where each settlement both formalizes legitimacy and becomes a basis for later legitimation work.
- Focal legitimacy construct: dispositional legitimacy, defined in the paper as recognition of the interests, rights, or voice of an actor in a given field.
- Process 1: expansive argumentation, consisting of problematizing the state of affairs and creating discursive resonance with broader discourses and values.
- Process 2: building momentum, through generating attention, rallying support, and exerting pressure in networks of actors.
- Process 3: seizing negotiation opportunities, including knowing when to negotiate, delay, refuse, or accept imperfect settlements.
- Process outcome: increased dispositional legitimacy, shown in the progressive formalization, broadening, defense, reinterpretation, and extension of Cree Indigenous rights across four settlements.

The process model explains legitimation as cumulative and temporally patterned. Ongoing expansive argumentation gives the Crees a basis for building momentum; that momentum creates or strengthens negotiation opportunities; and negotiated settlements formalize legitimacy in ways that increase voice, network centrality, and bargaining position for later rounds. The paper therefore treats settlements not as terminal outcomes but as stepping-stones that enable further institutional change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances research on institutional settlements by shifting attention from individual settlements as endpoints to settlements as part of a longer trajectory of institutional change. It contributes to legitimacy and legitimation research by placing dispositional legitimacy at the center and showing how recognition of interests, rights, or voice can accumulate through formal settlements. It also extends research on peripheral and marginalized actors by theorizing how actors with limited initial power can use argumentation, momentum-building, and negotiation timing to progressively improve their position.

**Practical Implication**
For peripheral actors seeking recognition of rights, the study implies that moral argumentation alone is insufficient: actors also need to build momentum through media, political, legal, and network activity, and then judge when to formalize gains through imperfect settlements. For governments and other central actors, the analysis suggests that settlements are not final closure points; unresolved issues and partial recognition may become the basis for later claims and renegotiations.

**Limitations**
The authors emphasize that the model is grounded in a specific cultural-institutional context: peripheral actors dealing with a government organization in a Western democracy. The case is also a relatively successful trajectory in which four settlements increased dispositional legitimacy, so the model may not apply unchanged to less successful, regressive, authoritarian, or more politically unstable settings. The study focuses on the Cree side of the process, leaving the other side and internal divisions among key parties less fully examined.

**Future Research**
Future research could compare the Cree case with other institutional and cultural contexts, including authoritarian governments, major political transitions, less successful legitimation efforts, and movements such as Black Lives Matter or Palestinian self-determination. The authors also call for deeper research on dispositional legitimacy, including its dimensions, relationship to reputation or stigma, and effects on voice and negotiation. Further studies could examine micro-level rhetorical strategies, the role of history and memory, outright power dynamics, actors' skills and learning, the other side of negotiations, and internal divisions within stakeholder groups.
