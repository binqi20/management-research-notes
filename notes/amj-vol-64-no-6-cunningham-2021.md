# Seeing Oneself as a Valued Contributor: Social Worth Affirmation Improves Team Information Sharing

**Abstract**
Teams often fail to reach their potential because members’ concerns about being socially accepted prevent them from offering their unique perspectives to the team. Drawing on relational self and self-affirmation theory, we argue that affirmation of team members’ social worth by trusted people outside the team helps them internalize an identity as a valued contributor, thereby reducing social acceptance concerns and facilitating information sharing in teams. We devised three intervention studies to demonstrate the causal effect of social worth affirmation in teams. In Study 1, senior executive teams in which members experienced social worth affirmation performed better (compared to control teams) on a crisis simulation that required information sharing in teams. In Study 2, with U.S. military cadets, we examined social acceptance concerns as a mechanism by which social worth affirmation influences information sharing. In Study 3, we showed that social worth affirmation improves virtual teams’ ability to share information by exchanging unique information cues. Our results suggest that affirmation of the social worth of team members through their personal relationships broadens their sense of self, thereby reducing their social concerns about being accepted by other members. This, in turn, leads to better information sharing in teams.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether affirming team members' social worth through narratives from close relationships outside the team reduces social acceptance concerns and improves information sharing in teams. It also asks whether social acceptance concerns explain the effect of social worth affirmation on team information sharing.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Social worth affirmation from close relationships.
- DV(s): Team information sharing, unique information sharing, direct information requests, and team performance in tasks requiring information integration.
- Mediators: Social acceptance concerns.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper.

The mechanism links external relational resources to team processes. Narratives from trusted personal contacts affirm members as valued contributors, broadening their working sense of self before or during team entry. This reduces concern about whether other team members will accept them, making members more willing to volunteer unique information and directly request information from others. Across the studies, the intervention improved team performance in a crisis simulation, problem solving in the Mt. Everest hidden-profile simulation, and exchange of unique cues in virtual teams.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to relational self theory by showing how close relationships outside a team can make a valued-contributor identity salient inside a new team. It extends self-affirmation theory by shifting affirmation from private reflection on personal values to social worth affirmed through others' narratives. It also contributes to team information-sharing research by identifying social acceptance concerns as a micro-level barrier that can be reduced before teams begin their work.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations can prepare members of new or temporary teams by prompting them to reflect on credible narratives of how they have contributed value to others. The intervention is especially relevant when teams must quickly pool unique expertise but have little time to build a shared team identity. Employers and team leaders can use social worth affirmation as a way to support communication and information sharing without relying only on within-team relationship building.

**Limitations**
The paper does not directly measure the identity internalization process that it theorizes, so the psychological shift toward seeing oneself as a valued contributor remains inferred from the intervention and downstream measures. The operationalization of social worth affirmation varies across studies, including differences in whether participants wrote self-narratives, shared narratives with teammates, or received different control exercises. The intervention was examined at the team level, so the studies cannot show whether affirming only one or two team members would produce similar effects for the whole team.

**Future Research**
Future research could directly measure how social worth affirmation changes valued-contributor identity and distinguish affirmation of preexisting self-views from incorporation of newly revealed reflected self-views. Studies could compare different sources, formats, durations, and frequencies of social worth affirmation, including recognition programs or thank-you notes. Future work could also examine whether social worth affirmation from close relationships interacts with affirmation or exclusion from the team itself.
