# Keep Your Eye on the Ball or on the Field? Exploring the Performance Implications of Executive Strategic Attention

**Abstract**
Scholars have highlighted executive attention as a vital organizational resource. However, as prior studies have examined different facets of attention, a theoretical tension has emerged as to whether attention to a broader or narrower set of strategic issues better facilitates firm performance. On the one hand, a narrower breadth of strategic attention limits the risk of managerial cognitive overload. On the other hand, a greater breadth of strategic attention helps managers avoid blind spots and identify more or better opportunities. We argue that this balance shifts based on the quantity and quality of opportunities available in the market and on firms’ recently demonstrated ability to execute against these opportunities effectively and efficiently. Using a novel text-analysis tool that we develop, validate, and make available, we find support for our arguments within a random sample of Standard & Poor’s (S&P) 500 companies. We find that broader strategic attention is associated with enhanced performance in weaker opportunity environments, with firms using their resources efficiently, or with firms’ failure to utilize their capabilities effectively. In supplementary analyses, we also provide new insights into the importance of following attention with subsequent organizational action.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether a greater breadth of executive strategic attention helps or hinders firm performance. It further asks how this relationship depends on the quantity and quality of external opportunities and on firms' recent effectiveness and efficiency in executing against those opportunities.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Breadth of strategic attention, measured as strategic breadth across 13 categories of strategic issues in executives' quarterly earnings-call communications.
- DV(s): Firm performance, measured as return on assets.
- Mediators: Not directly tested; the theory centers on the tradeoff between opportunity identification and cognitive overload, with supplementary analysis examining whether resource allocation follows changes in attention.
- Moderators: Industry munificence, average industry ROA, lagged firm performance, and current assets turnover.

The paper theorizes that broad strategic attention helps executives avoid blind spots and identify scarce or better-fitting opportunities, but also increases cognitive load and can dilute evaluation and execution. Broader attention is expected to be more beneficial when external opportunities are fewer or lower quality, when recent performance suggests poor fit between capabilities and opportunities, or when resource-use efficiency creates capacity to pursue more initiatives. The empirical results support all four moderation hypotheses and suggest in supplementary analyses that broader attention is more valuable when followed by increased resource allocation, especially R&D and capital intensity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the attention-based view and managerial cognition literatures by specifying when broad versus narrow strategic attention is performance-enhancing. It reconciles prior arguments favoring focus with arguments favoring broad responsiveness by showing that the attention-performance relationship depends on external opportunity conditions and internal execution conditions. It also contributes a validated text-analysis tool for measuring attention across 13 strategic issue categories in executive communications.

**Practical Implication**
Executives should not treat focus or breadth as universally superior. The paper suggests broadening attention in weaker opportunity environments, when recent performance indicates poor capability-opportunity fit, or when efficient resource use creates room for additional initiatives. It also warns that attention alone is insufficient: broader attention can harm performance if it is not followed by resource allocation that enables action.

**Limitations**
The paper does not investigate time in significant depth, especially whether firms attend to some strategic issues in the short term and others in the longer term. The strategic breadth measure can take the same value through different combinations of the 13 issue categories, so it does not fully distinguish coherent from incoherent attention portfolios. The supplementary resource-allocation analysis is limited because the data do not provide a precise breakdown of resource allocation by the specific strategic issues receiving managerial attention.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine longer time horizons and compare firms that adapt their breadth of strategic attention appropriately with firms that do not. Scholars could also study which individual strategic issues, or which combinations of issues, shape performance under different conditions. Additional work could connect attention to issue-specific resource allocation rather than only aggregate resource changes.
