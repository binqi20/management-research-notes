# In With the Old? Examining When Boomerang Employees Outperform New Hires

**Abstract**
As most careers now span across organizations, former employees represent a growing source of potential hires for many organizations. Yet, we know little about whether and when firms benefit by rehiring former employees. To answer these questions, we adopt a knowledge-based view of hiring to develop new theory about how returning former employees’ (“boomerangs”) post-hire performance might differ from that of external hires who have no previous experience with the firm (“new hires”). We theorize that, relative to new hires, boomerangs’ familiarity with the organization’s social system will allow them to more effectively engage in coordination and overcome internal resistance from organizational incumbents. As a consequence, boomerangs should have a particular advantage in roles that require a higher degree of coordination and in units that are likely to be more resistant to outsiders. Comparing the post-hire performance of 2,053 boomerangs and 10,858 new hires over an eight-year period in a large health care organization, we find that, upon being (re)hired into the organization, boomerangs outperform new hires in their initial job spell and that this performance advantage is larger in jobs requiring greater internal coordination and in contexts characterized by greater internal resistance to external hires.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether former employees who return to an organization perform differently from external hires with no prior experience in the firm. It also asks when boomerangs' prior familiarity with the organization's social system creates a larger post-hire performance advantage.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Boomerang status, defined as being a former permanent employee rehired by the same organization, compared with new-hire status.
- DV(s): Standardized annual individual performance rating during the first job spell after entry or reentry.
- Mediators: Not directly tested; the paper theorizes familiarity with the organization's social system, improved coordination, and reduced internal resistance as mechanisms.
- Moderators: Job internal interdependence, relational demands, administrative coordination, percentage of recent new hires in the work unit, and manager firm tenure.

The argument extends the knowledge-based view from organization-level coordination to individual entry and reentry. Boomerangs are expected to retain tacit, firm-specific knowledge of norms, routines, communication expectations, and social-system boundaries, allowing them to coordinate work more effectively and frame their ideas in ways that incumbents accept. The results show an average boomerang performance advantage, with stronger support in jobs with high relational demands and administrative coordination and in units where internal resistance is likely higher. The predicted moderation by internal interdependence is not supported.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to strategic human capital research by explaining why boomerang employees differ from other external hires and by specifying the job and work-context conditions under which their prior organizational experience matters. It extends the knowledge-based view by showing how familiarity with an organization's social system can shape individual performance during hiring and socialization, not only collective organizational capability. It also advances source-of-hire research by arguing that the value of a hiring source depends on role demands and local social context.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that organizations should not evaluate boomerang hiring only as a general alumni-recruitment practice; its value is greater in roles requiring relational subtlety, administrative coordination, and navigation of resistant internal contexts. Employers may benefit from using prior employment information more carefully, because supplementary analyses show that prior performance and voluntary exit predict performance after rehire even though the focal organization did not strongly exploit that information. The paper also suggests that firms with strong social systems may need more intensive integration support for external hires who lack prior organizational familiarity.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that the eight-year observation window prevents them from assessing longer-term employment outcomes after later lateral or upward moves. They also lacked full external work-history and individual-difference data for all workers, so boomerangs and new hires may differ in ways not captured by the personnel records. Because the study uses archival observational data from one organization, the findings are associational and cannot conclusively prove that social-system knowledge causes the observed performance differences.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine boomerang and new-hire outcomes over longer periods and after subsequent job changes. Additional studies could test how rehiring boomerangs affects colleagues, work groups, and perceptions of loyalty or resistance. The authors also call for work on whether the findings generalize across organizations that vary in social-system strength and openness to returning employees.
