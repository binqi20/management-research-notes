# The Catch-22 of Countering a Moral Occupational Stigma in Employee-Customer Interactions

**Abstract**
Past research has suggested that individuals in dirty work occupations can manage their self-views so as to derive positive self-definitions that allow them to perform their tasks with less of the burden of stigma. Results from our three studies show that this may not necessarily be the case when they try to manage how occupational outsiders view them. Findings from our studies show that in terms of this external perspective, rather than thwarting the occupational stigma, active stigma management by the stigma bearer can unintentionally reinforce the stigma. Drawing on the expectancy-confirmation framework from the stereotyping literature, we explain the cognitive mechanism that underlies this inadvertent stigma reinforcement in the stigma perceiver, and investigate the job performance implications for the stigma bearer. We test our hypotheses across two field studies and one experimental study, involving cross-industry and cross-sectional data, as well as one longitudinal data set including 128,549 employee–customer transactions. Our results contribute to research on dirty work by revealing that stigma management can be a double-edged sword with unintended negative consequences for the stigmatized worker.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether moral occupational stigma can be effectively countered when frontline employees try to manage how occupational outsiders view them. It specifically examines whether social comparisons and social proofing change customers' stereotypes of immorality and thereby affect frontline employees' job performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Frontline employees' use of stigma-countering tactics, specifically social comparisons and social proofing.
- DV(s): Frontline employee job performance, measured as transaction value, actual customer purchase, and customer purchase intention across the three studies.
- Mediators: Customers' occupational stereotypes of immorality.
- Moderators: Moral stigma intensity of the frontline job in Study 3.

The paper argues that moral occupational stigma activates customers' stereotypes of immorality, shaping how they interpret a frontline employee's attempts to counter the stigma. Social comparisons can backfire because customers interpret downward "us versus them" comparisons through the lens of the initial stigma, confirming rather than disconfirming stereotypes of immorality. Social proofing, by contrast, points to value created for other customers and can weaken the applicability of the immoral-salesperson stereotype. The experimental evidence further suggests that these indirect effects operate mainly when the frontline job carries high moral stigma intensity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends dirty work and stigma management research by shifting attention from inward-facing self-affirmation to outward-facing interactions with occupational outsiders. It shows that tactics previously treated as helpful for stigmatized workers' self-views can have different consequences when used to gain customer approval. The paper also applies the expectancy-confirmation framework to explain how deliberate stigma-countering can unintentionally reinforce the very stereotypes it seeks to reduce.

**Practical Implication**
For managers of customer-facing employees in morally tainted roles, the findings imply that not all stigma-countering tactics should be encouraged. Social proofing appears more useful because managers can equip employees with customer testimonials, satisfaction evidence, reviews, or other credible third-party signals. Supervisors should be cautious about employees' use of downward social comparisons, because these may harm customer perceptions and job performance in strongly stigmatized occupations.

**Limitations**
The paper notes that it focuses on interpersonal implications of stigma-countering in business exchanges, while intrapersonal consequences for the stigma bearer may also be positive or negative. It studies moral occupational stigma and is optimistic about broader relevance to occupational stigmas generally, but additional research is needed to test whether the same mechanism applies beyond moral stigma and beyond sales-like settings. The field studies measure inclinations to engage in stigma-countering rather than specific dyadic instances of each tactic, limiting claims about reciprocal employee-customer dynamics.

**Future Research**
Future research could integrate intrapersonal and interpersonal outcomes of stigma-countering, including emotional exhaustion, self-image preservation, and customer approval. Additional studies could test whether the mechanism generalizes to other stigmatized occupations such as investment banking, bill collection, telemarketing, real estate, insurance, or sex work. The paper also calls for dyadic designs that capture specific episodes of social proofing and social comparisons, research on triggers such as false consensus, and examination of other occupational contexts, status conditions, task complexity, and nonexhaustive stigma-countering tactics.
