# Struggling to Meet the Bar: Occupational Progress Failure and Informal Leadership Behavior

**Abstract**
We integrate theory and research about individuals’ responses to failures and develop a model in which occupational progress failures precipitate ruminative processes that limit the extent to which individuals subsequently act as informal leaders. Our first study, an experiment with a sample of advanced accounting students, found that manipulating poor performance on a simulated certification test promoted ruminative thoughts about the test, which were negatively related to peer ratings of informal leadership behavior during a subsequent task. A separate field study using a regression discontinuity design in a 14-week military training program found that failure to pass the required physical fitness examination early in group formation influenced psychosomatic symptoms—an indirect measure of sustained rumination—and consequently hindered enactment of informal leadership behavior. We also theorized and found that neuroticism enhanced the positive effect of failure on rumination in Study 1 and psychosomatic symptoms in Study 2. We discuss the implications for developing theories concerning how disruptive personal events may interfere with employees’ engaging in informal leadership behavior.

**Research Question**
How do occupational progress failures affect individuals' subsequent informal leadership behavior among peers? The paper also asks whether rumination or psychosomatic symptoms explain this relationship and whether neuroticism strengthens the effect of failure on those intrapsychic responses.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Occupational progress failure, operationalized as manipulated poor performance on a simulated CPA diagnostic test in Study 1 and failure to pass a diagnostic Army Physical Fitness Test in Study 2.
- DV(s): Peer-rated informal leadership behavior during a leaderless accounting case discussion in Study 1 and peer-rated informal leadership behavior at the end of military training in Study 2.
- Mediators: Ruminative thinking in Study 1 and psychosomatic symptoms as an indirect measure of sustained rumination in Study 2.
- Moderators: Neuroticism, measured before the failure event in both studies.

The paper argues that an unresolved occupational benchmark failure keeps the failed goal cognitively active, producing self-focused rumination about the failure and its implications. Drawing on the resource allocation model, the authors argue that this off-task cognitive demand leaves fewer surplus attentional resources for complex, discretionary behavior such as informal leadership. The two studies support a negative indirect relationship from failure to informal leadership through rumination-related states, with stronger effects among individuals higher in neuroticism.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to informal leadership research by theorizing occupational progress failure as an event that can inhibit would-be leaders' attempts to claim leadership, beyond stable individual differences and interpersonal factors. It links goal progress theory of rumination with resource allocation theory to explain why a personally important, reversible failure can reduce discretionary leadership behavior without necessarily reducing core task performance. It also shows that neuroticism conditions this process by intensifying rumination-related responses to failure.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that organizations should recognize that temporary failure on important occupational benchmarks can suppress informal leadership behavior even when the individual remains capable of performing core tasks. Managers may reduce these costs by helping employees prepare for benchmark requirements, providing growth-oriented support after failure, and offering renewed leadership opportunities after the failure has been reversed. The paper also cautions that low informal leadership after a failure may not indicate low leadership potential or motivation.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the studies did not directly test the full serial mediation process from occupational progress failure to rumination, psychosomatic symptoms, and informal leadership over time. Study 2 uses psychosomatic symptoms as a proxy for persistent rumination, so the inferred psychological process rests on theory, prior research, and convergence with Study 1 rather than a direct repeated rumination measure. The samples were U.S.-based, limiting conclusions about whether the same failure-leadership process operates similarly in cultures where responses to failure may differ.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for studies that randomly administer goal progress failure and track daily rumination, psychosomatic symptoms, and informal leadership behaviors over time. Future work could examine whether occupational progress failure harms core task performance in settings that require careful monitoring, detail work, or avoidance of errors, and whether ethical or safety behavior is similarly affected. The authors also encourage research on how failure base rates and cultural context shape ruminative responses and informal leadership outcomes.
