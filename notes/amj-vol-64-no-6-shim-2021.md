# The Impact of Leader Eye Gaze on Disparity in Member Influence: Implications for Process and Performance in Diverse Groups

**Abstract**
One important benefit of teamwork is the exchange and integration of diverse knowledge, experience, and opinions group members bring to the table. However, demographic attributes—such as race, gender, and functional background—may create asymmetric influence patterns between group members in diverse groups because these demographic characteristics are often associated with status differences. In the current research, we examine how to attenuate this disparity in member influence in diverse groups by focusing on the role of a leader’s gazing behavior. Across two studies, we found that asymmetric influence patterns in which high-status members tend to wield greater influence in group decision-making processes were attenuated when a leader increased their visual attention toward low-status members in the group. This reduced disparity in member influence which in turn improved group information elaboration and group performance in a collective decision-making task. Theoretical and practical implications for leaders’ visual attention, diversity, group decision-making processes, and group performance are discussed.

**Research Question**
How can a leader's visual attention attenuate disparity in member influence between high-status and low-status members in diverse groups? The paper also asks whether reduced disparity improves group information elaboration and, in turn, group performance in collective decision-making tasks.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader visual attention condition: greater attention to the low-status member, greater attention to the high-status member, or equal attention to both members.
- DV(s): Group performance in a collective decision-making task, measured through desert survival ranking accuracy in Study 1 and correct candidate choice in a hidden-profile task in Study 2.
- Mediators: Disparity in member influence and group information elaboration, modeled sequentially.
- Moderators: Not directly tested; the paper discusses diversity characteristic type across studies by comparing race and educational background.

The paper argues that demographic characteristics can operate as status cues, producing asymmetric influence patterns in which high-status members exert more influence in group decisions. A leader's greater positive visual attention toward a low-status member signals recognition and support, encouraging the low-status member to speak up while making the high-status member more likely to attend to that member's input. Reduced disparity in member influence then increases the group's motivation and opportunity to process and integrate diverse information, improving performance on tasks that require collective decision-making.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to diversity research by directly examining disparity in member influence as a process through which demographic diversity can undermine group information elaboration and performance. It identifies leader visual attention as a subtle nonverbal intervention that can reduce asymmetric influence patterns without relying on more burdensome procedural systems. It also extends work on gazing behavior into organizational research and develops an observer-rated measure of disparity in member influence.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that leaders in diverse groups should attend to how they distribute visual attention during initial group interactions, especially when some members are likely to carry lower status. Providing more positive visual attention to low-status members can create a more equalized communication atmosphere and improve information elaboration in collective decision-making tasks. The authors present gaze regulation as a trainable behavior that can be improved through interventions such as video feedback or sensitivity training.

**Limitations**
The studies used newly formed three-person groups consisting of two members and one leader, so the findings may not generalize directly to larger, ongoing, or more complex teams. The studies assume shared perceptions of status-relevant diversity characteristics, but leaders and members may not always agree about which characteristics create lower or higher status. The tasks were short-duration collective decision-making tasks, leaving open whether the effects persist, diminish, or strengthen over time and whether they apply to more structured, repetitive, or coordination-oriented work.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine group size as a moderator and test whether leader visual attention produces different dynamics in larger groups or multicultural and geographically distributed teams. Additional studies could investigate how a leader's own demographic characteristics shape the effect of visual attention on group dynamics. The authors also call for within-team designs and individual-level outcomes to understand how leader visual attention changes individual perceptions and influence over time.
