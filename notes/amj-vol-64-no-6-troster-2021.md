# When Victims Help Their Abusive Supervisors: The Role of LMX, Self-Blame, and Guilt

**Abstract**
Studies on abusive supervision typically posit that targets of abuse will either directly blame the perpetrating supervisor or indirectly blame the organization for allowing the abuse, and as a result reduce their cooperativeness at work. We pivot from this predominant logic and argue that, under certain circumstances, targets of abusive supervision may blame themselves, feel guilty, and then try to make it up to their abusive supervisors by helping them more. Drawing on the emotional process theory of abusive supervision and the more general sociofunctional perspective of emotions, we specify that such a dynamic is more likely to ensue when subordinates otherwise experience the relationship with their supervisors as good (high LMX). Two studies—an experiment and a twoweek bi-daily experience sampling study—provide support for our reasoning. As such, our study extends theorizing on the consequences of abusive supervision, which has typically found that it reduces cooperative behaviors. Moreover, it contributes to previous speculations that leaders may engage in abusive supervision because it has beneficial consequences for them.

**Research Question**
When and why might targets of abusive supervision blame themselves, feel guilty, and respond by helping the abusive supervisor rather than reducing cooperation? The paper asks whether high leader-member exchange (LMX) makes this self-blame and guilt pathway more likely.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Abusive supervision, manipulated as low versus high in Study 1 and measured as daily abusive supervision in Study 2.
- DV(s): Supervisor-directed helping, assessed as intended helping toward the supervisor in Study 1 and within-day supervisor-directed helping in Study 2.
- Mediators: Self-blame as the first mediator and guilt as the second mediator.
- Moderators: LMX as the boundary condition strengthening the abusive-supervision-to-self-blame path.

The paper argues that abusive supervision can be interpreted differently when it occurs inside an otherwise high-quality supervisor-subordinate relationship. In high-LMX dyads, subordinates may treat abusive episodes as inconsistent with the broader relationship, look inward for the cause, and blame themselves for possibly threatening the relationship. That self-blame produces guilt, and guilt motivates relationship-repair behavior, expressed here as increased supervisor-directed helping. Study 1 supports the model in a vignette experiment, and Study 2 finds the same moderated serial mediation pattern in daily work data while controlling for self-perceived performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the emotional process theory of abusive supervision by adding a self-blame and guilt pathway that can produce relationship-promotive behavior, not only retaliation, withdrawal, fear, anger, or sadness. It also qualifies the dominant victim-blame-supervisor narrative in abusive supervision research by showing that self-blame and supervisor-directed blame can coexist. By identifying supervisor-directed helping as a possible outcome, the paper contributes to debates about why abusive supervision may persist despite its harmful and morally unjustified character.

**Practical Implication**
The findings imply that increased helping after abuse should not be read as evidence that abusive supervision is effective or harmless. In high-LMX relationships, a subordinate's extra helping may reflect guilt-driven relationship repair rather than healthy cooperation, and it may hide a potentially dysfunctional pattern in which the target blames themselves for mistreatment. The paper explicitly states that it does not endorse abusive supervision even if some short-term supervisor-benefiting outcomes appear.

**Limitations**
Study 1 used a hypothetical scenario, so it could establish a possible causal process with internal validity but could not show that employees would actually respond this way in their real jobs. Study 2 covered only two weeks, which the authors state is too short for testing long-term consequences of helping an abusive supervisor. The authors also note that they do not know how supervisors perceive subordinates' helping efforts, because supervisors and employees may disagree about whether the behavior is extra-role helping or part of formal job requirements.

**Future Research**
The authors call for work on when self-blame versus supervisor-directed blame dominates over time, including dispositional factors such as locus of control and attribution styles and situational factors beyond LMX. They also suggest research on social comparisons in abusive supervision, shame as another possible route to helping, and the long-term consequences of helping an abusive supervisor. Future studies should examine whether supervisors intentionally use abusive behavior because they expect it to elicit help or other productive responses from subordinates.
