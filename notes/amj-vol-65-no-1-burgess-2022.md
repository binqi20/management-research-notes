# Longing for the Road Not Taken: The Affective and Behavioral Consequences of Forgone Identity Dwelling

**Abstract**
The literature on identity construction has argued that employees work to create coherent and distinctive identities that encapsulate individual characteristics and professional and organizational memberships. Models of identity construction have suggested that a past career decision—choosing one professional “road” while leaving behind a “road not taken”—should lose its salience over time. We challenged that consensus by introducing the construct of forgone identity dwelling—thinking about and reflecting on a professional path that could have been. Our field and experimental studies showed that employees did dwell on forgone professional identities, even when the decision events that resulted in them were years in the past. Drawing on cognitive-motivational-relational theory, we showed that forgone identity dwelling resulted in longing—a yearning for something missing. Such longing created a “double-edged sword” for the behavioral consequences of forgone identity dwelling. On the one hand, longing was associated with increased withdrawal behavior and decreased helping behavior because employees were distracted from the here and now. On the other hand, longing was associated with job crafting that in turn reduced those reactions—particularly for employees with an internal locus of control. We discuss the implications of our work for models of identity construction.

**Research Question**
Do employees continue to dwell on forgone professional identities—the occupational "roads not taken"—long after the identity construction process should have rendered them moot, and if so, what are the affective (longing) and behavioral (withdrawal, helping, job crafting) consequences of such dwelling?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Forgone identity dwelling (Study 1 measured; Study 2a manipulated)
- DV(s): Withdrawal behavior, helping behavior, and job crafting / intentions to job craft (Study 2b manipulated longing → job crafting)
- Mediators: Longing (proximal); job crafting (serial mediator linking longing to reduced withdrawal and increased helping)
- Moderators: Internal locus of control (moderates the longing → job crafting relationship)

Drawing on Lazarus's (1991) cognitive-motivational-relational theory, the authors argue that dwelling on a forgone professional identity triggers primary and secondary appraisals that produce longing—a secondary emotion blending sadness and happiness. The sadness component creates an avoidance action tendency (more withdrawal, less helping because the employee is distracted from the here and now), while the happiness/approach component motivates job crafting to recover unfulfilled values from the forgone path. Because internal-locus employees believe they can shape their own work lives, longing more strongly translates into job crafting for them, which in turn circulates back to reduce withdrawal and increase helping (a serial indirect effect). The field study and two experimental-causal-chain studies converge on these linkages.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the "forward progress" assumption in identity construction theory by showing that "identity echoes" from forgone professional identities continue to shape employee reactions after a current work identity is established, representing a lingering incoherence such models assume is resolved. It extends Obodaru's (2012, 2017) work on alternative selves by introducing identity dwelling as the mechanism through which forgone identities exert influence, and it foregrounds an affective construct—longing—to complement the predominantly cognitive and qualitative identity construction literature, addressing its noted "methodological myopia."

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that employees increasingly carry forgone professional identities and dwell on "what could have been," and should surface these through mentoring, coaching, or developmental conversations. They can then craft work contexts—providing flexibility, autonomy, and delegating tasks aligned with employees' forgone identities—to channel longing toward beneficial job crafting. Locus-of-control training that cultivates a more internal mindset may further encourage positive behavioral reactions to forgone identities.

**Limitations**
Participants may have faced multiple forks in the road and thus multiple forgone identities, but only the most salient was assessed, so findings may miss nuance. The forgone identity dwelling and longing measures were created for this study and need continued use to establish broader construct validity. There may also be reciprocal causation in the dwelling–longing relationship, since cognition and emotion are highly integrated.

**Future Research**
Researchers could examine dwelling on other selves (past, ideal, ought selves) and the cognitive—not just affective—consequences of identity dwelling, drawing on work on repetitive thought. They should probe reciprocal causality between longing and job crafting (e.g., virtuous cycles versus disenchantment) using cross-lagged panel data, and consider how identification strength, occupational prestige, and domain-specific locus of control shape who dwells on forgone identities.
