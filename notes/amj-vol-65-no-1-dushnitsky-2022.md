# Here Comes the Sun: The Impact of Incidental Contextual Factors on Entrepreneurial Resource Acquisition

**Abstract**
This paper focuses on investment decisions in nascent, seed-stage startups, where information is scarce and uncertainty is intense. Entrepreneurship literature has shown that investment is a function of the investor–startup pair; some studies have investigated startup characteristics, while others have focused on investor traits. We complement extant work by studying factors beyond the investor–startup pair; namely, incidental contextual factors such as changes in the physical environment. Drawing on affect-as-information theory, we conjecture that, in the face of intense uncertainty, sunnier days may affect investors' mood and result in a greater likelihood of investment. Archival and experimental analyses inform our predictions. The former utilizes proprietary data on 1,335 startups graduating from European accelerators. Every accelerator holds a "Demo Day," where startups pitch to investors. We find that graduating on a sunnier day increases the likelihood of investment. This effect is stronger under intense uncertainty, where (a) startups are nascent, or (b) founders have limited human capital. An experimental study where prospective investors evaluate a Demo Day pitch yields further insights. We find a positive sunshine–investment association, and show that the association is mediated by investors' mood. Our findings complement extant work and contribute insights regarding seed-stage investment decisions.

**Research Question**
What explains seed-stage investment patterns, and what factors affect investors' decisions to fund highly uncertain startups? The paper asks specifically whether incidental contextual factors outside the investor–startup pair — namely a daily change in sunshine on Demo Day — systematically shape seed-stage resource acquisition.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Sunnier — the interday change in realized sunshine hours between Demo Day and the preceding day (archival); a sunny-vs-cloudy weather mood-induction manipulation (experiment).
- DV(s): Demo Day funding (binary, whether a startup secures a funding round within 90 days); investment attractiveness and intended investment amount (experiment).
- Mediators: Investors' mood (PANAS-SF mood valence) mediates the sunshine–investment association in the experiment.
- Moderators: Startup maturity (younger startups → stronger effect) and founders' human capital (limited human capital → stronger effect), both capturing the level of uncertainty investors face.

Affect-as-information theory holds that people confuse incidental feelings (e.g., a sunshine-induced good mood) with integral feelings about the focal task, so that under intense uncertainty an investor's mood "tips the balance" toward more favorable subjective probability assessments of a startup. A sunnier Demo Day lifts investors' mood, which raises their evaluation of an otherwise opaque seed-stage venture and increases the likelihood of funding; the effect attenuates as uncertainty falls (mature startups, high-human-capital founders) because more objective information then displaces mood as the input to judgment.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces a third class of explanation for seed-stage investment patterns — incidental contextual factors situated outside the investor–startup pair — to complement the two dominant streams that locate variation in startup characteristics or investor traits. It demonstrates that funding-outcome variation can emerge even across a homogeneous pool of investors and startups, simply because some pairs are randomly exposed to contextual shocks, and it imports affect-as-information theory into entrepreneurship to specify mood as the mechanism, bounded by the level of uncertainty.

**Practical Implication**
Rather than ignoring incidental contextual factors, investors and accelerators should heed their effect, since transient conditions such as weather accrue in a foreseeable way on funding decisions. While one cannot control the sun, accelerators could design Demo Days (e.g., refreshment choices or "walking" Demo Days) to manage investors' mood, and scholars/practitioners should collect data on the context in which pairs are situated, not only on startups and investors.

**Limitations**
The arguments are calibrated to seed-stage investment and its intense uncertainty, so the affect-as-information logic need not extend to later-stage deals where a VC committee already knows the startup. The archival analysis captures funding outcomes for hundreds of accelerator graduates but lacks detailed data on the individual investors who attended, and the outcome is measured only as a binary funding variable.

**Future Research**
Future work could trace individual investors' movements to directly map how incidental contextual factors shape their decisions, and examine whether daily sunshine operates through entrepreneurs as well as investors. It could also test generalizability to other geographies and other contextual factors (e.g., choice of refreshment), and explore richer outcome measures such as premoney valuation.
