# “I Go Here…But I Don’t Necessarily Belong”: The Process of Transgressor Reintegration in Organizations

**Abstract**
When organizational members violate important organizational standards, they may face termination, or they may instead be retained by the organization and given a second chance. Retained transgressors experience the tension of liminality: they maintain their affiliation to the organization, making them structural insiders, but they have committed a transgression, making them moral outsiders. How might transgressors attempt to reintegrate and feel like full organizational insiders once again? And what makes transgressors feel more, or less, reintegrated? Previous work has studied reintegration from victims’ or third-parties’ perspectives, but little is known about transgressor reintegration. To build theory on transgressor reintegration, I studied transgressors at a military service academy. Through waves of qualitative data collection and inductive analyses, I find that transgressions threaten transgressors’ integration, leading transgressors to feel precarious in their perceptions of membership and their feelings of belonging. Transgressors attempt to restore both elements, but use distinct approaches for each. Because belonging restoration requires positive interactions with many organizational actors, transgressors can—and, in my data, frequently do—experience restoration of membership but not belonging. Therefore, it may be relatively rare for transgressors to feel highly reintegrated following transgressions, even in organizations that devote considerable resources to reintegration.

**Research Question**
How do retained transgressors—organizational members who violated core values and so became "structural insiders but moral outsiders"—attempt to reintegrate and come to feel like full insiders again, and what makes them feel more, or less, reintegrated?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: A transgression decouples membership and belonging, throwing the retained transgressor into liminality (structural insider, moral outsider) and producing precarious membership and precarious belonging.
- Restoration attempts (membership): Compliance behaviors aimed at obtaining institutional endorsements to feel secure in perceptions of membership.
- Restoration attempts (belonging): Investment in relationships aimed at strengthening relational belonging, while institutional belonging depends on endorsements from institutional actors.
- Emergent outcome: Belonging splits into relational belonging and institutional belonging; both, plus restored membership, are necessary for high reintegration.
- Boundary condition: Because belonging restoration depends on positive perceived interactions with many actors, transgressors often restore membership but not belonging, so high reintegration is rare (only 3 of 23 cadets).

Frey advances a process-with-variance model in which reintegration is an intrapsychic, perceptual process driven by transgressors’ own interpretations of interactions rather than by objective, triangulated behaviors. Transgressors use distinct logics—compliance to restore membership versus relational investment to restore belonging—and because lack of restoration in even one element (membership, relational belonging, or institutional belonging) blocks full reintegration, feeling highly reintegrated is difficult to achieve even in resource-intensive programs.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study conceptualizes transgressor reintegration from the transgressor’s own perspective, foregrounding its liminal starting point and the driving forces (restoring membership and belonging) rather than merely listing features of reintegration. It recasts integration from a static construct into a dynamic, perceptual, and negotiated process that can be formed, threatened, modified, and regained, and it differentiates belonging into relational and institutional types that are complements, not substitutes—offering new leverage for research on socialization, undersocialization, belonging, and nested relational phenomena.

**Practical Implication**
To give employees a genuine second chance, managers must support both restoring perceptions of membership (e.g., bestowing institutional endorsements such as desirable assignments, promotions, and access to perks) and fostering belonging (e.g., coaching peers and supervisors on how to interact with and affirm the transgressor). Managers should also balance transgressor affirmation against good-standing members’ justice perceptions to avoid backlash.

**Limitations**
Pre-violation integration data are retrospective because first interviews occurred after violations, so cadets’ recollections of initial integration may be inaccurate or colored by present feelings. The author could not collect data from transgressors’ interaction partners (personal acquaintances or institutional actors), leaving open whether transgressors’ perceptions are accurate, and the data are not fine-grained enough to definitively map specific actors to specific types of belonging.

**Future Research**
Survey-based quantitative work could test whether transgressors’ recollections of pre-transgression integration are accurate and whether their perceptions of others’ treatment generally align with reality. Researchers could more systematically identify which types or features of organizational actors foster relational versus institutional belonging, and examine how the observed dynamics differ in organizations with little or no formal structure around transgressor reintegration.
