# Joining Forces: How Can Founding Members’ Prior Experience Variety and Shared Experience Increase Startup Survival?

**Abstract**
In extant work, two arguments have emerged on the relationship between founders’ experience and startup survival. First, founders’ diverse experiences should foster startup survival because the combination of various experience-based knowledge components drives the needed innovation. Second, founders’ shared experience in an incumbent firm in the industry of the startup should positively relate to startup survival because of the transfer of shared knowledge and routines. This paper reconciles both streams of research by proposing that startups can combine both types of experience-based advantages when founding teams are composed of founders who individually possess a variety of firm experiences and other founders who jointly transition from the same industry incumbent. Using a sample of startups in technological manufacturing industries, I test the direct effect of the two types of experiences and their complementarity in decreasing the likelihood of startup failure. The results show that such complementarities exist so that startups are less likely to fail when their founding teams combine within-founder experience variety and across-team shared experience. I discuss the implications of these findings for startup survival and founding team literature streams.

**Research Question**
How can founding teams combine two distinct experience-based advantages—within-founder experience variety and founders’ shared experience from the same industry incumbent—to reduce the likelihood of startup failure, thereby reconciling the experience-variety and spinout literatures?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Number of founders with a variety of firm experiences (within-founder experience variety aggregated to the team level); length (years) of shared experience of founders transitioning from the same industry incumbent.
- DV(s): Startup failure (coded 1 in the year the startup fails, 0 in years it is alive).
- Mediators: None tested; the knowledge-integration and routine-transfer logic is theorized but not measured as a mediator.
- Moderators: Shared industry-incumbent experience moderates the relationship between the number of variety founders and failure (the focal interaction, Hypothesis 3).

Drawing on social cognition theory and organizational knowledge theory, the paper argues that founders who frequently changed jobs accumulate diverse, loosely connected knowledge components that fuel innovation but are costly to integrate, so adding such founders alone raises failure risk (H1b supported). Founders who jointly leave the same incumbent transfer a shared knowledge base and well-oiled routines that lower failure risk (H2 supported). When the two combine, shared routines supply common ground that lets the team integrate the variety founders’ components, so the interaction is negative—startups are least likely to fail when within-founder variety is paired with across-team shared experience (H3 supported).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study bridges the experience-variety and spinout literatures by integrating knowledge-combination and knowledge-transfer mechanisms under a single organizational-knowledge-and-routines framework, introducing the construct of within-founder experience variety (variety encapsulated in a single founder’s career rather than spread across the team). It shows that the level at which human-capital resources are measured matters: complementarities emerge only when variety is concentrated within individual founders and the shared experience is specifically tied to a common industry incumbent, refining prior work that relied on teams’ total number of prior experiences or any industry experience.

**Practical Implication**
Founders who frequently changed jobs before founding should team up with cofounders who jointly transition from the same industry incumbent; variety founders lower failure risk only when at least part of the team supplies shared knowledge and routines. Practically, founders with a variety of experiences are associated with lower startup failure when other founders share at least two years of prior work in the same industry incumbent, helping resource-constrained teams assemble complementary human capital.

**Limitations**
The findings derive from U.S. employer–employee LEHD data covering technological manufacturing startup survival from 2000 to 2008, so implications should be treated with caution in other institutional and economic contexts. The LEHD excludes pre-incorporation, pre-hiring, and self-employment stages, so average survival is likely overstated and implications apply only to "advanced" startups. Mutual selection among team members cannot be fully ruled out, and the data do not capture each member’s functional role or non-employment shared work relationships.

**Future Research**
Future work could model the mutual selection among all team members using newer techniques such as many-sided matching models to better isolate selection effects on startup performance. Building new surveys (e.g., with the U.S. Census Bureau) that span all entrepreneurial stages from idea inception to first hires would broaden generalizability, and linking team members’ occupations and broader forms of shared work relationships could reveal additional human-capital complementarities within founding teams.
