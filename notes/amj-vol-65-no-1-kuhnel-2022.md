# There Is a Time to Be Creative: The Alignment between Chronotype and Time of Day

**Abstract**
We examine the influence of chronobiological processes on creativity, specifically the influence of a person’s chronotype. Chronotype refers to the setting of a person’s biological clock that gives rise to a distinctive pattern of sleep habits and preferred diurnal activity. We propose a synchrony effect and predict that people are creative when the external clock is aligned with their internal, biological clock. According to our model, positive mood and creative self-efficacy act as affective and cognitive mechanisms of this synchrony effect. We present three studies that test our theorizing: a quasi-experimental field study with 260 employees, a day-reconstruction study with 238 employees, and a one-day experience sampling study with 319 employees. Across the studies, we find that chronotype moderates the effect of time of day on creativity. Overall, late chronotypes were more creative in the late afternoon and early chronotypes tended to be more creative in the morning. The alignment between chronotype and time of day also gave rise to positive mood and creative self-efficacy; however, the studies provide only partial support for the hypothesis that positive mood acts as a mediating mechanism. We discuss the implications of these findings against the background of an embodied cognition perspective on creativity.

**Research Question**
Does within-day fluctuation in employees’ creativity depend on the alignment between their chronotype and the time of day (a "synchrony effect"), and do positive mood and creative self-efficacy operate as the affective and cognitive mechanisms transmitting this chronobiological influence on creativity?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Time of day (Level 1); chronotype (Level 2, continuous, measured via MCTQ and rMEQ).
- DV(s): Creativity (objective alternative uses test in Study 1; self-rated work-related creativity in Studies 2 and 3, capturing creative fluency, cognitive flexibility, originality).
- Mediators: Positive mood (PANAS items) and creative self-efficacy (Tierney & Farmer, 2002).
- Moderators: Chronotype moderates the within-person effect of time of day (the synchrony effect); chronotype is modeled as a cross-level moderator of the time-of-day slope.

Drawing on Borbély's two-process model of sleep regulation and an embodied cognition perspective, the paper argues that when the time of day matches a person's chronotype, the executive functions that enable creativity are most accessible, allowing the person to form remote associations and recombine elements in novel ways. This synchrony state is theorized to elevate both positive mood (an affective pathway, via the circadian reward-motivation system) and creative self-efficacy (a cognitive pathway, as awareness that one can access one's creative potential), which in turn promote creativity. Empirically, chronotype reliably moderated the time-of-day effect on creativity, but positive mood mediated only in Studies 2 and 3 and creative self-efficacy mediated for earlier (not later) chronotypes, indicating the mediating processes—especially for late chronotypes—are more complex than a simple parallel-mediation model implies.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends creativity research by showing that within-person variation in creativity and its cognitive and affective antecedents are grounded in the current state of the body, calling for theories of creativity to attend to the mind–body interface from an embodied cognition framework. It contributes to the two-process model of sleep regulation by expanding its scope to creativity in a high-stakes work context and specifying affective (positive mood) and cognitive (creative self-efficacy) mechanisms, and shows that chronobiological drivers explain within-day fluctuations in work performance over and above day-specific sleep quality and duration.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that employees' ability to be creative is not a time-invariant trait but varies over the day and differs across individuals' chronotypes. Organizations should give employees autonomy to schedule creativity-demanding tasks for their state of synchrony (shielding that time from low-value meetings) while assigning routine tasks to other times; employees also need awareness of their own chronobiological preferences to make "wise" scheduling decisions.

**Limitations**
Creativity was operationalized via an objective alternative uses test and self-rated creativity, and the self-report measures may be subject to social-desirability and recollection biases (Study 2 used day reconstruction). Extreme chronotypes were underrepresented because start- and end-of-work times had to be set as participation requirements, yielding a conservative test, and Studies 1 and 3 took only two daily "snapshots" of creativity rather than continuously monitoring its temporal dynamics. The studies also neglected the social environment in which employees are creative.

**Future Research**
Future work should disentangle and directly measure the executive functions assumed to underlie the synchrony effect, examine why late chronotypes' creativity depends less on positive mood (e.g., links to thinking/decision-making styles and right-hemisphere processing), and capture the temporal dynamics of creativity across the entire workday without relying on employee recollection. Researchers should also extend measurement to earlier and later times of day, integrate the joint impact of the synchrony effect and sleep, and study how embodied processes interact with the social context.
