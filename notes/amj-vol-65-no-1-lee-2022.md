# The Distinct Effects of Wealth- and CSR-Oriented Shareholder Unrest on CEO Career Outcomes: A New Lens on Settling Up and Executive Job Demands

**Abstract**
Conceptualizing shareholder unrest as the aggregate scale and gravity of shareholder dissatisfaction with company practices, we examine the effects of two distinct types of shareholder unrest—wealth-oriented and corporate social responsibility (CSR)-oriented unrest—on CEOs’ career outcomes. Drawing on the concept of settling up, we hypothesize that CEOs are negatively sanctioned for both types of unrest in terms of reduced pay and increased likelihood of dismissal. Drawing on executive job demands theory, we argue that incumbent CEOs feel burdened by both types of unrest, leading them to voluntarily depart, and that new CEOs of unrest-laden firms receive larger pay packages as compensation for dealing with these inherited job demands. Overarching all these expectations, we argue that the effects of CSR-oriented unrest will be greater than the effects of wealth-oriented unrest, as CSR-oriented unrest occurs in a domain where alternative performance indicators are few and noisy, causing CSR-oriented unrest to be seemingly more meaningful. Testing our ideas on a longitudinal sample of U.S. corporations, we find considerable but not total support for our hypotheses, which we then interpret and discuss.

**Research Question**
How do two distinct forms of shareholder unrest—wealth-oriented and CSR-oriented—differentially affect CEOs' career outcomes (incumbent pay adjustments, dismissal, voluntary departure, and new-CEO pay), and which form is more consequential for boards versus CEOs themselves?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CSR-oriented shareholder unrest; wealth-oriented shareholder unrest (each a standardized composite of four indicators—number of resolutions, aggregate voting support, distinct institutional-investor sponsors, and ISS endorsements—coded over the two prior years)
- DV(s): Incumbent CEO pay change; CEO dismissal; CEO voluntary departure; new CEO compensation (TDC1)
- Mediators: None formally tested (informativeness / job-demands logic is theorized, not modeled)
- Moderators: None; the central comparative claim is the difference between the two unrest coefficients (Wald tests)

The paper positions settling up and executive job demands as "two sides of the same coin." Under settling up, boards make post hoc pay and retention decisions after observing shareholder unrest, sanctioning CEOs blamed for unrest on their watch; because CSR-oriented unrest arises in a domain where alternative performance metrics are few and noisy, it conveys more distinct information and so should provoke stronger board sanctions. Under executive job demands, both forms of unrest add quantitative and qualitative load that pushes incumbents to voluntarily depart and forces firms to pay successors a premium. Empirically, boards sanctioned CSR-oriented unrest (pay cuts and dismissal) but not wealth-oriented unrest, whereas CEOs voluntarily departed in response to wealth-oriented unrest but not CSR-oriented unrest—revealing a divergence in the indicators boards and CEOs weight. New-CEO pay rose equally with both forms of pre-succession unrest.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study elevates "shareholder activism" (the binary filing of a resolution) to "shareholder unrest"—a continuous, multifaceted construct capturing the scale and gravity of dissatisfaction—and develops a novel index to measure it. It advances settling-up theory by showing boards weight non-financial, CSR-related information more heavily than wealth-related information when its incremental informativeness is high, and it extends executive job demands theory by surfacing a board–CEO divergence in attended indicators and a predecessor–successor linkage in which inherited unrest buoys new-CEO pay.

**Practical Implication**
Boards appear highly attentive to CSR-oriented shareholder dissatisfaction and willing to penalize CEOs for it through reduced pay and dismissal, so executives and directors should treat CSR-oriented unrest as strongly informative rather than background noise. Firms hiring into unrest-laden contexts should anticipate paying compensating premiums to attract qualified successors.

**Limitations**
The design is observational, so causal inference rests on instrumental-variable (2SLS) and impact-threshold (ITCV) robustness checks rather than experimental control. The theorized informativeness and job-demands mechanisms are inferred rather than directly measured, and the sample is limited to large U.S. public corporations (S&P 1500), bounding generalizability.

**Future Research**
Scholars could examine whether other publicly registered stakeholder assessments—media criticism, boycotts, Yelp/Glassdoor ratings—affect CEO labor-market outcomes, and whether CSR-oriented unrest becomes less informative as CSR metrics improve. Promising directions include longer-term career consequences of unrest, effects on directors themselves, and spillover effects on the CEOs of non-targeted neighboring firms.
