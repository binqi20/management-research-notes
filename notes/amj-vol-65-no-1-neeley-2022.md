# How Global Leaders Gain Power through Downward Deference and Reduction of Social Distance

**Abstract**
We theorize about how people with positional power enact “downward deference”—a practice of lowering oneself to be equal to lower-power workers—based on a study of 115 top global leaders at a large U.S. company. These leaders were charged with advancing organizational goals in foreign markets. We find that some leaders enacted downward deference when they recognized that they had less expertise, networks, and influence relative to their local subordinates. This manifested in two ways: (a) attempts to reduce social distance, which involved seeking connection, earning trust, and participating in adjacent collaboration with local subordinates, and (b) yielding to subordinates’ expertise by privileging their judgment, transferring influence to them, and conforming to their local hierarchical expectations. Our supplementary quantitative analyses showed that previous experiences in foreign cultures, both in terms of total time spent abroad and exposure to cultures that were distant from their own, correlated with adopting downward deference. Those leaders also had higher job performance ratings, and were promoted to higher executive levels over time, compared to their counterparts who did not practice downward deference. Our study expands our understanding of positional power, social distance, and the conditions under which actors practice downward deference.

**Research Question**
The paper examines three interrelated questions: (a) why, and under which conditions, do those with positional power defer to lower-ranking individuals; (b) what behaviors do they adopt to practice downward deference; and (c) what role does social distance play in enacting downward deference?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Leaders hold higher positional power but recognize they have limited expertise, networks, and influence relative to local subordinates in nonnative markets.
- Antecedent (correlate): Deep previous experience in foreign contexts — total time spent abroad and cultural distance/novelty of those experiences — prompts learning and adaptation.
- Behavioral dimension 1 — Reducing social distance: seeking connection, earning trust, adjacent collaboration with subordinates.
- Behavioral dimension 2 — Yielding to subordinates' expertise: privileging subordinates' judgment, transferring influence, conforming to local hierarchical expectations.
- Emergent outcomes: Higher job performance ratings and more progressions into executive ranks for leaders who enacted downward deference.

Drawing on the social distance theory of power and transformative learning theory, the authors inductively develop a framework in which leaders who confront a salient expertise/knowledge deficit in culturally distant settings learn to lower their own position to become equal with subordinates. By reducing perceived social distance and ceding influence to local experts, leaders compensate for their diminished empathic accuracy and reliance on subordinate knowledge, which in turn supports goal accomplishment and their own career advancement.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study breaks from traditional social-psychology theories that predict the powerful expect upward-flowing deference and resist deferring downward, specifying when and why high-power actors instead defer to lower-ranking subordinates. It shows that positional power varies in its propensity to create social distance, that deep nonnative experience is a necessary condition for downward deference, and that downward deference can be both instrumentally (power-maintaining) and communally motivated — extending theory on power, social distance, international experience, and leadership.

**Practical Implication**
Downward deference behaviors may be valuable to assess and promote in global organizations' talent selection, internal promotion, and development decisions. Offering developmental assignments in unfamiliar, nonnative settings can give future global leaders the deep experiences that enable them to learn and apply downward deference, and such deference may itself serve as a selection criterion for leadership roles.

**Limitations**
The data capture only the leaders' perspective and not subordinates', so it is unknown how recipients perceived and responded to downward deference. The leaders represent an extreme case (highly senior, ~22-year tenures, within four levels of the CEO), limiting generalizability, and the design cannot establish causality between downward deference and the observed performance and promotion outcomes.

**Future Research**
Future work should study subordinates' experience of downward deference, replicate the phenomenon with less senior leaders and at multiple hierarchical levels, and use experimental or systematically varied designs to establish causality and map the construct's nomological network. A further promising direction is the role of gender — whether women who practice downward deference face backlash — and how downward deference unfolds across more versus less masculine industries.
