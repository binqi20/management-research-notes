# What Changes after Women Enter Top Management Teams? A Gender-Based Model of Strategic Renewal

**Abstract**
The question of what changes when women enter upper-echelons teams has long frustrated upper echelons and gender researchers. We build on the dynamic strategic renewal literature, combine it with upper echelons theory insights, and integrate knowledge about female executives’ career strategies to theorize how and when female appointments into top management teams (TMTs) cause firms to change their approach to knowledge-related strategic renewal. In doing so, we reconcile the tension among extant mediating processes invoked to explain how female TMT representation might affect strategic decisions: change orientation and risk-taking propensity. Estimating a dynamic ordinary least squares model on panel data from 163 multinationals, we find that following female (but not male) TMT appointments, TMT cognitions shift, becoming more change oriented and less risk seeking. Subsequently, these TMT cognitive shifts cause a decrease in mergers and acquisitions and an increase in research and development. Our model of female TMT appointments as catalysts that cause shifts in TMT cognitions, which, in turn, redirect knowledge-related strategic renewal from a buying to a building approach, is a novel effort at advancing research on women at upper echelons to examine time-dependent, within-firm mechanisms linking women in upper echelons and firm outcomes.

**Research Question**
How and when do female (versus male) appointments into top management teams cause firms to change their approach to knowledge-related strategic renewal? The paper asks what changes after women enter TMTs, moving the female-TMT–firm-outcome literature from descriptive ("What is the link?") to explanatory ("How does the linkage occur?") by specifying time-dependent, within-firm mechanisms.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Female TMT appointments (count of newly appointed female executives in a given year); male TMT appointments as a comparison.
- DV(s): Within-firm change in renewal strategy — change in R&D (capability "building") and change in technology-based M&A activity (capability "buying").
- Mediators: Shifts in two TMT cognitions — increase in TMT change orientation and decrease in TMT risk-taking propensity (year-to-year, measured from letters to shareholders).
- Moderators: TMT female incumbency (number of incumbent women, with diminishing returns / threshold at four or more) and the relative size of the incoming cohort of new TMT appointees (smaller cohorts amplify the shifts).

Female appointments act as catalysts: because female executives' life experiences and accession paths to the C-suite favor transformative, change-oriented values while their heightened scrutiny and career-derailment risks favor caution, their entry (but not men's) raises TMT change orientation and lowers TMT risk-taking propensity. These cognitive shifts then redirect renewal from buying (M&A) toward building (R&D). Integration conditions (female incumbency, small cohorts) determine whether the unique values of female appointees are voiced, heard, and incorporated, drawing on social identity theory's logic that similar in-group members are more readily integrated.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study makes three contributions to research on women in upper echelons, upper echelons theory (UET), and strategic renewal. First, it establishes shifts in TMT cognitions as a dynamic, within-firm mechanism linking female TMT appointments to strategic change, replacing static demographic-proxy models with a temporal model of antecedents of cognition. Second, it reconciles the apparent contradiction between women's higher risk aversion and their greater propensity for change by showing the two operate on distinct cognitive channels (risk-taking propensity → M&A; change orientation → R&D). Third, it extends UET and strategic-renewal research by identifying TMT integration as a boundary condition and offering a new temporal understanding of critical mass (combining the flow of new female appointees with the stock of female incumbents).

**Practical Implication**
CEOs and boards should not treat appointing women as "pixie-dust" that automatically yields favorable outcomes; female appointees reshape strategy only when integrated into the TMT. Diversifying TMTs more substantially, attending to female incumbency, avoiding tokenism, and ensuring receptive team-leader gender beliefs make it more likely that female appointees' contributions are incorporated into real-time strategic decisions and that renewal shifts (more R&D, fewer M&A) materialize.

**Limitations**
The model offers two non-exclusive explanations for cognition shifts (direct value transmission versus diversity-driven team-process change) that the data cannot disentangle. Threshold (diminishing-returns) estimates rest on small samples because female appointments to TMTs with existing female incumbents are rare, so they must be interpreted with caution. The study isolates only one appointee characteristic (gender), and its use of letters to shareholders as a proxy for TMT cognitions cannot fully parcel out impression-management content.

**Future Research**
Future work could identify other dynamic models linking female entry into upper echelons to firm decisions via cognition shifts — for example, female appointments' effects on TMT moral sensitivities and corporate social responsibility, or on TMT affective tone. Researchers could probe the processes through which shifts occur (e.g., whether stereotypical risk ascriptions versus actual gender differences drive change), extend the reasoning to other appointee dimensions (race, age, national origin, social class), develop additional moderators (managerial discretion, sociocultural context, demographic fault lines), and disentangle cognition from impression management in shareholder letters.
