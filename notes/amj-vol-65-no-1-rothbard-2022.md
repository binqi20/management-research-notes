# OMG! My Boss Just Friended Me: How Evaluations of Colleagues’ Disclosure, Gender, and Rank Shape Personal/Professional Boundary Blurring Online

**Abstract**
We propose and test a relational boundary-blurring framework, examining how employees’ evaluations of colleagues’ characteristics drive their decisions to connect with colleagues as friends online. We use a multi-method approach across four studies to investigate how self-disclosure of personal information, gender, and rank shape warmth evaluations of colleagues and subsequent boundary blurring decisions on online social networks such as Facebook. Study 1, a large archival study using a nationally representative sample, finds that connecting as friends with colleagues online is prevalent. Study 2, examining employees across several industries, shows that people experience connecting as friends with colleagues online as boundary blurring. Two experimental studies (Studies 3 and 4) ascertain that employees are more likely to connect as friends online with colleagues who engage in more (vs. less) self-disclosure and are less likely to connect with bosses (vs. peers). Further, self-disclosure, gender, and rank interact, such that employees are more likely to connect with female bosses who disclose more, compared to those who disclose less, and compared to male bosses, regardless of self-disclosure. Our work contributes to boundary management research by demonstrating that employees’ decisions to blur the personal/professional boundary online crucially depends on whom they are blurring the boundary with.

**Research Question**
How do employees’ evaluations of a colleague’s self-disclosure, gender, and rank — characteristics that convey warmth — shape their decisions to blur the personal/professional boundary by connecting as friends with that colleague online? The paper shifts boundary management from the focal employee to the relational partner, asking with whom employees choose to blur the boundary.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Colleague's self-disclosure (more vs. less); colleague's gender (female vs. male); colleague's rank (symmetric/peer vs. asymmetric/boss or subordinate); their two- and three-way interactions.
- DV(s): Likelihood of connecting as a friend with the colleague online (Study 3: stated likelihood of accepting a request; Study 4: actual Facebook connection, dichotomous).
- Mediators: Perceived warmth of the colleague (Fiske et al., 2002 scale).
- Moderators: Self-disclosure moderates the gender x rank interaction; specifically, disclosure ameliorates the negative effect of asymmetric rank for female but not male bosses.

Drawing on social cognition and person perception research, the authors argue that people evaluate others first along warmth, and that more self-disclosure, female gender, and symmetric (peer) rank each signal warmth and benevolent intent, lowering the perceived relational risk of granting a colleague visible, permanent, less-tailored access to one's personal life online. Warmth thus mediates the disclosure effect consistently, while gender and rank operate more conditionally. The three-way interaction reflects that disclosure most strongly improves connection odds for female bosses, whose hierarchical status otherwise violates gender-role expectations, whereas male bosses of asymmetric rank remain least likely to be connected with regardless of disclosure.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces a relational boundary-blurring framework that reorients boundary management theory from the characteristics of the focal employee to evaluations of the relational partner, showing that boundary blurring is decided relationship by relationship rather than uniformly within a domain. It extends social cognition and status-characteristics research by demonstrating that warmth-conveying cues (disclosure, gender, rank) are not equivalent in their effects on boundary blurring, and that gender and rank are less consistently mediated by warmth than disclosure is. It also theorizes online social networks as an extreme case that amplifies the visibility, permanence, and relational risks of boundary blurring.

**Practical Implication**
Employees should recognize that online connection decisions may rest on stereotypes about colleagues' benevolence (especially regarding gender and rank) and deliberate more carefully about the risks and rewards of connecting; moderate, noncontroversial disclosure may suffice to signal warmth. Bosses should recognize that subordinates are often hesitant to connect and avoid initiating requests that create discomfort. Because reinforcing gender-role stereotypes can place disclosing female bosses at greater risk, organizations should avoid one-size-fits-all policies and pursue training and safeguards that protect privacy without perpetuating gender discrimination in workplace social connection.

**Limitations**
Studies 1 and 2 are correlational and not designed to test the hypotheses. Study 3 maximized control using fictitious profiles but lacked realism, while Study 4 used an adapted critical-incident recall design that gained external validity but introduced recall error and could not firmly establish that disclosure precedes the connection decision. Both experiments relied on single-item dependent measures and a shortened warmth scale in Study 3, and the main effect of gender varied across studies.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine the amount and type of online versus offline disclosure more extensively, including appropriate versus politicized or stigmatized content and the role of privacy settings, and further establish the temporal precedence of real-world disclosure. Scholars should investigate other status characteristics such as race and their interaction with gender and rank, the persistence of gender inequalities on OSNs (e.g., whether connections help or harm disclosing female bosses), downstream consequences of online boundary blurring for performance and offline relationships, and extensions to other platforms such as Twitter, Instagram, and WeChat.
