# Regulating Top Managers’ Emotions during Strategy Making: Nokia’s Socially Distributed Approach Enabling Radical Change from Mobile Phones to Networks in 2007–2013

**Abstract**
Strategy making can be emotional for decision makers, especially when facing a major threat or a disruptive change. Yet, we know little about whether and how strategic decision makers’ emotions are regulated and how such regulation influences strategy making. Based on a longitudinal study of Nokia from 2007 to 2013, we develop a process model of socially distributed emotion regulation. This model shows how various organizational groups help regulate top managers’ emotions. Top managers contain their initial emotional reactions to strategic options thanks to activities performed by groups with power over top managers. This enables top managers to form data-informed reappraisals of strategic options, contributing to gradual changes in their emotions. The reappraisal process is aided by diverse groups performing distinct roles. Top managers’ revised emotions, in turn, enable them to form new, iterative data-informed reappraisals and ultimately enable radical strategic change. Our study contributes to research on emotions and strategy making by showing how socially distributed emotion regulation operates during strategy making and influences its outcomes. We contribute to the cognitive perspective on strategy by showing how cognition and emotion interact over time during strategy making.

**Research Question**
(a) Whether and how are strategic decision makers’ emotions regulated during strategy making in large organizations? (b) How does this regulation influence the strategy-making process?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: A strategic situation (a major threat or disruptive change) triggers intense emotional reactions among top managers, activating action tendencies that can produce myopic, rigid, and biased behavior and strategic inertia.
- Structural affordances (regulating groups): Governance structures (board of directors → power to dictate norms/behaviors), advisory structures (analytical teams/consultants → impartial, specialized cognitive capacity), and boundary-spanning structures (potential partner firms → capacity to increase the salience/vividness of options).
- Three regulation mechanisms: (1) increasing top managers’ impulse control (containing initial emotions via formal practices and psychological safety), (2) adding emotionally neutral cognitive capacity (enabling data-informed reappraisals), and (3) increasing the mental vividness of options (amplifying emotional reactions toward specific options).
- Enabling condition: A shared appreciation of emotion regulation (born of the negative experience of emotional inertia in Period 1) makes top managers react favorably to others’ regulation efforts.
- Emergent outcome: A recursive loop in which contained initial emotions → wider/deeper search → data-informed reappraisals → gradually changing emotions → still deeper search, ultimately enabling a radical strategic choice the managers initially disdained.

Across two periods, unregulated emotional reactions (denial, avoidance of the iPhone/Android threat) drove strategic inertia in Period 1, whereas in Period 2 various organizational groups, coordinated through formal structures, distributed the work of regulating top managers’ emotions so that those emotions changed qualitatively over weeks and months. Because the process is socially distributed, each group performs a sub-task with compartmentalized information, and the overall emotion-regulating effect emerges from their joint, complementary efforts rather than from any single leader’s individual emotional intelligence.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper enriches work on emotions and strategy making by theorizing emotion regulation as socially distributed across organizational groups and enabled by formal organizational structures, transcending the individual-level limitations that dominate prior emotion-regulation research. It shows how emotion regulation unfolds cumulatively over time (rather than as a one-off episode), and extends the cognitive-processual perspective by demonstrating how emotions shape the framing of strategic issues and how containing initial emotions can later support more adaptive, data-informed reappraisals.

**Practical Implication**
Management teams and boards should recognize that their governance functions naturally arouse emotions that influence strategy making. The authors recommend three practices: create processes that contain initial emotional reactions by letting executives study challenges in "digestible parts"; require executives to form data-informed reappraisals by generating and analyzing multiple strategic options (using consultants and outside advisors); and concretize options through interactions with potential partners, while nurturing patience as emotions and thinking evolve gradually.

**Limitations**
The model is context-dependent: the arguments assume emotions have fairly universal influences on biology and cognition, yet context-specific factors may shape how emotions translate into behavior and whether they can be regulated. Nokia was headquartered in Finland and listed on the NYSE, so its board could actively influence top managers; in other contexts boards may face more constraints. The study also focuses on the emotion-regulation roles of boards, analytical teams, and potential partner firms within a single firm.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how structural arrangements (e.g., industry norms, ownership structure) shape the occurrence and effectiveness of socially distributed emotion regulation, and how various structural positions elicit the emotions of different groups and influence their emotion-regulation behaviors. Researchers should also explore whether and how organizational groups beyond boards, analytical teams, and partner firms contribute to emotion regulation, and how the timing, sequencing, and rhythm of regulation actions affect strategic outcomes.
