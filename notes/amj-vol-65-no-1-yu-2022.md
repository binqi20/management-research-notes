# Glue or Gasoline? The Role of Interorganizational Linkages in the Occurrence and Spillover of Competitive Wars

**Abstract**
Despite their prevalence and significance, competitive wars have received limited attention in the strategy literature. Our knowledge of how interorganizational linkages influence competitive wars is particularly lacking. Drawing on the social embeddedness perspective, we argue that both direct linkages (i.e., strategic alliances) and indirect linkages (i.e., common ownership ties and common analyst ties) reduce the likelihood of war, thereby functioning as the glue that binds firms together. Yet once competitive wars are launched in related markets, indirect linkages through common third-parties may continuously function as glue, reducing the likelihood of war spillover, whereas direct linkages, such as strategic alliances, may facilitate the spillover of competitive wars, akin to adding gasoline to a fire. Using data from the U.S. domestic airline industry between 1991 and 2010, our empirical evidence offers strong support for our predictions.

**Research Question**
How do direct interorganizational linkages (strategic alliances) and indirect linkages (common ownership ties and common analyst ties) influence both the occurrence of competitive wars within a market and their spillover into a focal market once wars have broken out elsewhere?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Strategic alliances among incumbents (direct/relational linkage); common ownership ties (indirect/structural linkage via shared institutional investors); common analyst ties (indirect/structural linkage via shared financial analysts).
- DV(s): War occurrence — a dichotomous indicator that a price war began in a market-period (actual prices >20% below expected prices).
- Mediators: None modeled (theorized mechanisms are trust/resource interdependence for direct ties and third-party mediation/sanctioning for indirect ties).
- Moderators: Incumbents at war in other markets in the prior period (the spillover trigger); the three linkage types each moderate the spillover-to-focal-market relationship — strategic alliances amplify it (gasoline), while common ownership ties and common analyst ties attenuate it (stronger glue).

Drawing on the social embeddedness perspective, the paper argues that direct alliances suppress war onset because repeated interaction builds trust and resource interdependence, while indirect ties suppress onset because impartial common third-parties (institutional investors, analysts) mediate conflicts and can sanction deviating firms via resource holdings or socioeconomic influence. Once a war erupts in related markets, however, the mechanisms diverge: alliance partners' aligned, complementary operations plus war-induced distrust make spillover more direct and severe (alliances become gasoline), whereas non-partisan third-parties, whose own interests are harmed by spreading wars, intensify their mediating and sanctioning roles (indirect ties become stronger glue). All five focal hypotheses were supported in the airline data.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances competitive dynamics research by being, to the authors' knowledge, the first to systematically examine the occurrence and spillover of competitive wars as a distinct phenomenon from day-to-day rivalry. It extends social embeddedness theory by identifying the onset of a competitive war as a critical situational trigger that can transform direct linkages from constraining competition (glue) into a catalyst for escalation (gasoline), and by foregrounding the distinct mechanisms—trust/interdependence versus third-party mediation/sanctioning—through which direct and indirect ties operate. It also provides initial empirical evidence for the previously untested assumption underlying the multimarket mutual forbearance hypothesis that competitive interactions spread across markets when forbearance breaks down, and introduces common analyst ties as a novel form of embeddedness shaping interfirm competition.

**Practical Implication**
For managers and policymakers, the findings suggest that interorganizational linkages have asymmetric, state-dependent effects on competitive escalation: common ownership and common analyst ties consistently dampen the spread of destructive price wars, whereas strategic alliances—while preventing wars from starting—can accelerate their contagion once fighting begins. The authors note that when competition escalates into catastrophic warfare, common ownership ties can help prevent the situation from worsening, which is beneficial for the industry as a whole, implying alliance partners should anticipate heightened spillover risk during wartime.

**Limitations**
The arguments carry boundary conditions: the theory applies most cleanly to industries with low differentiation and to markets without competing alliance cliques (where high alliance density may not lower rivalry), and the glue-gasoline story fits best alliances aimed at resource sharing such as airline code-sharing. The study examines only lose-lose wars detrimental to performance (e.g., price wars), not value-enhancing competitive wars, and analyzes spillover only across geographic—not product—markets. It also could not directly test whether passively managed index-fund ownership weakens common-ownership effects because the sample ends in 2010.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how different alliance types and purposes shape war occurrence and spillover differently, study value-enhancing (e.g., marketing or product-development) wars whose dynamics differ from lose-lose wars, and extend the analysis to spillover across product markets. The authors also encourage borrowing from behavioral perspectives (prospect theory, the behavioral theory of the firm, the psychology of rivalry) and strategic-communication lenses, investigating the within-war process of how rivals actually interact, and exploring how competitive wars reshape subsequent cooperative and alliance relationships between rivals.
