# Addressing Performance Tensions in Multiteam Systems: Balancing Informal Mechanisms of Coordination within and between Teams

**Abstract**
Due to their distinctive features, multiteam systems (MTSs) face significant coordination challenges—both within component teams and across the larger system. Despite the benefits of informal mechanisms of coordination for knowledge-based work, there is considerable ambiguity regarding their effects in MTSs. To resolve this ambiguity, we build and test theory about how interpersonal interactions among MTS members serve as an informal coordination mechanism that facilitates team and system functioning. Integrating MTS research with insights from the team boundary spanning literature, we argue that the degree to which MTS members balance their interactions with members of their own component team (i.e., intrateam interactions) and with the members of other teams in the system (i.e., interteam interactions) shapes team- and system-level performance. The findings of a multimethod study of 44 MTSs composed of 295 teams and 930 members show that as interteam interactions exceed intrateam interactions, team conflict arises and detracts from component team performance. At the system level, a balance between intrateam and interteam interactions enhances system success. Our findings advance understanding of MTSs by highlighting how informal coordination mechanisms enable MTSs to overcome their coordination challenges and address the unique performance tension between component teams and the larger system.

**Research Question**
How do informal coordination mechanisms—specifically, the balance of MTS members' intrateam versus interteam interpersonal interactions—influence component team and system-level effectiveness, and through what process do imbalances translate into performance consequences?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Imbalance between intrateam and interteam interpersonal interactions (operationalized via response surface / polynomial terms; "imbalance" = interteam exceeding intrateam, and incongruence in either direction at the system level)
- DV(s): Component team performance (within-team energy-transfer success rate) and MTS system-level performance (between-team energy-transfer success rate)
- Mediator: Team conflict (Jehn & Mannix's 9-item measure; channels the team-level imbalance effect to team performance)
- Moderators: None hypothesized; post hoc, coordination foci (adjacent vs. nonadjacent interteam interactions) and forms (concentration of interteam interactions in a subset of members) qualify the effects
- Controls: Team familiarity, team size, MTS size; system models also control average team familiarity, team performance, and team conflict

Drawing on the team boundary spanning principle that teams must strike "a balance between internal and external activities" (Choi, 2002), the authors argue that interteam interactions are necessary to coordinate across component teams but generate role overload, divergent task understandings, and intrateam conflict when not matched by corresponding intrateam interactions. At the team level, imbalance (interteam exceeding intrateam) raises team conflict (H1), which in turn depresses component team performance (H2). At the system level, balanced interpersonal interactions serve a coordination function that enables the MTS to integrate adjustments locally, so incongruence in either direction undermines system performance (H3). All three hypotheses were supported.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends MTS theory by showing that informal coordination mechanisms—long viewed pessimistically as unworkable in large, distributed systems—can in fact facilitate MTS effectiveness when intrateam and interteam interactions are considered jointly rather than as independent, additive factors. By integrating MTS and boundary spanning literatures, it identifies the balance of interpersonal interactions as a coordination mechanism, names team conflict as the intermediary that transmits team-level imbalance to performance, and demonstrates the distinct, sometimes countervailing, effects of coordination at the team versus system levels of analysis.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and members of MTSs should consciously manage with whom and how much they interact internally versus externally: interteam interactions must be coupled with at least a similar level of intrateam interactions so that externally acquired information is integrated within the team. Post hoc evidence further suggests focusing external interactions on directly interdependent (adjacent) teams, and that concentrating boundary spanning in a subset of members may benefit team (though not system) performance. Social network and interaction-tracking tools can help monitor and rebalance informal interactions.

**Limitations**
The sample comprised U.S. engineering students completing a standardized sequential-interdependence task, which limits generalizability to organizational, cross-cultural, and other MTS structures, and the system-level sample of 44 limits statistical power. Interaction was operationalized only as time in physical proximity, so the quality and content of interactions and the presumed system-level knowledge-transfer mechanism were not directly measured. Causality could not be established, and conflict measured after the action phase leaves open recursive conflict–interaction dynamics.

**Future Research**
The authors call for studying the intersection of formal and informal coordination mechanisms (including semi-structured forms) in MTSs, examining informal coordination across the full MTS lifecycle and transition phases, and directly measuring system-level mediating mechanisms such as knowledge transfer and interteam conflict. They also encourage examining the absolute volume of interactions, the adjacent/nonadjacent foci distinction, and the use of computational modeling or experiments to identify causal effects in other cultural and organizational contexts and larger samples.
