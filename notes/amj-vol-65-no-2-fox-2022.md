# Top Management Team Experiential Variety, Competitive Repertoires, and Firm Performance: Examining the Law of Requisite Variety in the 3D Printing Industry (1986–2017)

**Abstract**
This study develops and tests a thesis derived from the law of requisite variety. We contend that the greater the experiential variety of a top management team, the more likely it is that the complexity and consistency of the firm’s competitive repertoire will be calibrated to relevant external variety. In addition, for firms that achieve such calibrated repertoires, we expect that their financial performance will be superior to that of their peers. We then integrate these arguments and examine whether top management team experiential variety indirectly, through calibrated repertoires, contributes to firm performance. Analyzing hand-collected data for firms operating in the 3D printing industry over the past three decades (1986–2017), we find support for the overall thesis and associated hypotheses. The discussion section elaborates on the study’s contributions, limitations, and future research potential.

**Research Question**
Does top management team (TMT) industry experience variety shape firm performance indirectly, by enabling firms to calibrate the complexity and consistency of their competitive repertoires to the external variety embodied in top performers' actions, as predicted by the law of requisite variety (LRV)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): TMT industry experience variety (Shannon-entropy index of distinct NAICS-coded prior industry experiences across team members)
- DV(s): Calibrated repertoire complexity (H1) and calibrated repertoire consistency (H2) as proximal outcomes; firm performance, return on assets / ROA (H3–H6) as the distal outcome
- Mediators: Calibrated repertoire complexity (complexity path, H5) and calibrated repertoire consistency (consistency path, H6) jointly mediate the variety → performance relationship
- Moderators: None hypothesized; supplementary analyses probe TMT industry experience skew, within- vs. between-firm variance, and the calibration time frame

Drawing on the LRV, the authors argue that experientially varied TMTs possess a richer reserve of cognitive categories and behavioral routines, reducing both "routinized" (over-consistent, under-complex) and "headless chicken" (over-complex, under-consistent) competitive misfires. This allows such firms to calibrate the complexity and consistency of their repertoires to the external variety signaled by contemporaneous top performers, which in turn improves performance. The variety → performance relationship is fully indirect: TMT experiential variety operates through repertoire calibration rather than directly, consistent with the LRV's claim that system performance depends on matching internal to external variety.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces and operationalizes "calibrated competitive repertoires," shifting the LRV from an undefined theoretical-maximum notion of external variety to a measurable standard anchored in top-performing competitors, and extends the law beyond complexity to encompass repertoire consistency (a temporal component). It provides one of the first direct empirical tests of the LRV's performance thesis, modeling the full chain from TMT experiential variety through calibration to performance, and shows that "more is better" is too simplistic—what matters is matching, not maximizing, variety. For upper echelons theory it reframes attention from why executives act to how managerial perceptions produce calibration misfires.

**Practical Implication**
Firms should not pursue maximal complexity or rigid consistency in competitive action, but should calibrate both to the moving standard set by industry top performers; either under- or over-shooting that standard degrades performance. Because calibration is hard to achieve, assembling top management teams with a broad spread of qualitatively different prior industry experiences gives firms the cognitive breadth needed to track shifting external variety and avoid competitive inertia or scattershot responses.

**Limitations**
The overall sample is relatively small, which constrains the power to detect firm-performance effects and to jointly examine earlier and later life-cycle stages. The single-industry design buys less-confounded testing at the expense of cross-industry generalizability, and other TMT experience characteristics (e.g., functional or educational diversity) could yield similar predictions. ROA is a summary financial proxy for adaptation, and the authors cannot directly observe TMTs discriminating among stimuli and constructing their action repertoires.

**Future Research**
Examine whether an inherent trade-off exists between calibrating complexity and calibrating consistency, and which is more valuable when firms are forced to choose. Identify the behavioral routines and rigidities (beyond cognition) that drive miscalibration, and study how different TMT diversity types produce distinct forms of miscalibration—how firms miscalibrate may matter as much as how much. Finer-grained, more micro-level decision-making data could parse the perceptual and selection processes the theory posits.
