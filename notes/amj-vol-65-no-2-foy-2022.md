# Identity–Society (Mis)Alignment and the Instrumentalization of Firm Creation: Creative Destruction and Creative Reconstruction

**Abstract**
Research on founder identity has significantly advanced our understanding of entrepreneurship and related literatures. By departing from the widely held—though often implicit—assumption that culture defines the parameters of identity formation, this paper investigates how the alignment, or misalignment, between a founder’s identity and their perceived sociocultural context influences new firm creation. We engage in an in-depth qualitative study of 49 founders in Taiwan that reveals how founders’ identity structures can be (mis)aligned with their perceived social structures, and how the reinforcement or tensions generated by (mis)alignment can fuel the creation of fundamentally different types of ventures—including those that challenge the perceived sociocultural status quo with a social mission intended to push society toward a new equilibrium. Our theory of founder identity–society (mis)alignment is therefore able to specify a key mechanism linking the founder’s identity with perceived sociocultural context. Furthermore, we advance the influential Schumpeterian theory of creative destruction beyond the purely economic context to address the broader sociocultural environment and to incorporate the concept of creative reconstruction, in which entrepreneurs seek to restore elements of society past.

**Research Question**
How does the alignment, or misalignment, between an entrepreneur’s identity and perceived surrounding sociocultural context influence new venture creation?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: founder *identity structure* (tradition-, inner-, image-/discovery-directed) juxtaposed against perceived *social structure* (traditional, industrial, postmodern); (mis)alignment is the (in)congruence between the forces driving the two.
- Intermediate psychological outcomes: alignment yields a sense of societal *reinforcement* (emotional, cognitive, social); misalignment generates *tensions* (low to high) reflecting dissatisfaction with the perceived "rules of the game."
- Venture-outcome dimensions: (1) the *sociopolitical dimension* embedded in the venture (absent → dominant, i.e., whether it challenges the sociocultural status quo) and (2) *temporal orientation* (inventing the future vs. restoring the past).
- Emergent typology: six venture types mapping founders onto the two outcome dimensions, including high-tension misaligned founders who build social-mission ventures.

The paper theorizes a micro-level mechanism: when founders perceive misalignment between their identity structure and their surrounding social structures, the resulting tensions push them—via a logic of appropriateness rather than purely rational choice—to instrumentalize firm creation as a vehicle to change society, embedding a sociopolitical dimension oriented either toward a new future (creative destruction of the sociocultural environment) or toward restoring a valued past (creative reconstruction). Aligned founders, experiencing reinforcement, instead venture within the prevailing rules of the game and challenge only the economic status quo.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study specifies founder identity–society (mis)alignment as a driving mechanism in entrepreneurship that produces qualitatively different venture types, including social ventures, thereby relaxing the implicit assumption that founders’ identities are congruent with their sociocultural context. It extends Schumpeter’s theory of creative destruction beyond the economic domain to the sociocultural environment and introduces the complementary concept of *creative reconstruction*, in which entrepreneurs seek to re-establish elements of a past sociocultural environment. It also contributes to research on organizational emergence by showing early-stage strategic choices follow a logic of appropriateness rather than rational, choice-based decision-making.

**Practical Implication**
The (mis)alignment lens can help founders better understand themselves and prospective partners when forming venture teams, since divergence in identity structures or perceptions of the sociocultural environment shapes the tensions that arise. Recognizing that social-mission ventures often originate in identity-based tensions (rather than a deliberate aim to become "social entrepreneurs") clarifies the motivational roots of socially oriented venturing.

**Limitations**
The study mainly observed ventures created by single individuals (with only a few two-founder teams whose members had similar identities), precluding analysis of how founders with divergent identity structures co-create ventures and the tensions that arise between them. Not all misaligned founders acted on their tension to the same degree; in-depth analysis of why three outlier founders disengaged was outside the study’s scope, and other factors such as self-efficacy and personality were not examined.

**Future Research**
Longitudinal designs could examine the dynamics of identity–society alignment over time and the role of identity-structure shifts, since founders recounted life events that moved them between identity structures. Researchers could also drill into specific identity-structure × social-structure configurations (nine pure permutations) and investigate which levels of sociocultural context (immediate surroundings, industry, nation, global region, global society) are most salient for reinforcement or tension.
