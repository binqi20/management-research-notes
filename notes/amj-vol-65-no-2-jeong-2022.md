# Strategic Nepotism in Family Director Appointments: Evidence from Family Business Groups in South Korea

**Abstract**
Controlling families of family firms strive to preserve their socioemotional wealth (SEW) through family involvement in company leadership. Prior research points to a potential trade-off of family involvement, as underqualified and underprepared family members’ involvement would bring SEW gains at the expense of financial wealth. We examine how controlling families seek to shape this trade-off to be in their favor by systematically appointing family members to board positions favorable for building managerial competencies through what we call “strategic” nepotism. We find that compared to nonfamily insiders, family members are preferentially appointed to prestigious firms that provide opportunities for cultivating knowledge, skills, and reputation in a protected organizational environment. We also find that controlling family members are kept away from stigmatizing appointments that would undermine their managerial resource-building efforts. Consistent with an effort to overcome intergenerational deterioration of managerial talent and skills, we find that strategic nepotism is concentrated on later-rather than earlier-generation family members.

**Research Question**
What determines and drives family director appointments in family business groups — that is, where and when do controlling families appoint family members (versus nonfamily insiders) to boards, and how does this "strategic nepotism" vary across family generations?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm market performance (Tobin's Q); firm reputation ("most admired" list); intragroup board interlock centrality; financial distress (negative net income); number of existing family directors on the focal board.
- DV(s): Likelihood a board appointment is a family member versus a nonfamily inside director (binary probit); and, for generational tests, a categorical appointment outcome (nonfamily insider / earlier-generation / later-generation family member).
- Mediators: None modeled (resource-building and stigma-avoidance are theorized but not directly measured).
- Moderators: Family generation (earlier vs. later) — operationalized via the multinomial-probit contrast with earlier-generation appointments as baseline (Hypotheses 1b–5b).

Building on the SEW perspective and the "nepotism mixed gamble," the paper argues that controlling families proactively shape the long-run trade-off between SEW and financial wealth by deliberately routing family heirs to board positions that build human, reputational, and social capital (resource-building nepotism: high-performance, high-reputation, highly interlocked firms) while steering them away from positions that would taint those resources (stigma-avoiding nepotism: financially distressed firms and boards already crowded with family directors). Because founder-specific advantages erode across generations ("intergenerational deterioration"), later-generation members most need such grooming, so strategic nepotism is predicted — and found — to concentrate on them. Empirically, intragroup centrality (H3a), financial-distress avoidance (H4a), and avoidance of family-crowded boards (H5a) are supported; market performance (H1a) is only marginally supported and firm reputation (H2a) is not, but the generational tests show market performance (H1b), reputation (H2b), and distress avoidance (H4b) operate more strongly for later-generation members.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces "strategic nepotism" to extend the SEW/nepotism-mixed-gamble literature by recasting controlling families as proactive actors who manage the endogenous process of family director selection rather than passive beneficiaries of nepotism. It opens the "black box" of how family involvement arises, brings the SEW perspective into business-group research, reframes family business groups as internal labor markets for grooming family heirs, and supplies a previously neglected SEW-based account of director selection in contexts where family principals dominate.

**Practical Implication**
Families controlling businesses are advised to approach board appointments and family members' career paths strategically and dynamically — especially for later generations — since deliberately nurtured heirs are more likely to develop into qualified executives with firm-specific human and social capital, helping reconcile tensions between SEW and financial wealth.

**Limitations**
The study relies on archival data, so it infers underlying motives (e.g., stigma avoidance) from observed patterns rather than measuring them directly, and real-world motives may be more complex (e.g., avoiding intra-family conflict or dividing labor among kin). Because the data are drawn solely from South Korea, the generalizability of the theory and findings to family firms and business groups in other institutional and cultural contexts is an open question.

**Future Research**
Future work could use surveys, interviews, and ethnographic observation to uncover controlling families' fine-grained motives and intentions, and test the theory cross-nationally to establish institutional and cultural boundary conditions. Scholars could also extend strategic nepotism to multi-business family firms (e.g., assigning kin to favorable business units), and examine whether strategic nepotism actually yields the intended downstream outcomes — improved managerial resources and more positive stakeholder evaluations.
