# The Fragility of Experts: A Moderated-Mediation Model of Expertise, Expert Identity Threat, and Overprecision

**Abstract**
Experts play a crucial role in modern organizations, but evidence regarding the soundness and reliability of their decision-making is mixed and often contradictory. We develop and test a moderated-mediation model of expert decision-making linking expertise, identity threat, and overprecision to understand when and why experts offer overly precise judgments, and how they can cope with disconfirming feedback. We find support for this model in a series of lab experiments which show that (a) experts are more likely than novices to double down and produce overly precise predictions following disconfirming feedback, (b) this feedback-induced overprecision by experts is mediated by perceived level of expert identity threat, (c) the source of the feedback matters for identity threat and overprecision, and (d) self-affirmation attenuates identity threat and reduces overprecision. We supplement these experimental findings by investigating experts' response to disconfirming feedback in two real-world settings: Major League Baseball umpiring and chief financial officer predictions of stock market returns. Our model and results show that feedback can harm expert decision-making by leading experts to be overly precise in their judgment, challenging existing notions on the ability of expert decision-makers, and providing insight into when and why experts should be relied upon in organizational decisions.

**Research Question**
When and why do experts make sound versus unsound decisions following negative performance feedback? Specifically, the paper asks whether disconfirming feedback leads experts (more than novices) to "double down" and become overprecise in their judgments, whether perceived expert identity threat mediates this effect, and how the feedback source and self-affirmation moderate it.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Expertise (expert vs. novice, operationalized via domain experience in Study 1 and via priming/manipulation in Studies 2-4); disconfirming (negative) performance feedback (pre- vs. post-feedback, within-subjects).
- DV(s): Overprecision (excessive certainty in one's judgments), measured primarily via the Subjective Probability Interval Elicitation (SPIES) method and an overprecision-count alternative; in archival studies, umpire "border certainty" (deviation from 0.5) and CFO confidence-interval miscalibration.
- Mediators: Perceived expert identity threat.
- Moderators: Source credibility of the feedback (group of experts vs. group of novices vs. actual results); self-affirmation (vs. control); monetary reward (tested, but ineffective).

Negative performance feedback poses a salient threat to people's expert identity; because experts' self-concept is grounded in superior knowledge and competence, they experience a more acute threat than novices and respond by reaffirming self-worth—boosting confidence and issuing narrower, overly precise predictions. This identity-threat mechanism is amplified when feedback comes from credible sources (other experts or actual outcomes) and attenuated when experts self-affirm core values beforehand, which restores global self-integrity and removes the need to assert expert identity through overconfidence.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces expert identity threat as a motivational (not merely cognitive or task-based) factor explaining when experts exhibit sound versus distorted decision-making, integrating identity-threat and self-affirmation theory into the literature on expertise and overconfidence. It reframes overconfidence as a motivated, identity-protective response to threatened valued identities rather than only a heuristic bias or strategic response to external incentives, and shows that performance feedback—long viewed as a debiasing tool—can paradoxically worsen expert judgment.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and organizations that rely on experts (e.g., professional service firms, hospitals) should recognize that bluntly confronting experts with their errors can backfire by triggering identity-protective overprecision. Framing feedback as constructive learning opportunities, deploying self-affirmation interventions that reaffirm individual and organizational values, and cultivating psychological safety can mitigate identity threat and reduce experts' tendency to double down. Monetary incentives, by contrast, did not curb the bias.

**Limitations**
The prediction tasks studied were genuinely difficult with low accuracy rates (often under 40%, and under 20% in the investment task), so a ceiling effect means the findings may not generalize to high-accuracy, routine judgment tasks—an important boundary condition. Providing identical negative feedback to all participants regardless of actual performance limits external validity and raises the possibility of demotivation/frustration artifacts, though identity threat fully mediated the effect. The study examined only one defensive response (overprecision) among many possible forms of defensiveness.

**Future Research**
Future work could extend the identity-threat model to other forms of identity threat (e.g., status loss, rivalry, race- or gender-based stereotype threat) and to inequality within organizations, and could test the model with other defensive behaviors such as rejection, denigration, discounting, derogation, or reframing. Researchers could also examine how the correctness of feedback interacts with identity concerns, particularly for tasks with higher baseline accuracy.
