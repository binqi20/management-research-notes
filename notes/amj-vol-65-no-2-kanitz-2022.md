# No Change Is an Island: How Interferences between Change Initiatives Evoke Inconsistencies That Undermine Implementation

**Abstract**
Organizational change research has concentrated on the challenges of implementing isolated changes, paying little attention to the interactions among concurrent change initiatives. Our longitudinal real-time study of a multinational technology firm examines how two corporate change initiatives interfered with each other. The interfering initiatives provoked inconsistency judgments (cognitive, normative, and procedural) and the emergence of collective emotions that undermined change performance. Top managers’ responses fueled the sharing of inconsistency judgments and emotions that fed into a recursive process that, over time, provoked emotional uncertainty, elicited moral emotions, and eroded emotional attachment to change. Our process model reveals inconsistency judgments as a previously overlooked socio-psychological mechanism underpinning interferences between change initiatives. We reveal the limitations of examining organizational change in terms of isolated initiatives and call for research that considers the dynamics between change initiatives.

**Research Question**
How do interferences between change initiatives emerge? And what are the mechanisms linking those interferences to change outcomes?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Interfering organizational change initiatives — two quasi-concurrent strategic initiatives (Project Foundation, an internally focused culture/process change, and Project Rebranding) whose separate, quasi-autonomous execution generated friction rather than fit.
- Key constructs / judgments: Perceived inconsistencies between initiatives, coded as cognitive (lack of agreement in content), procedural (lack of agreement in required actions), and normative (lack of agreement in norms and values).
- Emotional reactions: Three shared emotional processes — uncertainty-related (confusion, worry), moral-related (indignation, shock), and detachment-related (annoyance, apathy).
- Social sharing activities: Challenging, reproaching, and distancing — recursive sharing that spreads cues and fosters collective emotions, amplified by internal social media.
- Top managers’ responses: Ambiguous top-down communication and restricted bottom-up communication that fueled rather than calmed negativity.
- Emergent outcome: Collective (dis)engagement from change initiatives, producing underperformance in speed, costs, and impact.

Employees socially construct the meaning of concurrent initiatives using consistency criteria; when initiatives are judged inconsistent, this triggers negative emotions that are shared and converge into collective emotions. Top managers’ responses act as triggers that amplify or dampen this sharing, and the resulting collective (dis)engagement — not abstract cognition alone — drives change outcomes through a recursive, self-reinforcing process operating over time.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends theories of navigating large-scale change by developing a consistency perspective on interacting change initiatives, identifying inconsistency judgments (cognitive, normative, procedural) as a previously overlooked socio-psychological mechanism through which one change project interferes with another. It also contributes to the literature on emotions and organizational change by showing how perceptions of inconsistency trigger emotional reactions and how social sharing and top managers’ responses amplify the emergence of collective emotions during change.

**Practical Implication**
Top managers should anticipate that strategic change carried out through multiple initiatives can breed perceptions of inconsistency, and should respond to recipients’ emotional cues with purposeful sensegiving (sensebreaking, sense specification) rather than restricting dialogue. Establishing a centralized transformation team to monitor cross-initiative interactions and address both task- and emotion-related issues can attenuate inconsistency perceptions and sustain momentum.

**Limitations**
The study has the usual limitations of a single case study, so its insights need validation in other settings. The authors cannot entirely rule out that the observed outcomes were also driven by other factors, such as the cultural context of the studied firm.

**Future Research**
The authors invite research testing whether similar inconsistency judgments arise when a change is perceived as inconsistent with an organization’s history or identity (rather than another change), and on hypocrisy between espoused values and leaders’ cues. They also call for studies of "enabling interactions" in strategic initiative portfolios, a narrative/rhetorical lens on the temporal interplay of framing and retelling, and the dynamics of positive, future-oriented emotions (e.g., hope, optimism) — with social media as a contextual factor.
