# Big Fish versus Big Pond? Entrepreneurs, Established Firms, and Antecedents of Tie Formation

**Abstract**
Entrepreneurial and established firms form collaborative relationships to commercialize products. Through such ties, entrepreneurs seek (a) development help to hone ideas into marketable products and (b) access to markets. In most cases, entrepreneurs face a trade-off: they can be a big fish in a small pond, getting more attention and development help from a smaller firm with less market access, or a small fish in a big pond, getting less attention and help from a larger firm with more market access. Our study investigates what goes into choosing between these options. Drawing from resource dependence theory and an empirical study of tie formation between developers and publishers of PlayStation 2 video games, we develop and test a framework that identifies the key decision variables and focuses on two moderators—resource need evolution and resource uncertainty related to competition—that explain whether a big fish (more development help) or a big pond (more market access) drives tie formation. Our findings point to prospective peers as one of the significant decision criteria at tie formation and highlight the dynamic nature of resource dependence. Altogether, the results give resource dependence theory a dynamic element it has lacked in the past.

**Research Question**
What conditions lead an entrepreneur to become a big fish in a small pond (prioritizing development help) versus a small fish in a big pond (prioritizing market access) when forming a tie with an established firm? Specifically, how do anticipated development help and market access drive tie formation, and how are these weightings moderated by an entrepreneur's evolving resource needs and competition-induced resource uncertainty?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Anticipated development help (H1); anticipated market access (H2)
- DV(s): Tie formation between developer (entrepreneur) and publisher (established firm) in year *t*
- Moderators: Entrepreneur's resource need evolution (product experience) (H3); competition-induced resource uncertainty (anticipated game overlap) (H4)
- Controls: Prior collaboration, in-house development, quality difference, geographic distance, same country, established firm size/experience/portfolio size, entrepreneur size, year fixed effects

Resource dependence drives entrepreneurs and established firms to form ties to satisfy mutual resource needs: entrepreneurs supply novel ideas but lack development resources and market access, which established firms control. Because personalized development help is rationed in rank order across the established firm's portfolio of prospective peers, while market access is scalable, entrepreneurs face the "big fish, big pond" trade-off. The paper argues resource dependence is dynamic: as entrepreneurs gain experience their needs shift from internal (development) to external (market) resources (H3), and as competitive intensity rises the value of personalized, differentiating development help increases (H4), so the relative weighting of the two resources at tie formation shifts accordingly.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study gives resource dependence theory a dynamic element it has lacked, showing that both resource needs (organizational dynamism) and resource uncertainty (environmental dynamism) evolve over time and reshape partner choice. It spotlights the neglected role of prospective peers as a source of competition at the *ex ante* tie-formation stage, demonstrating that partner choice is not simply "find the biggest pond" and that the partner with the best resources is not always the most sought-after.

**Practical Implication**
Resource-constrained, inexperienced entrepreneurs can attract partners and stand out by emphasizing their relative standing among an established firm's prospective peers, seeking "big fish" ties that offer personalized development help. Small, up-and-coming established firms or platforms lacking market access can compete for partners by offering personalized development help, differentiating themselves from larger, more rigid players.

**Limitations**
The conditional logit specification cannot report developer-level (e.g., resource needs) or industry-level (e.g., resource uncertainty) variables that are invariant across a developer's partner choices in a year. Realized ties were rare, requiring rare-events and matching robustness checks. The findings are bounded by conditions specific to "markets of know-how," including limited scalability of established-firm resources, heterogeneity of established firms, and the unpredictability of breakthrough ideas.

**Future Research**
The "big fish, big pond" framework could inform ecosystem and platform scholarship on which platforms attract complementors and which funding partners start-ups prefer, and whether established firms develop reputations as desirable partners. Studying the effectiveness and outcomes of formed ties (not just their formation), and how accumulated dyadic choices reshape alliance portfolios and industry networks, are promising directions.
