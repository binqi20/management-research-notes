# Which Ideas for Change Are Endorsed? How Agentic and Communal Voice Affects Endorsement Differently for Men and for Women

**Abstract**
This paper explores how gender affects idea endorsement. We depart from the existing approach, which considers the effect of gender and other factors in isolation, and propose that endorsement depends on who suggests which ideas and in what ways. Drawing upon expectancy violations theory, we argue that "positive counter-stereotypical voice"—conceptualized as an idea that signals desirable traits stereotypically associated with the opposite gender—is more likely to be endorsed compared to "positive yet stereotypical voice" (i.e., that signals desirable traits consistent with stereotypical expectations of the speaker's gender). We argue that communal voice for men and agentic voice for women constitute positive counter-stereotypical voice, whereas agentic voice for men and communal voice for women constitute positive stereotypical voice. Then, we examine why positive counter-stereotypical voice results in positive evaluations—endorsement—by arguing that it affects attributions of competence and benevolence, albeit differently for men and for women. We test our hypotheses across three studies—one field and two experimental. Combined, we contribute to both the voice and gender literatures and shed new light on our understanding of how and why women and men get their ideas endorsed.

**Research Question**
How does the gender of a speaker, combined with the content and presentation style of the idea they raise, affect whether that idea for change is endorsed—and through which attributional mechanisms (competence vs. benevolence) does this effect operate for women versus for men?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Positive counter-stereotypical voice (agentic voice for women = task content + assertive language; communal voice for men = relational content + polite language), versus positive stereotypical voice; manipulated/coded against speaker gender.
- DV(s): Endorsement of the idea for change (five-item scale adapted from Burris, 2012).
- Mediators: Attributed competence (for women) and attributed benevolence (for men).
- Moderators: Speaker gender (moderates which attributional pathway links voice to endorsement; indexed via moderated mediation).

Grounded in expectancy violations theory (EVT) and social role theory, the paper argues that behaviors violating gender expectations draw heightened perceptual attention, and that *positive* violations are evaluated more favorably via the augmentation principle. When women voice agentically (counter-stereotypical), evaluators credit unanticipated competence; when men voice communally (counter-stereotypical), evaluators credit unanticipated benevolence. These attributions, in turn, raise the likelihood of endorsement, but the operative dimension of trustworthiness differs by gender. Support was consistent for women (competence pathway) across all studies; for men, the benevolence pathway emerged only in Study 3 when both content and language use jointly signaled communality.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconceptualizes voice endorsement as jointly contingent on "who" (speaker gender) and "what/how" (idea content and presentation), rather than treating these factors in isolation, creating a framework that maps the two fundamental dimensions of behavioral content—agency and communality—onto gender stereotypes. It identifies a new attributional mechanism—evaluators' inferences about the speaker's competence and benevolence—linking voice to endorsement, and shows the operative dimension differs by gender. It also advances EVT by testing the augmentation process and reconciling gender role theory with EVT by clarifying that positive versus negative violations drive divergent (benefit vs. backlash) outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Employees should take a holistic approach—considering who they are (especially gender), what they say (content), and how they say it (presentation style) in tandem—to raise the likelihood their idea is endorsed, because identical content or delivery is evaluated differently depending on the speaker's gender. The findings offer "optimism" to women and men who defy gender stereotypes positively: women voicing agentically and men voicing communally may be especially likely to have such counter-stereotypical ideas endorsed.

**Limitations**
Study 1 relied on managers recalling and categorizing a subordinate's voice event (critical incident technique), so the evaluator's perception of content—rather than the subordinate's intended content—is what was captured, and language use could not be controlled in that study. Studies 2 and 3 used vignette experiments, which have been criticized for limited external validity, though this design maximized control over content and language manipulations.

**Future Research**
Future scholars should examine counter-stereotypical voice for other categories of employees (e.g., ethnic minorities, sexual orientations, cultural backgrounds) and intersectional stereotypes such as how women of color are evaluated when voicing agentically. Researchers are also encouraged to study the extent to which an idea positively or negatively violates expectancies (rather than taking valence as given), additional content–language combinations, and the interaction of promotive versus prohibitive voice with agency and communality.
