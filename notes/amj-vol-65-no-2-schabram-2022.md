# How Other- and Self-Compassion Reduce Burnout through Resource Replenishment

**Abstract**
The average employee feels burnt out, a multidimensional state of depletion likely to persist without intervention. In this paper, we consider compassion as an agentic action by which employees may replenish their own depleted resources and thereby recover. We draw on conservation of resources theory to examine the resource-generating power of two distinct expressions of compassion (selfand other-directed) on three dimensions of burnout (exhaustion, cynicism, inefficacy). Utilizing two complementary designs—a longitudinal field survey of 130 social service providers and an experiential sampling methodology with 100 business students across 10 days—we find a complex pattern of results indicating that both compassion expressions have the potential to generate salutogenic resources (self-control, belonging, self-esteem) that replenish different dimensions of burnout.

**Research Question**
"When can compassionate action reduce an actor's own burnout?" The paper asks whether and how employees can act compassionately toward themselves or others to generate the specific resources depleted by each dimension of burnout (exhaustion, cynicism, inefficacy) and thereby recover.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Enacted self-compassion; enacted other-compassion (Study 2 also includes directed/randomly-assigned self- vs. other-compassion vs. neutral instructions)
- DV(s): Three burnout dimensions—exhaustion, cynicism, inefficacy (later time point / next day)
- Mediators: Self-control (energy resource), belonging (condition resource), self-esteem (attribute resource)
- Moderators: None hypothesized; controls include gender, age, tenure, union status (Study 1) and prior-day burnout, day, sleep quality (Study 2)

Grounded in conservation of resources theory's primacy-of-resource-loss and resource-investment principles, the paper argues that burnout persists because early depletion erodes the very resources needed to combat further demands, so recovery requires agentic, resource-generating action. Compassion is most restorative when its particular expression generates the resource that fits the depleted dimension: self-compassion builds self-control to remedy exhaustion, other-compassion builds belonging to remedy cynicism, and both can build self-esteem to remedy inefficacy. Across the field survey (direct effects) and the diary study (indirect effects through resources), self-compassion reduced exhaustion and other-compassion reduced cynicism, while effects on inefficacy were more varied, supporting a compassion–burnout "fit" logic rather than a uniform benefit of compassion.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances burnout research by treating recovery as an agentic act the sufferer can initiate—shifting focus from what others do to the burnt-out employee to what employees can do for themselves—and by retaining all three burnout dimensions, surfacing robust cynicism effects that exhaustion-only models miss. It imports self-compassion from psychology and distinguishes it from other-compassion, showing their consequences differ by resource generated and dimension remedied. For COR theory, it provides proof-of-concept for under-tested dynamics (resource caravans, equifinality, multifinality) by modeling three resource categories simultaneously.

**Practical Implication**
Compassion can help address the workplace burnout epidemic, but the calculus is not straightforward—not all compassion combats all burnout, and benefits depend on matching the compassion expression to the depleted dimension. Because the studies found only indirect effects when compassion was prompted by instruction, reaping benefits likely requires ongoing investment in assessing burnt-out employees' specific needs and matching appropriate recovery strategies; employers can use the validated self- and other-compassion measures to track fluctuations.

**Limitations**
The generalization-and-extension approach varied multiple elements at once (sample, time frame, voluntary vs. directed compassion), making it difficult to attribute inconsistent direct/indirect findings to result instability versus design choices. Both samples were largely women and of equal status (coworkers or fellow students), and both studies relied on self-reports; in Study 2, a morning measurement of self-control inadvertently time-separated it from the night-time resource measures.

**Future Research**
Future work should replicate inconsistent results by varying only one element at a time, extend to male-dominated professions and to leader–subordinate (unequal-status) interactions, and use controlled experiments with objective or multisource measures. The authors call for incorporating compassion recipients to study interpersonal/dyadic resource crossover, probing whether compassion can serve as a prophylactic, examining order effects across burnout dimensions and partial-burnout profiles, and pursuing field experiments and clinical trials to test COR-based interventions.
