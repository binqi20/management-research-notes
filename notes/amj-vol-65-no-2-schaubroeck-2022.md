# “I” Am Affirmed, but Are “We”? Social Identity Processes Influencing Refugees’ Work Initiative and Community Embeddedness

**Abstract**
Facilitating refugees’ transitions to host country society is of interest to their host countries and municipalities, employers within those countries, and the refugees themselves. We develop and test a model of how social identity processes, as outlined in selfcategorization theory, influence how perceiving that one is treated as an insider encourages behaviors reflecting social engagement with host country nationals, both within and outside of work. In a sample of 389 Syrian refugee employees in 88 supervisory units, perceived insider status was indirectly related to work initiative and community embeddedness through organizational identification. These indirect effects were moderated by diversity climate and perceived stigmatization of refugees in the broader society. Perceived insider status had its weakest effect on identification, and was not related indirectly to the outcomes when diversity climate was lower and perceived stigma was higher. We discuss the implications for theory development and practice concerning how social identity salience can inhibit personal affirmations at work from encouraging members of marginalized groups to demonstrate a deeper commitment to the organization and society.

**Research Question**
How do social identity processes—specifically the salience of the refugee social identity—shape whether refugee employees' perceptions that they are personally treated as insiders translate, through organizational identification, into work initiative and community embeddedness in the host society? When does personal affirmation at work fail to promote engagement?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived insider status (Time 1)
- DV(s): Work initiative (Time 3), community embeddedness (Time 3)
- Mediators: Organizational identification (Time 2)
- Moderators: Diversity climate (supervisory-unit level, Time 1); perceived societal stigma toward refugees (individual level, Time 1) — modeled as a higher-order (three-way) moderator

Drawing on self-categorization theory, the authors argue that perceiving high insider status affirms refugees' personal identity and thereby builds organizational identification, which serves as a transitional/bridging identity that supplies the security and confidence to take the interpersonal risks involved in work initiative and in embedding within the broader community. However, an unfavorable diversity climate raises the "category fit" between refugees and nonrefugees, rendering the refugee social identity more salient; per the functional antagonism between social and personal identity salience, a salient refugee identity weakens the effect of personal affirmation (insider status) on identification. This salience-amplifying effect of poor diversity climate operates only among refugees who perceive high societal stigmatization of refugees (high category accessibility), so the indirect effects are weakest when diversity climate is low and perceived stigma is high.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends diversity-climate theory by showing that diversity climate operates as a collective signal that conditions whether individualized, personal-identity-affirming treatment (perceived insider status) builds organizational identification—reframing diversity climate beyond a simple positive perception of the organization toward a determinant of identity salience and collective sensemaking. It also extends work on organizational identification as a bridging/transitional identity (Rogers et al., 2017) from ex-inmates to employed refugees and, potentially, to other stigmatized and marginalized groups, applying self-categorization theory's accessibility-and-fit logic to organizational engagement outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Employers can harness refugees' human capital by promoting policies that signal fair treatment and inclusion of all employees and by ensuring individuals feel informed and included, which strengthens identification and downstream initiative and community engagement. Because perceived insider status is a scarce, status-based resource that managers cannot grant to everyone, fostering a favorable shared diversity climate—and educating nonrefugee employees against stigmatizing refugees—is an important complementary lever, especially where the broader climate is unfavorable.

**Limitations**
Generalizing beyond Syrian refugees depends on how their sensemaking is shared with other refugee populations, and the sample comprised refugees employed in established businesses working routinely with nonrefugees, distinguishing them from the larger population of unemployed or camp-confined refugees. The design does not establish causality, relying on theory and temporally separated measures (multi-week lags) to infer causal order; the predicted main effect of diversity climate on identification was not supported, possibly owing to the relatively small number of work units.

**Future Research**
Future work could develop theory on how nonrefugee employees' perceptions and behaviors interact with refugees' in ways that support or impede refugee thriving, and compare nonrefugees with refugees to isolate the transitional-identity mechanism. Researchers might examine whether specific personal affirmations (e.g., positive feedback, merit raises, promotions) show weaker identification effects under unfavorable diversity climate and high stigma, and test whether the diversity-climate-by-insider-status process generalizes across different refugee populations and other stigmatized groups.
