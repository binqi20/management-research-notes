# Pumping the Brakes: Examining the Impact of CEO Political Ideology Divergence on Firm Responses

**Abstract**
Research on CEO political ideology has demonstrated the construct’s impact on many firm outcomes. Yet, political ideology does not exist in a vacuum; beyond representing a CEO’s values, political ideology also affects the CEO’s perception of the political environment. It remains unclear how a difference between the national political climate and the CEO’s own political ideology might affect strategic decision-making. Applying upper echelons and threat response perspectives, we propose that divergence between the national political climate and the CEO’s own ideology provokes a threat response, leading CEOs to cut research and development spending and increase retained earnings. We then examine two boundary conditions for this relationship. First, we propose that CEO wealth tied to the firm in the form of vested stock options will increase the perceived threat of ideology divergence, strengthening the threat response. Second, we propose that industry regulation will dampen the threat perceived from divergence, as well as hindering the CEO’s ability to respond to the threat at all, because of greater stability and lower discretion, respectively. We test these predictions on a sample of Standard & Poor’s 500 CEOs from 2004 to 2017 and find support for our hypothesized relationships.

**Research Question**
How does divergence between the national political climate and a CEO's own political ideology — rather than the CEO's ideology in isolation — affect strategic firm decisions, and how do CEO vested stock options and industry regulation condition that effect?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO political ideology divergence (absolute difference between CEO liberalism index, from 10-year pre-CEO donation records, and the president-level national political climate)
- DV(s): R&D intensity (R&D spending / sales) and retained earnings
- Mediators: None formally tested; perceived threat is the theorized (unmeasured) intervening mechanism
- Moderators: CEO vested stock options (logged vested option value; amplifies the threat response) and regulated industry (dummy for the 10 most highly regulated industries; dampens the threat response)

Drawing on upper echelons theory and threat response theory, the authors argue that when the national political climate diverges from a CEO's deeply held political ideology, the CEO perceives a threat to their beliefs and responds defensively by retreating to the known and familiar — curtailing forward-looking, uncertain R&D investment (H1) and accumulating internally controllable retained earnings (H2). Because vested stock options tie the CEO's personal wealth to firm outcomes (an "endowment effect"), they heighten sensitivity to the threat and strengthen both responses (H3, H4). Industry regulation, by reducing managerial discretion and providing stability, both lowers the perceived threat and constrains the CEO's ability to respond, weakening the responses (H5, H6). GEE models on S&P 500 firms support all six hypotheses.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the CEO political ideology literature by shifting attention from a CEO's absolute ideology to the divergence between that ideology and the national political climate, thereby capturing the causal-belief (not merely value) dimension of ideology whose effects flip with the political environment. It also extends threat response theory from realized threats (e.g., resource scarcity, competition) to a purely perceived threat experienced only by those discerning the divergence, and identifies CEO wealth and managerial discretion (via regulation) as boundary conditions on when a perceived threat translates into firm action.

**Practical Implication**
Boards should recognize that a CEO may alter firm decisions — cutting R&D and hoarding earnings — when the political climate diverges from the CEO's ideology, creating a potential agency problem, especially where CEO and board ideologies converge. Because vested stock options intensify these responses, boards should weigh this form of compensation carefully, and ensure CEO decisions to cut R&D or build retained earnings are justified to stakeholders who may not perceive the same threat.

**Limitations**
The study is set in the United States' long-established two-party system, so the divergence construct and theory may not generalize directly to multi-party parliamentary systems. The sample comprises large S&P 500 firms with multinational operations, leaving open whether the effects hold for smaller or mid-sized firms embedded in more regional or local political environments. Ideology is characterized along a single liberal–conservative spectrum, whereas political attitudes are increasingly viewed along separate economic and social dimensions.

**Future Research**
Scholars could examine whether convergence between CEO ideology and the national climate generates the opposite of threat — opportunity or (over)optimism — and test for asymmetric effects of convergence versus divergence. Research could extend the theory to other countries with multi-party systems and to regional or local political environments, and decompose divergence along separate social and economic ideological dimensions to see whether effects differ across them.
