# Too Much on the Plate? How Executive Job Demands Harm Firm Innovation and Reduce Share of Exploratory Innovations

**Abstract**
Building on psychological research on job demands and executive job demands theory, we explain why executive job demands negatively influence a firm’s overall innovation and shift the balance of innovative activities toward a larger share of exploitative innovations at the expense of exploratory innovations, leading to a smaller share of innovations that are exploratory. In addition, we explain how variety in executives’ gender, age, and tenure and an innovative climate weaken the negative effects of job demands on both overall innovation and the share of exploratory innovations. Our theory suggests that a controlling climate and employees’ education weaken the negative effect of job demands on overall innovation but exacerbate the negative effect of job demands on the share of exploratory innovations. Using surveys collected on-site from 243 Chinese firms, we find support for five of our 10 hypotheses and marginal support for three additional hypotheses but no support for the two moderating effects of innovative climate. This study shifts innovation scholars’ attention away from executive cognition and characteristics to their job environment attributes. It also develops executive job demands theory by examining its boundaries and applicability to the domain of innovation management.

**Research Question**
How do executive job demands—a fundamental attribute of top executives' objective job environment rather than their cognition or characteristics—influence a firm's overall innovation and its share of exploratory (versus exploitative) innovations, and which features of the executive job environment (TMT variety in gender/age/tenure, controlling climate, innovative climate, employees' education) moderate these effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Executive job demands (measured via causal/formative indicators: task challenges [environmental dynamism, hostile suppliers/buyers, hostile competitors], performance challenge, executive aspirations [CEO need for achievement])
- DV(s): Firm overall innovation (12-item Jansen et al. 2006 scale); firm share of exploratory innovations (exploratory ÷ [exploratory + exploitative], standardized)
- Mediators: None formally tested (the cognitive-resource depletion logic is theorized but not measured as a mediator)
- Moderators: Variety in executives' gender, age, and tenure (TMT variety); controlling climate; innovative climate; employees' education (percentage with at least a bachelor's degree)

Executive job demands consume top executives' limited cognitive and emotional resources, forcing them to attend selectively to familiar information, rely on heuristics (e.g., the availability heuristic), weaken long-term orientation, lower risk-taking propensity, and centralize decision-making—reducing their ability to identify innovation opportunities and integrate knowledge. Because exploratory innovation requires divergent thinking, longer horizons, and greater risk, job demands harm exploration more than exploitation, lowering the exploratory share. TMT variety supplies cognitive diversity that buffers both effects; a controlling climate frees cognitive resources (helping overall innovation) but reinforces exploitation (worsening the exploratory share); educated employees compensate cognitively (helping overall innovation) yet are rewarded for exploitation (worsening the exploratory share).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study shifts innovation research from executives' cognition and individual attributes toward their objective job environment, identifying executive job demands as a novel and important determinant of firm innovation. It advances strategic-leadership/upper-echelons research by examining the previously untested boundary conditions and applicability of executive job demands theory to innovation, and—by showing systematic effects on overall innovation and the exploratory share—offers what the authors describe as the first piece of direct empirical evidence of the validity of executive job demands theory. It also extends the broader job demands literature to the most influential group of organizational members, top executives.

**Practical Implication**
Executives should be acutely aware that high job demands can damage firm innovation, especially exploratory innovation. Firms can counteract these effects through levers in the executive job environment: cultivating TMT variety in gender, age, and tenure to buffer both overall innovation and the exploratory share; recognizing that a controlling climate and a highly educated workforce help overall innovation but can crowd out exploration; and attending to how the design of the executive job environment shapes the exploration-exploitation balance.

**Limitations**
The one-wave (cross-sectional) survey design limits strong causal inference. Evidence on two moderators was inconsistent: the innovative-climate hypotheses (7 and 8) received no support, and the significance of the innovative-climate and employees'-education moderators shifted depending on whether TMT demographic controls were included, so those moderating findings warrant cautious interpretation. The sample comprised small and medium-sized firms in China, constraining generalizability to other firm sizes and national contexts.

**Future Research**
Future studies could systematically examine why different indicators of executive job demands (e.g., task challenge appeared most influential) have differential effects on strategic decisions, and assess generalizability across countries with stronger or weaker institutional environments and differing cultural individualism. Researchers could explore CEO–TMT interface dynamics (e.g., patterns of dependence between the CEO and other executives, harmony-oriented social norms) as conditioning factors, and extend the framework to other strategic consequences of executive job demands such as competitive behavior, mergers and acquisitions, and other risk-taking decisions.
