# Prosocial Occupations, Work Autonomy, and the Origins of the Social Class Pay Gap

**Abstract**
Despite decades of research on social mobility and wage disparities, it remains a puzzle why people from lower-class families earn less than people from upper-class families even when similar in education and occupational prestige. Taking a sociocultural perspective on social class, we argue that a key contributor to the class pay gap is that people from upper-class origins tend to work in occupations with greater autonomy, whereas their lower-class counterparts tend to work in occupations that are more prosocial. We further propose that autonomous occupations pay better than prosocial occupations. Across two distinct nationally representative samples in the United States, we find that people with upper-class (vs. lower-class) parents are more likely to work in autonomous occupations, but less likely to work in prosocial occupations, even when controlling for education, occupational prestige, and other potential confounds. This pattern of occupational sorting explains a substantial portion of the class pay gap. Our study extends the literatures on social class, occupational segregation, and social mobility, and joins an important scholarly conversation that has, until recently, taken place outside the field of management.

**Research Question**
Why do people from lower-class origins earn less than people from upper-class origins even when they are similar in education and occupational prestige? The paper asks whether class-based sorting into occupations that differ in work autonomy versus prosocial orientation helps explain this residual social class pay gap.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Social class of origin (standardized composite of parental education, occupational prestige, and income; treated as continuous)
- DV(s): Personal income (the class pay gap); occupational work autonomy and prosocial orientation as intermediate occupational outcomes
- Mediators: Work autonomy (positive path to income) and prosocial orientation (negative path to income) of the respondent's occupation — dual-path mediation of the class–income relationship
- Moderators: Exploratory demographic moderators (gender; race — weaker negative class–prosocial link for Black vs. White respondents in GSS)

Drawing on a sociocultural perspective, the authors argue that childhood resource abundance fosters an independent model of self that draws upper-class people toward high-autonomy occupations, while resource scarcity fosters an interdependent model of self that orients lower-class people toward prosocial occupations; this two-sided supply- and demand-side sorting, combined with the wage premium autonomous occupations command over prosocial ones (devaluation of communal work plus willingness to sacrifice pay for meaningful work), reproduces the class pay gap above and beyond education and prestige.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study fuses the sociocultural perspective on social class with research on occupational wage inequality to develop and test a theory of class reproduction through occupational sorting, identifying an unexplored path—differential attainment of autonomous versus prosocial occupations—by which class origin shapes income. It extends research on social class, occupational segregation, and wage inequality by moving class-based autonomy/prosociality differences from non-work behavioral settings into labor markets, examining nearly all occupations in the economy rather than only access to a narrow set of prestigious jobs.

**Practical Implication**
Compensation managers can curb the class pay gap by avoiding overpaying for autonomy and underpaying for prosociality—e.g., using job evaluation of compensable factors and adding accountability and transparency to pay decisions. Managers can limit class-based occupational segregation by prioritizing task performance and structured, job-analysis-based selection. Business schools and collective-voice institutions such as unions are also identified as broader levers.

**Limitations**
Findings may be subject to omitted-variable bias from unmeasured occupational characteristics, and the authors could not directly control for individual cognitive ability. The occupation-level data lack the granularity to capture intra-occupational variation (e.g., where prosocial-titled jobs diverge in practice from their prosocial promise), and the two data sources differ in available controls and sampled populations.

**Future Research**
Future work could establish the extent to which additional variables (e.g., cognitive ability, social capital, personality) explain the class pay gap, and examine whether the proportion of upper- versus lower-class incumbents shapes an occupation's own characteristics over time. The authors also call for studying intra-occupational variation, such as how the gap between an occupation's prosocial promise and its day-to-day realities affects job seekers from different class origins.
