# Investing In Communities: Forging New Ground in Corporate Community Codevelopment through Relational and Psychological Pathways

**Abstract**
Within management scholarship and practice, corporate community investments are heralded as an important form of corporate social responsibility. Yet, community development scholars have engaged in discourse and debate regarding corporate community investment, surmising that in some instances it does more harm than good. Where management research has not focused—and can substantially contribute to both theory and practice—is on how to implement corporate community investment for mutual gain. Integrating the fields of management and community development, I provide an evidence-based theory for understanding corporate community codevelopment, addressing relational and psychological pathways for behavioral change. This model emerged from a longitudinal qualitative ethnographic study of corporate-community investment programs, involving 1,176 hours of observations, 63 interviews, as well as narratives and reflections from participants representing 11 large corporations, at-risk remote Indigenous Australian communities, and a nonprofit organization. The 35 programs in focus involved initiatives such as family income management, educational trusts, literacy programs, and nutritional and substance abuse campaigns. The results in competency-building and community development have exceeded the expectations of all parties. Implications for theory, research, policy, and practice in management and community development are included.

**Research Question**
"How does the implementation of CCI contribute to mutual development in corporations and communities?" The study seeks to resolve the tension between the managerial focus on returns that characterizes the corporate community investment (CCI) literature and the capacity-building emphasis advocated by community development scholars.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering conditions: deep, direct, and ongoing corporate involvement enabled by local embeddedness of corporate secondees and the intentional early exit of the nonprofit broker (Bridge) once programs were underway.
- Relational processes: mutual perspective-taking, reciprocating respect, and communal advocacy (the "connective tissue" between corporations and communities).
- Psychological pathways: identity synergy, dignity, and self-determination (autonomy, competence, relatedness) for Indigenous partners; intercultural competence, meaningfulness, and thriving for corporate secondees.
- Behavioral changes (intermediate): technical/professional skills, proactivity, and strategic capability in communities; intercultural adjustment, prosocial behavior, and informed strategic decisions in corporations.
- Emergent outcomes (longer term): community-wide uplift and social impact (economic participation, educational attainment, community safety), plus reputational and productivity gains and feedback into firms' social-impact strategies.

As corporations and communities collaborated over an extended period, three relational processes each commonly cascaded into specific psychological gains and then behavioral changes among individuals, communities, and corporations. For example, communal advocacy fostered self-determination, which enabled strategic capability; mutual perspective-taking facilitated identity synergy and intercultural competence. These pathways exhibited equifinality and sometimes broadened beyond direct participants—a "cascade of mutual transformation"—shifting CCI from an arm's-length influx of funding toward self-sustaining codevelopment.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops an evidence-based theory of corporate community codevelopment that integrates the management literature on CCI with community development and cross-sector partnerships, identifying the relational and psychological mechanisms that connect investment to lasting behavioral change. It extends self-determination theory by tracing how communal advocacy meets autonomy and competence needs, contributes to community development theory by foregrounding identity synergy (distinct from identity holism or enhancement) and dignity, and documents a previously undocumented mechanism by which cross-sector partnerships produce transformative, cascading change across racial and ethnic lines.

**Practical Implication**
Funding alone is an unreliable CCI strategy; meaningful impact requires deep, ongoing relationships that build local control and autonomy. Practitioners can identify the behavioral outcome of greatest interest and work backward through the pathways—e.g., mutual perspective-taking for skill development, reciprocated respect for proactivity, communal advocacy for strategic capability—using participatory processes such as community visioning, storytelling, and action planning that center community ownership.

**Limitations**
Formal financial analysis was not undertaken because of an insufficient sample size, so business-value claims rest on self-reported gains in loyalty, performance, reputation, and productivity. The approach is unlikely to apply to all types of community development—following Ebrahim and Rangan's contingency framework, simpler change theories (e.g., emergency relief) may warrant a focus on activities rather than longer-term impact—and the findings emerge from a specific Indigenous Australian context.

**Future Research**
Future work should capture each element of the model using longitudinal and quasi-experimental designs that compare codevelopment with more traditional CCI modes and varying program durations. Additional priorities include measuring intermediate community impacts (e.g., strategic capabilities, cultural-heritage maintenance) with multifaceted indicators, examining mutuality in inputs and activities (not just relational processes), and exploring how Bridge's initial brokerage role can be transferred to other settings.
