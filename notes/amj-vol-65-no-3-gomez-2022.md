# The Policy–People Gap: Decision-Makers Choose Policies That Favor Different Applicants than They Select When Making Individual Decisions

**Abstract**
This work documents a contemporary organizational problem—a gap between selection policies and individual selection decisions—and suggests one intervention to address it. In college admissions and workplace hiring contexts, we find that decision-makers are more likely to favor disadvantaged applicants over applicants with objectively higher achievements when choosing between selection policies than choosing between individual applicants. We document this policy–people gap among admissions officers, working professionals, and lay people using both within-subject and between-subject designs and across a range of stimuli. We find that the gap is driven in part by shifting standards of fairness across the two types of decisions. When choosing between individuals, compared to choosing between policies, decision-makers are more likely to prioritize what is fair to individuals (a microjustice standard of fairness) over what is fair in the aggregate (a macrojustice standard of fairness). As a result, an intervention that has decision-makers prioritize the same standard of fairness across the decisions mitigates the policy–people gap. This research helps us understand why decision-makers’ choices so frequently violate espoused organizational policies and suggests one way to increase the representation of disadvantaged groups in organizations.

**Research Question**
Why do decision-makers choose selection policies that favor lower-scoring, disadvantaged applicants yet select higher-scoring, advantaged applicants when making the corresponding individual decisions, and how can this "policy–people gap" be mitigated?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Decision type — policy decision vs. individual decision (manipulated between- and within-subjects); macrojustice-priming intervention vs. control (Study 4).
- DV(s): Choice of the higher-scoring, advantaged option vs. the lower-scoring, disadvantaged option; likelihood-of-choice rating; which option is "fairer" to select.
- Mediators: Relative reliance on microjustice vs. macrojustice standards of fairness (validated scales developed in Study 3).
- Moderators: The macrojustice intervention (moderated mediation in Study 4); education emerged as the only demographic moderator across studies.

Policies are construed at a higher (more abstract) level than individual decisions. Drawing on construal-level theory and the microjustice/macrojustice distinction, the authors theorize that the abstractness of policy decisions shifts decision-makers away from a microjustice standard (what is fair to specific individuals, given their merit) toward a macrojustice standard (what is fair in the aggregate, e.g., equality across groups). Because microjustice favors the higher-achieving applicant and macrojustice favors the disadvantaged applicant, the same person makes systematically divergent choices across the two framings—even when consequences are identical. An intervention asking decision-makers to prioritize their institution's macrojustice standard roughly halved the gap by shifting individual decisions, while leaving policy decisions (already macrojustice-motivated) unchanged.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper identifies and explains a novel, robust "policy–people gap" and offers a fresh account of long-noted principle–implementation gaps: rather than failing to think through implementation or yielding to temptation, decision-makers earnestly pursue fairness in both decisions but rely on different standards of fairness that shift with construal level. It imports the microjustice/macrojustice constructs into organizational selection contexts, develops validated scales to measure them, and links justice scholarship to construal-level theory, preference reversals, and the psychology of policymaking.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations seeking to align espoused selection policies with the individual decisions those policies are meant to guide can deploy a simple, transparent intervention: have decision-makers learn about both standards of fairness and explicitly prioritize the organization's macrojustice standard when making individual choices. This low-touch step reduced the gap by about half and increased selection of disadvantaged applicants, though it did not change decision-makers' underlying beliefs about which choice is fair.

**Limitations**
The findings rest on vignette experiments that maximize experimental control but are low in external validity, and policies were operationalized narrowly (information held constant across policy and individual framings). The studies measured reliance on microjustice/macrojustice but did not directly measure construal level, and they sampled only a subset of possible macrojustice and microjustice principles (e.g., egalitarian and meritocratic ones), leaving open whether the gap generalizes beyond these specific principles.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine the policy–people gap in more naturalistic, higher-external-validity settings, directly test whether construal level is the operative mechanism, and study macrojustice and microjustice across a broader range of contexts (e.g., aid and helping decisions where microjustice may align with need). Researchers are also encouraged to probe how personal characteristics—beyond the education effect observed here—shape the magnitude of the gap.
