# From Sheltered to Included: The Emancipation of Disabled Workers from Benevolent Marginalization

**Abstract**
While there have been several studies on overt forms of marginalization, few have examined benevolent marginalization, where people may unquestioningly participate in their own paternalistic subjugation by following a prescribed identity. How might such individuals end up achieving emancipation from an infantilizing identity? To address this puzzle, we conducted a longitudinal study of a German sheltered workshop, an organization providing employment for disabled people. We observed that workers with disability initially maintained a regulating organizational identity based on paternalistic segregation. However, over time, they constructed their own self-determining identity and coproduced a change in the workshop’s identity from “shelter” to “inclusion.” First, we show that in order to coconstruct their preferred self-concept, benevolently marginalized individuals need to gain the support of those in power, who change their role from a guardian to an ally. Second, while achieving liberation from overt marginalization is likely to involve confrontation with one’s oppressors, we suggest that achieving emancipation from benevolent marginalization is a collaborative process of mutual consciousness raising and sensitization. Third, while changes of identity may occur after exposure to alternative discourses, often involving the contentious performances of external activists, we show how insider activists mobilize collective action for change within a protectionist organization.

**Research Question**
How may benevolently marginalized individuals whose self-concept is tightly regulated by an organizational identity collectively achieve emancipation from that infantilizing identity?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: benevolent marginalization; the "filter of paternalistic benevolence"; a regulating organizational identity ("shelter") vs. an alternative "inclusion" discourse; spoiled/infantilizing identities; the double-edged sword whereby benevolent marginalization makes resistance harder yet is more amenable to non-threatening change.
- Process / phases: (1) Maintaining identity constraints — staff and workers reproduce a paternalistic organizational identity and maintain workers' prescribed "in need of protection" identity; (2) Mobilization of internal activists — placatory measures unexpectedly yield positive lived experiences and "moments of epiphany," an initial group of workers draws on alternative inclusion conceptions to co-construct a self-determining self-concept, then reframes the discourse reassuringly (via "safe spaces" and enabling measures) to overcome remaining workers' resistance; (3) Driving organizational identity change — a critical mass of self-determining workers clashes with the paternalistic identity, prompting both staff and workers to reshape the workshop's identity and structures toward inclusion.
- Triggering conditions: external criticism (UN CRPD activism, media coverage), placatory and enabling measures, confidence-building experiences, redefinition of powerful actors from guardians to allies.
- Emergent outcomes: a self-determining worker identity (~90% of employees) and a reshaped, participative organizational identity that accommodates both inclusion and those who still prefer protection.

Emancipation from benevolent marginalization is theorized as a collaborative, gradual process of "conscientization" (Freire, 1970) rather than confrontation: marginalized actors work *with* rather than against those in power, leveraging differing priorities among powerful actors to recast them as allies through "joint labor." Insider activists animate currents from external emancipatory discourses inside a protectionist organization, frame them as non-threatening identity opportunities, and mobilize collective identity work that ultimately revises the regulating organizational identity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to the organizational identity literature on three fronts. First, it shows how benevolently marginalized individuals can remove or loosen identity constraints by collaborating with powerful actors to change "who we are," offering a more tempered view of regulating identities and a form of emancipation between micro- and macro-emancipation. Second, it illuminates benevolent (rather than overt) marginalization, where subjugation occurs subtly and in solidarity with those in power, so that emancipation is a collaborative process of confidence building, mutual consciousness raising, and sensitization. Third, it foregrounds insider activists who let currents from external discourses permeate a paternalistic organization by reframing those discourses as identity opportunities.

**Practical Implication**
Benevolent marginalization is harder to tackle through legislation than overt discrimination, so practitioners should dismantle protectionist assumptions, shift from "telling" to "listening," and have those in power act as allies offering nondidactic support. Organizations should create enabling mechanisms and "safe spaces," tailor inclusion experiences to individuals' differing impairments and preferences (including the preference to be protected), and educate employers to view disabled workers as valuable employees—"no decision should be made about them without them."

**Limitations**
The findings derive from a single German sheltered workshop, which constrains generalizability across other forms of benevolent marginalization and institutional contexts. The boundary between internal and external activists is not always distinct, and dividing respondents into nondisabled staff and disabled workers is somewhat simplistic given that a few staff also had disabilities, pointing to fluid disability boundaries.

**Future Research**
Examine other forms of benevolent marginalization (e.g., the infantilizing treatment of older adults in care homes exposed by Covid-19 lockdowns) to surface obstacles to emancipation; study activists who are simultaneously insiders and outsiders; and adopt an intersectionality lens, since people define themselves not only through disability but also ethnicity, caste, gender, or age, all of which may compound oppression.
