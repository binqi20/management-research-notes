# “Am I Next?” The Spillover Effects of Mega-Threats on Avoidant Behaviors at Work

**Abstract**
“Mega-threats”—negative, identity-relevant societal events that receive significant media attention—are frequent occurrences in society, yet the influence of these events on employees remains unclear. We draw on the theory of racialized organizations to explain the process whereby exposure to mega-threats leads to heightened avoidant work behaviors for racial minority employees. We theorize and find—across two studies centered upon various mega-threats, including a mass shooting targeting Asian Americans and police killings of Black civilians—that event observers who share identities with mega-threat victims become vicarious victims, which triggers an experience of “embodied threat,” an appraisal of the increased likelihood of personally encountering identity-based harm. The experience of embodied threat coupled with the racialized nature of organizational structures, which limits the agency of racial minorities, then compels employees to engage in threat suppression. Furthermore, we find that threat suppression consumes psychological resources, leading to heightened avoidant work behaviors, or higher work withdrawal and lower social engagement, but, when the psychological safety of identity-based discussions is high, it attenuates this effect. Altogether, our paper advances research on mega-threats and race in organizations, and yields practical insights that can assist managers in reducing the detrimental effects of mega-threats on employees.

**Research Question**
How and why does exposure to "mega-threats"—negative, identity-relevant, heavily publicized societal events—influence the work behaviors of racial-minority employees who share an identity with the events' victims, and under what organizational conditions are those effects attenuated?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Shared identity with mega-threat victim(s) (operationalized as race; 0 = White, 1 = Asian/Black)
- DV(s): Avoidant work behaviors — work withdrawal (higher) and social engagement (lower)
- Mediators: Embodied threat (Mediator 1) → threat suppression (Mediator 2), in serial sequence
- Moderators: Psychological safety of identity-based discussions (attenuates the embodied-threat → threat-suppression link)

When a mega-threat harms a member of one's identity group, observers become "vicarious victims" and, through embodied cognition, experience "embodied threat"—a heightened appraisal that they could personally encounter identity-based harm ("I could be next"). Because organizations are racialized structures that constrain the agency and emotional expression of racial minorities, this threat is suppressed at work rather than expressed. Threat suppression is effortful and depletes psychological resources, so (per conservation of resources theory) employees enact protective avoidance—withdrawing from tasks and disengaging from colleagues. When employees perceive that identity-based discussions are psychologically safe, the need to suppress is reduced, interrupting the deleterious chain.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper centers race in organizational theory by extending Ray's (2019) theory of racialized organizations to explain how macro societal events spill across organizational boundaries to shape individual behavior. It introduces and validates the construct of "embodied threat," distinct from identity threat and stereotype threat in that it concerns the fear of personally encountering identity-based physical harm, including death. It also advances work at the intersection of diversity and psychological safety by identifying the psychological safety of identity-based discussions as a distinct, identity-relevant form of safety.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that mega-threats can cause multitudes of minority employees to suppress threat and withdraw at once, and should increase the psychological safety of identity-based discussions (e.g., through open, honest manager–subordinate relationships and sustained—not merely reactionary—diversity commitments). Organizations can scrutinize racialized norms and routines, and offer flexibility to step away from work after a group-relevant mega-threat so employees can replenish depleted resources.

**Limitations**
The studies rely on online research samples and self-reported measures, raising concerns about common method variance and external validity, and required newly developed scales. All studies centered on mega-threats involving racial-minority victims in the United States, and were constrained to the specific events that occurred during the research period; the Phase 3 (George Floyd) social-engagement effect lost significance after controls, possibly due to COVID-era disruption of workplace interaction.

**Future Research**
Scholars could use experience-sampling or experimental methods to trace the duration and causal structure of mega-threat effects, and examine mega-threats involving other identities (e.g., religious or organizational) and contexts outside the United States. Further work could clarify how mega-threats vary in severity, reach, and cumulative effect, how employees form beliefs about the safety of identity-based discussions, and how mega-threats implicating dominant-group identities differ in their effects.
