# “Invisible” Discrimination: Divergent Outcomes for the Nonprototypicality of Black Women

**Abstract**
By integrating the intersectional invisibility hypothesis with the behaviors from intergroup affect and stereotypes map framework, we examine the extent to which Black women’s dual-subordinated identities render them nonprototypical victims of discrimination, relative to White women and Black men, and the corresponding consequences. We predicted that Black women’s categorical nonprototypicality would reduce the believability of their discrimination claims, but that their nonprototypical attributes would lead to divergent treatment, depending on the type of discrimination alleged. Our predictions were supported across six experimental studies (Studies 1–4b). Specifically, Black women’s gender and racial discrimination claims were believed less compared to those made by White women and Black men, respectively. Moreover, after they alleged discrimination, Black women received less financial remedy versus White women, but more financial remedy versus Black men. Mediation testing revealed that the mechanisms underlying the believability and treatment of Black women were their nonprototypical categorization and attributes. Using discrimination data from the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, Studies 5a and 5b replicated the effects observed on believability and financial remedy. By focusing on nonprototypicality at both categorical and attribute levels, we thus help to disentangle when Black women’s intersectional invisibility may result in either intersectional advantages or disadvantages.

**Research Question**
Why do people tend to exclude Black women from considerations of gender and racial discrimination, and what are the positive and negative implications of this relative "invisibility" for the believability of their discrimination claims and the financial remedy they are awarded?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Race of female claimant (White vs. Black) and gender of Black claimant (man vs. woman); type of discrimination alleged (gender vs. racial)
- DV(s): Categorical prototypicality, believability of discrimination claims, and financial remedy awarded
- Mediators: Categorical (non)prototypicality for believability; prototypical warmth and its elicitation of pity (gender cases) or contempt (racial cases), tested as serial mediators of financial remedy
- Moderators: Axis of discrimination (gender vs. racial) functions as the contingency that flips the direction of the nonprototypicality effect on financial remedy

Black women's dual-subordinated identities make them categorically nonprototypical of both "women" (prototyped by White women) and "Black people" (prototyped by Black men), which depresses the believability of their gender and racial discrimination claims. At the attribute level, the BIAS map predicts that warmth governs helping behavior: because gender-discrimination victims are stereotyped as warm and racial-discrimination victims as cold, Black women's intermediate warmth (lower than White women, higher than Black men) elicits less pity than White women but less contempt than Black men. These divergent emotional reactions translate into less financial remedy than White women in gender cases but more financial remedy than Black men in racial cases — so the same nonprototypicality is simultaneously a disadvantage and an advantage depending on the outcome and the axis of discrimination.

**Theoretical Contribution**
By integrating the intersectional invisibility hypothesis with the BIAS map, the paper distinguishes categorical prototypicality (which drives recognition-based outcomes like believability) from prototypical attributes (which drive support-based outcomes like financial remedy), showing that victim recognition does not necessarily translate into victim support. This integration explains when multiply subordinated individuals experience double jeopardy versus intersectional advantage, reconciling two perspectives prior research has tended to pit against one another and extending intersectional theory into organizationally relevant discrimination contexts.

**Practical Implication**
Initiatives to combat discrimination may inadvertently exclude Black women because their claims are categorically less believable; allies (particularly White allies), accountability-based diversity programs (e.g., diversity committees, mentoring), and tailored efforts addressing intersectional experiences are needed. The authors further argue for structural and legal solutions — recognizing intersectional discrimination claims (per the 2006 EEOC guidance) and amending Title VII to cover combinations of protected bases — so Black women are not forced into single-axis comparisons to White women or Black men.

**Limitations**
Experimental samples were largely homogeneous online convenience samples relying on vignettes, limiting diversity of populations and stimuli. The archival EEOC data lacked variables such as claimants' organizational position or salary that likely affect financial remedy, and the correlational mediation models cannot provide an explicit causal test of categorical prototypicality versus prototypical attributes or rule out alternative explanations such as broader racism and sexism.

**Future Research**
Future work should manipulate the proposed mediators and test the framework among more diverse populations and with a wider array of stimuli, and examine factors such as status and salary that may shape financial remedy. The principles should also be tested in other multiply subordinated groups (e.g., Asian women and men), whose distinct warmth stereotypes may yield different prototypicality and treatment patterns.
