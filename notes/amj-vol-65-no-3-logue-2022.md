# Living Up to the Hype: How New Ventures Manage the Resource and Liability of Future-Oriented Visions within the Nascent Market of Impact Investing

**Abstract**
Hype is a collective vision and promise of a possible future, around which attention, excitement, and expectations increase over time. Within nascent markets, hype can thus serve as a cultural resource by which entrepreneurs might encourage greater early stakeholder support and resources. Yet, as hype-driven support couples with mounting temporal and categorical expectations, this can also limit ventures' flexibility during the entrepreneurial process. Drawing on an inductive, longitudinal, and comparative study of three new ventures within the much-hyped nascent market of impact investing, we develop an emergent theory of hype management, illustrating the field organizing practices that give rise to different forms of social proof, thereby allowing new ventures sufficient flexibility to convert hype into a sustained entrepreneurial opportunity. Our account contributes directly to contemporary public conversations of hype by revealing how entrepreneurs might engage with, and indeed live up to, the hype surrounding nascent markets without succumbing to the deceit and disappointment typically associated with hype. Beyond this, our findings extend existing scholarship on entrepreneurship within nascent markets, the sociology of expectations, and the realization of distant futures.

**Research Question**
How do entrepreneurs in nascent markets engage with and manage hype so as to retain temporal and categorical flexibility? The study asks how new ventures convert the short-term cultural resource of hype into sustained opportunity while managing its long-term relational liabilities.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Hype as a short-term unbounded cultural resource versus a long-term relational liability; temporal flexibility versus categorical flexibility.
- Field-organizing practices: Cultural practices (motivating future state, locating the opportunity, role claiming) and relational practices (configuring boundaries, smoothing exchange, local network brokerage).
- Emergent mechanism — social proof: Practices generate third-party endorsement (varying in salience) and engagement (varying in proximity), yielding salient vs. proximate social proof.
- Boundary conditions / outcomes: Salient social proof increases categorical flexibility but reduces temporal flexibility; proximate social proof increases temporal flexibility but reduces categorical flexibility.

Across the three SSEs (London's SSX, Singapore's IIX, Toronto's SVX), the ventures' differing cultural and relational field-organizing efforts produced distinct forms of social proof. That social proof, rather than scarce material proof (e.g., listings or share-price metrics), is what allowed stakeholders' disappointment to be deferred and shaped the categorical and temporal flexibility each venture retained while attempting to "live up to the hype." The model thus theorizes how entrepreneurs actively stimulate social proof rather than passively receiving it through herding.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops an emergent theory of hype management, theorizing hype at the collective level as a vision of a possible future that is simultaneously a short-term cultural resource and a long-term relational liability, distinct from venture-level promises. It challenges and extends research on social proof by showing that entrepreneurs' own field-organizing efforts can directly generate social proof (rather than it merely emerging from imitative or herding behavior), and by distinguishing salient from proximate social proof and their differential effects on categorical and temporal flexibility. It contributes to scholarship on entrepreneurship in nascent markets, the sociology of expectations, and the realization of distant futures.

**Practical Implication**
For entrepreneurs in hyped nascent markets, the study reveals at least two pathways to navigate hype's relational liabilities: developing salient social proof to gain the categorical flexibility to pivot toward more feasible, less distant possibilities (as with IIX), or producing proximate social proof through deep stakeholder engagement to gain the temporal flexibility to make incremental progress toward the original distant vision (as with SVX). Managing hype through field organizing, rather than only revised storytelling, lengthens and broadens the runway available to deliver on collective visions.

Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
Future research could investigate how ventures attempt to produce different types of social proof at the same time or over time—oscillating between salient and proximate forms—as a way to gain different forms of required flexibility along the entrepreneurial journey, and how such proof types interact alongside material proof as complements rather than substitutes.
