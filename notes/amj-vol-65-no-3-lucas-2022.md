# Remaking Capitalism: The Strength of Weak Legislation in Mobilizing B Corporation Certification

**Abstract**
Myriad cross-sector initiatives seek to remake capitalism into a more just, sustainable, and inclusive system. But how do these distributed efforts—which often vary in strength—interact? To answer this question, we attend to the interaction between weak and strong governance reforms. Drawing on longstanding research on organizational values and the sociology of law, we theorize how the enactment of weak and broad sustainability legislation is likely to increase pressure on values-driven businesses to pursue both values authentication and material authentication by way of strong third-party certification. We test our conceptual model by examining the effects of the frequently criticized benefit corporation legislation passed in 36 U.S. jurisdictions on the related B Corporation certification. We find that new certifications and recertifications both increase in jurisdictions with such legislation and these effects are amplified or attenuated depending on corporate sustainability norms in the region. Taken together, our findings contribute to the intensifying societal conversation regarding the prospects for remaking capitalism, illustrating how even weak legislation can contribute to systems change not only by encouraging incremental sustainability reforms within a field but also by triggering an authentication imperative that mobilizes values-driven businesses to pursue rigorous certifications.

**Research Question**
How does the enactment of weak sustainability legislation (benefit corporation legislation) affect companies' adoption and retention of related, albeit voluntary, B Corporation certification, and under what regional institutional conditions are these effects amplified or attenuated?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Enactment of benefit corporation legislation (BCL); legislative weakness (Model-style vs. weaker Delaware-style BCL); legislative scope (continuous count of 24 statutory criteria addressed)
- DV(s): Initial B Corp certifications (state-year counts); B Corp recertification / decertification (firm-level survival)
- Mediators: Implied "authentication imperative" — values authentication (initial certification) and material authentication (recertification); not separately estimated
- Moderators: Regional corporate sustainability norms (reversed, normalized count of ESG concerns by U.S. Census division)

The authors argue that weak, broad sustainability legislation paradoxically strengthens demand for rigorous voluntary certification. Weak legislation enables low-cost, low-scrutiny affiliation (e.g., the benefit corporation legal form), heightening the risk that non-sustainable firms counterfeit field membership; genuinely values-driven firms respond by seeking strong certification for values authentication. Separately, as the scope of legislation grows, it expands future enforcement opportunities and noncompliance risk, prompting already-certified firms to recertify for material authentication. Regional sustainability norms condition these effects: the values-authentication effect is amplified where norms are weak ("dirty"), whereas the material-authentication effect is amplified where norms are strong ("clean").

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces a systemic, governance-strength perspective on intersecting reforms, parsing legislation into distinct dimensions of strength (unambiguous requirements and strict enforcement) and scope (breadth of regulative requirements) rather than treating legislation as uniformly strong and mandatory. It shows that institutional incompatibility between distributed, imperfectly aligned governance efforts can be generative—even weak legislation can mobilize systems change by triggering an authentication imperative—thereby challenging the assumption that reform efforts must be strong, robust, and aligned to drive change.

**Practical Implication**
For advocates seeking to remake capitalism, "weak" legislation is not necessarily a failure: even ambiguous, lax benefit corporation statutes can catalyze adoption and retention of rigorous certifications like B Corp. Movement organizations such as B Lab can treat legislation and certification as complementary levers, recognizing that legal scaffolding—however imperfect—can mobilize values-driven firms while regional sustainability norms shape where each lever works best.

**Limitations**
The staggered-adoption difference-in-differences design relies on strong assumptions (e.g., quasi-random policy timing), and the proposed values- and material-authentication mechanisms are inferred from differential effects rather than directly measured. The setting is confined to U.S. B Corps and benefit corporation legislation, and some firm-level timing (e.g., exact decertification dates and variable recertification timelines) is imperfectly observed, requiring "certification spell" coding choices.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine intersecting weak and strong reforms in other empirical contexts and governance domains to assess the generalizability of the authentication mechanisms, and could more directly measure the values- and material-authentication processes posited here. Researchers might also explore how additional forms of distributed, overlapping governance interact—reinforcing or undermining broader systems-change efforts—beyond the legislation–certification pairing studied here.
