# Upward Mobility, the Cleft Habitus, and Speaking Up: How Class Transitions Relate to Individual and Organizational Antecedents of Voice

**Abstract**
This research explores the relationship between upward mobility and voice. We build hypotheses and find evidence that rather than being imprinted with a lower sense of efficacy, the upwardly mobile possess a high internal sense of efficacy and are likely to speak up. However, this positive pathway to voice for the upwardly mobile is offset by managers being more inclined to solicit voice from those who come from, and have remained in, higher social class positions. We test our hypotheses in three studies: a field survey, a preregistered analysis of an archival dataset, and a preregistered experiment. This work provides evidence that the internal self-views long associated with those from lower social class backgrounds may not adequately describe the upwardly mobile. Contrary to having a persistent low sense of their abilities, we find that the upwardly mobile espouse high efficacy and do speak up but that managers appear less likely to provide them with equal opportunities for voice, instead seeking it from employees from more elite backgrounds. This work also extends theories of employee voice by showing how managers' decisions about whose input to solicit are influenced by employees' socially significant characteristics in ways that could lead to systematic disadvantages.

**Research Question**
Do the prior lower-class experiences of the upwardly mobile imprint them with a reduced sense of their own abilities and result in less voice, or does upward transition correspond to a high general sense of efficacy that facilitates voice? And do managers view the upwardly mobile as equally valuable sources for input as employees from higher-class backgrounds, or do they provide more opportunities for input to those with elite pedigrees?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Upward mobility / social class background (operationalized via parental and own occupational prestige and education; in Study 3, manipulated class trajectory: upwardly mobile vs. stable middle-class)
- DV(s): Employee voice (Studies 1-2); managerial voice solicitation (Studies 1, 3)
- Mediators: General self-efficacy (individual pathway); managerial voice solicitation (organizational pathway); in Study 3, perceived competence and perceived cultural fit
- Moderators: Evaluator's own social class position (Study 3 additional analysis — competence association strengthens at higher evaluator class)

The paper integrates the "cleft habitus" idea (that class transitions partially rewrite, rather than fully imprint, one's dispositions) with the dual-pathways model of voice. Along the individual pathway, upward mobility coincides with a high internal sense of self-efficacy that motivates the upwardly mobile to overcome fears and speak up. Along the organizational pathway, lingering signals of a lower-class origin lead managers to perceive the upwardly mobile as less competent and worse cultural fits, so managers solicit their input less often. These two pathways operate in opposing directions, such that the upwardly mobile jump over barriers to voice while managers lower the barriers for those from more privileged backgrounds.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The work changes how management theory accounts for class background: rather than imprinting an enduring low sense of initiative, the self-views of the upwardly mobile better reflect their current class position, evidencing high self-efficacy. It extends voice research by shifting attention from how managers respond to or recognize voice toward how they actively *solicit* it, showing that managers use socially significant cues like class signals when deciding whose input to seek — a previously unexamined source of inequity. It also demonstrates that the individual and organizational antecedents of voice, rarely studied jointly, can compete rather than complement one another.

**Practical Implication**
Firms should treat evidence of an upward trajectory as a positive signal of efficacy and other desirable qualities when screening employees or composing teams. Managers should consciously counteract the tendency to rely on elite cultural signals as proxies for quality and make a deliberate effort to solicit input from people who reached the same destination from less privileged origins, helping rebalance disparities in the opportunity to contribute.

**Limitations**
Studies 1 and 2 used correlational designs and cannot establish causal habitus or efficacy change; efficacy spirals leave open whether high-efficacy lower-class individuals are simply the ones who become upwardly mobile. Voice in Studies 1-2 was self-reported, and Study 3's vignette experiment, while causally informative, sacrifices ecological validity. The Study 1 offsetting competing mediations (yielding a nonsignificant total effect) occurred within a single prestigious organization and may not generalize across contexts.

**Future Research**
Future work could disentangle the causal direction between upward mobility and efficacy via performance/efficacy spirals, examine downward mobility, and test whether the competing-pathways dynamic extends to diversity dimensions beyond social class. Researchers could also study what topics people from different class backgrounds speak up about, use more direct (non-self-report) voice measures, and assess managerial solicitation in more ecologically valid settings and in contexts beyond the workplace.
