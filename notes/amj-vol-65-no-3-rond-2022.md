# To Catch a Predator: The Lived Experience of Extreme Practices

**Abstract**
Across several countries, ever-growing societal alarm about the threat of online child sexual exploitation has provoked a controversial civic response: volunteer pedophile hunting teams that expose predators in livestreamed confrontations. Their practices have generated strong criticisms from a police force unsure how to engage them because they lack an empathic understanding of hunters’ lived experience. Through a three-year phenomenological ethnography of a U.K. hunting team, we advance efforts across organization research to theorize the role of lived experience in social action. Specifically, we deploy the phenomenological concept “way-of-being” to explain hunters’ use of extreme practices. Our interpretive account shows how the multiple ways-of-being that characterize the team’s lifeworld suffuse their practices with a complex layered affectivity that is constitutive of the commitment necessary for their persistence. This offers a phenomenological alternative to social psychological models of motivations for vigilantism while also advancing emotions research in organizational institutionalism and practice theory. Practically, our study contributes to the policing challenge of mitigating hunting’s harmful effects by facilitating more constructive mutual engagement. This offers a possible pathway for addressing the broader challenge posed by epistemically closed, social media–enabled communities that act out their concerns in ways that disregard our common humanity.

**Research Question**
Why do pedophile hunters persist with harmful, extreme, and difficult-to-justify practices, and how can a phenomenological account of "lived experience" — rather than dispositional social-psychological models — explain their use of extreme practices? More broadly, how can organization research theorize the role of lived experience in social action so as to engage epistemically closed communities?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: "way-of-being" (Heidegger), "lifeworld," teleoaffectivity, and the layered affectivity of "lived practice"
- Multiple ways-of-being identified: pragmatic, moralistic, narcissistic, and traumatic — with "heroic being" inferred as a fifth way-of-being constituted in the other four
- Identity pairs (authoring self / authoring the other): Protectors of Children vs. Police as Impotent; Community Cleansers vs. Predators as Evil Monsters; Respected Professionals vs. Other Teams as Narcissistic Amateurs; Trauma Survivors vs. Children as Innocents to Be Saved
- Triggering conditions: societal anxiety over CSE, distrust of authorities, and social media lowering the threshold for collective action
- Emergent outcome: complex layered affectivity that insulates hunters against external moral criticism and sustains commitment to extreme practices

The team's phenomenological lifeworld is constituted in its network of practices (decoying, doorknocking, livestreaming). Engaging these practices attunes members to multiple collective ways-of-being that disclose "what is mattering" and bring distinctive thoughts and emotions to the fore. As hunters author identities for themselves and others through the chatroom, these ways-of-being suffuse their practices with a layered affectivity that renders otherwise "puzzling practices" self-evident to them — explaining their persistence and their imperviousness to outside concern.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study develops the phenomenological concept of "way-of-being" as a conceptual vocabulary for theorizing lived experience in social action, offering a phenomenological alternative to social-psychological models that essentialize vigilantes through fixed dispositional traits. It advances emotions research in organizational institutionalism and practice theory by specifying how multiple, layered ways-of-being carry the affective complexity of "lived practice," and contributes to cross-disciplinary vigilantism research by foregrounding experience over disposition and countering psychological essentialization.

**Practical Implication**
By making hunters' lived experience legible, the study offers police a more empathic basis for constructive mutual engagement rather than distancing, helping mitigate hunting's harmful effects on unconvicted suspects and their families. It also suggests a pathway toward increasing hunters' moral reflexivity, and a more general route for bridging the polarizing, existential differences that characterize epistemically closed, social-media-enabled communities.

**Limitations**
The findings derive from a single U.K. hunting team studied phenomenologically, which necessarily limits the generalizability of the account. The interpretations are explicitly value-laden and rest on the ethnographer's preunderstanding, and they inevitably belie the many idiosyncrasies that characterize the lifeworlds studied.

**Future Research**
The authors position their findings as a starting point for developing deeper understanding of the lived experience of epistemically closed, radicalized online communities more generally, and for further developing the concept of lived experience so that organization research can meaningfully contribute to reducing the harmful effects of polarization.
