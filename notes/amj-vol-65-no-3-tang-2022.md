# When Conscientious Employees Meet Intelligent Machines: An Integrative Approach Inspired by Complementarity Theory and Role Theory

**Abstract**
Over the past century, conscientiousness has become seen as the preeminent trait for predicting performance. This consensus is due in part to these employees’ ability to work with traditional 20th-century technology. Such pairings balance the systematic nature of conscientious employees with the technology’s need for user input and direction to perform tasks—resulting in a complementary match. However, the 21st century has seen the incorporation of intelligent machines (e.g., artificial intelligence, robots, and algorithms) into employees’ jobs. Unlike traditional technology, these new machines are equipped with the capability to make decisions autonomously. Thus, their nature overlaps with the orderliness subdimension of conscientious employees—resulting in a non-complementary mismatch. This calls into question whether the consensus about conscientious employees’ effectiveness with 20th-century technology applies to 21st-century jobs. Integrating complementarity and role theory, we refine this consensus. Across three studies using distinct samples (an experience sampling study, a field experiment, and an online experiment from working adults in Malaysia, Taiwan, and the United States), each focused on a different type of intelligent machine, we show not only that using intelligent machines has benefits and consequences, but, importantly, that conscientious (i.e., orderly) employees are less likely to benefit from working with them.

**Research Question**
Does the long-standing consensus that conscientious (specifically, orderly) employees are most effective with workplace technology still hold when employees work with autonomous intelligent machines, and for whom do the benefits versus costs of intelligent machines accrue?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Usage of intelligent machines at work (daily AI usage in Study 1; robotic-work condition in Study 2; "smart" algorithm condition in Study 3)
- DV(s): Task/service performance (coworker report in Study 1, supervisor report in Study 2, independent-judge report in Study 3)
- Mediators: Role breadth self-efficacy; role ambiguity (role conflict modeled as a controlled alternative mechanism)
- Moderators: Conscientiousness (orderliness subdimension)

Drawing on complementarity theory, the authors argue that because intelligent machines autonomously create order and structure workflows, they form a non-complementary mismatch with orderly employees, who prefer to direct work themselves and are therefore less likely to view these machines as credible. Through a role-theory lens, this mismatch weakens orderly employees' role breadth self-efficacy and heightens their role ambiguity, which in turn shape task performance. The negative indirect effect via role ambiguity was supported across all three studies (stronger at higher orderliness), whereas the role breadth self-efficacy path was supported only in Study 3.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper refines the established consensus on conscientiousness by showing it requires contextualization to the type of technology employed: orderly employees may experience reduced efficacy and heightened ambiguity with intelligent machines. It identifies orderliness as the moderator answering "for whom" intelligent machines help versus hurt, extends complementarity theory beyond human-to-human interactions to human-machine interactions, and returns role theory to the study of how modern technology reshapes role perceptions.

**Practical Implication**
Managers may wish not to emphasize conscientiousness when recruiting for jobs involving intelligent machines, and to pair such machines with employees lower in conscientiousness (orderliness) where possible, since these employees may be better positioned to benefit. The authors also suggest including work-sample tasks pairing candidates with intelligent machines during selection.

**Limitations**
Studies 1 and 2 each drew on a single organization in Eastern cultural contexts, limiting generalizability, and Study 3 relied on an online sample. Study 1 used shortened scales and coworker performance ratings, and several hypotheses (the role breadth self-efficacy path) went unsupported in Studies 1 and 2, with the authors noting moderating effects are harder to detect in field settings.

**Future Research**
Future work should identify boundary conditions such as the nature of the job and whether intelligent machines offer high-quality or user-friendly output, develop ways to classify the richness of the user experience with intelligent machines, and examine how the employee-machine working relationship varies across machine types, jobs, and industries, including via a trait-activation lens.
