# The Impostor Phenomenon Revisited: Examining the Relationship between Workplace Impostor Thoughts and Interpersonal Effectiveness at Work

**Abstract**
Prevailing wisdom paints the impostor phenomenon as detrimental. I seek to rebalance the existing conversation around this phenomenon by highlighting that it may also have interpersonal benefits. To identify these benefits, I revisit seminal theorizing to advance the construct of workplace impostor thoughts which I define as the belief that others overestimate one's competence at work. Incorporating theory on contingencies of self-worth, I present an integrative model that outlines why such thoughts may be positively associated with other-perceived interpersonal effectiveness and why they may not be. I test my theory across four studies (n 5 3,603) that feature survey, video, and pre-registered experimental data. I find that employees who more frequently have such thoughts are evaluated as more interpersonally effective because they adopt a more other-focused orientation. I do not find that this interpersonal benefit comes at the expense of competence-related outcomes (i.e., performance, selection)—a point I revisit in my future directions. When examining my theorized competing pathway, I find that, whereas workplace impostor thoughts do encourage those who have them to self-handicap—consistent with prevailing wisdom—such thoughts do not operate through self-handicapping to harm other-perceived interpersonal effectiveness. I conclude by situating my findings alongside prior work.

**Research Question**
Are workplace impostor thoughts — the belief that others overestimate one's competence at work — associated with greater (not just lesser) other-perceived interpersonal effectiveness, and if so, through what mechanism, and does that interpersonal benefit come at the expense of competence-related outcomes?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Workplace impostor thoughts (measured via a validated five-item self-report scale; manipulated via recall in the two pre-registered experiments).
- DV(s): Other-perceived interpersonal effectiveness (rated by supervisors, standardized patients, or yoked evaluators with hiring/supervisory experience).
- Mediators: Other-focused orientation (the proposed primary defensive response; measured via video coding and behavioral question-asking counts); self-esteem and other-directed positive affect (serial micro-mechanism upstream of other-focused orientation).
- Moderators: None formally tested; self-handicapping is modeled as a competing (alternative) mediator rather than a moderator, and self-presentational concerns, self-monitoring, self-promotion, and self-denigration are examined as alternative explanations / mechanisms.

Drawing on contingencies-of-self-worth theory, the paper argues that workplace impostor thoughts threaten the self-esteem of people who stake their self-worth on competence, prompting a "domain-switching" response: rather than turning inward, they shift effort toward the interpersonal domain by adopting a more other-focused orientation (attending to, asking questions of, and showing care for others). Because perceivers reward these verbal, paraverbal, and nonverbal behaviors, the other-focused orientation translates into higher other-rated interpersonal effectiveness. A competing self-handicapping pathway is theorized but is not found to harm interpersonal effectiveness. The positive indirect effect through other-focused orientation held across all four studies, even though a significant total effect appeared only in the two field studies.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconceptualizes the impostor phenomenon — typically treated as an affective, uniformly harmful experience — as a sociocognitive construct ("workplace impostor thoughts"), recovering a "forgotten" conceptualization and supplying a validated measure. It identifies other-focused orientation as the previously missing mechanism that explains why such thoughts can have an interpersonal upside, integrating contingencies-of-self-worth theory with impostor-phenomenon theory to show that threatened self-esteem can prompt people to turn outward rather than inward. In doing so it resolves a latent tension in prior literature between assertions that the phenomenon damages relationships and passing hints that impostors are seen as socially adept and likable.

**Practical Implication**
The findings caution against blanket practitioner advice to "overcome" or "get rid of" impostor feelings: because the same thoughts can foster an other-focused orientation that others reward, guidance should be more nuanced and attend to the workplace context. Interventions such as cognitive reappraisal may help individuals down-regulate the negative emotional undertones while preserving potential interpersonal benefits, but managers should weigh the phenomenon's net effect — especially the degree to which a role is socially embedded — before deciding whether "overcoming" advice is appropriate.

**Limitations**
The supplementary competence-related null effects (no significant harm to performance or selection) should not be overgeneralized, as the studies were not designed primarily to detect such downstream effects, and the competence-forward interview setting in the experiments may have constrained both the interpersonal upside (no significant total effect in Studies 3A and 3B) and the ability to observe negative spillovers. Self-esteem did not significantly mediate the path to other-focused orientation in Study 3B (only a serial path through other-directed positive affect did), and several measures relied on single sources or small subsamples (e.g., 45 coded videos in Study 2).

**Future Research**
Future work could map boundary conditions on the interpersonal upside — for example, whether power attenuates it (greater power increases self-focus) and whether the degree of a job's social embeddedness amplifies or constrains domain-switching. Scholars could examine gender as a moderator of effects rather than as an antecedent, search for additional alternative or competing mechanisms beyond those measured (self-handicapping, self-denigration, self-promotion), and consider the phenomenon's simultaneous "net" effects across interpersonal, competence-related, and well-being outcomes.
