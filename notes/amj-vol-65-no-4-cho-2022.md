# How Work Orientation Impacts Objective Career Outcomes via Managerial (Mis)perceptions

**Abstract**
Views differ on whether individuals with a calling orientation toward work (i.e., seeing work as personally fulfilling and contributing to a better world) enjoy more favorable objective career outcomes, such as higher income and chance of promotion, versus those with a job orientation (i.e., seeing work as a means to a financial end). We suggest that the impasse is partially due to prior research's exclusive focus on how work orientation affects one's effort and subsequent job performance. Drawing on theories of signaling, cognitive biases, and reciprocity, we propose that calling-oriented employees enjoy better objective career outcomes than job-oriented employees via an external pathway: managers misperceive employees' calling orientation as evidence of better performance and stronger organizational commitment. In Study 1—analyses of the Wisconsin Longitudinal Study—we find support for the main effect, and in Study 2—an online experiment—we constructively replicate this effect and find evidence for our predicted explanatory mechanisms. Furthermore, observing a calling-oriented employee prompts managers to perceive them more favorably in other domains, creating a halo effect. Our research sheds light on how individuals' subjective view of the meaning of work influences their objective career success, highlighting workplace signals and managerial perceptions as important mechanisms.

**Research Question**
How does an employee's work orientation (calling vs. job) relate to their objective career outcomes (income, raises, bonuses, promotion), and does this relationship operate through an external pathway in which managers (mis)perceive calling-oriented employees as higher performing and more organizationally committed?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee work orientation (calling vs. job orientation; control condition added in Study 2)
- DV(s): Objective career outcomes — total wages/income (Study 1); bonus, raise percentage, and promotion support (Study 2)
- Mediators: Managers' perceived employee job performance; managers' perceived organizational commitment
- Moderators: None formally tested (paper instead specifies boundary conditions, e.g., contexts dominated by one orientation, purely objective-metric reward systems, expert vs. nonexpert evaluators)

Because calling- and job-oriented employees emit different observable workplace signals, managers draw inferences (per signaling theory) and, through common cognitive biases (e.g., availability bias, fallacy of association), misperceive calling-oriented employees as better performers and more committed to the organization—even when objective performance and commitment are held constant. Per the theory of reciprocity, managers then repay these favorable perceptions with greater financial rewards and stronger promotion endorsement. Study 1 shows calling-oriented WLS respondents earn higher wages; Study 2 experimentally demonstrates the perceptual mediation and a halo effect (calling-oriented "Sam" is also seen as more passionate, intrinsically motivated, and positive despite expert-validated objective equivalence).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper redirects the meaning-of-work and work-orientation literatures away from their exclusive focus on the internal pathway (how orientation shapes one's own effort and performance) by theorizing and demonstrating an external, organizational pathway operating through managers' misperceptions of performance and commitment. It also conceptually and empirically differentiates calling orientation from passion, and uncovers a novel way passion itself may be (mis)perceived when physical manifestations of passion are held constant.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that their judgments of subordinates rest partly on employees' verbal expressions and visible behaviors and may be inconsistent with employees' actual psychological and behavioral states, yet still drive consequential outcomes like income and promotion. Job-oriented employees, even when objectively comparable in performance and commitment, may earn less and receive weaker promotion support than calling-oriented peers.

**Limitations**
Both studies have empirical limits: Study 1's WLS categorical work-orientation measure cannot capture intensity, and the data lack precise measures of job performance or organizational commitment, while work orientation was measured only in 2004 (limiting causal claims about reverse causality). Study 2's manipulation bundled four behavioral indicators at once (precluding isolation of any single indicator), may not have fully held passion constant, relied on short one-off video vignettes rather than repeated real-life interactions, and used a control condition that may have under-supplied performance/passion cues.

**Future Research**
Future work should collect richer data on performance and commitment, isolate which behavioral signals drive perceptions, and disentangle a possible "calling orientation premium" from a "job orientation penalty." Scholars are encouraged to study why the bias-to-reward asymmetry arises, why organizational commitment mediates bonuses but not raises/promotions, the antecedents/consequences of interpersonal congruence of passion, the neglected career orientation, and whether rising income reshapes work orientation over time (including crowding-out effects).
