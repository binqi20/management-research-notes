# Who Will Stay When Crisis Strikes? Venture Capitalist Threat-Defiant Learning Response to Ventures in Crisis

**Abstract**
Firms often depend on relationships with other firms to access important resources. When a crisis strikes a firm, the continuation of those relationships can be jeopardized, as many indirectly affected firms will leave the relationship. This abandonment can be particularly harmful if the crisis-struck firm is dependent on the relationship for valuable resources. However, some firms choose to continue their relationship with the crisis-struck firm. We consider what makes those firms resist the temptation to respond rigidly and abandon the crisis-struck firm. We contend that firms that defy such indirect threats do so as a result of threat-defiant learning, which stems from confidence gained from their prior relational experiences. We test this concept in the context of venture capital firms' (VCs) investments after venture crises in the form of venture involvement in a lawsuit. Studying 435 VCs' decisions on whether to continue to invest in 105 ventures experiencing lawsuits, we find support for threat-defiant learning. This study contributes to the literature on crisis, threat rigidity, and entrepreneurship by showing how firms can resist threat rigidity in interfirm relationships, specifically how VCs learn from experience to defy threats posed by venture lawsuits and make post-lawsuit follow-on investments.

**Research Question**
How does learning from prior experience affect a firm's decision whether to continue an interfirm relationship when the other firm faces a crisis? Empirically: what forms of VC experience make a VC threat defiant and likely to make a follow-on investment in a venture facing the crisis of a lawsuit, rather than responding rigidly and abandoning it?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): VC overall (general) investment experience; VC focal-venture experience (rounds invested in the focal venture); VC lawsuit experience (depreciated count of own prior lawsuits)
- DV(s): VC investment after lawsuit (dichotomous — whether the VC made a follow-on investment in the first round after the venture's lawsuit was filed)
- Mediators: Threat-defiant learning / confidence in own abilities, the focal venture, and crisis-response capacity (theorized, not directly measured)
- Moderators: VC defendant experience (more negative own-crisis experience, weakens the lawsuit-experience effect); VC settlement experience (less negative own-crisis experience, strengthens it)

Crises act as indirect threats that normally provoke threat-rigidity (conservative, standardized responses and relationship abandonment). The authors theorize "threat-defiant learning": through experience with three relational systems — similar interfirm relationships, the focal venture, and the VC's own prior lawsuits — VCs gain confidence in their ability to control and withstand the crisis, enabling them to dismiss, challenge, or attack the threat (Oliver's defiance) and continue investing. The negativity of the VC's own crisis experience is a boundary condition: defendant experience erodes identification and confidence, whereas settlement experience builds it.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops and labels the concept of threat-defiant learning, integrating the threat-rigidity hypothesis with the organizational-learning-from-crisis and relational (post-traumatic growth) literatures to explain how firms resist rigidity in the face of indirect crises affecting a partner firm. It extends learning-from-crisis theory beyond a firm's own future crisis management to firms' responses to external, indirect threats, and contributes to entrepreneurship by showing how distinct forms of VC experience — and the negativity of that experience — shape follow-on investment in ventures in crisis.

**Practical Implication**
VCs should recognize whether their decision to continue or abandon a ventureship in crisis reflects a rigid versus defiant response, since poor management of a relationship in crisis can impair a firm's ability to attract future partners. Entrepreneurs are alerted to investigate VCs' crisis and lawsuit histories and to build deeper relationships with experienced VCs, prioritizing those less likely to abandon the venture when crisis strikes.

**Limitations**
The authors could not assess every aspect of VC experience and lawsuit characteristics and did not model possible interactions between VC experience and venture/lawsuit attributes. Learning was not measured directly; only its behavioral effects were captured. The qualitative post hoc analysis was limited, and the study relied on federal (not state) lawsuit data due to data-availability constraints.

**Future Research**
Future work could analyze additional types of VC experience, specific lawsuit outcomes and amounts at issue, and their interactions; replicate the findings in state courts as recordkeeping improves; use richer qualitative designs to find direct evidence of the theorized learning; study other venture crises beyond lawsuits; and examine the conditions under which a defiance response outperforms a rigid one, including for direct crises and how rigidity/defiance affects ventures' subsequent success.
