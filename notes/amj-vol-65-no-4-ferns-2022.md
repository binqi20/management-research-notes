# The Analogical Construction of Stigma as a Moral Dualism: The Case of the Fossil Fuel Divestment Movement

**Abstract**
We explore the global fossil fuel divestment movement to show how climate activists worked to stigmatize the fossil fuel industry using analogy. In doing so, we develop a model that illustrates how constructing a “moral dualism” is central to stigmatizing an organizational category. This involves concurrently establishing “stigmatizers” (ingroup) as morally superior and amplifying the deviancy of the fossil fuel industry (outgroup), both in relation to analogical contexts. Stigmatizers strategically employed two types of analogy: “deep” and “surface.” Deep analogies produce emotive power, facilitating the moralization of the ingroup through the transfer of affective meanings from a source context to a target domain. Surface analogies generate causal power to inform wider audiences of the target’s deviance through association with already-stigmatized organizational categories. Analogical power underpinning the morally dualistic nature of stigmatization can therefore empower fringe actors to stigmatize an incumbent as they appropriate meanings from analogical source domains.

**Research Question**
How do actors strategically use analogy to stigmatize an organizational category? The study foregrounds the under-examined "stigmatizers' perspective," asking how fringe actors shift meanings of an organizational category from "normal" to "fundamentally flawed."

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: *Moral dualism* — a "clear-cut and decisive contrast" between a moral ingroup (stigmatizers) and an immoral outgroup (the stigmatized target), with "no middle ground."
- Two analogical forms: *Deep* analogies (e.g., South African apartheid) generate emotive power and moralize the ingroup; *surface* analogies (e.g., tobacco) generate causal power and amplify the target's deviance.
- Process / phases: (1) *Initial framing* — problem articulation and solution proposal; (2) *Establishing ingroup morality* — virtue transfer and affective association (deep analogies); (3) *Amplifying outgroup deviance* — instituting commonalities and reinforcing negative traits (surface analogies); (4) *Stigma uptake and dispersion* — proximal adoption and distanced enactment by stakeholders.
- Emergent outcome: a self-reinforcing moral dualism that disperses stigmatizing associations across diverse stakeholder audiences (faith-based, healthcare, policy, financial actors).

Stigmatization is theorized as a discursive, relational process: activists do not "start from scratch" but appropriate already-stigmatized meanings from analogical source domains, simultaneously moralizing themselves and demonizing the target. Deep analogies transfer affective, virtue-laden meaning (justice, political struggle, morality) to build a moral "we," while surface analogies build intuitive cause-and-effect links (disease, death, deception) that frame the fossil fuel industry as existentially flawed. The interdependence of the two analogical forms supplies both an emotional and a logical imperative that drives—and spreads—stigmatizing action.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper's central contribution is to show that stigmatization is contingent on constructing a moral dualism in which stigmatizers actively build their own moral superiority alongside the target's deviance—an inversion of stigma research that has focused on the stigmatized rather than the stigmatizers. It extends analogy scholarship (notably Glaser et al., 2016) by distinguishing deep and surface analogies and showing how they work interdependently to amplify inferential power, and it reframes stigma as potentially *empowering* for fringe actors who use it to loosen an incumbent's grip on a field-level grand challenge.

**Practical Implication**
The moral-dualism model offers fringe actors a transferable playbook: to stigmatize an incumbent, they must clearly delineate a moralized ingroup and a vilified outgroup, and "borrow" meanings from already-stigmatized analogical domains. The authors suggest the approach could help address grand challenges (e.g., stigmatizing high-carbon industries such as beef or shipping) and may illuminate why movements like Occupy Wall Street relatively failed to stigmatize the financial sector—because they did less work to establish their own morality.

**Limitations**
Because the focus was on stigmatizers rather than the stigmatized, the fossil fuel industry's reactions to and defenses against stigmatization were not extensively analyzed. The study took a broad view of "analogy" rather than dissecting specific tropes (metaphor, synecdoche, irony), and although visual/material artifacts were incorporated where relevant, multimodality (videos, images, social media) was not the analytic focus.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how incumbent actors defend against stigmatization by contesting fringe actors' analogical construction of morality; on the specific forms of analogy (metaphor, synecdoche, irony) used throughout stigmatization efforts; and on multimodal analogical work (videos and images), especially on social media. They also suggest the moral-dualism lens may extend to other social evaluations and to delegitimation work more broadly.
