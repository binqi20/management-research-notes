# Forming Entrepreneurial Teams: Mixing Business and Friendship to Create Transactive Memory Systems for Enhanced Success

**Abstract**
Successfully navigating critical uncertainties during the incipient stages of new ventures requires these ventures to develop learning systems, and building the right team may be key in this process. Drawing on prior work indicating that entrepreneurial teams form using either an interpersonal-attraction strategy (relationships with similar others in a close network) or a resource-seeking strategy (instrumental focus on complementary skills), we theorize that a dual formation strategy, although challenging to execute, is critical for early performance. Using dual formation strategies from the onset fosters the development of stronger transactive memory systems, because close relationships facilitate smooth coordination among founders specializing in complementary tasks. Transactive memory systems thus mediate the relationship between formation strategies and early entrepreneurial success. Findings from two field observational studies and a field intervention study support our theory: teams formed based on a dual strategy raised greater seed funding on Kickstarter—a leading crowdfunding platform—(Study 1), were more successful in a prestigious entrepreneurial competition (Study 2), and gained more profits from selling their initial products (Study 3). Our research advances knowledge on entrepreneurial team formation and offers practical recommendations to facilitate this process at such nascent, but critical, stages.

**Research Question**
How and why do the initial strategies that founders use to form new venture teams—interpersonal attraction, resource-seeking, or a dual combination of both—shape early entrepreneurial success, and what learning mechanism explains this effect?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Initial team formation strategy (dual strategy vs. interpersonal-attraction-alone vs. resource-seeking-alone); in Study 3, fully crossed manipulations of high/low interpersonal attraction and high/low resource-seeking.
- DV(s): Early entrepreneurial success (Study 1: Kickstarter seed funding amount and number of backers; Study 2: entering and surviving in the competition; Study 3: team profits from initial product sales).
- Mediators: Transactive memory systems (the shared structural and process system for encoding, storing, and retrieving "who knows what" on the team).
- Moderators: In Study 3, interpersonal attraction and resource-seeking moderate each other (transactive memory systems strengthen only when both are high).

Forming a team with attention to both relational closeness and complementary expertise from the onset lets founders leverage each strategy's selection criteria in their earliest interactions, so that familiar cofounders who also bring complementary knowledge can quickly establish role structure, trust, and coordination. This accelerates the emergence of stronger transactive memory systems, which in turn enable the team to match members to tasks, integrate diverse expertise, and navigate critical early milestones. Because the dual strategy is valuable, rare, and difficult to imitate (if not adopted at the onset), it provides a source of early competitive advantage, with transactive memory systems mediating the link between formation strategy and success.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates the macro entrepreneurship literature on team formation with the micro teams-and-learning literature to identify transactive memory systems as a novel mechanism through which initial formation strategies drive early entrepreneurial success. It extends entrepreneurial team formation research by theorizing and testing the value, rarity, and inimitability of a dual strategy, and contributes to the team-learning literature by reconciling mixed findings on prior familiarity—showing that familiarity aids transactive memory systems only when members also bring different expertise.

**Practical Implication**
Aspiring founders should attend to team assembly early, deliberately overcoming the tendency to search only relationally or only instrumentally, and proactively seek cofounders who offer both close relations and complementary skills. Entrepreneurial education programs, accelerators, and cofounder-matching platforms should teach partner-selection processes alongside business-model training, and investors should prioritize teams that combine close relationships with diverse knowledge.

**Limitations**
In Study 3, the dual strategy affected success only indirectly through transactive memory systems (not directly as in Studies 1 and 2), possibly because experimental assignment constrained variance relative to naturally formed teams. Study 1's sample could only include teams that voluntarily disclosed their formation strategy on their campaign, raising potential selection bias and the possibility that disclosed strategies differ from actually employed ones.

**Future Research**
Future work could disentangle self- versus facilitated selection and separate selection from training effects in entrepreneurial programs; examine other team processes (e.g., shared mental models) and external-stakeholder dynamics alongside transactive memory systems; and test whether dual strategies applied within each member versus across different members differentially accelerate transactive memory system emergence. Researchers might also explore the antecedents of formation strategies and their long-term effects at later growth stages.
