# Ritual Work and the Formation of a Shared Sense of Meaningfulness

**Abstract**
Drawing from a 21-month field study of an athletic footwear and apparel company, I build theory on how a shared sense of meaningfulness forms through interaction ritual and emotion. I find that a shared sense of meaningfulness is composed of a shared feeling and a shared representation that are constructed sequentially. Leaders trigger and propel formation through a set of actions I refer to as ritual work that proliferates emotional energy across the population of members. Members subsequently share their feelings of emotional energy through talk and gesture to develop a shared feeling of meaningfulness. Leaders then articulate words and connect these words to shared feelings to produce a shared representation of meaningfulness. I integrate these findings into a three-step process model. This model contributes to our understanding of the triggers, cultural mechanisms, and role of emotions in the formation of meaningfulness in organizations.

**Research Question**
How does a shared sense of meaningfulness toward an organization's work and activities emerge and form from members' shared experiences, and what actions, actors, and resources trigger and propel that formation?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition (Step 1 — Proliferating emotional energy): leaders perform *ritual work* — comprising *material production* (creating space, buying objects, hiring instructors), *mobilizing assembly*, and *inclusion practices* — that creates and scales an *organizational interaction ritual* ("Liven," a communal workout) with barriers to outsiders, shared attention, and shared mood.
- Process / phases (Step 2 — Sharing emotional energy): members display and talk about emotional energy through talk and gesture, growing participation and producing a *shared feeling of meaningfulness*.
- Emergent outcome (Step 3 — Representing shared feeling): leaders articulate significance and connect words to the shared feeling, yielding a *shared representation of meaningfulness*.
- Boundary conditions: physical coassembly is possible; leaders do not initially trigger formation through deliberate top–down communication; the focal activity resonates with broader cultural scripts and is conducive to rhythmic synchronization.

Effective interaction rituals "pump participants up with emotional energy"; ritual work assembles members and propels the flow of bodies through these situations, so that personal emotional energy, once shared, constitutes a collective feeling that leaders subsequently endow with symbolic representation. The model recasts leaders as "gatherers" rather than only rhetoricians, and treats meaningfulness as emerging from felt experience before it is cognitively articulated.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper expands understanding of the *triggers* of meaningfulness by showing formation can be triggered unintentionally through ritual work, in contrast to prior theory emphasizing deliberate top–down rhetoric and visioning. It identifies new *cultural mechanisms* residing at the level of situations — materiality and inclusion practices that assemble members and facilitate their flow through interaction rituals. It advances the *role of emotions*, offering a correction to predominantly cognitive views by showing meaningfulness has an emotional component (a shared feeling) that can precede and justify its cognitive representation.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations seeking to cultivate a shared sense that "what we do" is meaningful can do so by investing in ritual work — providing spaces, objects, and people, and using mobilizing assembly and inclusion practices to draw the maximum number of members into a recurring, activity-centered interaction ritual — rather than relying first on leaders' communication. Starting with ritual (rather than cheap words) may make later claims of purpose feel authentic rather than opportunistic, though it carries financial and opportunity costs.

**Limitations**
As a single deviant case at "FitCo," the theory generalizes to organizational settings with similar features (e.g., leaders who do not initially communicate to trigger formation; members able to physically assemble) and may hold less relevance for distributed or virtual organizations where physical coassembly cannot occur frequently. The author cannot definitively rule out that the emotional energy observed was simply the hormonal effect of intense exercise, though other research finds non-exercise rituals also produce emotional energy.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine whether and how a shared sense of meaningfulness forms in virtual/distributed settings; whether exercise versus other activities is more or less effective at synchronizing members; how other forms of energy (e.g., adrenaline, caffeine) boost or inhibit ritual outcomes; the tradeoffs of starting with communication versus ritual work; and the conditions under which forming meaningfulness through ritual work generates feelings of exclusion or marginalization.
