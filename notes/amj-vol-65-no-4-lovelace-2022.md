# The Push and Pull of Attaining CEO Celebrity: A Media Routines Perspective

**Abstract**
Why do some CEOs become celebrities, while others with seemingly equal accomplishments do not? After two decades of research, far more is known about the substantial consequences of CEO celebrity than about its determinants. Drawing on the media routines literature, we develop and test a “push–pull theory” of CEO celebrity attainment, arguing and finding that (a) journalists pull certain CEOs into the limelight, particularly those whose firms are nonconformists within their industries and who themselves are demographically atypical; (b) CEOs, through various self-promotion tactics, can push themselves and their stories into public view; and (c) these push tactics are particularly beneficial for helping atypical CEOs achieve celebrity. We conceptualize CEO celebrity as an ordinal construct with discrete gradations, which we refer to as noncelebrities, B-list celebrities, and A-list celebrities. We test our theory using a longitudinal sample of CEOs, and develop a novel, ordinal measure of CEO celebrity that encompasses a broad array of media (newspaper, broadcast, magazine, and online), allowing us to set forth and test a more nuanced theory about different levels of celebrity attainment.

**Research Question**
Why do some CEOs become celebrities while others with seemingly equal accomplishments do not? Specifically, what "pull" factors (firm and CEO distinctiveness) and "push" tactics (CEO self-promotion) — operating through journalists' media routines — drive a CEO's degree of celebrity attainment (B-list and, beyond that, A-list)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm strategic nonconformity (standardized absolute deviation of eight resource-allocation indicators from industry means); CEO demographic atypicality (dummy = 1 if CEO is non-Caucasian or female); CEO self-promotion / push tactics (standardized sum of CEO mentions in firm press releases and tweets).
- DV(s): CEO celebrity, an ordinal three-category construct (noncelebrity = 0, B-list = 1, A-list = 2), built from 11 volume-of-attention indicators multiplied by a positive-valence (emotional resonance) score and binned annually by quartile/decile cutoffs.
- Mediators: None modeled (media routines are the theorized but unmeasured channel translating push and pull into coverage).
- Moderators: CEO self-promotion (push tactics) is hypothesized to amplify the effects of strategic nonconformity (H4) and demographic atypicality (H5) on reaching the A-list.
- Controls: Firm size, firm performance (industry-adjusted ROA, TSR), firm/CEO presuccession visibility, total press releases, total tweets, CEO tenure, year and industry dummies.

Theorizing from media routines, the authors argue journalists — facing time, information, and audience constraints — "pull" distinctive CEOs (nonconformist firms, demographically atypical leaders) into heroic narratives because they make compelling, newsworthy stories, while CEOs can "push" themselves into coverage by supplying information subsidies and self-promotion that ease journalists' production. Ordered probit results support H1 (strategic nonconformity), H2 (demographic atypicality), and H3 (self-promotion) as positive predictors of celebrity; H5 is supported (self-promotion amplifies atypicality's effect, especially for the A-list) but H4 (self-promotion × nonconformity) is not.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts CEO celebrity research from consequences to antecedents, introducing a media-routines-grounded "push–pull theory" that distinguishes who CEOs are (demographic atypicality) from what they do (strategic nonconformity and self-promotion). It reconceptualizes celebrity as an ordinal construct (noncelebrity, B-list, A-list) rather than a binary, and contributes a comprehensive multimedia, valence-weighted ordinal measure spanning newspaper, magazine, broadcast, and online sources.

**Practical Implication**
For CEOs aspiring to celebrity, distinctive personal attributes matter more than self-promotion or strategic actions alone, and combining high self-promotion with demographic atypicality is especially potent for reaching the A-list. Boards should calibrate vigilance to a CEO's self-promotion and distinctiveness, given celebrity's documented risks (complacency, hubris, backlash) for firms.

**Limitations**
The sample is restricted to newly appointed CEOs, excluding longer-serving incumbents who were already celebrities. Modified state-based sampling (oversampling WSJ-covered CEOs) means the sample may differ from a random one, biasing effect magnitudes, so the authors interpret only sign and significance, not effect sizes. The celebrity measure equally weights newspaper, magazine, broadcast, and Internet sources, and the nonconformity measure treats actions exceeding or lagging industry norms equivalently.

**Future Research**
Investigate the motivational mechanisms (e.g., narcissism versus altruistic or firm-promoting motives) driving CEO self-promotion, and how self-promotion shapes susceptibility to backlash when strategic efforts fail. Use the ordinal measure to study trajectories or "arcs" of celebrity — whether levels are attained gradually or abruptly and how celebrity persists or decays. Examine whether more specialized or business-oriented media, or directional (aggressive vs. conservative) nonconformity, yield different interactive effects.
