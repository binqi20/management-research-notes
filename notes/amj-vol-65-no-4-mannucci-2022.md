# “Who Are You Going to Call?” Network Activation in Creative Idea Generation and Elaboration

**Abstract**
Considering creativity as a journey beyond idea generation, scholars have theorized that different ties are beneficial in different phases. As individuals usually possess different types of ties, selecting the optimal ties in each phase and changing ties as needed are central activities for creative success. We identify the types of ties (weak or strong) that are helpful in idea generation and idea elaboration and, given this understanding, whether individuals activate ties in each phase accordingly. In an experimental study of individuals conversing with their ties, we provide evidence of the causal effects of weak and strong ties on idea generation and idea elaboration. We also find that individuals do not always activate ties optimally and identify network size and risk as barriers. Our results in a series of studies reveal that individuals with large networks, despite providing more opportunity to activate both strong and weak ties, activate fewer weak ties and are less likely to switch ties across phases than individuals with smaller networks, particularly when creativity is perceived as a high-risk endeavor. Finally, we find that activating the wrong ties leads to either dropping creative ideas or pursuing uncreative ones.

**Research Question**
Which types of ties (weak or strong) are helpful in different phases of the idea journey (generation vs. elaboration), and—given this—what helps or hinders individuals from activating the appropriate ties in each phase and switching ties between phases?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Tie strength activated (weak vs. strong); network size (small vs. large); idea-journey phase (generation vs. elaboration); tie switching vs. not switching across phases.
- DV(s): Creativity of the elaborated idea; proportion of weak ties activated; likelihood of switching ties; likelihood of dropping/pursuing the idea (false negatives vs. false positives).
- Mediators: Conversation behaviors of alters—"sharing" new ideas vs. "building" on the creator's idea (moderated mediation of the tie-strength → creativity link, differing by phase).
- Moderators: Perceived risk (high vs. low) conditions the effect of network size on weak-tie activation and on tie switching.

Weak ties supply non-redundant knowledge and cognitive flexibility that aid idea generation, whereas strong ties supply constructive feedback and support ("building," yes-anding) that aid idea elaboration. Because networks are cognitive representations subject to limited mental capacity, large networks induce cognitive narrowing and anchoring: creators disproportionately call to mind salient strong ties and stick to prior choices, so they activate fewer weak ties and switch less—especially when creativity feels risky. Failing to switch produces two errors: over-relying on strong ties yields false positives (pursuing uncreative ideas), and sticking to weak ties yields false negatives (abandoning creative ideas).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops and experimentally tests a theory of network *activation* across phases of the idea journey, shifting the creativity-and-networks debate from the static configuration of ties to which ties creators cognitively call to mind and whether they switch ties as phases change. It supplies a causal test distinguishing weak- versus strong-tie benefits by phase, and explains the long-standing mixed findings on network size by showing that large networks paradoxically impede optimal activation through cognitive narrowing amplified by perceived risk.

**Practical Implication**
Creators and managers should treat tie selection as phase-specific: deliberately consult diverse weak ties when generating ideas and trusted strong ties (especially "inner-circle" confidants) when elaborating, and consciously switch contacts between phases. Because large networks and high perceived risk push people toward over-relying on strong ties, organizations that lower the perceived risk of creativity (e.g., cultures of "plussing"/yes-anding) can help creators activate and switch ties more optimally.

**Limitations**
The experimental design isolates causality but cannot reproduce the complexity, duration, and richer social dynamics of real organizational creative projects, and it captured only a single, clear-cut generation-to-elaboration transition rather than repeated iterations. The studies inferred but did not directly measure the underlying cognitive processes, used multiple studies rather than direct replication, and drew on subject pools predominantly composed of women.

**Future Research**
Future work could use neuroimaging or other methods to directly capture the cognitive mechanisms of tie activation, adopt longitudinal designs to study repeated iterations and blurred phase boundaries, and examine how alter characteristics (e.g., status), indirect ties, and the broader network of contacts shape activation. Scholars are also encouraged to test whether the effects differ in predominantly male settings and to use qualitative methods to further probe conversation dynamics across the idea journey.
