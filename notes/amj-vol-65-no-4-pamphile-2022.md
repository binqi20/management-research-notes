# Paradox Peers: A Relational Approach to Navigating a Business–Society Paradox

**Abstract**
Despite advancements in our understanding of organizational paradox, we know little about how individuals navigate paradox when tensions are core to one's occupational role but not a dominant concern for senior leaders. This study explores how grantmakers working in corporate philanthropy navigate a business–society paradox when their leaders prioritize business over social objectives at the organization level. By analyzing interview data from grantmakers, alongside observations of their group meetings, I induce a peer-based model of navigating paradox. The findings reveal the important supportive role of relationships with "paradox peers"—ongoing, cooperative connections to individuals external to one's organization but facing similar paradoxical challenges. I detail three relational mechanisms for engaging paradox that together facilitate the ability of individuals to navigate paradox in their home organizations: connecting with others to relieve tension, collectively protecting the paradox, and collaboratively brainstorming responses. My findings shift paradox scholarship toward a more relational understanding of navigating paradoxes, exploring how engaging tensions outside of one's organizational boundaries can support the navigation of paradox internally.

**Research Question**
How do individuals navigate paradox that is core to their occupational role but not a dominant organizational concern for senior leaders? The study examines this through corporate grantmakers who must jointly pursue business and social objectives even when firm leaders prioritize economic goals and treat social impact as peripheral.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: A business–society paradox (persistent, interdependent yet contradictory social and economic objectives) that is core to grantmakers' occupational mandate but dismissed or minimized by profit-focused leaders.
- Triggering condition: Grantmakers occupy a relatively low-power, internally isolated position lacking interactional partners and resource-rich leaders who appreciate the paradox.
- Enabling relationship: "Paradox peers"—ongoing, cooperative, noncompetitive ties to grantmakers at other organizations facing a similar paradox, built and strengthened through repeated interactions (e.g., monthly meetings).
- Relational mechanisms (process): (1) connecting with one another to relieve tension; (2) collectively protecting the paradox (keeping both elements legitimate rather than letting one dominate); (3) collaboratively brainstorming responses.
- Emergent outcome: Working through paradox by recalling peer interactions to remain energized, motivated, and committed to both social and business objectives back at the home organization.

Drawing on an inductive grounded-theory analysis, the study theorizes a peer-based model in which relationships with paradox peers, operating through the three relational mechanisms, sustain individuals' ability to work through a business–society paradox internally. The process is fundamentally relational and collective: lower-power individuals build a sense of efficacy and resilience through cross-organizational peer interaction even without leader support, reinforcing both the relationships and continued engagement with paradox in a positive cycle.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a more relational approach to paradox theory, unpacking how and why interactions with external peers enable individuals to navigate paradox—shifting the field beyond individualized cognitive/behavioral accounts and beyond intra-organizational dynamics. It extends the paradox-mindset construct to include a relational, collective dimension; foregrounds the role of lower-power relational partners (rather than high-power leaders); and bridges paradox theory with research on occupations by showing that cross-organizational occupational communities supply relational resources for living with persistent tensions.

**Practical Implication**
Firms that staff CSR, philanthropy, ethics, sustainability, or diversity roles may shift business–society paradoxes from the organizational level to the occupational level, leaving individuals to absorb persistent tensions; without organizational engagement this can stall or reverse social impact. The findings suggest that cross-organizational peer networks and professional associations are valuable supports that help such individuals sustain commitment to social objectives, and that organizations should attend to these positions and relationships.

**Limitations**
The referral-chain sampling in one geographic location with an active peer group likely over-sampled connected grantmakers rather than isolated ones, and all informants worked for large Fortune 500 firms, so the model may not transfer to smaller organizations with scarce philanthropic resources. The study did not capture whether some peer connections matter more than others, nor negative/destructive peer dynamics, and generalizability beyond corporate philanthropy to other competing-demand occupations remains to be tested.

**Future Research**
Researchers could examine variation in how strongly individuals connect with paradox peers (including isolated individuals), whether mature versus still-professionalizing occupations display different relational mechanisms, and the factors that prevent destructive cycles while enabling positive relational dynamics. Further work could explore how occupational groups move social issues from the periphery to the core of the firm, and whether actors lacking a clear occupational community can form paradox-peer ties.
