# Stranger Leaders: A Theory of Marginal Leaders’ Conception of Learning in Organizations

**Abstract**
Not all marginal leaders took the same trajectory toward firm ground for their identities. Some left the margins to lead, embracing either an instrumental or a humanistic view of their function. Others learned to lead from the margins, casting that duality as a paradox. Taking a systems psychodynamic approach to examine marginal leaders’ trajectories through a defining duality, this study reveals the interplay between existential and strategic layers of leader identity construction. Theorizing the conception of learning as the process through which leadership comes to life and becomes organized, the study expands and bridges the literatures on leader identity and on the management of dualities.

**Research Question**
How do marginal leaders navigate defining dualities in organizations? The authors study senior L&D executives as an "extreme case" of marginal leaders who hold a formal leadership role yet lack an established template for their leader identity and face conflicting (instrumental vs. humanistic) prescriptions for their function.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: the "defining duality" of learning (instrumental vs. humanistic views of the function); leadership conceived as an identity rather than a position; marginality / "stranger" status (insiders with loose ties, caught between principles).
- Process / phases: the "conception of learning" — finding a place in relation to significant counterparts, taking a stance on learning, and building learning spaces — through which marginal leaders craft a leader identity.
- Trajectories (emergent leader identities): custodian (instrumental view, leave the margins), challenger (humanistic view, leave the margins), and connector (holds the duality as a paradox, leads from the margins).
- Systems psychodynamic underpinnings: splitting and projection (projecting the instrumental facet onto established leaders and the humanistic facet onto learners) drive the custodian/challenger bifurcation; integration and relatedness underpin the connector trajectory.

The conception of learning is the process through which leadership "comes to life and becomes organized": by relating to significant counterparts and taking a stance on why and what learning counts, marginal leaders settle existential angst into a viable leader identity and translate it into a strategy for organizing learning spaces. Custodians and challengers reduce ambiguity by splitting the duality and grounding identity in a single pole, whereas connectors resist splitting and integrate both poles, treating the duality as a paradox to be held rather than a conflict to be resolved.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study describes the predicament of marginal leaders — a boundary region between emerging and established leadership — and surfaces existential and strategic layers of leader identity work that lie beneath and beyond the interpersonal/social layer scholars know best. It theorizes the "conception of learning" as the link between leaders' becoming and organizing, and argues the instrumental–humanistic duality may sit at the core of all leaders' identities, thereby bridging the leader-identity literature to the literature on the management of paradoxes.

**Practical Implication**
The work informs the management-learning conversation on the purpose, value, and practice of learning, and the relationship between L&D executives and academic providers of executive education. It advises the proliferating cadre of marginal leaders (heads of ethics, diversity, well-being, learning, etc.) to recast marginality as a positive, creative experience — keeping a healthy distance from the organization while sustaining connections with diverse internal and external groups — since holding the instrumental–humanistic duality requires embracing rather than escaping marginality.

**Limitations**
The sample, though spanning many industries and locations, is a theoretical (not representative) one, so generalizability must be treated cautiously. The cross-sectional design precludes inferences about how leaders' views shift over time, and the authors did not collect data on personality differences or stages of adult development. Interviews better mirror informants' internalized reality than their social acts, limiting insight into the tactics leaders use to influence others and build consensus.

**Future Research**
Further research is needed to determine the conditions under which the theorizing is most informative and the prevalence of different trajectories across leadership functions. Longitudinal research could examine whether the same person takes different trajectories at different times, organizations, or in relation to different populations, and ethnographic methods within a smaller subset of organizations could add nuance about how these interpretations are enacted or denied.
