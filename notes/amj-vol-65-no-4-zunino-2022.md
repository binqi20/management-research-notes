# How Do Investors Evaluate Past Entrepreneurial Failure? Unpacking Failure Due to Lack of Skill versus Bad Luck

**Abstract**
Research has shown that most ventures fail, yet there has been limited work on investors' views of entrepreneurs who have failed in the past. We address this gap and call attention to an innate asymmetry between past failure and success. This asymmetry arises because success requires skill and luck jointly, whereas failure materializes due to either lack of skill (mistakes) or bad luck (misfortune). We ask: Are investors "failure-averse" and discount a failed entrepreneur even in the presence of additional information about entrepreneurial skill? Or do they make "rational inferences" in light of the additional skill information and proceed to fund the new startup? To test whether investors are failure-averse or engage in rational inference, we use experiments in the context of equity crowdfunding. The results suggest that prospective crowdfunding investors rationally integrate informational cues regarding past outcomes and entrepreneurial skill.

**Research Question**
How do investors evaluate entrepreneurs who have previously experienced failure, and does information about entrepreneurial skill moderate the negative evaluation of past failure? Specifically, are investors "failure averse" (categorically discounting anyone who failed) or do they make "rational inferences," looking beyond past outcomes to incorporate credible skill cues?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Past venture outcome (Failure = 1 if previous venture failed, 0 if succeeded); Skill cue availability (Skill = 1 if a credible skill cue was presented); No Experience (first-time vs. serial entrepreneur).
- DV(s): Investment Attractiveness (5- or 7-point Likert), Amount Invested (winsorized at 95th percentile), and Expected Investment (their product).
- Mediators: None formally tested.
- Moderators: The Failure × Skill cue interaction (bFSk) — the credible skill cue moderates (attenuates) the failure discount.

The paper builds on a skill-and-luck framework: success requires both skill and luck jointly, whereas failure can arise from lack of skill (mistakes), bad luck (misfortune), or both. Past failure is therefore a *noisy* rather than a *negative* cue of skill. Absent additional information, investors discount failed entrepreneurs (Hypothesis 1). A credible, third-party skill cue (inclusion in Forbes's "30 Under 30" list) resolves the ambiguity about the root cause of failure, allowing Bayesian-like inference of high skill and mitigating the failure discount (Hypothesis 2, supported for investment amount and expected investment). The alternative view that investors are behaviorally "failure averse" and ignore skill cues (Hypothesis 3) finds no support. Study 3 shows that a credible *luck* cue (an E. coli outbreak) does not similarly restore funding, because luck is inherently less diagnostic of skill.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study challenges the implicit symmetry assumption in the entrepreneurial resource-acquisition literature — that if success signals skill, failure signals its absence — by recasting past failure as a noisy (not negative) cue whose informational value depends on the cues available at the time of investment. It contributes to the crowdfunding literature (crowders make rational inferences rather than being failure averse), foregrounds the under-studied role of misfortune/luck in resource acquisition, and informs the experimental-capitalism literature by identifying a micro-level mechanism (non-aversion to failed founders) that sustains continued entrepreneurial experimentation.

**Practical Implication**
At the seed stage, entrepreneurs may be reluctant to disclose past failures, but the findings show disclosure is not detrimental when paired with an adequate, credible cue of skill. Crowdfunding platform owners have an opportunity to increase transaction volumes by including serial entrepreneurs and introducing mechanisms for reporting credible skill cues.

**Limitations**
The study adopts a binary view of entrepreneurial skill (above/below a threshold) and operationalizes the skill cue with a single proxy (Forbes's "30 Under 30"); moderate-credibility cues may not undo the failure effect. The context is equity crowdfunding with prospective (not always actual) investors, and the experiments focus on two facets — past outcomes and skill cues — among many that matter to serial entrepreneurs.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine whether the interplay between previous-outcome cues and skill cues decays as a venture matures and clearer performance cues emerge; study other sources of negative sentiment (e.g., founder gender or race) and whether credible skill cues dissipate them; test generalizability to other investor types (e.g., venture capitalists); and revisit the role of learning and luck, alternative skill-cue credibility levels, and additional facets such as venture longevity or number of entrepreneurial attempts.
