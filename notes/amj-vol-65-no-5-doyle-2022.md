# Surging Underdogs and Slumping Favorites: How Recent Streaks and Future Expectations Drive Competitive Transgressions

**Abstract**
Any single competition is rarely a "one-off" event and instead is often part of a larger sequence of related competitions. Thus, we contend that in order to better understand people's competitive experiences, we must take a more holistic view, where their experiences and behaviors in the present are a function of their past and expected future outcomes. This research expands the temporal lens of competition by examining how past outcomes (i.e., winning vs. losing streak) and future expectations (i.e., underdog vs. favorite standing) collectively influence an actor's cognitive and affective reactions to a competition, with implications for their willingness to transgress. Studies 1 (fantasy football managers) and 2 (the English Premiere League teams) show that streaks and underdog vs. favorite standing interact to predict competitive transgressions: winning streaks increase transgressions for underdogs, and losing streaks increase transgressions for favorites. Studies 3 (public defenders) and 4 (Democrats and Republicans) experimentally manipulate streaks and standing and unpack the cognitive (i.e., outcome uncertainty) and affective (i.e., excitement for underdogs, and anxiety for favorites) mechanisms that precipitate these transgressions. Theoretical implications for the competition literature, as well as managerial insights, are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do an actor's recent performance trajectory (winning vs. losing streak) and future performance expectations (underdog vs. favorite standing) jointly shape the cognitive and affective reactions that drive competitive transgressions? The paper asks when and why competitors who are embedded in a sequence of related contests are willing to depart from accepted rules of conduct.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Winning vs. losing streak (past outcomes); underdog vs. favorite standing (future expectations); their interaction.
- DV(s): Competitive transgressions (intentions in Studies 1 and 3; behavioral measures — yellow cards in Study 2, misreported quiz scores in Study 4).
- Mediators: Outcome uncertainty (first-stage); excitement (for underdogs) and anxiety (for favorites) as serial second-stage affective mediators.
- Moderators: Underdog vs. favorite standing moderates the streak-to-transgression relationship.

The paper integrates research on underdogs/favorites with work on winning/losing streaks through a temporally embedded model grounded in mental time travel and event segmentation theory (EST). Streaks create continuous "cognitive dots" that let competitors mentally traverse from past to projected future, generating psychological momentum. When these streak-based projections clash with one's standing (a winning streak as an underdog, or a losing streak as a favorite), the mismatch raises outcome uncertainty. This uncertainty makes alternative futures vivid and elicits high-arousal emotions — excitement for underdogs anticipating an unexpected win, anxiety for favorites fearing an unexpected loss — which crowd out moral considerations and precipitate transgressions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a temporally embedded model of competition that connects past competitive outcomes and future expectations, which prior work largely studied in isolation, showing that any single contest is encoded within a larger "event schemata." It deepens understanding of underdog/favorite psychology by shifting attention from motivation to cognitive (outcome uncertainty) and affective (excitement, anxiety) mechanisms, and identifies two previously undocumented competitive conditions — favorites on losing streaks and underdogs on winning streaks — that generate enough uncertainty to trigger transgressive behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should be cautious about introducing or sustaining workplace competitions, because under certain configurations of past performance and standing their deleterious effects can outweigh motivational benefits. Common exhortations to "put the past behind them" or "not get ahead of ourselves" are not equally useful in all situations; managers should attend to the interplay between an actor's competitive past and future expectations, intervening especially when situations arouse uncertainty, excitement, or anxiety.

**Limitations**
Study 1 is subject to common-method bias because all variables were assessed by the same rater at the same time. The theory operationalizes competitive standing as binary (one underdog, one favorite), which does not capture competitions with many players or varying degrees of underdog/favorite status. The focus on streaks as the sole element of the competitive past does not fully represent the broader claim that "the past matters," since non-coherent past outcomes may not shape mental projections in the same way.

**Future Research**
Future work should move beyond the binary conceptualization of standing to examine whether the psychology and behavior of underdogs and favorites differ by the magnitude of standing or the number of competitors. Researchers could explore other factors beyond streaks — such as rivalry-based wins/losses or carryover from prior seasons — that contribute to momentum and mental projection. Scholars should also study conditions that promote good (rather than bad) competitive behavior, and collect multi-wave, multi-source data to address common-method concerns.
