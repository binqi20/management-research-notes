# Enabling Rapid Financial Response to Disasters: Knotting and Reknotting Multiple Paradoxes in Interorganizational Systems

**Abstract**
While market-based solutions are increasingly being proposed to address major societal and development issues, they are also often considered antithetical to issues such as climate change, poverty alleviation, and disaster response. In particular, the interorganizational systems involved in such market solutions give rise to multiple contradictory tensions, known as paradoxes. We, therefore, adopt a paradox lens to explain the dynamics through which different actors within these systems navigate the contradictions that are generated. Drawing on a global qualitative study of multi-country risk pools that provide rapid capital in the immediate aftermath of a disaster, we advance paradox theory by showing how organizational actors' interactions (a) maintain equilibrium by generating mutually reinforcing balance among paradoxes, while (b) the clustering of poles from different paradoxes generates disequilibrium, and (c) the reknotting of poles from different paradoxes restores equilibrium. As our process framework shows, these dynamics form an iterative cycle between equilibrium and disequilibrium that is essential in enhancing the promise of market-based solutions to address development issues, which in our study refers to increasing the rapid availability of capital to respond to disasters such as hurricanes, earthquakes, and droughts.

**Research Question**
How does the knotting of multiple paradoxes enable, or undermine, dynamic equilibrium within interorganizational systems? The authors ask how coexisting paradoxes are entangled, shape each other, and are managed by actors interacting within an interorganizational system.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: three knotted paradoxes — market–development (Smith & Besharov, 2019), part–whole (Poole & Van de Ven, 1989), and temporal short–long-term (Slawinski & Bansal, 2015) — co-occurring as an inseparably entangled "paradox knot."
- Process / phases: an iterative cycle between equilibrium (paradoxes balanced and knotted) and disequilibrium (knot unravels), interrupted by "critical incidents" (annual renewals; disasters).
- Knotting practices: four interactions (training on the solution, reviewing/refining the solution, collaborating across stakeholders, communicating the solution) that maintain mutually reinforcing balance.
- Triggering conditions: "clustering" of the short-term and part poles from two different paradoxes, which de-emphasizes the long-term and whole poles and unravels the knot, generating disequilibrium.
- Reknotting practices: six interactions binding the market pole, and separately the development pole, to the clustered short-term and part poles (e.g., running scenarios, developing new products, funding countries' projects, working around product triggers).
- Emergent outcomes: reknotting indirectly restores balance across all three paradoxes, reinstates equilibrium, and strengthens the interorganizational solution over time.

The central process is that organizational actors knot multiple paradoxes into a mutually reinforcing balance to keep the interorganizational system viable; when a critical incident causes specific poles to cluster, the knot unravels into disequilibrium; rather than reasserting the neglected pole, actors reknot the dominant poles to a third paradox's poles, which indirectly rebalances the original paradoxes. Iterating between knotting and reknotting is a generative dynamic that not only restores equilibrium but refines and embeds new initiatives that strengthen the solution.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the concept of paradox knots (Sheep et al., 2017) from discursive framing to the interactional realm by specifying the social-interaction processes through which knotting and reknotting occur. It introduces "clustering" of individual poles as an explanatory mechanism for how paradox knots unravel into disequilibrium, and theorizes "reknotting across specific poles" as a fundamentally new response to paradox (distinct from reasserting the suppressed pole). It further characterizes disequilibrium concretely and positions the iterative cycle of equilibrium–disequilibrium as a generative dynamic for sustaining interorganizational systems addressing grand challenges.

**Practical Implication**
The study shows that governments of different countries can work together, and with development organizations, to find disaster-response solutions that satisfy both their own partial interests and wider regional ones. Inevitable tensions and lapses into disequilibrium need not destroy such interorganizational solutions; rather, solutions can be incrementally refined and strengthened through constant interactions to knot and reknot paradoxes as actors learn more about the challenges and how their shared purpose can evolve.

**Limitations**
The longitudinal study, based on intensive personal engagement, documented the dynamics of paradox knotting and disequilibrium only over the relatively short period of the authors' engagement. Not all evolutionary paths may be as benign as those observed, and the study examined a context with three specific entangled paradoxes (market–development, part–whole, temporal), so findings may not generalize to other configurations of paradox multiplicity.

**Future Research**
Future research might examine whether the specific clustering of short-term and partial interests is a feature of interorganizational systems where actors can privilege immediate over collective long-term interests, and study paradox knotting within varied types of interorganizational systems (e.g., networks around a central organization, project-based alliances, supply chains) that may exhibit different dynamics. Longer-term historical studies could reveal how these dynamics expand, shift, or reverse, and how variation in paradox multiplicity shapes knotting and its relationship with (dis)equilibrium, including within (not only across) organizations.
