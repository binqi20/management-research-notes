# Going Viral or Growing Like an Oak Tree? Towards Sustainable Local Development Through Entrepreneurship

**Abstract**
Nurturing venture capital–backed, high-growth entrepreneurship has been strongly promoted as an effective means to achieve local development in impoverished places. Yet, growing evidence has suggested that, despite its notable successes in resource-rich regions, this approach creates limited impact in economically challenged locales. We address this conundrum by calling into question the crux of high-growth entrepreneurship—the pursuit of quick scaling through venture capital financing. Our field research in two entrepreneurship-nurturing organizations in Detroit reveals important heterogeneity in resourcing modes and venture growth in time and space. Specifically, we find that ventures developed through different modes of resourcing (financing vs. local bricolage) grow at different spatiotemporal scales (scaling up toward fast geographical expansion vs. scaling deep toward locally anchored endurance), and consequently generate distinctive yet complementary contributions to their depleted place of origin. Unlike scaling-up ventures whose local impact was explosive yet short-lived, scaling-deep ventures created jobs, products or services, and spillover effects that stayed local and addressed specific local problems. Building on these findings, we challenge the exclusive pursuit of high-growth entrepreneurship for poverty alleviation and suggest that entrepreneurship-driven local development requires cohabitation of ventures growing at varying scales.

**Research Question**
How are different resourcing modes and the scale of venture growth related to the sustainable development of impoverished locales? The study problematizes the prevalent assumption that venture-capital-backed, high-growth entrepreneurship is the most viable path to poverty alleviation, asking what it is about ventures that "deviate from the successful template" on resourcing and scale that makes them more promising in economically challenged places.

**Mechanism Process**
- Modes of resourcing (the primary driver): *Financing* (securing external venture capital/equity) at ACCEL vs. *local bricolage* (making do by recombining locally available resources) at GREEN.
- Spatiotemporal scale of venture growth (the focal construct): *Scaling up* — "the process whereby an organization pursues fast expansion" (quick, broad geographic coverage) vs. *scaling deep* — "a process whereby an organization pursues enduring growth anchored to its original location" (slow, locally rooted).
- Process / phases (idea-development ethnography): Open coding → identifying "time–space idea codes" → analyzing key interaction scenes that infuse spatiotemporal orientations → tracing actual venture growth and contributions via archival data.
- Triggering conditions: At ACCEL the imperative to win investor funding bred a "pressure for speed" (the time value of money / "speed trap"), which translated into a "spatial mandate for expansion"; at GREEN the focus on repurposing local assets deepened ventures' spatial anchors to Detroit and extended their temporal horizon.
- Emergent outcomes (contributions): Scaling-up ventures produced explosive but impermanent local impact (jobs and value that left Detroit through relocation/acquisition); scaling-deep ventures produced enduring, locally customized contributions to Detroit's economic, social, and environmental problems, with complementarity between the two types.

The paper theorizes a self-reinforcing coupling between resourcing and scale: financing places a venture in the rigid timeframe of venture capital, where speed begets a mandate for geographic expansion and pivoting toward universal, readily exploitable demands, which in turn requires still more financing—locking the venture onto a scaling-up trajectory. Local bricolage, by contrast, confines a venture's spatial scope to a particular place and requires slow accumulation of social capital and deep relationships, locking it onto a scaling-deep trajectory whose growth is "inextricably tied to the region's restoration." Because sustainable local development requires contributions that stay local across generations, the authors argue that vigorous revitalization requires the cohabitation of ventures growing at diverse spatiotemporal scales.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study's central innovation is to bring the time–space (scale) lens into entrepreneurship, reconceptualizing scaling not as rapid growth in size but as growth that "unfolds at a particular spatiotemporal scale," thereby distinguishing scaling up from a previously undertheorized scaling deep. It extends entrepreneurial bricolage from the individual to the place-based community level ("local bricolage"), showing bricolage is a complementary alternative—not a marginal substitute—to venture capital, and it advances venture-growth research by foregrounding "how" (not just "how much") ventures grow. It also surfaces an inward, identity-based microfoundation of bricolage: founders' deep reflection on their own "deep root" enables them to perceive what local resources can be repurposed.

**Practical Implication**
The findings caution policymakers and ecosystem builders that the prevalent focus on infusing venture capital can obscure a more important need—nurturing entrepreneurial imagination to unlock the latent value of existing local assets in seemingly "barren" landscapes. Scaling-deep ventures can do the critical work of lifting impoverished locales toward self-reliance and can capture and redistribute wealth created by scaling-up ventures, so revitalization strategies should support both types of ventures rather than betting exclusively on high-growth "gazelles."

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge it is reasonable to ask whether the scaling-up dynamics they observe stem from the peculiarity of venture capital specifically, rather than from financing as a general mode of resourcing (they argue financing's risk-return logic generally intensifies pressure for quick growth). To preserve focus, they also chose not to examine other salient patterns in the data—most notably the dynamics between incubating contexts and participating entrepreneurs (the relative roles of treatment vs. selection effects).

**Future Research**
The authors call for research into alternative venture-financing vehicles—such as "patient capital" and "slow money"—and how they shape invested ventures' scale of growth and local impact; into how individual entrepreneurs' varied responses to incubating-context influence imprint different growth patterns; and into how ventures growing at different scales build connections and create synergies, and what kinds of local ecosystems facilitate such complementary collaborations.
