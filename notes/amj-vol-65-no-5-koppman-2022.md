# Overcoming Conflict Between Symmetric Occupations: How “Creatives” and “Suits” Use Gender Ordering in Advertising

**Abstract**
In knowledge-based organizations, conflict among interdependent occupations can be exacerbated by the absence of a clear hierarchical ordering of these occupations within the organization. Moreover, given women’s inroads into some traditionally maledominated occupations but not others, these workplaces are increasingly horizontally gender segregated. In this paper, we study how members of these symmetric and segregated occupations manage conflict in U.S. advertising agencies through the case of relationships between “creatives” (copywriters, designers, and creative directors) and “suits” or account practitioners (account executives, strategists, and managers). Creatives and suits are at the same organizational level in their agencies. While creatives are primarily men, suits, traditionally also men, are now primarily women. Drawing on participant observation in five different U.S. advertising agencies and over 100 interviews, we show how creatives and account practitioners use gender ordering to overcome jurisdictional conflict. These practices are grounded in enacting essentialist gender differences that transform symmetric occupational relationships into hierarchical ones by embedding the gender hierarchy. We find that, while gender ordering helps women and men in cross-occupational pairs get work done, it also reinforces women’s disadvantage, because, for women, it involves low-status and emotionally taxing scut work that it does not involve for men.

**Research Question**
How do members of horizontally gender-segregated, symmetric (same-level) occupations manage jurisdictional conflict when relative rank is ambiguous and no formal hierarchy orders them? Specifically, how do female-dominated account practitioners and male-dominated creatives in U.S. advertising agencies overcome occupational conflict?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: "gender ordering" (enacting essentialist gender roles/stereotypes that import an informal gender status hierarchy into cross-occupational relationships); jurisdictional conflict over legitimacy, authority, and knowledge claims between symmetric occupations; "scut work" (low-status, menial, emotionally taxing work that does not draw on occupational knowledge).
- Sites of conflict: agency–client interactions (legitimacy), revisions (authority), and concept development (knowledge).
- Practices (female account–male creative pairs): protecting (mediating, buffering) to resolve legitimacy conflict; nurturing (conceptual, physical) to resolve authority conflict; counseling/preparing to resolve knowledge conflict.
- Comparison conditions: minority cross-gender pairs (male account–female creatives) use gender ordering via "salesman"/"men are good at math" framings but without performing scut work; same-gender pairs use "occupational entrenchment" (dominance or reasoning), which usually fails to resolve conflict.
- Emergent outcome: creatives acquiesce; women secure cooperation but cede status.

Through gender ordering, cross-gender pairs convert competition between similarly ranked occupations into a hierarchical relationship by enacting taken-for-granted gender status beliefs (Ridgeway, 2011), so women cede status to men in exchange for cooperation. Because gender carries shared expectations agreed upon by both groups, it supplies an alternative informal hierarchy that resolves disagreements over whose work is more valuable, who may direct work, and whose knowledge is most relevant. For women, however, eliciting this cooperation requires performing emotionally taxing scut work (mothering, buffering, bringing food) that men in the same occupation do not perform, reproducing the gender hierarchy within the everyday functioning of the organization.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the literature on managing occupational conflict beyond asymmetric occupations to show how symmetric, horizontally gender-segregated occupations resolve jurisdictional conflict through gender ordering. It reconceptualizes the role of demography in occupational conflict: whereas race-, nationality-, or age-based differences aligned with occupational differences exacerbate conflict, shared status beliefs about gender can instead facilitate cross-occupational cooperation. It also identifies cross-occupational relationships as a previously unrecognized source of women’s disadvantage, since gender ordering embeds the gender hierarchy through women’s scut work.

**Practical Implication**
The findings surface a hidden cost borne by women in mixed-gender, interdependent professional work: securing cooperation often requires low-status, emotionally taxing scut work that does not advance their occupational claims or status. Organizations and managers should recognize that informal "getting along" practices that keep work moving can simultaneously entrench women’s disadvantage, and should attend to how coordination burdens are distributed across gender-segregated occupations.

**Limitations**
Most coded examples are drawn from interviews rather than direct observation of paired interactions, and the study lacks dyadic-level data with both members of each dyad, limiting triangulation of situations and dynamics. The studied occupations are uniformly white, college educated, and U.S. born, which constrains the ability to observe demographic similarity-based mechanisms and limits generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine gender ordering in other symmetric, gender-segregated occupational contexts (e.g., engineering, architecture, television) to assess how it operates elsewhere, and should ideally collect dyadic-level data with interviews from both members of a dyad to triangulate descriptions of situations and dynamics.
