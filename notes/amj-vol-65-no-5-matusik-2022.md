# The Highs and Lows of Hierarchy in Multiteam Systems

**Abstract**
Multiteam system research operates under the assumption that multiteam systems should be structured hierarchically, or consist of component teams nested under a formal, centralized leadership team that oversees and orchestrates system activities. Although multiteam system research has certainly provided evidence suggestive of the merits of more hierarchical (vs. egalitarian) structures, we take a more balanced view and argue that hierarchy both facilitates and impedes multiteam system success. Using an experimental study and a sample of 76 multiteam systems observed over three performance episodes, we provide evidence that structural hierarchy (a) facilitates multiteam system learning by mitigating cognitive depletion among members, but (b) undermines multiteam system learning via its deleterious effects on horizontal coordination. Critically, however, we find that the benefits of hierarchy (i.e., reduced cognitive depletion) wane over performance episodes. This investigation underscores the important role that different hierarchical arrangements play in multiteam system functioning. Although a majority of multiteam system researchers have utilized hierarchical structures—which indeed have their virtues—we illustrate that egalitarian structures have key advantages as well.

**Research Question**
How, why, and when do different hierarchical arrangements (hierarchy vs. egalitarianism) affect multiteam system operations? The paper asks whether hierarchy is simply beneficial or detrimental, or whether it simultaneously facilitates and impedes multiteam system success through distinct mechanisms (horizontal coordination and member cognitive depletion) that evolve over performance episodes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Hierarchical arrangement (1 = hierarchical, 0 = egalitarian), manipulated at the multiteam-system level.
- DV(s): Multiteam system learning (proximal) and multiteam system performance (distal).
- Mediators: Horizontal coordination (first-stage; negative path from hierarchy) and member cognitive depletion (first-stage; negative path from hierarchy), with multiteam system learning serving as a second-stage mediator linking the two first-stage mediators to performance.
- Moderators: Performance episode (T1-T3), moderating the hierarchy→coordination and hierarchy→cognitive-depletion relationships.

Hierarchy elicits competition, power struggles, and conflict, leading component teams to focus resources and information inward, which lowers horizontal coordination and thereby impedes learning (a coordination "barrier"). Simultaneously, hierarchy provides structure, order, predictability, and cognitive fluency, reducing the mental effort of navigating the complex multiteam-system environment, which lowers cognitive depletion and thereby facilitates learning (a cognitive "boon"). Because routines become self-reinforcing while members build schemas and mental models over time, the coordination disadvantage of hierarchy persists across episodes whereas its cognitive-depletion advantage wanes once members no longer need hierarchy as a cognitive "crutch."

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study qualifies the dominant assumption in the multiteam-system literature that hierarchy (a strong, centralized leadership team) is superior, by importing the intra-team insight that hierarchy is both functional and dysfunctional and showing it operates at the inter-team level. It elucidates *why* hierarchy matters—egalitarianism aids horizontal coordination while hierarchy attenuates cognitive depletion—and, through a longitudinal design, *when* these opposing effects hold, demonstrating that the cognitive benefit decays while the coordination cost endures over performance episodes.

**Practical Implication**
Because organizations are gravitating toward interdependent systems of teams and more egalitarian structures, managers should match the hierarchical arrangement to task demands: egalitarian designs may be preferable when component-team process interdependence is high (e.g., obstetric labor-and-delivery systems), even if initially more depleting, whereas the cognitive benefits of hierarchy may outweigh egalitarianism's coordination advantage when interdependence is lower (pooled or sequential) or teams are temporally limited (e.g., ad hoc teams).

**Limitations**
The authors examined only power-based hierarchies, not status hierarchies, so generalization to status arrangements is untested. The research used undergraduate students in a laboratory military-like simulation, raising external-validity concerns, and cognitive depletion was measured before (not during) task execution. The multiteam systems also had relatively stable membership and tasks with multi-week gaps between episodes, which may not represent more fluid or back-to-back real-world multiteam systems.

**Future Research**
Future work should investigate status hierarchies in multiteam systems to test whether the present theorizing generalizes beyond power. Researchers should also manipulate the level of task interdependence among component teams—rarely varied despite its theoretical importance—and study multiteam systems with more fluid membership, more variable tasks, and shorter (e.g., back-to-back) intervals between performance episodes to assess boundary conditions on the coordination and cognitive-depletion effects.
