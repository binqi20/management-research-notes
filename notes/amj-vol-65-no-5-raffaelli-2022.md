# How Leaders with Divergent Visions Generate Novel Strategy: Navigating the Paradox of Preservation and Modernization in Swiss Watchmaking

**Abstract**
How do leaders with divergent visions for their organization come together to create a novel strategy? This paper employs paradox as a lens to investigate how leader dyads can integrate opposing strategies to produce a new, generative approach. Drawing on a qualitative historical case study of Switzerland's largest watch company during the quartz crisis in Swiss watchmaking, we induce a process model from the activities of two leaders whose relationship embodied the tensions and strategic contradictions of preserving the past and modernizing for the future. The model specifies a set of individual, relational, and structural mechanisms by which leaders productively engage with a "preservation–modernization paradox" to facilitate novel strategy in the wake of a discontinuity. We interpret our findings in terms of the demands of navigating the management and outcomes of strategic paradoxes. While tracing the theoretical and practical implications of our model and our findings, we address leadership conundrums characteristic of organizations confronting paradox.

**Research Question**
How do leaders with divergent visions for their organization come together to create a novel strategy? Specifically, the paper unpacks how and why a leader dyad integrates seemingly inconsistent strategies—preservation versus modernization—to produce a new, generative approach in the wake of a technological discontinuity.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: a technological discontinuity (the quartz crisis) to which two leaders respond with divergent visions—industrial modernization (Hayek) and craft preservation (Biver).
- Core tension: a "preservation–modernization paradox" surfaced when SMH acquired Blancpain, manifest as organizational inconsistencies in core technologies, economic models, and time orientation of practices.
- Individual practices: redefining personal goals and subjugating egos (willingness to moderate narcissistic/self-absorbed behavior) as precursors to joint engagement.
- Relational exchanges: cultivating affective trust and interacting frequently (daily private phone calls before teams could weigh in).
- Structural boundaries: carving out shared territory (Biver on SMH board / Omega; Hayek on Breguet committee) and synthesizing skills (each acquiring capabilities initially associated with the other).
- Emergent outcome: a sustained paradox that generated a novel "both/and" strategy and ongoing strategic reorientation at SMH.

The authors induce a recursive process model in which two leaders with opposing visions locate the paradox in their *dyadic relationship* rather than in a single leader or the whole top team. Individual-level practices (goal redefinition, ego subjugation) enable relational exchanges (affective trust, frequent interaction), which are enacted through structural boundaries (shared territory, skill synthesis). These mutually reinforcing mechanisms allow the dyad to embrace and sustain contradictory preservation and modernization demands, productively generating new strategy and continual organizational renewal rather than the destructive infighting prior work would predict.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study relocates the "locus of paradox" from the singular senior leader or top management team (leader-centric and team-centric models) to the *relationship within a leader dyad*, providing an empirical illustration of a previously largely theoretical "relational" conception of paradox. It specifies three mutually reinforcing classes of leader-dyad mechanisms (individual practices, relational exchanges, structural boundaries) and identifies, defines, and elaborates a generative and generalizable category—the preservation–modernization paradox—reframing the strategic question from "preserve or modernize" to "which elements to preserve while modernizing."

**Practical Implication**
Senior teams whose members hold opposing visions can generate novel strategy if leaders redefine personal goals, subjugate their egos, build affective trust through frequent private interaction, and carve out shared territory that lets them swap positions and synthesize skills. Leaders facing a technological discontinuity need not commit to a single either/or response; sustaining the tension between heritage and modernization can itself be profoundly generative.

**Limitations**
As case-based qualitative research, the findings face scope conditions tied to the empirical setting: the preservation perspective stems from Swiss watchmaking's rich history and a craft orientation characteristic of creative and artistic industries, so the paradox may be less salient where tradition is less bound up in the technology itself. The discontinuity studied was technological, and applicability to other forms of change (e.g., radical environmental change) is uncertain. The study period ended in 2001.

**Future Research**
Researchers could seek other antecedent conditions that enable a convergence of visions (e.g., dyadic psychological safety) and probe the interdependence of affective trust and frequent interaction. Promising directions include mapping how leaders with paradoxical visions reshape industries and fields over time, testing the model in settings where tradition is less entangled with the technology, and examining how confronting and sustaining paradox operates when discontinuity is precipitated by radical environmental change (e.g., COVID-19) rather than a new technology.
