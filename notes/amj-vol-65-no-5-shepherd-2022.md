# Intersectionality in Intractable Dirty Work: How Mumbai Ragpickers Make Meaning of Their Work and Lives

**Abstract**
Recent dirty work research has begun to explore intersectionality, attending to how meaning is made at the intersection of multiple sources of taint. This research has shown that individuals often construct both positive and negative meanings, which can be challenging to manage because the meanings people construct require a certain coherence to provide a foundation for action. This challenge is intensified when dirty work is intractable—when it is difficult, if not impossible, for a person to avoid doing this work. Our study of meaning-making in the face of intractable dirty work examines ragpickers in Mumbai, India, who handle and dispose of garbage, and are further tainted by belonging to the lowest caste in Indian society, and living in slums. These ragpickers constructed both an overarching sense of helplessness rooted in the intractability of their situation, and a set of positive meanings—survival, destiny, and hope—rooted in specific facets of their lives and enacted through distinct temporal frames. By holding and combining these disparate meanings, they achieved “functional ambivalence”—the simultaneous experience of opposing orientations toward their work and lives that facilitated both acceptance and a sense of agency, and enabled them to carry on in their lives.

**Research Question**
How do people engaged in intractable dirty work at the intersection of multiple sources of taint make and manage meanings of their work and lives?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Intersecting sources of taint (dirty work, tainted caste, dirty place); the tension between negative meaning (helplessness) and positive meanings (survival, destiny, hope); "functional ambivalence" as the simultaneous holding of opposing orientations.
- Triggering conditions: Intractable dirty work arising from long-term individual and familial embeddedness in a community, such that the work is a structurally determined life course rather than a choice.
- Process / phases: Constructing opportunities for meaning-making from intersecting sources of taint → multifocal meaning-making that pairs a specific (vs. global) focus with distinct temporal frames (present = survival, past = destiny, future = hope; global/enduring = helplessness) → holding functional ambivalence.
- Mechanisms sustaining the outcome: Acceptance (flowing from the global meaning of helplessness) and agency (flowing from the specific positive meanings) mutually support and constrain each other, so acceptance does not become passivity and agency does not become frustration.
- Emergent outcome: A self-reinforcing constellation of negative and positive meanings that enables workers to carry on with their work and tainted lives.

The central process is multifocal meaning-making: by adopting a global focus the ragpickers constructed negative meaning (helplessness rooted in intractability), while by focusing on specific facets of their lives through distinct temporal frames they constructed positive, enabling meanings (survival, destiny, hope). Holding these disparate meanings simultaneously produced functional ambivalence, in which the resulting acceptance and sense of agency sustained one another and grounded the workers' capacity to keep going.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to dirty work and intersectionality scholarship by identifying "functional ambivalence" as an outcome of meaning-making, showing that the relationship between negative and positive meanings can be constructive and empowering rather than only a tension to be managed. It extends intersectionality research by demonstrating that workers can experience intersecting sources of taint both as tightly coupled and as separable dimensions, and it identifies the process of "multifocal meaning-making"—pairing a specific focus with a specific temporal frame—through which people in intractable dirty work construct a functionally ambivalent constellation of meanings.

**Practical Implication**
The study foregrounds the embodied wisdom of marginalized workers whose voices are rarely heard, suggesting that interventions and support (e.g., by NGOs or policymakers) should recognize how combining acceptance with agency helps such workers care for their families' immediate needs and sustain hope for their children, rather than treating their coping as mere resignation.

**Limitations**
As research rooted in an extreme case (the tightly coupled occupation, caste, and living conditions of Mumbai ragpickers), the findings offer naturalistic rather than statistical generalizability, and this extreme intractability likely shaped results such as the prominence of helplessness. The study focused on common experiences and did not examine within-group differences; it excluded child ragpickers and included only seven women, so age and gender differences could not be systematically examined.

**Future Research**
Future dirty work research should broaden the lenses used to study coping by attending to the embeddedness and relationality of dirty work in social and familial ties across past and future. Scholars should also examine the longer-term consequences of such coping—whether the constellation of survival, destiny, hope, and helplessness "locks in" workers—by studying populations with greater variance in their work and lives and following them longitudinally, and by attending to age and gender differences.
