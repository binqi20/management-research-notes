# When and Why Bias Suppression Is Difficult to Sustain: The Asymmetric Effect of Intermittent Accountability

**Abstract**
The justice literature has suggested that adhering to justice rules should be selfreinforcing. Yet, the literature on bias suppression (a facet of procedural justice) has indicated that the effects of debiasing initiatives are transient. We aim to explain why sustained bias suppression is intrinsically challenging under the condition of intermittent accountability. We theorize that bias suppression with high accountability induces counterfactual thinking and regret and, ultimately, with subsequent low accountability, decision reversal. However, this process does not occur when accountability is initially low and subsequently high. Thus, intermittent accountability has an asymmetric effect on bias suppression over time—bias suppression is reversed (and biased decisions sustained). We initially provide proof of concept using Standard & Poor's 500 Firms' CEOs. We then conduct a field study of prominent decision-makers: U.S. Supreme Court judges (Study 1). We find that bias suppression is negatively associated with consistency when accountability is initially high but later reduced, as seemingly mediated through counterfactual thinking. Next, we test our full model in two experiments using recall and a self-focused vignette (Studies 2 and 3). Finally, we explore why bias yields consistency even when accountability increases. Our work highlights the power of cognitive and emotional processing relative to external control.

**Research Question**
When and why is sustaining bias suppression (adherence to the procedural-justice bias suppression rule) from one decision to the next intrinsically difficult, and how does the sequence of accountability across decisions shape whether bias suppression is reversed?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Bias suppression at T1 (a decision contravening one's own bias/ideological attitude in favor of the organization's neutral principles)
- DV(s): Consistency at T2 (sustaining versus reversing the prior decision when facing a similar choice)
- Mediators: Counterfactual thinking ("would/could/should have" cognition), then regret (a self-focused, self-reactive counterfactual emotion); pride mediates the biased-decision-to-consistency path in a supplemental analysis
- Moderators: Accountability at T1 (high amplifies bias-suppression -> counterfactual thinking) and accountability at T2 (low strengthens regret -> inconsistency); the indirect effect is strongest under high-T1 / low-T2 accountability

Drawing on the social cognitive theory of self-regulation, the authors argue that suppressing a bias under high accountability leaves an unresolved internal tension between self-interest and the organization's principles, triggering self-reflective counterfactual thinking and self-reactive regret. When subsequent accountability is low, regret supplies the impetus to reverse the earlier just decision; when accountability stays high, or rises from low to high, this self-regulatory cascade does not fire, so biased decisions persist. The effect is therefore asymmetric: intermittent accountability undermines the bias-suppression-consistency link only when accountability falls from high to low.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shows that, unlike other justice rules, bias suppression is not self-reinforcing, challenging the dominant model of justice enactment and the assumption that bias suppression and biased decision-making simply occur under high versus low accountability respectively. It reframes justice enactment as dynamic and sequence-dependent, demonstrating that accountability operates jointly with internal self-regulatory processes (counterfactual thinking, regret, and—surprisingly—pride) rather than as a standalone external control, and argues for studying the bias suppression rule in isolation rather than aggregating it with other procedural-justice facets.

**Practical Implication**
Sustaining bias suppression is an uphill battle: accountability used intermittently can backfire because withdrawing it removes the only force holding biased decisions in check. Organizations should apply accountability consistently or, better, cultivate internalized commitment to organizational values; for high-discretion roles, they should pursue "bias alignment" (matching decision-makers to decisions where their values serve the organization) rather than relying on compliance, and recognize that adding accountability later may be "too little, too late."

**Limitations**
Study 1 operationalized bias suppression only via decisions inconsistent with a judge's general ideology and relied on an imperfect archival proxy (separate opinions) for counterfactual thinking, with effects of small magnitude and a long average lag (5.9 years) between initial and revisited decisions. The proof of concept and Study 1 focus on political-ideology bias, while Studies 2-3 used a generic conceptualization of bias; the studies did not examine the full range of contextual antecedents that generate bias suppression in the first place.

**Future Research**
Future work should test whether the asymmetric accountability effect generalizes to other bias types made explicit (e.g., gender or racial bias), examine the broader set of contextual factors and antecedents that shape the post-bias-suppression process, and illuminate the internal motives or innate individual differences that initially drive or help sustain bias suppression over time regardless of external accountability.
