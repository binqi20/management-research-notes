# Ruminating on What You Think of Me: A Grounded Model of Construed Image Work

**Abstract**
Research on identity has provided key insights into the challenges individuals experience when their professional self-concept is disrupted. But there has been little consideration of individuals' sense of how they are viewed and defined by others—their construed image—also a key dimension of the professional self, one that is similarly compromised during such disruptive events. As a result, a widespread and theoretically rich phenomenon has received virtually no attention from scholars: construed image disruption and (re)construction. We develop a grounded model of "construed image work" based on qualitative data from former professional athletes. We find that, soon after a career-disrupting event (i.e., voluntary/involuntary retirement at a young age, in this case), individuals encounter cognitive and affective disorientation (or "drift") that impedes their careers. Our findings reveal that systematic differences in how individuals make sense of causal forces underlying the disruptive event shape the paths they take trying to achieve a new, secure construed image and alleviate the problems of "drift." Understanding construed image work is an important first step in acknowledging the importance of construed image for our theories regarding self-perceptions in the context of careers. Our grounded process model offers an initial foray into a critical—and foundational—dimension of the professional self-concept, one that has previously been ignored in favor of explorations into identity.

**Research Question**
(a) What happens to an individual's construed image under conditions of career disruption? (b) How (and why) do individuals adapt to the associated disruption of their construed images? And (c) what explains differences in adaptation outcomes?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Construed image (one's internal sense of how one is defined by others) vs. identity (who one believes one is); construed image security vs. "construed image drift"
- Triggering condition: Construed image disruption ("shattered looking glass")—a career-disrupting exit (voluntary/involuntary retirement) that undermines construed image stability
- Process / phases: Construed image disruption (shattered looking glass) → construed image drift (0–12 months; cognitive drift + affective drift) → construed image reconstruction (12+ months)
- Pivotal mechanism (explains divergence): Attributional framing of the disruption—uncontrollable/external (prevention-focused) vs. controllable/internal (promotion-focused)
- Emergent outcomes: Wanderers (external/uncontrollable attribution → "aimless tinkering," unresolved drift) vs. Rebuilders (internal/controllable attribution → "focused experimentation," deconstruction and meaning-making → secure construed image)

After a career-disrupting exit, former athletes lose the ability to predict how others define them, producing "drift"—a broken psychological link between their actions and perceived audience expectations—on both cognitive and affective dimensions. How individuals attribute the cause of the disruption (external/uncontrollable vs. internal/controllable) functions as the central psychological mechanism: it shapes their motivational outlook and the reconstruction path they take. Wanderers, framing the disruption as an external injustice, fixate on preventing further loss and "tinker" aimlessly without building momentum; Rebuilders, claiming "ownership" of the disruption, proactively deconstruct their prior construed image and engage in focused experimentation to build a new, secure sense of how they are defined by others.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces "construed image work"—the systematic activities people engage in to build a secure internal sense of how they are defined by others—theorizing construed image at the individual level for the first time (prior work treated it only at the organizational level). It extends professional self-concept and liminality research by showing that construed image (not just identity) is disrupted and can linger maladaptively after role exit, that impression management and identity work alone are insufficient for "moving on," and that attributional framing and a time-extended "deconstruction" orientation are decisive for successful reconstruction. It contributes to bereavement/loss-in-careers scholarship by introducing a temporal dimension (aimless tinkering) and reframing deconstruction as a positive precursor to career mobility.

**Practical Implication**
The model counsels against the common advice to "quickly move on" after a career-disrupting exit: individuals appear to need roughly 12–18 months to process the shattering event, and rushing into aimless tinkering undermines durable reconstruction. It highlights that successful transition requires awareness of one's attributional tendencies and a willingness to deconstruct prior self-assumptions, rather than simply adopting image-management or "trying new things" strategies. Support organizations for transitioning professionals (e.g., former athletes) should help individuals reframe attributions and disentangle role-based attributes from transferable abilities.

**Limitations**
The authors frame these primarily as boundary conditions. Regarding "who," the model is argued to transfer to other strong, self-concept-dominated occupations (military, entrepreneurship, the arts, executive leadership, skilled trades, "callings"), but its mechanisms' relevance may vary with occupational factors such as average retirement age and severance/pension norms. Regarding "when," the model treats identity disruption as a conceptual boundary it does not cross; the exact degree to which construed image shapes identity, and the pathways between them, is an empirical question the study does not answer. Regarding "where," it does not speak directly to construed image disruption in nonprofessional (personal) contexts.

**Future Research**
Scholars should examine how construed image work resembles and differs from identity work—how the two processes interrelate and under what conditions they reinforce versus undermine one another—through an integrative approach that could revitalize the identity literature. Research might explore how liminal identities and liminal construed images interact (reinforcing or counteracting one another). Further work could test the model's transferability to other self-concept-dominated occupations and to personal (nonprofessional) disruption contexts, and investigate the role of time horizons in responding to self-concept threats.
