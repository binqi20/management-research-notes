# Paradoxical Leadership and Innovation in Work Teams: The Multilevel Mediating Role of Ambidexterity and Leader Vision as a Boundary Condition

**Abstract**
In light of ever-increasing demands for innovation in work teams, we recommend paradoxical leadership to manage team and individual innovation. Integrating the paradox perspective and ambidexterity theory, we propose a multilevel model of how and when team leaders’ paradoxical leadership enhances team and individual innovative outcomes. We conducted two multi-wave, multisource field studies and one online survey study with a cross-lagged panel design. Our findings show that paradoxical leadership has a positive indirect effect on team members’ individual innovation through individual ambidexterity and a positive indirect relationship with team innovation through team ambidexterity. Our results further show that leader vision strengthens the effects of paradoxical leadership on team and individual ambidexterity and innovative outcomes. We discuss the theoretical and practical implications of these findings.

**Research Question**
How and when does team leaders' paradoxical leadership — taking a "both–and" approach to competing organizing and belonging demands — promote innovation at both the individual and team levels in work teams tasked with providing innovative solutions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Paradoxical leadership (leaders' seemingly competing yet interrelated behaviors addressing the organizing and belonging paradoxes)
- DV(s): Individual innovation and team innovation
- Mediators: Individual ambidexterity (individual exploration × exploitation) and team ambidexterity (team exploration × exploitation)
- Moderators: Leader vision (clarity of a communicated overarching team direction)

Drawing on the paradox perspective and ambidexterity theory, paradoxical leaders deliver divergent role expectations and model how to manage competing demands at the individual level, while building a simultaneously autonomous and structured work atmosphere at the team level. These dynamics raise individual and team ambidexterity — the integration of exploratory and exploitative activities — which in turn fuels the generation and implementation of novel ideas, increasing innovation. A clear leader vision lets members make sense of paradoxical behaviors, strengthening the leadership-to-ambidexterity-to-innovation pathways (supported across three studies, including a cross-lagged design that rules out reverse causation).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces the paradox perspective to innovation management and specifies ambidexterity as the multilevel mediating mechanism linking paradoxical leadership to individual and team innovation. It extends ambidexterity theory by identifying *which* specific leader behaviors promote ambidexterity and *when* (conditioned on leader vision), and it advances team-leadership research by showing paradoxical leadership fits complex, volatile team contexts.

**Practical Implication**
Managers leading innovation-focused teams should adopt paradoxical leadership principles — e.g., ensuring flexibility while enforcing work requirements and holding output control while granting process autonomy — and organizations should provide paradoxical-leadership and "paradoxical mindset" training. To maximize effectiveness, leaders must also communicate a clear, forward-looking vision so members can "see the big picture" behind seemingly contradictory behaviors.

**Limitations**
Team innovation was rated subjectively by leaders (and self-rated in Study 3) rather than measured objectively (e.g., patents), raising possible social-desirability bias. The limited number of teams (105 and 93) forced separate testing of structural and measurement models, and the cross-lagged design validated causality only for the individual-level portion of the model. The dual-sided measure and the multiplicative ambidexterity operationalization capture only some facets of these constructs.

**Future Research**
Future work could incorporate objective or multi-source measures of team innovation, use quasi-experimental designs to strengthen causal inference for team-level effects, and qualitatively unpack the differing forms of individual and team ambidexterity. Researchers should also test alternative underlying mechanisms (e.g., role modeling), examine when ambidexterity may be undesirable, explore other facets of vision (shared vs. organization-consistent), and clarify the team–individual innovation relationship.
