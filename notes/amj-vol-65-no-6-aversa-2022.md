# The Grand Tour: The Role of Catalyzing Places for Industry Emergence

**Abstract**
We advance the concept of “catalyzing places” that support industry emergence and growth through an ongoing, cyclical process of three forces: “centripetal” (i.e., attracting), “catalyzing” (i.e., reacting), and “centrifugal” (i.e., ejecting). The forces attract communities of practice to the place, expose them to intense, transformational experiences toward entrepreneurship, and induce them to establish their businesses elsewhere. By redeploying the resources and reputation acquired in the place, these scattered communities enact a collective phenomenon of user entrepreneurship and ultimately industry emergence. We claim that the ongoing activities of the place, and the periodic ones of the event, are mutually reinforcing. We advance two novel elements—“portable economies” and “springboard firms”—that, in catalyzing places, exert the antithetical effect of “agglomeration economies” and “anchor firms” in clusters. We discuss our contribution to research on industry emergence, new practices, and user entrepreneurship.

**Research Question**
"What are the mechanisms by which certain places catalyze the emergence of industries in areas away from their birthplace?" The paper asks why some industries emerge away from the locations to which their origin is ascribed—that is, why certain places catalyze industry emergence without producing a local cluster of firms.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: "catalyzing places" (renowned locations that support emergence and growth of non-clustered industries); "communities of practice" bound by shared passion; "portable economies" (valuable, redeployable resources/reputation not bound to the place); "springboard firms" (local firms with a dual role).
- Three sequential forces (aggregate dimensions): centripetal (attracting communities to the place via distinctive resources and personal incentives), catalyzing (reacting—exposing visitors to intense, transformational experiences that convert enthusiasts into entrepreneurs), centrifugal (ejecting—inducing them to establish ventures elsewhere).
- Treatment vs. selection effects: "treatment" (serendipitous/unplanned exposure, more common in incubation) and "selection" (planned exposure once the place is renowned, in emergence/growth).
- Mutual reinforcement: ongoing place activities and the periodic RockMaster event reinforce each other via place-specific emotional energy and spatial/temporal compression.

The process model explains industry emergence as cyclical rather than linear and cumulative: a catalyzing place repeatedly attracts a scattered community of practice, transforms its members' passion into entrepreneurial capability and reputation (portable economies), and then—because springboard firms saturate local demand and the area lacks agglomeration economies—ejects them to found ventures in other geographies. Because the acquired resources are portable and often gain value elsewhere, the result is a collective phenomenon of user entrepreneurship that builds an industry not clustered where it originated, while the place continually re-energizes the community on return visits.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances the concept of catalyzing places and a cyclical, three-force process model of industry emergence, putting "geography back on the map" for industries that are not locally clustered—a phenomenon cluster theory, with its linear-cumulative logic and emphasis on agglomeration economies and anchor firms, cannot fully explain. It introduces "portable economies" and "springboard firms" as the antithetical counterparts of "agglomeration economies" and "anchor firms," and contributes to the literatures on user entrepreneurship, new practices, institutional work (places that shape rather than are shaped by institutions), and field-configuring events.

**Practical Implication**
By understanding the geographical dimensions of industry emergence ex ante, executives and policymakers can take a more proactive, informed approach to phenomena such as clustering, spillovers, and delocalization rather than addressing them ex post. The authors invite executives and policymakers to consider how targeted actions can facilitate the catalyzing role of places and events, creating generative mechanisms that support the transformation of amateur practices into businesses.

**Limitations**
The qualitative design relies on interviews that may carry informant biases or sensemaking, which the authors tried to limit through careful use of archival data, and it cannot provide a test of causality regarding the relative influence of the place versus the event. The model is primarily applicable to the industry "emergence and growth" phase and cannot explain what causes early users to first select a place that later becomes catalyzing; the authors also acknowledge alternative explanations (e.g., the rise of the Internet and modern communication closing geographic and social distances).

**Future Research**
Future studies should use different (e.g., quantitative) techniques to identify and measure new factors, mechanisms, and outcomes and to test the causal nexus between places and events with ad hoc instruments. Researchers should examine the selection and genesis of catalyzing places more systematically, and explore the catalyzing role of community-based "virtual places" (e.g., spanning academia, arts, science, and activism), whose role—amplified by COVID-19—remains unclear.
