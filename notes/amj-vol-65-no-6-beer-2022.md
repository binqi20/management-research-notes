# Meaning, Mission, and Measurement: How Organizational Performance Measurement Shapes Perceptions of Work as Worthy

**Abstract**
A compelling organizational mission can contribute to employees' sense of work as worthy and thereby meaningful. Yet realizing this potential depends on whether and how the mission is conveyed to employees and connected to their day-to-day work, with organizational performance measurement practices playing a critical but poorly understood role. To develop empirically grounded insights into how measurement practices shape individuals' perceptions of work as worthy, we leverage a qualitative, inductive study of two U.K. social enterprises. We find that employees' encounters with measurement practices both affirm and challenge perceptions of work as worthy by influencing whether employees can accomplish their work tasks, see the impact of their work, and have a credible and valued voice in their interpersonal interactions. Building on these findings, we develop a model that theorizes practical, existential, and relational pathways through which measurement encounters create ongoing expansions and contractions of work worthiness. Taken together, our findings and model broaden understandings of the sources and processes of meaningful work, develop a dynamic conception of meaningfulness, and point toward a more agentic view of organizational performance measurement processes.

**Research Question**
How do performance measurement practices shape employees' perceptions of work as worthy? The authors ask how employees' day-to-day encounters with formal, standardized measurement tools affirm or challenge the sense that their work is worthy and thereby meaningful.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core unit / construct: "measurement encounters" — instances in which employees engage with specific measurement tools (KPIs, SROI, outcome star, case studies, blogs) in the context of work activities and interpersonal interactions.
- Practical pathway: encounters either interfere with work tasks (burdensome data collection, irrelevant information) creating practical challenges, or "provide traction" (planning, focusing, deciding) creating practical affirmation of work worthiness.
- Existential pathway: encounters "miss the mark" when tools fail to capture mission or impact (means-ends decoupling), creating existential challenges, or "convey impact" of human/social/environmental change, creating existential affirmation.
- Relational pathway: encounters that "silence" employees (no voice in designing/using tools) create relational challenges, while those that "give voice" foster respect and high-quality connections, creating relational affirmation.
- Emergent outcome: work worthiness as continually expanding and contracting (an "elastic" quality) along the three co-occurring pathways.

The process theorizes that measurement encounters shape perceptions of work as worthy through interdependent practical, existential, and relational mechanisms grounded in whether employees can "do" their work, "see" its impact, and "connect" with others. By grounding the poetics of meaningfulness in the precision of measurement, the model explains how expressive and instrumental aspects of organizational life — usually treated as separate — are interwoven, and how worthiness dynamically contracts and expands as encounters affirm or challenge it.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study brings meaningfulness and measurement together by theorizing mechanisms through which formal performance measurement practices not only challenge but also affirm meaningfulness, broadening the recognized sources of meaningful work to include highly standardized tools (not just job crafting or leader sensegiving). It advances a dynamic, "elastic" conception of work worthiness as continually expanding and contracting, and it points toward a more agentic view of measurement — a shift from measurement as a noun (control) to measuring as a verb (employees actively shaping and benefiting from measurement processes).

**Practical Implication**
Performance measurement practices can foster meaningful work, and collaborative, inclusive approaches to designing measurement tools (engaging employees across levels and functions, plus resource providers and beneficiaries) can produce instruments that better reflect users' needs and help them progress toward the mission. Involving beneficiaries in data collection can convey impact and thereby affirm work worthiness. Practitioners should adopt a more expansive view of measurement that takes account of its expressive, not just instrumental, purposes.

**Limitations**
The two cases are "extreme," revelatory organizations with strong social/environmental missions and well-developed measurement practices, which aids theory building but raises questions about generalizability. The core mechanisms, though likely to apply more broadly, may vary in strength and prevalence across settings — for example, they may be less influential in organizations with more limited and homogenous measurement practices, and the balance of challenge versus affirmation may differ with mission content and employees' attachment to the mission.

**Future Research**
The authors encourage studies that refine and extend the insights across a wider variety of settings and that leverage variation across organizations to identify managerial practices and individual differences (e.g., sensegiving by managers/funders, comfort with quantitative data, expertise and status) that foster worth-affirming versus worth-challenging encounters. They call for deeper investigation of how affirming and challenging experiences interact and cumulate over time, recommending longitudinal studies to examine how past experiences shape present responses and buffer challenges to work worthiness.
