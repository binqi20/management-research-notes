# When Helping Hurts Helpers: Anticipatory versus Reactive Helping, Helper’s Relative Status, and Recipient Self-Threat

**Abstract**
Research on workplace helping has suggested that helpers receive positive outcomes from recipients in return for their help. We argue that this predominantly positive view of recipient reactions to helpers is because the literature has not adequately distinguished the outcomes of reactive helping (i.e., assistance provided in response to a request) from those of anticipatory helping (i.e., assistance offered or provided in advance of being asked). We propose that anticipatory helping, especially from helpers with higher status than the recipient, is more self-threatening to recipients than is reactive helping; hence, recipients are less likely to accept this help and more likely to lower their evaluations of both the helper’s performance and their relationship with the helper. We find support for these hypotheses with four studies and one supplemental study that use experimental and field methodologies across a range of work contexts and social exchange relationships. Because our findings imply that both peer- and higher-status employees should withhold anticipatory help, which is impractical and potentially detrimental, we identify how these helpers can instead mitigate the negative effects of anticipatory helping by signaling a more balanced social exchange relationship with the recipient. We discuss how our findings expand research on helping, social exchange, and status.

**Research Question**
Why and when does workplace helping lead to *negative* rather than positive reactions toward the helper? Specifically, how does anticipatory (vs. reactive) helping affect recipients' self-threat, help acceptance, and performance and relational evaluations of the helper, and how do the helper's relative status and the balance of the social exchange relationship moderate these effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Helping type (anticipatory vs. reactive helping)
- DV(s): Recipient's willingness to accept help; performance evaluation of the helper (including bonus allocation); relational evaluation (likeability) of the helper
- Mediator: Recipient self-threat (perceived challenge to competence and status)
- Moderators: Helper's relative status (lower vs. peer vs. higher than recipient); balance of the social exchange relationship (and a signaled past/future reciprocity balance in Study 4)

Integrating social exchange theory with self-affirmation theory, the authors argue that anticipatory help (offered without being requested) signals to recipients that their competence is doubted or their status is challenged, generating self-threat. Threatened recipients defensively protect their self-worth by refusing or dismissing the help and by derogating the helper's performance and likeability. Because people weight evaluations from higher-status others more heavily, these self-threatening effects are stronger when the helper has higher (or peer) rather than lower status. Recipients who perceive (Study 3) or are shown a signal of (Study 4) a balanced, mutually reciprocal exchange relationship experience less self-threat, which mitigates the negative downstream consequences of anticipatory helping.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper distinguishes anticipatory from reactive helping and shows that the helping literature's "helping pays off" conclusion applies primarily to reactive helping, offering a self-threat explanation for when positive helping turns negative. It extends social exchange theory by specifying why and when a positively valenced act (anticipatory helping) elicits a negative response rather than reciprocity. It advances status research by identifying an exception to the premise that higher-status actors enjoy greater latitude—here, higher-status helpers provoke stronger defensive reactions when helping in anticipatory fashion.

**Practical Implication**
Lower-status employees can offer anticipatory help—a more assertive, socially risky behavior—without triggering defensive recipient reactions, giving them an avenue to contribute without backlash. Peer- and higher-status helpers should pause before offering unrequested help and can reduce its threat by signaling a balanced exchange (e.g., framing help as reciprocating past or future assistance). Leaders can build helping norms by encouraging and rewarding help-seeking, help-giving, and help-accepting.

**Limitations**
Absolute self-threat levels were low (though consistent with prior research and differential across conditions), partly because people are reluctant to admit feeling threatened. The field study (Study 2) had a small sample (n=64 dyads) and operationalized relative status as a difference score, raising reliability and generalizability concerns. Outcomes were rated by direct recipients rather than third-party observers, which could alter the effects.

**Future Research**
Future work could use manipulations of the psychological process to circumvent recipients' reluctance to self-report threat, and compare recipient versus third-party observer ratings of helper outcomes. Researchers could apply the newly developed anticipatory and reactive helping scales in field settings, examine additional moderators (e.g., recipient workload, helping norms, interactional justice, whether help is seen as controlling), and study how recipients value reactive versus anticipatory help over time.
