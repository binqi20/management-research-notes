# Creating and Sustaining Stakeholder Emotional Resonance with Organizational Identity in Social Mission-Driven Organizations

**Abstract**
How do senior managers of social mission-driven organizations build and sustain stakeholders’ emotional resonance with organizational identity beliefs over time in the face of repeated existential threats? This is an important question, given the dependence of many such organizations on external stakeholders who provide the resources necessary for survival. In this paper, we investigate the case of Solidum, a philanthropic organization devoted to poverty causes. Drawing on ethnographic, interview, and archival data over 20 years, we develop a process model showing how senior managers may create and sustain stakeholder emotional resonance through three practices of emotional resonance work: (a) building emotional bridges, (b) enrolling stakeholders in collective soul-searching, and (c) materializing an appealing identity symbol. We show that stakeholder emotional resonance needs to be continually renewed and reshaped in the face of ongoing challenges associated with macro-organizational trends and the routinization of existing practices that can result in the dissipation of emotional resonance over time. The paper contributes to the literature on organizational identity maintenance by drawing attention to the active managerial work required to sustain stakeholder emotional resonance over time to allow mission-driven organizations to survive and prosper.

**Research Question**
How do senior managers of social mission-driven organizations build and sustain stakeholders’ emotional resonance with organizational identity beliefs over time in the face of repeated existential threats?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: stakeholder emotional resonance with organizational identity beliefs; tension between preserving a "caring" core identity/mission and ceding to corporate donors' pressure for "designated donations" (mission-drift risk).
- Practices (emotional resonance work): (1) building emotional bridges — seeding resonance via direct immersion of stakeholders in mission activities, stimulating identification; (2) enrolling in collective soul-searching — engaging central stakeholders in sharing existential worries and joint deliberation; (3) materializing an appealing identity symbol — rendering identity beliefs tangible to propagate resonance to a wider community.
- Triggering conditions: recurring existential/identity threats (strategic CSR, financial crisis, generational change), each provoking interest in "designation."
- Effects (proximal to distal): displayed emotions (compassion, shock, enthusiasm); expressed alignment/commitment to Solidum's cause and identity; shifts in wider-community donations and participation.
- Process dynamic: the three practices have distinct rhythms, can fall out of sync, and resonance dissipates through routinization and macro trends, requiring continual renewal.

Through interaction with central corporate stakeholders, senior managers iteratively seed, amplify, and propagate emotional resonance with the organization's identity beliefs. Identification is the underpinning mechanism: bridging stimulates it, enrolling consolidates it, and materializing gives stakeholders tangible tools to act as "evangelists" toward the wider community. Because resonance is fleeting and dissipates over time, this "emotional resonance work" is a never-ending cycle punctuated by recurring existential threats.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the literature on organizational identity maintenance and stakeholder relations by unpacking the active, ongoing managerial work required to sustain stakeholder emotional resonance with identity beliefs over long periods, rather than at single moments of crisis or pivot. Its core contribution is identifying three interrelated practices of "stakeholder emotional resonance work" — bridging, enrolling, and materializing — and theorizing how they operate together (with distinct rhythms) to seed, amplify, and propagate emotional resonance, framing this as a form of social-symbolic work that must be continually renewed.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of social mission-driven organizations can engage central stakeholders' emotions to build support for core identity beliefs by bringing them into direct contact with the mission, enrolling them in collective soul-searching, and materializing identity in concrete symbols. They should reflect on the trade-off between acceding to immediate stakeholder demands and preserving the organization's valued mission, and recognize that emotional resonance is powerful but fleeting and must be continually renewed to stay fresh.

**Limitations**
The study was conducted in the specific setting of a single philanthropic organization, and access to data was uneven across time (Periods 1 and 3 were more intense and better documented than Period 2). The stratified, closely intertwined nature of Solidum's stakeholder network may be somewhat idiosyncratic, and the second researcher's involvement as an action researcher in Period 1 required reflexivity, though the authors argue it did not substantively bias the findings.

**Future Research**
The authors hope further research will build on these ideas to develop studies of emotional resonance work in other social-mission-based organizations, including settings where key stakeholders are more widely dispersed and less intertwined with the organization. They also suggest examining how emotional resonance work might be enacted to promote entirely new ventures and how recurring tension points might be anticipated or avoided.
