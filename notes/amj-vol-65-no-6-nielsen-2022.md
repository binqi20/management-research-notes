# It’s Not Always Sunny in Relationally Rich Jobs: The Influence of Negative Beneficiary Contact

**Abstract**
Contact with beneficiaries has been described as an important job characteristic for shaping perceptions about the social value of work; however, little is known about how to navigate experiences in which contact with beneficiaries is negative, which can explicitly undermine the conclusion that work is socially valuable. We draw from two tenets of social information processing theory to propose that negative contact with beneficiaries has a dual effect on employees. Whereas negative contact may make employees perceive low social worth, it may simultaneously lead employees to believe they are engaging in self-sacrifice for a worthy cause—a relatively positive justification of such experiences. We investigated these ideas in three studies. In Study 1, a three-wave survey of registered nurses and their supervisors supported the hypothesized dual effect. Further consistent with our theorizing, the effect of perceived self-sacrifice on job satisfaction and performance was contingent on coworker emotional support: with higher support, perceived self-sacrifice exhibited a null relationship with satisfaction and a positive relationship with performance; with lower support, these effects were negative. In Study 2, we again studied nurses using an experimental vignette method, showing that negative contact exhibits a causal effect on employee perceptions, and that negative contact is more likely to lead to perceived self-sacrifice when the contact is attributed to the nature of the work versus one’s own performance. In Study 3, a two-wave survey of people from various occupations replicated the effects of negative contact on perceived social worth and perceived self-sacrifice. Moreover, the effect of negative contact on sacrifice was contingent on affective commitment to beneficiaries.

**Research Question**
How does negative contact with beneficiaries affect employees' perceptions of the social value of their work, and under what conditions can employees derive positive meaning from such negative contact? Specifically, when is negative beneficiary contact rationalized as self-sacrifice, and when does that rationalization produce greater satisfaction and performance rather than less?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Negative beneficiary contact (also positive beneficiary contact as a comparison predictor); manipulated negative-contact and attribution conditions (Study 2)
- DV(s): Job satisfaction, task performance (job performance), helping behavior; with perceived social worth and perceived self-sacrifice as proximal outcomes/mechanisms
- Mediators: Perceived social worth (information-saliency path) and perceived self-sacrifice (rationalization path) linking negative contact to employee outcomes
- Moderators: Coworker emotional support (self-sacrifice → satisfaction/performance, Study 1); attribution for contact — nature-of-work vs. own performance (negative contact → self-sacrifice, Study 2); affective commitment to beneficiaries (negative contact → self-sacrifice, Study 3)

Drawing on two distinct tenets of social information processing theory—information saliency and the relatively neglected rationalization tenet—the authors argue that negative beneficiary contact conveys salient information lowering perceived social worth, while simultaneously prompting employees to rationalize the work as self-sacrifice for a worthy cause. Because self-sacrifice carries both a sense of victimhood and a sense of contribution, its effect on satisfaction and performance is ambiguous and depends on coworker emotional support, which lets employees focus on the contribution side rather than the cost. Rationalizing contact as self-sacrifice is more likely when employees attribute it to the nature of the work (an external, credible attribution) and when they feel strong affective commitment to beneficiaries.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends relational job design and the meaningful-work literature by theorizing and testing the effects of negative (rather than positive) beneficiary contact on the perceived social value of work, surfacing perceived self-sacrifice as a key mechanism. It highlights a duality in relational job design—contact both makes work's value salient and exposes employees to value-threatening information—and shifts the meaningful-work literature from a "realization" perspective toward an understudied "justification" perspective (Lepisto & Pratt, 2017). It also reconceptualizes self-sacrifice as an interpersonal cognitive frame born of justification rather than a collective leadership behavior, and shows affective mechanisms (commitment, coworker support) are central to how employees make meaning of negative contact.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should help frontline employees develop justifiable accounts of negative work experiences (e.g., a self-sacrifice account) and cultivate supportive coworker relationships so that self-sacrifice translates into performance rather than disillusionment. Because self-sacrifice interpretations are credible only when employees are affectively committed to beneficiaries and when contact is genuinely inherent to the work, managers should foster such commitment and avoid manipulatively encouraging external attributions for poor performance; organizations weighing job redesign that increases beneficiary contact should weigh the costs of exposure to negative contact against its motivational benefits.

**Limitations**
Studies 1 and 2 were confined to nursing—a gendered, calling-prototypic occupation with likely range restriction on affective commitment—limiting generalizability and conclusions about how men are affected. Study 1's design limits causal inference about the effects of self-sacrifice (Studies 2 and 3 replicated only the first stage of the model, not the self-sacrifice → outcomes effects), and the close spacing of the first and third surveys may have limited observable change in job satisfaction.

**Future Research**
Future work could replicate the self-sacrifice → outcome effects in other contexts using longitudinal or laboratory designs, test whether negative contact and perceived self-sacrifice are reciprocally related, and examine demographic moderators such as occupational status or wage level and the reasons men report higher social worth and self-sacrifice. Researchers might also examine cross-level interactions between job-level and beneficiary-specific attributions, distinguish primary from secondary beneficiaries, study the content of coworker messages, and clarify the job types and performance metrics for which perceived social worth predicts performance.
