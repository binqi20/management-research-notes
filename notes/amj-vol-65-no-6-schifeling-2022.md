# Advancing Reform: Embedded Activism to Develop Climate Solutions

**Abstract**
Activists increasingly seek to influence organizations that also espouse support for social movement goals, encouraging the use of collaborative tactics. While there has been growing research on insider activists who import social movement resources, little is known about how internal activism might operate through a coordinated and collaborative approach with external social movement organizations, which we refer to as embedded activism. Likewise, collaborative activists encounter organizations with a wide range of prior reform experience, and the resulting “opportunity structure” for collaboration is not well understood. We investigate how a network of embedded activists can operate to advance reform efforts across diverse organizations. Our analysis combines surveys, interviews, and archival records from the Environmental Defense Fund’s Climate Corps program, which embeds graduate students in partner organizations to advance climate change reforms. Embedded activists accomplish this work by matching external resources with the organizational context in order to generate a fertile mixture of support and ambiguity and create new solutions. External resources are especially important for organizations that are at the extremes of prior issue development.

**Research Question**
How can a network of embedded activists—who coordinate and collaborate with external social movement organizations—operate to advance internal reform across organizations that differ in their prior issue development? Specifically, how do embedded activists use movement resources to navigate the tension between organizational support and issue ambiguity to create new solutions?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: organizational support (resources to act) vs. issue ambiguity (opportunities to act); the "opportunity structure" for collaborative (vs. contentious) activism.
- Contributing conditions (organizational context): new initiative, program newcomer, open-ended project, small organizational scale.
- Movement resources: external search (intensity of EDF resource use) and external focus (external vs. internal advice seeking).
- Outcome: high solution creation (additions to project scope from initial proposal to final report).
- Process / configurations (fsQCA): three equifinal groupings—Low Ambiguity (catalyzing established issue understandings via external resources), Moderate Ambiguity (navigating organizational fit via internal focus), High Ambiguity (building legitimacy via external search plus internal focus).

Embedded activists span movement–partner boundaries, matching external movement resources to the partner organization's balance of support and ambiguity. Where prior issue development is high (low ambiguity), external resources catalyze established efforts by introducing new perspectives; under moderate ambiguity, fellows turn internally to sell the issue and cultivate allies; under high ambiguity, fellows combine external search for knowledge and exemplars with an internal focus that legitimizes proposed solutions. The configurational analysis shows external resources are especially consequential at the extremes of prior issue development, and the inverse-outcome analysis confirms the absence of external resources characterizes low solution creation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces "embedded activism" to characterize activists who are neither insiders nor outsiders but cross movement–partner boundaries, combining knowledge of organizational opportunity structures with access to a wider range of movement resources. It develops a revised theory of opportunity structures fitted to collaborative (rather than contentious) tactics, centered on the organization's prior issue development, and theorizes a non-linear tension between support and ambiguity that integrates issue-selling and proactivity research.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders should cultivate external connections—with SMOs, professional associations, and alumni networks—to empower internal climate-change advocates, especially when their organizations are either new to an issue or entrenched in an established approach. These ties supply not only motivation and social support but also knowledge, tools, and expertise that can be matched to the specific organizational context to catalyze reform.

**Limitations**
The study is an initial, exploratory analysis of a single prominent program (EDF Climate Corps), so generalization is bounded to collaborative initiatives that cross movement–partner boundaries and seek internal (rather than external-facing) change. fsQCA imposes tight limits on the number of conditions, so influential features such as goal alignment, openness to change, and progressive values were not modeled; the inverse-outcome findings rely on a relaxed consistency threshold and are described as suggestive.

**Future Research**
Field experiments could randomize the use of external resources to test whether they are more effective at high or low ambiguity than at moderate ambiguity. Future work could examine when temporary organizational members (embedded activists, consultants, professionals) act as issue sellers, study the boundary work that lets organizations admit activists, and investigate the link between reform ideas and implementation as well as alternative ways of conceptualizing and measuring reform depth and impact.
