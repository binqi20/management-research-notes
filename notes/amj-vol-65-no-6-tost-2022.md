# Different Roots, Different Fruits: Gender-Based Differences in Cultural Narratives about Perceived Discrimination Produce Divergent Psychological Consequences

**Abstract**
We further argue that these effects contribute to a reduction of well-being among members of both genders, with the negative effect on well-being being more pronounced among women than among men. We examine these predictions in five studies of working adults. We discuss the implications for research on perceptions of discrimination and for organizations seeking to reduce the negative consequences of perceptions of discrimination.

**Research Question**
Do women and men differ in their understandings of the nature of discrimination against people of their gender, and do these different understandings lead to differences in workplace-related consequences for women and men?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived workplace gender discrimination (and, in Study 3, an experimentally manipulated cultural narrative: patriarchal vs. reverse gender discrimination vs. control setback)
- DV(s): Self-efficacy, sense of belonging, and gestalt well-being
- Mediators: Self-efficacy and belonging (mediating the discrimination → gestalt well-being path)
- Moderators: Gender (woman vs. man); race/ethnicity tested exploratorily as a non-significant moderator

The authors argue that women and men appeal to different dominant cultural narratives to make sense of perceived gender discrimination: women interpret it through a patriarchal narrative (derogation of abilities, ascribed domestic roles, sexual objectification), whereas men interpret it through a reverse-gender-discrimination narrative (diversity/affirmative-action efforts that favor women). Because the patriarchal narrative impugns competence while the reverse-discrimination narrative does not, perceived discrimination lowers self-efficacy only among women, while lowering belonging among both genders. These cognitions in turn reduce gestalt well-being, with a stronger (more consistent) effect for women because two mechanisms operate (self-efficacy and belonging) rather than one (belonging).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces a theoretical framework explaining when and why the psychological effects of perceived gender discrimination diverge between advantaged and disadvantaged groups, addressing the long-noted absence of a well-supported explanation (and the mixed support for the rejection-identification/pervasiveness account). Its second contribution is importing the construct of cultural narratives into the conversation on perceived discrimination, showing that understanding outcomes requires attending not just to the extent of perceived discrimination but to the substantive meaning individuals assign to it.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders who understand the dominant cultural narratives operating in their organization can better identify and eliminate managerial behaviors that elicit perceived gender discrimination; because narratives vary, leaders should gather qualitative data rather than assume which narrative dominates. Interventions should bolster both men's and women's sense of belonging while additionally bolstering self-efficacy among women.

**Limitations**
Study 1's sample was too small to examine how non-dominant narratives varied across response types (personal, observed, hypothetical). Study 3, though experimental, relied on lower-stakes vignettes, and Studies 4a/4b are subject to common method bias (mitigated by the dichotomous, nonsubjective gender-interaction design). The work focuses on people identifying as women or men and excludes trans/nonbinary individuals, and statistical power was insufficient to confidently detect race/ethnicity moderation.

**Future Research**
Future work should probe why the gestalt well-being effect for men was inconsistent across Studies 4a/4b, examine the cultural narratives that nonbinary and transgender individuals adopt, and extend the framework to other forms of discrimination (race, religion, age, disability, LGBTQ) and to intersectional combinations. Larger, more highly powered samples are needed to examine race/ethnicity differences, and intervention studies could test the effectiveness of narrative-aware managerial training.
