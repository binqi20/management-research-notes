# The Social Aftershocks of Voice: An Investigation of Employees’ Affective and Interpersonal Reactions after Speaking Up

**Abstract**
We draw on cognitive-motivational-relational theory to build a theoretical model that outlines how speaking up affects voicers’ emotions and subsequent social behavior. Across three studies—an experimental pilot study, a daily within-person study of employee–coworker dyads, and a preregistered experiment—we test our proposal that promotive voice elicits pride due to a sense of social accomplishment, whereas prohibitive voice elicits anxiety due to a sense of social uncertainty. We demonstrate that these feelings of pride and anxiety have diverging effects on voicers’ tendency to withdraw from social interaction during the rest of the day. In turn, these diverging effects on voicers’ interpersonal avoidance influence voicers’ daily interpersonal citizenship behaviors. We further propose that recipients of voice have the potential to “hijack” voicers’ affective appraisals in a manner that can amplify or attenuate their emotional reactions and subsequent social behavior. Our results disentangle the complex experience of speaking up and provide novel insights into how voicers and organizations can maximize the benefits of voice while minimizing its harmful social side effects.

**Research Question**
How does speaking up shape the voicer's own emotions and subsequent social behavior — specifically, when does voice generate pride versus anxiety, how do these emotions affect interpersonal avoidance and downstream interpersonal citizenship behavior, and how does a recipient's reception of the voice alter these appraisal processes?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Promotive voice; prohibitive voice (manipulated in the experiments, daily-measured in the ESM)
- DV(s): Interpersonal avoidance (proximal); interpersonal citizenship behavior (distal terminal outcome)
- Mediators: Pride (from promotive voice) and anxiety (from prohibitive voice) as primary appraisal-driven emotions; interpersonal avoidance as the serial behavioral mediator linking emotions to citizenship
- Moderators: Voice reception (positive vs. negative vs. no reception) "hijacks" the appraisal, amplifying/attenuating pride and anxiety

Grounded in cognitive-motivational-relational (appraisal) theory, the affective process is modeled as a decision tree: promotive voice is appraised as a beneficial, credit-worthy event that elicits pride, whose expansive action tendency reduces social withdrawal; prohibitive voice is appraised as an uncertain social threat that elicits anxiety, whose restrictive action tendency increases withdrawal. Because pride broadens and anxiety narrows social engagement, the two voice types exert opposite serial indirect effects on interpersonal citizenship behavior. A recipient's response operates as a relational lever that can redirect the appraisal down a different branch, intensifying or dampening the dominant emotion.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper resolves a standing tension in the voice literature — whether speaking up makes voicers feel accomplished or anxious — by showing that promotive and prohibitive voice branch into distinct emotions (pride vs. anxiety) rather than a single mixed affective state, using episodic within-person theory rather than retrospective between-person assessment. It reveals that one discretionary behavior (prohibitive voice) can inhibit another (interpersonal citizenship behavior) through anxiety-driven avoidance, extending the nomological network of voice. It also extends cognitive-motivational-relational theory by identifying recipients as relational agents who can "hijack" the appraisal process and redirect which emotion is elicited.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations benefit from promotive voice not only through the ideas communicated but indirectly via increased interpersonal citizenship after speaking up, so managers need not respond enthusiastically to every idea — avoiding a negative reception is what matters, since both positive and no reception preserved voicer pride. Rather than suppressing prohibitive voice (which risks missing critical problems), managers should respond constructively and in a positively framed way to defuse the anxiety it provokes.

**Limitations**
The authors focused on constructive voice (promotive and prohibitive per Liang et al., 2012), so other forms such as supportive, defensive, and destructive voice were not examined and may elicit different appraisal processes. The framework targets the impact of voice events rather than the anticipation of voicing, leaving pre-voice affect (e.g., anticipatory pride or hope) unexamined. The studies capture short-term, same-day affective and behavioral consequences rather than cumulative or long-term dynamics.

**Future Research**
Researchers could apply the affective framework to other voice types (supportive, defensive, destructive) to see how they shape voicers' emotions and behavior, and examine the pre-voice phase, including the role of hope and anticipatory appraisals. Longitudinal growth models could trace how discrete voice events accumulate to shape employees' overall propensity to voice and the potential overlap between avoidance and silence over time.
