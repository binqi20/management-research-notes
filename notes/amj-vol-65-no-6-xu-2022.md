# Leader Perfectionism—Friend or Foe of Employee Creativity? Locus of Control as a Key Contingency

**Abstract**
This research aims to explain whether leader perfectionism toward employees fosters or hinders employee creativity. From a self-regulation perspective, we theorize that depending on employees' locus of control, leader perfectionism can influence two regulatory states of employees (i.e., engagement and emotional exhaustion) linearly or curvilinearly, which in turn affect their creativity in opposite directions. In a lab experiment and a multisource, multiwave field study, we found that for internals, leader perfectionism had a curvilinear effect on their engagement (but no effect on emotional exhaustion) and subsequent creativity such that the effect was positive but became weaker when leader perfectionism was extreme. By contrast, we found partial support across the two studies that for externals, leader perfectionism had a positive effect on their emotional exhaustion (but no effect on engagement), which undermined their creativity. We discuss the theoretical contributions of this research and its practical implications for organizations.

**Research Question**
Does a leader's perfectionism toward employees foster or hinder employee creativity, and through what self-regulatory mechanisms (engagement and emotional exhaustion), for whom (employee locus of control), and to what degree (linear vs. curvilinear effects of perfectionism level)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Leader perfectionism toward employees (manipulated in Study 1 as no / perfectionism / extreme perfectionism; employee-perceived continuous variable in Study 2), modeled with linear and quadratic (curvilinear) terms.
- DV(s): Employee creativity (Study 1: consensual assessment of an idea-generation proposal by expert judges; Study 2: leader-rated creativity using Zhou & George's 13-item scale).
- Mediators: Employee engagement and employee emotional exhaustion (two opposing self-regulatory states).
- Moderators: Employee locus of control (internal vs. external).

Drawing on self-regulation theory, the authors argue that leader perfectionism creates a discrepancy between current and desired work outcomes that drives employees to regulate their efforts (raising engagement) while also depleting personal resources (raising emotional exhaustion). Whether these states translate into higher or lower creativity depends on employees' locus of control: internals, who believe they control outcomes, convert perfectionistic challenge into engagement (with diminishing returns at extreme levels) while being buffered from exhaustion; externals, who attribute outcomes to external forces, experience leader perfectionism mainly as a stressor that escalates emotional exhaustion and undermines creativity. The predicted negative downturn for internals at extreme perfectionism was only partially borne out across the two studies.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconciles conflicting predictions about leader perfectionism and employee creativity by showing it is a double-edged sword whose effect runs through two opposing self-regulatory states (engagement and emotional exhaustion) contingent on employees' locus of control and the level of perfectionism. It extends the perfectionism literature beyond self-oriented perfectionism by theorizing leader (other-oriented) perfectionism as an interpersonal leader characteristic, and extends the locus of control literature by demonstrating the motivational limit of internal locus of control when perfectionism becomes extreme.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders should manage their level of perfectionism, recognizing it as a double-edged sword: even for internals the favorable effect weakens or vanishes when perfectionism is extreme, so leaders should avoid standards far beyond employees' capabilities and tolerate inevitable errors. Leaders should monitor employees—especially externals—for exhaustion and provide resources, timely positive feedback, and instrumental and socioemotional support, while encouraging trial-and-error and constructive negative feedback to sustain creativity.

**Limitations**
Because locus of control was measured rather than manipulated, causal inference about the moderator is limited. In the field study, leaders rated subordinates' creativity, raising potential rating bias (the authors ran a supplementary experiment suggesting perfectionism did not systematically bias ratings). The two studies did not fully replicate each other—particularly the indirect effect on externals' creativity via emotional exhaustion—and both were conducted in China, a high-power-distance culture, which may limit cross-cultural generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future work could manipulate locus of control to strengthen causal inference and identify interventions that shape employees' sense of control; obtain objective or coworker/customer ratings of creativity to mitigate leader rating bias; examine temporal components, contextual factors, and research settings to explain cross-study inconsistencies; test whether employees in low-power-distance cultures react more negatively to extreme leader perfectionism; and study the effects of leaders' self-oriented perfectionism on employees.
