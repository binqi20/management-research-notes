# Fast or Slow: How Temporal Work Design Shapes Experienced Passage of Time and Job Performance

**Abstract**
Experienced passage of time, the extent to which employees perceive the passage of work time as being fast or slow, is a fundamental aspect of work experience. We identify two novel temporal work design characteristics that can speed up employees' experienced passage of time: temporal predictability and task segmentation. Jobs with high temporal predictability do not make employees go through uncertain wait times before embarking on their next task. High task segmentation occurs when a large chunk of work time is segmented by categorically different temporal markers. We tested a model in which temporal predictability and task segmentation affect experienced passage of time, which in turn influences job performance, with five studies: two experiments that established the internal validity of temporal predictability and task segmentation (Studies 1a and 1b), a naturalistic field study in a factory that investigated the natural consequences of distinct temporal work design (Study 2), an organizational field study that constructively replicated the model using a sample of knowledge workers and their supervisors (Study 3), and an online survey in which we connected our model with the broader work design literature (Study 4). Altogether, the studies support a new temporal approach to work design.

**Research Question**
Is it possible to alter employees' experienced passage of time via work design—and, if it is possible, are there any performance benefits of doing so?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Temporal predictability (extent to which a job minimizes unpredictable waiting periods before a task or event); task segmentation (extent to which a large chunk of work time is divided into smaller blocks by categorically distinct temporal markers).
- DV(s): Job performance (objective task performance in Studies 1a/1b; archival ratings in Study 2; supervisor ratings in Study 3; self-rated in Study 4).
- Mediators: Experienced passage of time (perceiving work time as passing fast vs. slow).
- Moderators: None hypothesized; the five established job-characteristics dimensions (autonomy, task variety, task significance, task identity, feedback) and their critical psychological states are modeled as controls/competing mechanisms in Study 4.

Drawing on the attentional-gate perspective, the paper argues that temporal and non-temporal information compete for the same neural resources, so when work design directs attention away from temporal cues, employees process the passage of time less and therefore experience time as passing faster. Temporal predictability removes uncertain "fore-periods" that draw attention to time, and task segmentation inserts categorically different markers that restore diminishing task attention before it drifts to temporal cues. The resulting faster, more pleasant time experience is treated as an intermediate work experience that, in turn, improves job performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends work design theory beyond enrichment-based mechanisms (the job characteristics model and its relational and motivational extensions) by introducing two attention-based temporal work characteristics—temporal predictability and task segmentation—that shape outcomes not by enriching jobs but by redirecting attention away from temporal cues. It establishes experienced passage of time as a previously neglected intermediate work experience linking work design to performance, and connects work design research to the emerging management literature on time.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can design work time, not just work content, to help employees stay focused: eliminate or reduce uncertain wait times (e.g., giving idle "standby" workers other tasks), design better-scheduled meetings, and punctuate the workday with categorically different temporal markers/breaks timed to when attention wanes. Because these levers are flexible and cheap relative to job enrichment, they are especially valuable for simple, standardized, routinized jobs that are hard to enrich, but segmentation should be customized and used cautiously for complex jobs.

**Limitations**
The authors identify only two temporal work characteristics rather than an exhaustive set, and they do not examine boundary conditions (e.g., individual differences such as mindfulness, or whether segmentation is self-initiated versus externally imposed). Although the design attempts to establish the temporal precedence of experienced passage of time over performance, reverse causality—performance shaping experienced time—cannot be fully ruled out. The two experiments (Studies 1a, 1b) are relatively weak in external validity.

**Future Research**
Future work should explore additional temporal work characteristics that alter experienced passage of time (e.g., unusual schedules such as night shifts for ER doctors or air traffic controllers, including chronotype differences), investigate boundary conditions and moderators such as mindfulness and whether segmentation is self-initiated or imposed, and further disentangle the causal ordering between experienced passage of time and job performance.
