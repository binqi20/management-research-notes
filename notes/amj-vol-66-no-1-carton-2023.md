# Painting a Clear Picture while Seeing the Big Picture: When and Why Leaders Overcome the Trade-Off Between Concreteness and Scale

**Abstract**
One of the most effective ways leaders can promote change is by generating visions with image-based rhetoric ("make children smile"). By conjuring visual snapshots of the future, leaders paint a portrait of what their organizations can one day achieve. It would thus stand to reason that leaders who naturally think and speak in terms of picture-like detail (a "concrete orientation") would promote more organizational change than those who are inclined to think vaguely (an "abstract orientation"). Yet, research has established that individuals with a concrete orientation tend to focus on short-term, narrow details (e.g., small features of a single product) rather than long-term visions requiring the coordinated effort of all employees. To determine how and when concrete-thinking leaders induce large-scale change, we integrate theory on attention, organizational hierarchy, and construal. We predict that leaders who have a concrete orientation will promote change by redirecting their attention toward long-term visions of the future, if their organizations have strong, rather than weak, hierarchies. By contrast, hierarchical strength will have no such effect on leaders with an abstract orientation. We test these predictions in an archival study of CEOs and then examine the attention-based process that helps explain this effect in a preregistered experiment.

**Research Question**
When and why do leaders with a concrete cognitive orientation — who naturally focus on short-term, narrow details — overcome that predisposition and instead spearhead large-scale organizational change? Specifically, how does organizational hierarchy interact with leader concreteness to shape attention toward large-scale goals and, in turn, organizational change?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO concrete (vs. abstract) orientation (dispositional, measured via linguistic analysis of earnings-call transcripts in Study 1 and the Reyt & Wiesenfeld 2015 scale in Study 2).
- DV(s): Organizational change, indexed as the z-scored average of R&D spending, acquisitions, and property sales (Study 1) or CEO decisions on the same three dimensions (Study 2).
- Mediator: CEO attention to large-scale (long-term and broad-scope) goals.
- Moderator: Organizational hierarchical strength (number of management layers and concentration of authority; five operationalizations in Study 1, manipulated in Study 2).

The decoupling mechanism runs as follows: leaders with a concrete orientation normally co-attend to short-term, narrow problems because concreteness and small-scale focus tend to covary in cognition. When such leaders helm organizations with strong hierarchies, however, structural distance from day-to-day operations and a wide span of control redirect their finite attention toward long-term, organization-wide problems. Because concrete thinkers can calibrate attentional shifts in discernible increments, they translate this redirected attention into pursuit of large-scale change (R&D, acquisitions, property sales). Abstract-oriented leaders, lacking the same precise sense of timeline and scope, are not similarly affected by hierarchy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances construal level theory by introducing the notion of cognitive decoupling — showing that concreteness of thought and scale of attention, long presumed to covary, can be deliberately reconfigured by organizational structure. It builds a bridge between macro-organizational theory (organizational hierarchy) and organizational psychology (attention and construal) using attention as a linking pin, and it challenges person–job fit assumptions by showing that the leaders with the greatest change potential are initially misaligned with the top-leader role until structure redirects their attention.

**Practical Implication**
Boards selecting CEOs should evaluate person–job fit not just on individual cognitive style but on the interaction between style and organizational hierarchy: concrete-thinking leaders are better positioned to drive substantial change in strongly hierarchical organizations, while flat organizations may need a different selection logic. Executive search firms that already assess leader concreteness should also encourage clients to account for hierarchical structure when making hiring and promotion decisions.

**Limitations**
Study 1 lacks complete panel data, preventing fine-grained tracking of changes over time, and data for The Official Board variables were collected in a different year than Compustat/ExecuComp data, raising temporal-equivocality concerns. The concreteness measure captures speech rather than thought directly. Study 2's predicted interaction on change itself was not significant (only on the attention mediator), and most participants lacked CEO experience, limiting generalization to the Study 1 population.

**Future Research**
Future scholars can examine how powerfully and consistently cognitive decoupling can occur across contexts, and integrate the present findings on attention redirection with prior research on how concreteness aids collective-action mobilization during change implementation. Researchers can also more systematically account for the evolving interplay between hierarchy and concreteness as the change process moves from enactment through implementation phases.
