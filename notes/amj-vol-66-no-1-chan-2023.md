# The Role of Discernment and Modulation in Enacting Occupational Values: How Career Advising Professionals Navigate Tensions with Clients

**Abstract**
Enacting occupational values is vital to expert professionals' solidarity and sense of purpose. Yet, many professionals face audiences in their relational contexts—especially powerful clients—who can hold incongruent values and may threaten professionals' jurisdictional control. How can experts enact their values without jeopardizing their jurisdictional control amid clients holding incongruent values? We examine career advisers in undergraduate business schools, whose occupational values often contrasted with values common among their student clients. Through an ethnography of one school's career advisers, combined with interviews of such advisers throughout the United States, we find that advisers navigated interactions by discerning student values and accordingly modulating their value-enactment practices through masking, moderating, or magnifying their values. This allowed advisers to uphold their jurisdictional control when facing students exhibiting incongruent values, while enacting their values with students exhibiting unclear or congruent values. We contribute to the relational perspective on occupations and professions by positing how discernment and modulation help experts navigate relational tensions by recognizing and drawing on intraclientele heterogeneity, unpacking how professionals might not entirely resist or change amid incongruence but instead pursue a more mixed approach, and highlighting when and how experts mask or moderate rather than overtly enact their values.

**Research Question**
How can expert professionals enact their occupational values without jeopardizing their jurisdictional control when working with powerful clients who hold incongruent values?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: occupational values (purpose, individualized fit) vs. client values (wealth, prestige, social approval); jurisdictional control as the legitimacy and autonomy with which experts hold their task domain.
- Triggering conditions: skewed but heterogeneous clientele, advisers' inability to refuse clients, and downstream threats to jurisdictional control (negative student feedback to deans, school-ranking surveys, parental complaints).
- Discernment: advisers notice cues in the interaction (a student's declared career commitment, type of questions, openness to exploration) to infer whether the client's values are incongruent, unclear, or congruent.
- Modulation — three value-enactment approaches: (a) value-masking when values are discerned as incongruent — impartial career-attainment help, encouraging future visits, exclusively pragmatic resources; (b) value-moderating when values are unclear — gentle reframing, raising open questions, using neutral resources; (c) value-magnifying when values are congruent — undermining extrinsic pressure, encouraging alternative career paths, drawing on values-rich resources.
- Emergent outcome: experts uphold jurisdictional control while still enacting occupational values whenever the client situation permits; negative cases occur when discernment or modulation fails.

Across one ethnographic site and 48 interviews at other U.S. business schools, advisers navigated relational tensions not by abandoning their values nor by overtly resisting incongruent clients, but by reading each student's enacted values and adjusting how visibly and strongly to enact their own. The process unifies covert concealment, low-risk moderation, and rare overt enactment within a single mixed strategy for protecting jurisdictional control amid client power asymmetry.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the relational perspective on occupations and professions by theorizing discernment and modulation as previously overlooked mechanisms through which experts navigate value tensions with clients. It reframes the field's "compliance-or-resistance" dichotomy as a continuum that includes situated masking, moderating, and magnifying, and shows that recognizing intraclientele heterogeneity is itself a strategic resource — overturning the common assumption that an occupation's clientele is monolithic. It also identifies a middle ground (value-moderating) between overt value enactment and outright value abstention that prior work on concealment has not articulated.

**Practical Implication**
For professionals whose clients have grown more powerful (career advisers, lawyers, physicians, therapists, consultants), the study suggests cultivating diagnostic skill at reading client values and a repertoire of variably visible value-enactment practices, rather than committing wholesale to either accommodation or resistance. Occupational training that emphasizes relational skills and a service-oriented disposition toward heterogeneous clients can help experts protect their jurisdiction without surrendering their professional purpose.

**Limitations**
Findings derive from one ethnographic site and an interview sample of business-school career advisers at U.S. ranked undergraduate institutions, so the specific value tensions (purpose/fit vs. wealth/prestige/social approval) may not generalize beyond similar advice-giving contexts. The authors propose two boundary conditions: discernment and modulation likely require relatively lengthy client interactions and occupational socialization that emphasizes relational, service-oriented skills; occupations with brief, scripted, or adversarial client encounters may not show the same mechanisms.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine variation across occupations in how experts read and exploit intraclientele heterogeneity, including whether experts actively cultivate (rather than merely discern) client diversity, and whether individual-level differences in discernment ability shape outcomes. Studies of occupations where negative cases dominate could reveal when sustained value enactment with unreceptive clients threatens an occupation's overall survival, and additional contexts could test the proposed boundary conditions on interaction length and occupational socialization.
