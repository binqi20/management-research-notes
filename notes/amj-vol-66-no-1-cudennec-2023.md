# Valuing Spanners: Why Category Nesting and Expertise Matter

**Abstract**
Organizations need to both differentiate themselves while conforming to their audiences' expectations. To meet this demand, organizations may span different categories. However, valuing spanners is challenging for audiences. We contend that spanners' valuation depends on category nesting, as the congruence of informational cues varies between basic categories and subcategories. Furthermore, we expect that more expert audiences find spanners to be more congruent (and hence, more valuable) at a subordinate level than at a basic level of categorization. We test our hypotheses using a mixedmethods design in the context of venture capital investments. We analyze observational data on more than 29,000 venture capital deals and develop two experimental studies. Our findings support our hypotheses that subcategory spanning lowers valuation, and that this effect is attenuated as investors' expertise increases. Our experimental studies further show that congruence is a causal mechanism explaining these effects. Our findings have important implications for research on organizational conformity and optimal distinctiveness, categorization in markets from an information processing perspective, and the impact of expertise on valuation.

**Research Question**
How does category nesting (subordinate vs. basic category spanning) and audience expertise jointly affect investors' valuation of category-spanning firms, and to what extent does perceived inter-category congruence mediate this relationship?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Category membership condition (subordinate category spanning [SubCS], basic category spanning [BCS], basic category membership [BCM]); congruence treatment (within-domain vs. across-domain thinking) in Study 3
- DV(s): Firm valuation (pre-money value in Study 1; willingness to recommend investing in Studies 2 and 3)
- Mediators: Perceived inter-category congruence (assessed via Fleck & Quester's six-item scale)
- Moderators: Investor expertise (operationalized via prior investment experience in the focal category in Study 1; a 12-item objective finance knowledge test in Studies 2 and 3)

The central mechanism is information processing: subordinate categories are more specific but less distinctive than basic categories, so finding congruence among spanned subcategories demands greater cognitive effort. Average investors fail to perceive harmony among subcategory cues and therefore devalue subcategory spanners relative to basic-category spanners. Expertise increases the ability to discriminate subcategories within one's knowledge domain and to perceive their complementarity as congruent, which attenuates and can reverse the subcategory-spanning penalty for expert evaluators.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to three streams. First, it advances the optimal distinctiveness literature by showing that category nesting and audience information-processing capacity are essential dimensions of an organization's positioning. Second, it reframes congruence in market categorization from an evaluation of similarity to preexisting types into a meaningful correspondence among categorical cues across nested levels, recalibrating the supposed benefits of categorical purism (subcategory spanners may have been miscounted as purists in prior studies). Third, it documents how expertise differentially modulates valuation penalties at subordinate versus basic levels of nesting, resolving prior "mixed results" on expertise effects on valuations of deviations from categorical standards.

**Practical Implication**
Identifying with subcategories can be a rewarding differentiation strategy, but only if entrepreneurs target investors whose expertise lies within the focal domain. Founders should pay attention not only to market structure and category nesting but also to their target audience's degree of expertise in order to maximize valuation. Category spanning is best understood as a tool for achieving simultaneous conformity and distinctiveness through congruent informational cues.

**Limitations**
The industrial category structure used is relatively simple and does not account for spanning across subordinate categories belonging to different basic categories or for highly normative categorical boundaries that could trigger moral sanctions. The study focuses on VC investors as a single audience type; other audiences (consumers, crowdfunding users, media, regulators) may evaluate categorical combinations differently. The expertise measure relies on objective knowledge tests in experimental settings rather than real-world expert samples or direct manipulation of expertise.

**Future Research**
Scholars could replicate the framework with more complex nested category associations, test the theory under highly normative categorical boundaries, examine other audiences (consumers, media, legal bodies, crowdfunding users), and ensure exogeneity of expertise through real-life expert samples or direct manipulation. Alternative or complementary mechanisms such as flexibility, framing (concrete "how" vs. abstract "why"), interest, conscientiousness, or intelligence deserve further scrutiny, as does whether more expert investors actually make better valuation decisions.
