# Gains and Losses: Week-To-Week Changes in Leader–Follower Relationships

**Abstract**
Although the importance of leader–follower relationships in the workplace has been widely acknowledged, the impact of changes in such relationships has not been adequately examined in the literature. Leader–member exchange (LMX) theory and research have suggested that leader–follower relationships develop, mature, and then stabilize. Integrating recent work that has found important and impactful within-individual variations in what was thought to be relatively unvarying relationships, we theorize that leader–follower relationships (like other linkage types), are not only differentiated (varying between people) but also fluctuate across time periods (varying within people). We also discuss how people can understand their relationships better by comparing them to a relevant and proximal referent; this underlines how prior levels of LMX serve as a lens by which followers evaluate their current LMX. Across two studies, we discuss how comparing this week's LMX to last week's LMX can signal improvement (i.e., gains) or deterioration (i.e., losses) in the relationship, with differential implications for positive and negative affect. We also provide additional insights into LMX fluctuations by contrasting within- and between-individual variance in established and newly founded relationships, and by examining those incidents that are more likely to influence LMX in a given week.

**Research Question**
How do week-to-week changes (gains and losses) in leader–member exchange (LMX), evaluated against the follower's own prior-week LMX as a referent standard, relate to followers' positive and negative affect, and what within-week incidents and prior states explain such LMX change?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Current-week LMX, prior-week LMX, and their interaction/quadratic terms (operationalizing gains, losses, and stability); positive- and negative-incident intensity and incident relevance to LMX (Study 2).
- DV(s): Weekly positive affect (PA), weekly negative affect (NA), and weekly LMX (in incident models).
- Mediators: None formally tested.
- Moderators: Incident relevance to LMX (Study 2, H7); prior-week LMX as a moderator of incident effects (Study 2, H8); relationship maturity contrast between newly hired and longer-tenured employees (H5).

Drawing on social exchange and LMX theory, the authors argue that followers evaluate their LMX by comparing the current week to the most proximal and available referent — their own previous week — and that the resulting gains or losses generate affective responses through approach (PA) and avoidance (NA) systems. Losses are theorized to weigh more heavily than gains; stable-high LMX yields higher PA and lower NA than stable-low LMX; and weekly incidents drive LMX change with positive incidents amplified by relevance and prior LMX, while negative incidents are unconditionally aversive.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper relaxes LMX theory's long-standing stability assumption and shows that even mature LMX dyads exhibit meaningful week-to-week fluctuation, with gains tied to PA and losses tied to NA in an asymmetric pattern. It distinguishes differentiation (between-dyad variance, which grows with relationship maturity) from stability (within-dyad variance, which does not shrink with maturity), and integrates referent-comparison logic into social-exchange theorizing on hierarchical relationships.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders should not treat established LMX relationships as static: maintaining relationships matters as much as cultivating them, because week-to-week deterioration generates negative affect even in otherwise high-quality dyads. Low-quality relationships can be improved but more slowly, since prior LMX moderates the impact of positive incidents; negative incidents, by contrast, hurt regardless of context, so leaders should guard against them and "cushion" unavoidable declines.

**Limitations**
The studies examined only one leadership construct (LMX), one temporal frame (week), one referent (prior self), and one main outcome family (affect), all from a single (follower) source. Common-method concerns remain despite the time-separated, nonlinear design. The newcomer subsample had a low within-individual response rate, limiting tests of change dynamics in newly formed relationships.

**Future Research**
Future work should integrate other referents (self vs. others, episode vs. average), other time frames, and other leadership constructs; examine antecedents of sustained upward or downward LMX trends and "crossing-over" between in- and out-groups; investigate person- and situation-level moderators (e.g., neuroticism, power distance) of sensitivity to gains and losses; and study leader-side reactions to perceived LMX changes for a more complete dyadic view.
