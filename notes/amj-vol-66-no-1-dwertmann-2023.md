# Receiving Service from a Person with a Disability: Stereotypes, Perceptions of Corporate Social Responsibility, and the Opportunity for Increased Corporate Reputation

**Abstract**
Whereas advocates point to benefits of employing people with disabilities for organizations, employers’ concern over negative customer reactions is still a barrier to the employment of people with disabilities in service occupations. We contribute to this discussion and the management literature on disability by examining the effects of receiving service from employees with a hearing disability and employees who use a wheelchair on corporate reputation. Based on signaling theory, stereotypes, and valuation-by-association logic, we argue and find in a multistudy, multimethod approach that employing people with disabilities can be perceived as corporate social responsibility and leads to better corporate reputation. A field study with 317 customers of a large international supermarket chain in Lithuania demonstrates higher ratings of corporate reputation for customers receiving service from an employee with a hearing disability than for customers receiving service from an employee without disabilities. In an online experiment using a Solomon four-group design, we utilize video vignettes to test our model with corporate social responsibility perceptions as a mediator. Together, our findings show that managers’ concerns about how biased customers might respond to service employees with disabilities are likely unfounded and highlight—in addition to an ethical case for inclusion—the potential for organizations to benefit from employing of people with disabilities, as it leads to favorable reputation effects for organizations.

**Research Question**
Does customer experience of receiving service from an employee with a disability improve customer-based corporate reputation, and if so, through what mechanism (CSR perceptions) and under what boundary conditions (contact quality, CSR motive attributions)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Receiving service from a person with a disability (hearing disability; wheelchair user vs. no disability)
- DV(s): Customer-based corporate reputation; CSR perceptions; disability stereotypes (competence, warmth, and disability–job fit)
- Mediators: CSR perceptions; competence stereotypes; disability–job fit stereotypes
- Moderators: Contact quality (service satisfaction); attributed CSR motives (sincere vs. egoistic)

Drawing on signaling theory, the authors argue that hiring people with disabilities in customer-facing roles signals organizational concern for social well-being and is interpreted as a costly, sincere CSR act, which in turn boosts corporate reputation. Stereotype-disconfirming positive contact with employees with disabilities updates customers' competence (and to a lesser extent warmth) stereotypes via causal reasoning. Through valuation by association, the positive evaluation of the stigmatized employee transfers to the employing organization, raising its reputation — but only when customers attribute sincere (not egoistic) motives to the hiring.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides the first multimethod empirical evidence linking employment of people with disabilities to better corporate reputation through CSR perceptions, refining stigma-by-association theory by demonstrating positive "valuation by association" at the organizational level. It connects disability research with signaling theory and the stereotype content model, and identifies CSR motive attributions as a critical boundary condition that determines whether disability-inclusive hiring is interpreted as reputational asset or liability.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should view employment of people with disabilities in customer-facing roles as an opportunity rather than a reputational threat: such employment signals CSR and improves corporate reputation. Organizations should communicate sincere, value-driven motives for inclusive hiring rather than emphasizing tax incentives or self-interest, and should ensure good disability–job fit through accommodations, onboarding, and training so that service contact is satisfactory.

**Limitations**
Both studies were conducted in Lithuania with one supermarket chain, limiting generalizability across industries and national contexts. Social desirability is a concern in disability research, although the authors mitigate it via unobtrusive measures, control variables, and the Solomon four-group design. CSR and corporate reputation correlate highly (.71–.79) in Study 2, raising discriminant-validity questions despite supportive CFA results.

**Future Research**
Future studies should test whether positive contact effects on stereotypes generalize across disabilities or remain disability-specific, investigate effects in different industries and countries, and disentangle the distinct roles of disability visibility vs. customer awareness of the disability. Researchers should also probe the CSR–corporate reputation distinction further given the high observed correlation.
