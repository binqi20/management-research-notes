# Does Power Protect Female Moral Objectors? How and When Moral Objectors’ Gender, Power, and Use of Organizational Frames Influence Perceived Self-Control and Experienced Retaliation

**Abstract**
Organizational scholars have called upon higher-power individuals to serve as moral objectors to combat unethical behavior at work because it is assumed they will face less retaliation. However, research has painted an unclear picture of whether power protects women in the same way as it does men. In this paper, we draw on two distinct role theories (i.e., power role theories and gender role theories) as well as expectancy violation theory, theorizing and finding that female moral objectors benefit less from power than male moral objectors because they are viewed as lower in self-control. We further investigate an alternative remedy, or organizational frame, that may mitigate retaliation against higher-powered female moral objectors, finding that using this frame increases perceptions of self-control and reduces retaliation. We test and find support for our theory across four studies, including an archival study (n 5 33,715), a critical incident technique experiment, and two preregistered experiments testing our intervention.

**Research Question**
Does structural power protect female moral objectors from retaliation in the same way it protects men, and under what conditions (i.e., how a moral objection is framed) can higher-powered female moral objectors avoid retaliation?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Moral objector's structural power; moral objector's gender; moral objection framing (standard vs. organizational)
- DV(s): Experienced/enacted retaliation against the moral objector
- Mediator: Observers' perceptions of the moral objector's self-control
- Moderators: Gender (moderates power -> retaliation); moral objection framing (moderates the power x gender interaction)

Drawing on power role theories, gender role theories, and expectancy violation theory, the authors argue that moral objection is an agentic act that observers expect from higher-power actors but not from women. When higher-powered women morally object using a standard frame, they violate salient gender role expectations, are perceived as lower in self-control (impulsive and selfishly imposing their morality on the group), and face retaliation. Reframing the same objection in organizational/communal language signals other-oriented benefit to the group, neutralizes the gender role violation, restores self-control perceptions, and reduces retaliation across gender and power levels.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to gender and power research by systematically showing that power does not automatically protect female moral objectors—instead, moral objection framing functions as a more effective antidote because organizational frames do not violate gender role expectations. It extends threat-based accounts of moral objection by establishing perceived self-control as a key mediating mechanism explaining retaliation, and it contributes to organizational ethics theory by demonstrating that, counter to prior assumptions, power does not shield all moral objectors—particularly women—from retaliation.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should encourage all moral objectors, not just women, to use organizational rather than standard frames, since organizationally framed objections face less retaliation across the board. Managers can train employees in framing, debias observers' tendency to view female moral objectors as lower in self-control through perspective-taking workshops, and publicly highlight the communal benefits of moral objections to reduce their perceived agentic threat.

**Limitations**
Hypothesis 1 (main effect of power on retaliation) was not supported in Studies 2 and 4, though the interaction effects were consistent. The studies tested framing only at average and high power levels, leaving extremely low-power positions unexamined, and three of four studies relied on MTurk samples. Retaliation was treated as a unitary construct rather than its multiple forms, and the objected-to behavior was not itself gendered.

**Future Research**
Future work should investigate framing effects at lower organizational levels, examine multiple forms of retaliation, test whether organizational framing helps women objecting to gendered topics like sexual harassment or gender equity, and explore alternative non-other-oriented frames. Researchers should also study other downstream consequences of competence perceptions, such as promotion and investment opportunities for female moral objectors.
