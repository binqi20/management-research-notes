# Losing Control: The Uncertain Management of Concealable Stigmas When Work and Social Media Collide

**Abstract**
Scholars have typically portrayed employees' management of concealable stigmas in face-to-face encounters in which social groups are easily separable (e.g., friends, family, and coworkers). This analytical predisposition overlooks the possible roles of social network sites (SNSs) such as Facebook and Instagram. These online platforms enable a cohabitation of different audiences—that is, a context collapse that allows a growing, invisible audience to easily access information about one's stigma. In our qualitative analysis in a Latin American organization, we develop a model that documents the everyday dynamics of context collapse among gay male employees. In a disclosure dilemma, employees are uncertain about how to be a professional online and simultaneously keep SNSs as platforms where they can show more relaxed versions of themselves. As a response, most employees adopt mirroring, and attempt to reflect their face-to-face disclosure levels on SNSs. Some employees engage in destigmatization efforts online, and as an outlier case we mapped an employee in collapse denial. Our study questions the established idea of disclosure as a relatively controlled process in microinteractions. We also nuance the assumption of SNSs as safe spaces and show the unintended impacts of context collapse on the stigmatized.

**Research Question**
How does the context collapse caused by social network sites (SNSs) impact employees' management of concealable stigmas?

**Mechanism Process**
- Types of context collapse: bottom-up context collisions at work (spontaneous, taken-for-granted online–offline interactions between employees) and top-down context collusions at work (managers' intentional stimuli to use SNSs for work-related purposes)
- Disclosure dilemma: "How can I be a professional online and keep SNSs as safe spaces?"
- Tensions: professionalism understood as heteronormativity vs. SNSs as safe spaces; default publicness of SNSs vs. need to compartmentalize audiences
- Response patterns (employees with concealable stigmas):
  - Mirroring (dominant): replicating face-to-face disclosure levels on SNSs, either toward concealing or toward revealing — characterized by reproducing face-to-face professionalism as heteronormativity on SNSs and constant effort to maintain online safe spaces
  - Online destigmatization efforts (minor): strategic use of SNSs to normalize and differentiate being gay at work
  - Collapse denial (outlier): treating online and face-to-face as independent contexts, producing mismatched disclosure behaviors

The process documents how SNSs collapse previously separable audiences, generating uncertainty about who sees what and when. Gay male employees lose the relative control over disclosure that prior literature assumed they possessed in face-to-face microinteractions. To cope, most fall back on what they already know — face-to-face heteronormative scripts — and attempt to mirror these online, even though SNSs' default publicness, peers' unsolicited tagging, and managers' work-related demands repeatedly break that mirroring and force renewed boundary work. A minority leverages SNSs as activist platforms, while a denial case illustrates the costs of treating the two contexts as separate.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper sets boundary conditions on the literature that has portrayed concealable-stigma disclosure as a relatively controlled individual decision, showing that under context collapse this process becomes uncertain and employees lose control over who sees their stigma-related information and when. It nuances the assumption of SNSs as safe spaces by documenting the continuous monitoring and behavioral adjustment required to keep them so, especially when work invades them. It also challenges the assumption in context-collapse-at-work research that the phenomenon affects all employees similarly, surfacing unintended negative impacts of context collapse on employees with concealable stigmas.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should recognize the repercussions of spontaneous and incentivized online interactions for employees with concealable stigmas — antidiscriminatory policies and HR training should explicitly cover online behavior. Managers should be aware that incentivizing SNS use for work-related purposes can be disproportionately burdensome for LGBTQIA+ employees. Identifying employees already engaging in online destigmatization efforts and involving them in diversity committees can help organizations develop more responsive practices, as Globalcar did after the study.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on gay male employees in one Latin American automotive subsidiary, so patterns may not generalize to other stigmatized groups or industries. The masculine, heteronormative automotive setting may amplify the dynamics observed. Access constraints and the topic's sensitivity meant that nine potential informants declined to participate, which both validates the topic's salience and may bias the sample toward employees willing to discuss it.

**Future Research**
Future work should extend the model to lesbian, bisexual, and transgender employees and examine intersections with race, religion, class, and culture. Researchers could study specific organizational practices such as the role of social media in hiring, the impact of COVID-19 on SNS-mediated work interactions, and longitudinal feedback loops between disclosure and outcomes. Comparative work across different SNS affordances (e.g., Facebook vs. Reddit vs. anonymous platforms) could deepen understanding of how platform features shape stigma management.
