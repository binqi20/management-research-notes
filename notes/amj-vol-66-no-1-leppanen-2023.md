# When Do Novel Business Models Lead to High Performance? A Configurational Approach to Value Drivers, Competitive Strategy, and Firm Environment

**Abstract**
The strategy literature has viewed novel business model design as a universally positive antecedent of high performance in entrepreneurial ventures. Not only have scholars emphasized novelty as a necessity, but they have almost considered it a sufficient condition for performance. Challenging this assumption, we hypothesize that novelty can produce high performance only in combination with specific configurations that feature other value drivers, such as efficiency, lock-in, and complementarity, which help firms not only create value but also capture more of it. Fuzzy set qualitative comparative analysis of two samples of Internet-enabled firms largely supports our hypotheses. We find that novelty alone is insufficient for high performance, even when it appears as a necessary condition for very high performance in new technological environments. Our results highlight how novelty combines effectively with other value drivers (in particular with efficiency) and strategies (in particular with differentiation) contingent on the intensity of competition, firm size, and firms’ technological environment. Our study contributes to literature streams on value creation, business model design, and innovation.

**Research Question**
When and how does novel business model design lead to high firm performance, given the interdependence between business model value drivers (novelty, efficiency, lock-in, complementarity), competitive strategy (differentiation, low-cost), and firm environment (competition, firm size, technological maturity)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Business model value drivers — novelty, efficiency, lock-in, complementarity; competitive strategies — differentiation, low-cost
- DV(s): High firm performance and very high firm performance (calibrated from Tobin's Q)
- Moderators / contingencies: Intensity of competition, firm size (small vs. large), technological-environment maturity (new vs. established Internet)
- Mediators: Not modeled; the configurational approach analyzes conjunctural causation and equifinality rather than mediation

The paper develops a configurational mechanism in which novelty primarily drives value creation ("growing the pie") but is insufficient on its own to deliver high firm performance because it does little for value capture. Performance arises only when novelty co-occurs with at least one value-capture-oriented driver (efficiency, lock-in, or complementarity) and a coherent competitive strategy, and the right configuration depends on firm size and whether the enabling technology is new or mature. fsQCA reveals equifinal high-performing configurations and shows novelty as necessary for very high performance only in new-technology contexts.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends transaction-design and business-model-innovation research by showing that novelty is neither sufficient nor universally necessary for high performance; rather, it works through interdependent combinations with other value drivers, strategy, and environmental contingencies. By taking a neo-configurational lens, the authors uncover equifinality across novelty-centric, novelty-enhanced, novelty-neutral, novelty-averse, and novelty-phobic configurations, and demonstrate that novelty and efficiency can act as either complements or substitutes depending on strategy and competition.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should avoid a "novelty trap": novel business model design pays off only when it is connected to clear value-capture mechanisms (efficiency, lock-in, or complementarity) and to a coherent differentiation or low-cost strategy in competitive industries. As environments mature, firms should re-evaluate their business model design choices and ensure that any novel configuration remains worth more than the sum of its parts.

**Limitations**
Researcher choices about which conditions to include constrain fsQCA model complexity, potentially omitting relevant firm- and industry-level controls. The method's inferential power and out-of-sample generalizability are still debated, and the sampling strategy may introduce survival bias. The exclusive focus on Internet-enabled IT-sector firms may limit how the findings travel to less innovation-friendly or more capital-intensive industries.

**Future Research**
Future work could deploy content analysis or NLP on IPO prospectuses and annual reports to scale up business model coding and enable longitudinal designs across industries. Researchers could also examine how business model configurations evolve via vicarious learning or institutional pressure, and whether Red Queen dynamics or causal ambiguity explain why firms converge externally yet diverge in performance.
