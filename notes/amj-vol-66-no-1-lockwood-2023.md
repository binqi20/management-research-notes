# Polishing the Gilt Edge: Elite Category Endurance and Symbolic Boundaries in U.S. Luxury Hotels, 1790–2015

**Abstract**
We conducted an inductive study of discourse associated with the U.S. luxury hotel category, from its beginnings in 1790 through 2015, to explain how cultural processes contribute to elite category endurance. Analyzing data from historical archives, interviews, and observations, we use both qualitative and quantitative methods to reveal how the endurance of an elite category is an ongoing cultural accomplishment that occurs via periodic redefinition of its "symbolic boundary"—that is, the set of meanings and practices that distinguish the elite from nonelite. Modeling the boundary in terms of semiotic codes of opposition, we find that the encoding of the symbolic boundary serves as a temporary settlement that conveys both the category's elite exclusivity and its cultural consonance with broader societal beliefs, tastes, and practices. Over time, however, with industry and societal changes, tensions erupt in the balance between the needs for exclusivity and consonance, propelling a redefinition of what it means to be elite. Our research extends current theorizations of elite category endurance by revealing how the elite symbolic boundary is neither permanent nor unassailable; rather, we show how it changes to enable long-term elite category endurance.

**Research Question**
How does an elite market category endure over the very long term in the face of recurrent industry- and society-driven threats to its status? Specifically, how is the symbolic boundary that distinguishes the elite from the nonelite discursively constructed, settled, disrupted, and redefined across historical periods?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: elite exclusivity vs. cultural consonance; symbolic boundary as a temporary "settlement" encoded in a semiotic code of oppositions (connotations and denotations) between luxury and nonluxury.
- Process / phases: three historical periods — Grand Hotel Period (1790s–1920s, luxury = opulence), Chain Hotel Period (1930s–mid-1990s, luxury = cosmopolitanism), Lifestyle Hotel Period (late 1990s–2015, luxury = originality) — each demarcated by symbolic boundary settlement followed by disruption.
- Practices (three discursive mechanisms): social partitioning (drawing the boundary around an idealized elite clientele), oppositional appropriation (selectively absorbing elements from the nonelite category), and temporal translation (reinterpreting the category's own past for the present).
- Triggering conditions: industry shifts (e.g., chain hotels, lifestyle hotels) and societal sociocultural movements (modern progressive, artisanal) that heighten the tension between exclusivity and consonance.
- Emergent outcomes: a new symbolic boundary settlement and a new semiotic code that re-partitions luxury from nonluxury, sustaining elite category endurance.

Across all three periods the three mechanisms interlock — one is "primary" at category emergence (social partitioning), growth (oppositional appropriation), or maturity (temporal translation), while the other two play supporting roles. The process resembles a punctuated equilibrium of long stable settlements separated by brief disruption-and-redefinition phases, with a notable correspondence between the primary settlement mechanism and the source of the next disruption.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper recasts elite category endurance as an active, ongoing cultural accomplishment rather than an inertial property of status hierarchies, showing how symbolic boundaries adapt by holding elite exclusivity and cultural consonance in productive tension. It identifies three novel, interlocking discursive mechanisms — social partitioning, oppositional appropriation, and temporal translation — and exposes a periodicity in which each mechanism takes the lead at a different phase of the category's evolution. It also bridges debates between impermeable and porous category boundaries by showing that elite symbolic boundaries oscillate between sharp definition in stable times and greater fluidity under threat.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of elite categories and luxury brands should monitor shifts in broader societal tastes and values, not just competitive moves, because cultural consonance is as crucial to enduring eliteness as exclusivity. Sustaining elite status calls for organizational ambidexterity: preserving continuity with the category's storied past while selectively absorbing nonelite practices and reinterpreting tradition to align with contemporary values.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a single category (luxury) within a single industry (U.S. hotels), which constrains generalizability across sectors and national settings. The analysis takes a broad-brush, category-level approach to public discourse and does not deeply track individual organizations, specific actors, or membership-level boundary dynamics.

**Future Research**
The authors call for replication of the symbolic boundary settlement–disruption framework in other cultural-industry contexts (restaurants, retail, watches, wine, haute couture, "masstige" brands) and in non-cultural or credence-good settings. They also encourage cross-national comparisons of how class structures shape boundary dynamics and finer-grained tracking of which actors and organizations come to influence elite category discourse over time.
