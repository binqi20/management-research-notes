# Balancing “Protective Disguise” with “Harmonious Advocacy”: Social Venture Legitimation in Authoritarian Contexts

**Abstract**
This paper seeks to advance understanding of how new social ventures can gain legitimacy in authoritarian contexts. Through a study of a new disability rights organization in post-revolutionary Egypt, we theorize how authoritarianism poses distinct challenges for social ventures that require different legitimation strategies than those commonly reported in the literature. Specifically, we use our case study to build a theoretical model that suggests social ventures need to achieve “optimal assimilation” by balancing “protective disguise” with “harmonious advocacy.” By explicitly theorizing social venture legitimation in authoritarian contexts, we advance the budding literature on social venture legitimation that has so far predominantly considered legitimation in more democratic contexts. Moreover, our study shows that organizational legitimacy may need to be conceptualized differently when examining social ventures—and indeed other forms of organization—in authoritarian regimes.

**Research Question**
"How do new social ventures gain legitimacy in authoritarian contexts?"

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: protective disguise vs. harmonious advocacy; political vs. cultural manifestations of authoritarianism; "fitting in" vs. advocating for beneficiaries
- Legitimation challenges: (a) political manifestation — social activism perceived as threat to national interests; (b) cultural manifestation — entrenched beliefs and practices that devalue the human rights of marginalized groups
- Protective disguise practices: (1) cloaking social mission under rhetorical alignment with regime goals (economic prosperity, national stability, tourism); (2) camouflaging social action through an innocuous organizational design (positioning as a commercial accessibility consultancy rather than a human-rights NGO)
- Harmonious advocacy practices: (3) experiential construction of social problems (community days, blindfolded simulations, ambassador stories that build empathy); (4) reactive campaigning for social mission (leveraging conducive events such as the 2017 disability law and the "Year of People with Disabilities," while explicitly shielding government from criticism)
- Emergent outcomes: venture perceived as nonthreatening, then as worthy of support; growing audience backing
- Boundary conditions: continuous balancing required; ventures may need to pivot back toward disguise during crackdowns and forward toward advocacy during conducive periods

The process Amal followed shows that legitimation in authoritarian contexts is underpinned by a continuous balancing of disguise and advocacy that the authors label "optimal assimilation." Disguise is necessary because confrontation triggers regime aggression; advocacy is necessary because pure disguise prevents the venture from acting on its mission. Disguise lays the platform that makes later, carefully calibrated advocacy possible, and the two strategies are continuously rebalanced in response to political-cultural dynamics.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper builds a process model of social-venture legitimation in authoritarian contexts that identifies "protective disguise" and "harmonious advocacy" as the two overarching strategies and "optimal assimilation" as the governing principle — a counterpart to "optimal distinctiveness" in democratic contexts. It addresses the democratic-society bias in organizational-legitimacy research by showing that core assumptions (e.g., the evaluative benefits of "standing out," the four-state progression toward "accepted") do not transfer to authoritarian regimes, where ventures occupy a liminal zone between "debated" and "proper" and political elites uniquely bestow or withdraw legitimacy.

**Practical Implication**
Social ventures operating in authoritarian regimes should resist transplanting democratic-context playbooks: confrontational framing, hero/villain rhetoric, and overt rights-based campaigning can endanger founders and trigger forced closure. Founders should instead cloak their mission in language that aligns with regime priorities, adopt an innocuous organizational form (e.g., a service-providing consultancy), and use empathy-building experiential activities plus reactive campaigning tied to government-initiated events to advance their cause without attracting unwanted political attention.

**Limitations**
The study rests on a single case in one authoritarian context (post-revolutionary Egypt), focused on a relatively less politically contentious issue (disability inclusion); the findings may differ for ventures tackling more sensitive issues (e.g., free speech, LGBTQ rights) or operating in other authoritarian or hybrid regimes (China, Russia, Saudi Arabia, Albania, Nigeria). The authors also flag that whether "optimal assimilation" is the morally best path is a normative question they cannot answer.

**Future Research**
Comparative work across authoritarian and hybrid regimes is needed to test whether the disguise-advocacy balance generalizes. Future studies should examine more politically and culturally contentious causes, explore how a commercial vs. social orientation shapes legitimation under different regimes, and investigate the state's role as a uniquely powerful audience whose interdependency with other audiences shapes legitimacy judgments — even in democracies.
