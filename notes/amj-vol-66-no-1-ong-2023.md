# Toward a Configural Theory of Job Demands and Resources

**Abstract**
Job demands–resources research has largely adopted a variable-centered approach to test main and interactive effects of demands and resources on employee outcomes. However, this approach cannot detect distinct configurations of job demands and resources that may lead to the same outcomes, and the many multiplicative models attempting to do so have received little support. We take a person-centered theoretical approach to examine configurations of demands and resources that are sufficient to produce exhaustion, engagement, and other strain outcomes. Using fuzzy set qualitative comparative analysis across three studies, we find three equifinal configurations sufficient to produce exhaustion: (a) high workload and emotional demands, (b) low social support, and (c) high workload and low autonomy. We also find evidence for causal asymmetry in both exhaustion and engagement. Our person-centered approach yields more theoretically consistent results compared to a variable-centered approach. The findings highlight that certain job demands may only be buffered by specific resources, and that certain configurations of demands cannot be buffered at all.

**Research Question**
Which configurations of job demands and resources are sufficient to produce the presence and absence of exhaustion and engagement (and other strain outcomes), and do these configurations exhibit equifinality and causal asymmetry that variable-centered models cannot detect?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Workload, emotional demands (job demands); autonomy, social support, feedback (job resources)
- DV(s): Exhaustion, engagement, turnover intentions, physical symptoms
- Mediators: Not modeled; strain and motivational processes are the theoretical mediating mechanisms posited by JDR theory
- Moderators: The configural approach replaces traditional moderation logic — configurations of conditions (not interactions) jointly produce outcomes through equifinality and causal asymmetry

Drawing on the demand–control–support extension of JDR theory and the "bad-is-stronger-than-good" cognition literature, the authors theorize that several qualitatively different configurations of high demands and low resources can be equally sufficient to produce exhaustion (equifinality), and that the configurations producing an outcome are not the inverse of those producing its absence (causal asymmetry). They predict that high-workload/low-autonomy and high-workload/low-social-support combinations are each sufficient for exhaustion, that some configurations of multiple high demands cannot be buffered by any resource, and that there are more pathways to negative than positive outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reinterprets the JDR model in configural terms, replacing the additive/multiplicative variable-centered logic with set-theoretic propositions about equifinal and causally asymmetric configurations of demands and resources. It shows that certain demands (high workload combined with emotional demands) cannot be buffered by any resource, that the absence of social support can itself function as a demand, and that there are more configurations sufficient for exhaustion than for engagement, instantiating a "bad-is-stronger-than-good" pattern within JDR theory.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should diagnose employees' overall configurations of job demands and resources rather than intervening on any single demand or resource in isolation: simply lowering workload will not help an employee whose exhaustion comes from low social support, and adding social support will not help when high workload combines with high emotional demands. Because exhaustion has multiple sufficient pathways but its absence has none, prevention is more tractable than cure.

**Limitations**
Studies 1 and 2 measured demands, resources, and outcomes at the same time from the same source, raising common-method concerns (addressed in Study 3 with time-lagged measurement). The observational design leaves the causal ordering of JDRs and outcomes open, and samples were drawn primarily from the United States, which may limit cross-cultural generalizability. The set of demands and resources examined was finite and did not include nonwork antecedents of strain.

**Future Research**
The authors call for longitudinal designs that test lagged effects between JDRs and outcomes, replications in non-U.S. and especially collectivistic cultural contexts to test whether social support and autonomy play different buffering roles, and extensions to additional outcomes such as performance. They also suggest examining antecedents of configuration membership (e.g., personality traits, organizational and national institutions) and identifying configuration-specific mediating mechanisms.
