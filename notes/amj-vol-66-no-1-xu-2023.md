# The Impact of Communist Ideology on the Patenting Activity of Chinese Firms

**Abstract**
The Chinese Communist Party began decoupling its policies and practices from Maoist communist ideology more than four decades ago. Yet, why does Maoism continue to have an impact on the behavior of Party members? In this study, we argue that, although the influence of Maoist ideology has become weaker among younger Party members and Party members with higher educational attainment, such ideological decay is countered by a process of secondhand ideological imprinting. Based on data from 1,298 nonstate-owned Chinese listed firms for 2000–2017, we find that firms with Party-member board chairs file fewer patent applications and are more likely to commit patent infringement. These effects are weaker if a board chair is younger or has higher educational attainment. Importantly, the moderating effect of young age is reduced as the presence of older Party-member corporate directors in a region becomes more prominent. However, the moderating effect of education appears to be unaffected by the presence of older Party-member directors. These findings generate fresh insights on the dynamics of ideological decay and persistence.

**Research Question**
Why does Maoist communist ideology continue to affect the patenting behavior of Chinese Communist Party members—including younger members who never directly experienced the Maoist era—and through what specific imprinting mechanism does this ideological influence persist amid decades of decoupling between Party policy and Maoist doctrine?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Board chair's Communist Party (CCP) membership (binary)
- DV(s): Number of patent applications (count); likelihood of patent infringement (binary)
- Moderators: Board chair's young age (100 − actual age); educational attainment (5-level categorical); presence of older Party-member directors in the region (% of older Party-member directors among all Party-member directors in the province)
- Controls: Founder chair, government/SOE experience, overseas experience, chair ownership, R&D intensity, state shares, firm size, firm age, ROA, industry competition (1 − HHI), city-level GDP growth, Internet development, provincial legal development, year and industry dummies

Drawing on imprinting theory, the authors argue that older Party members carry firsthand Maoist imprints from the politically sensitive 1960s–1970s, while younger Party members—who joined under Dengism—acquire Maoist orientations only indirectly, through "secondhand imprinting" by older Party-member peers in their elite social and role category. Maoist beliefs (anti-private-property, anti-IPR, devaluation of scientific work and intellectuals) translate into lower patent application volumes and higher patent infringement propensity. Higher education and younger age weaken this effect (ideological decay), but a high regional presence of older Party-member directors reactivates Maoist influence among younger chairs (offsetting age-based decay), though it does not offset education-based decay.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends imprinting theory by empirically demonstrating secondhand imprinting in a setting where the original imprinting environment has disappeared—showing that older Party-member directors in the same regional elite category pass Maoist ideological imprints onto younger Party-member board chairs who never experienced the Maoist era directly. It contributes to the political ideology literature by modeling the interplay between ideological decay (via age, education) and ideological persistence (via secondhand imprinting) within a single integrated framework, departing from the Western liberal/conservative dichotomy that dominates extant work.

**Practical Implication**
When assessing Chinese non-SOEs as innovation partners or rivals, observers should weigh board chairs' ideological background (CCP membership) alongside their age, education, and the demographic composition of older Party-member directors in their regional social network—because firms led by Party-member chairs file fewer patents, more often violate other firms' patent rights, and may instead diversify into multiple industries as an alternative growth strategy. Education in particular appears to robustly counteract Maoist ideological influence on innovation.

**Limitations**
The study isolates Maoist ideological elements, but these are intermixed with broader Chinese cultural features (e.g., Confucian skepticism of individualism and IPR; historically modest cultural emphasis on science and technology) that also bear on innovation activity. The authors caution against overgeneralizing the ideology effect to all corporate decisions, noting that when evidence is unambiguous Party members often act pragmatically (e.g., founding private firms after SOE collapse).

**Future Research**
Cross-national comparisons of how different versions of communist ideology shape firm behavior in China, Cuba, Vietnam, and former Soviet Bloc countries would test the generalizability of the secondhand-imprinting mechanism. Future work could also examine how a board chair's home province, prior SOE/planned-economy experience, or other contextual moderators interact with political ideology to shape innovation orientations.
