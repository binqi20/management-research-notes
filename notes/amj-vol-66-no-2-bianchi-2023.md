# Does Job Satisfaction Rise and Fall with the Economy? Cross-Sectional, Longitudinal, and Experimental Evidence That Job Satisfaction Increases during Recessions

**Abstract**
While recessions have many adverse consequences for individuals and organizations, we propose that they have positive implications for job satisfaction. We argue that during bad economic times, people will be less attuned to other possible jobs, and more likely to see their own jobs favorably. We find support for these predictions across three studies. Study 1 (n 5 23,335) utilizes a large cross-sectional survey of American adults collected over four decades and finds that job satisfaction increases during recessions and declines during booms. Study 2 (n 5 12,859) replicates this result using a large longitudinal survey of British adults and finds that job satisfaction rises and falls with the unemployment rate even within the same people. Finally, Study 3 (n 5 512) uses an experimental design and finds that the relationship between economic conditions and job satisfaction is mediated by the reduced salience of alternative jobs. While scholars have long recognized that job satisfaction is affected by situational features inside organizations, our findings suggest that conditions outside the workplace can also influence how people think about and evaluate their jobs.

**Research Question**
Do macroeconomic conditions outside the workplace shape how satisfied people are with their jobs? Specifically, the authors ask whether job satisfaction rises during recessions (when unemployment is high) and falls during booms, and whether this relationship is driven by the reduced salience of alternative jobs.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Economic conditions, operationalized as the unemployment rate (national and state-level in Study 1; UK national in Study 2; experimentally manipulated bad-vs-good economy in Study 3).
- DV(s): Job satisfaction (single-item GSS measure in Study 1; single-item BHPS/Understanding Society measure in Study 2; Brayfield & Rothe 5-item scale in Study 3).
- Mediators: Reduced salience of alternative jobs (Miller's adapted 6-item Attention to Alternatives Index, Study 3).
- Moderators: None formally tested (the paper proposes personality/demographic moderators as future work).
- Controls: Age, age squared, gender, income, linear time trend; industry and occupation; tenure (Study 3); state dummies (Study 1 state models).

When job alternatives are plentiful and salient, jobs are judged against higher reference standards and comparisons surface their negative features, depressing satisfaction (the Cornell frame-of-reference and investment models). During recessions, scarce and unreachable alternatives become less salient, so people focus on the positive features of their existing jobs and use system-justifying, compensatory-control strategies to view their situation more favorably. Study 3's mediation confirms that worse economic conditions lower the salience of alternatives, which in turn raises job satisfaction.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends job-satisfaction research—which has focused almost exclusively on situational conditions inside the workplace—by showing that contextual conditions outside organizations also shape workplace attitudes. It contributes to work on the psychological effects of recessions and provides theoretical and empirical support for the idea that adverse societal events can promote positive (not just disengaging) sense-making processes that bolster workplace attitudes.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations may benefit from acknowledging and addressing external economic events rather than ignoring them. Because employees seem driven to view their jobs favorably during downturns, efforts to raise satisfaction may be well-received then, and hiring during recessions may yield workers who rate their jobs more highly. Conversely, firms should attend more to satisfaction during booms, when salient alternatives make dissatisfaction costlier and turnover easier.

**Limitations**
The magnitude of the effects is relatively small, plausibly because countervailing forces—greater workplace stress and job insecurity during recessions—depress satisfaction at the same time the identified sense-making processes elevate it. The first two studies are correlational; only the experiment establishes causality and tests the mediator.

**Future Research**
Future work could simultaneously capture the competing forces that elevate and depress satisfaction during downturns, and examine when the elevating factors dominate. Researchers could also test moderation by personality (e.g., need for cognitive closure) or demographics (recessions are unevenly experienced, e.g., low-wage workers), and explore additional psychological mechanisms such as tempered expectations or gratitude, and why alternatives undermine satisfaction.
