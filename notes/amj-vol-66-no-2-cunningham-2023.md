# Do I Dare? The Psychodynamics of Anticipated Image Risk, Leader-Identity Endorsement, and Leader Emergence

**Abstract**
Although many organizations value leadership across levels, individuals are reluctant to step up and lead. We explore how anticipated image risk (i.e., individuals' beliefs that the act of leading might harm their image with others) and lay beliefs about leadership ability (i.e., that this ability is fixed versus malleable) may diminish individuals' endorsement of a leader identity and thus their leadership emergence. Across MBA consulting teams, supervisor–employee dyads, and virtual workers, we find that lay theories of leadership ability moderate the negative relationship between anticipated image risk in leadership and leader-identity endorsement, which affects subsequent leadership emergence (Study 1). Using qualitative survey data, Study 2 identifies three specific image concerns that individuals associated with leading: seeming bossy, seeming unqualified, and seeming different from one's peers. Study 3 tests whether a newly developed measure of three specific image risks similarly predicts leader-identity endorsement and emergence. Last, in Study 4 we experimentally manipulate the anticipation of image risk to show its causal impact on leader-identity endorsement and emergence.

**Research Question**
Why don't more people internalize a leader identity and emerge as leaders? Specifically, how does anticipated image risk—individuals' belief that leading might harm how others see them—reduce leader-identity endorsement and thereby leadership emergence, and how do lay (fixed vs. malleable) theories of leadership ability moderate this process?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Anticipated image risk in leadership (general measure; and three specific image risks—fear of seeming bossy, unqualified, and different)
- DV(s): Leadership emergence (peer- and supervisor-rated; willingness to lead)
- Mediators: Leader-identity endorsement
- Moderators: Lay (fixed vs. malleable) theories of leadership ability

When individuals anticipate that leading will damage their image, ego/self-protection prompts a psychodynamic form of identity work in which they decline to endorse "leader" as a self-descriptor ("that's just not who I am"), neutralizing the threat while avoiding seeing themselves as lacking courage. Reduced leader-identity endorsement in turn lowers leadership emergence. A fixed lay theory of leadership ability strengthens this negative path because reputational damage is seen as permanent and growth as impossible, whereas a malleable view buffers it. The qualitative study specifies three concrete image concerns (bossy, unqualified, different) underlying the construct, with fear of seeming unqualified the most debilitating.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts leader-identity scholarship from a lasting "once and for all" construction toward situational, fluid endorsement, and introduces a rarely invoked ego-defense (psychodynamic) explanation for why people deny a leader identity. It advances the concept of metaperception—how people think others think of them—as an underexplored driver of identity, and elaborates image risk from a molar construct into three specific reputational concerns (seeming bossy, unqualified, different).

**Practical Implication**
Organizations seeking more leadership at all ranks should reckon with negative, outdated stereotypes of leaders as aggressive or domineering and foster a consensus that leadership is independent of formal title. They should also promote a malleable view of leadership ability—reinforcing that leadership is a learnable skill and praising attempts to lead—to help employees circumvent the ego-defense response that suppresses leader-identity endorsement and emergence.

**Limitations**
The studies emphasize within-person perceptions over social context, leaving out other context-specific variables (e.g., perceived organizational support, hierarchy) that may shape image risk and endorsement. Study 1 used single-source survey measures vulnerable to common method variance, and support for the lay-theory moderation hypotheses (H5, H6) was mixed across studies—significant in Studies 1 and B but not when image risk was manipulated and lay theories measured in Study 4.

**Future Research**
Scholars could further test the moderating role of lay theories across varied, multisource settings to clarify when this mindset matters, and probe additional context variables (e.g., organizational support, hierarchy) shaping image risk. Researchers might examine the full range of anticipated reactions (negative, neutral, positive), whether people over- or accurately estimate social backlash, and why fear of seeming unqualified is the most debilitating concern.
