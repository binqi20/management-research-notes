# Leveraging Knowledge Diversity in Hierarchically Differentiated Teams: The Critical Role of Hierarchy Stability

**Abstract**
Past research has been equivocal about the information sharing and performance effects of knowledge diversity in teams. In an attempt to resolve this equivocality, scholars have suggested that status hierarchy may play a role, proposing that status differences can constrict team-level information sharing in diverse teams by privileging the knowledge of higher-status members and discounting the knowledge of lower-status members. Although there is some evidence to support this notion, there is also evidence to suggest that status differences may amplify information sharing in diverse teams by offering status enhancement incentives to members who share unique knowledge. In this paper, we reconcile these different predictions by suggesting that the effects of status hierarchy on the relationship between knowledge diversity and team information sharing will depend on the stability of the hierarchy. Using a diverse sample of 156 teams across 110 organizations, we found that status differences constricted information sharing in knowledge-diverse teams when hierarchy stability was high and amplified information sharing in knowledge-diverse teams when hierarchy stability was low. These information sharing effects, in turn, affected team performance. Our study highlights how hierarchy stability is critical for understanding whether status differences constrict or amplify information sharing in knowledge-diverse teams.

**Research Question**
When and how does a team's status hierarchy constrict versus amplify the relationship between knowledge diversity and team information sharing (and, in turn, team performance), and does hierarchy stability determine which effect prevails?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Knowledge diversity (composite of educational-background and job-role diversity, Blau's index)
- DV(s): Team information sharing (6-item scale); team performance (3-item supervisor-rated scale)
- Mediators: Team information sharing (mediates knowledge diversity → team performance)
- Moderators: Status hierarchy (peer-rated influence, prominence, respect dispersion) moderated in turn by hierarchy stability (a moderated-moderation / three-way interaction)

The paper argues that status hierarchy has two countervailing effects on diverse teams: a constricting effect (high-status members' perspectives dominate, low-status members feel inhibited) and an amplifying effect (hierarchy offers status-enhancement incentives for sharing unique knowledge). Drawing on the concept of hierarchy stability, the authors propose that stable hierarchies activate four constricting dynamics—greater dominance of one perspective, reduced accountability of high-status members, increased personal risk to low-status members, and weakened status-enhancement incentives—so that diversity's benefit to information sharing (and downstream performance) is weakest when status differences are large and stable. The empirical three-way interaction supports this: knowledge diversity was negatively related to information sharing when status hierarchy and stability were both high, and only marginally positive when hierarchy was high but unstable.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study integrates the historically separate diversity (horizontal differentiation) and hierarchy (vertical differentiation) literatures, showing that the information-sharing and performance benefits of knowledge diversity cannot be understood without accounting for status hierarchy and its stability. It extends hierarchy-stability research—previously focused on homogeneous teams, where stability is beneficial—by demonstrating that stability can instead be detrimental in knowledge-diverse teams with steep hierarchies.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of knowledge-diverse teams should ask, in order, how important diverse-knowledge exchange is, whether status differences exist, and whether the status hierarchy is stable. Where status differences are unavoidable, managers should deliberately destabilize the hierarchy—rotating leadership, changing roles, refreshing membership, and structuring interactions to require all members' input—to keep diverse knowledge flowing.

**Limitations**
Hierarchy stability was measured via team-member perceptions rather than directly observed changes in the hierarchy. The temporally separated but cross-sectional field design cannot establish causality or rule out reverse effects of information sharing on hierarchy. The aggregated team-level information-sharing measure cannot decompose high- versus low-status sharing or unique versus common information content (partially addressed by a supplementary dyadic analysis that still did not capture actual content exchanged).

**Future Research**
Future work should test the correspondence between perceived and actual hierarchy stability and whether they predict different outcomes, use (field) experiments or longitudinal designs to probe the reciprocal information-sharing/hierarchy relationship, examine why stability effects appear stronger in homogeneous than diverse teams, and capture the actual content of information exchanged across diversity, hierarchy, and stability conditions.
