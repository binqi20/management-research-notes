# Mixed Messages: Crisis Communication–Dismissal (In)Coherence and Shareholder Trust Following Misconduct

**Abstract**
We explore how firms’ post-transgression crisis communication and executive dismissals jointly influence shareholder trust following financial misconduct. We argue the coherence of a firm’s crisis management strategy—the degree to which its elements fit together consistently and logically—plays an important but previously unconsidered role in shareholder trust repair. We utilize multiple methods to abductively develop and test our theory. First, we conduct a qualitative comparative analysis of 51 cases of financial misconduct among S&P 1500 firms that disclosed a misstatement via press release and dismissed either the CEO or CFO within 90 days of the disclosure. Analyzing aspects of these firms’ crisis communication and the type of dismissal executed, this study reveals four configurations that highlight the influence of crisis management (in)coherence on shareholder trust. Second, a policy-capturing study examines the underlying mechanisms that drive shareholders’ perceptions and intended behaviors relative to manipulated crisis management strategies in a controlled setting. Together, findings from these studies indicate that shareholder trust following misconduct depends in part on the (in)coherence between what firms say about their misconduct, how they communicate that information, and what they do to resolve the problem.

**Research Question**
How does a firm's communication about its transgression influence shareholder reactions to its dismissal of an executive following financial misconduct? Specifically, how does the (in)coherence between post-transgression crisis communication and executive dismissal shape shareholder trust repair?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: crisis management strategy coherence (degree to which communication and corrective action fit together logically and consistently); (in)coherence as the focal driver of shareholder trust repair.
- Communicative elements (causal conditions): blame acceptance, severity minimization, first-person disclosure.
- Corrective action (causal condition): type of executive dismissal (CEO vs. CFO).
- Emergent configurations: four "recipes" — e.g., major violation + CEO dismissal and isolated reporting error + CFO dismissal yield favorable reactions; major violation + CFO dismissal and ambiguous problem + unexpected dismissal yield unfavorable reactions.
- Outcomes: cumulative abnormal returns (Study 1); perceived (in)coherence, expected recidivism, and likelihood of increasing/reducing holdings (Study 2).

Shareholders jointly evaluate what a firm says about its misconduct, how it says it, and what it does (executive dismissal), and respond to the coherence among these tactics rather than to any tactic in isolation. When the dismissal logically resolves the problem described in the firm's communication, the strategy reads as coherent and trustworthy; mismatched "mixed messages" signal incoherence, raising expectations of recidivism and prompting divestment. Study 2 shows perceived (in)coherence statistically mediates the interaction of communicative sentiment and dismissal type on shareholders' intended holdings.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends crisis-management and corporate-governance research by theorizing the firm's overarching crisis management *strategy*—and its coherence—as a higher-order construct and important unit of analysis, rather than evaluating communication and dismissal tactics in isolation. Adopting a strategic coherence lens and a neo-configurational (fsQCA) approach, it explains previously inconsistent findings about market reactions to isolated tactics by showing that tactics combine in causally complex, equifinal ways to help or hinder shareholder trust repair.

**Practical Implication**
When restoring shareholder trust after a transgression, firms should avoid sending mixed messages and instead craft a coherent crisis management strategy that deliberately coordinates communications with corrective actions. For example, dismissing the CFO after the CEO has personally accepted blame can lead shareholders to question whether the action resolved the problem; the exemplar cases offer a roadmap for aligning words and deeds.

**Limitations**
Study 1 could not directly measure shareholders' perceptions of (in)coherence and used a relatively small sample with a 90-day dismissal window that created right-censoring. Study 2 tested only simplified versions of select configurations and, as a survey-based quasi-experiment, is susceptible to priming effects and respondent biases; the two studies are designed to counterbalance each other's limitations.

**Future Research**
Researchers could generalize the (in)coherence framework to other stakeholder groups (e.g., employees) and other misconduct types (e.g., consumer-safety or environmental issues), and examine executive *retention* and other corrective actions (e.g., changing reporting or governance practices) as elements of crisis management strategy. Further work could explore sequencing, path dependencies, and contextual factors (severity, magnitude, firm/industry preconceptions) that shape overall coherence.
