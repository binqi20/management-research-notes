# Appraising and Reacting to Perceived Pay For Performance: Leader Competence and Warmth as Critical Contingencies

**Abstract**
While pay for performance (PFP) is widely adopted in organizations to boost employee performance, the empirical evidence on its incentive effect has been mixed. Based on Park and Sturman's PFP perception framework, we integrated two motivation theories—transactional theory of stress and role engagement theory—to delineate two countervailing mechanisms that explain how individuals appraise and react to perceived PFP. Our research considers employees' perception of PFP-I (perceived PFP based on individual performance) and highlights leader competence and warmth as critical contingencies. We propose that perceived PFP-I is appraised by employees as a challenge when leader competence is higher, which leads to employees' work engagement and enhanced task performance, but is appraised as a threat when leader warmth is lower, which leads to employees' work withdrawal and decreased task performance. In two three-wave survey studies, we found support for our model. The present research advances our knowledge regarding the incentive effect of PFP-I by emphasizing the importance of perceived PFP-I and proposing a new way of seeing employee motivation and performance ensuing from perceived PFP-I through the lenses of stress and role (dis)engagement. Our findings provide insights into how organizations and leaders can effectively utilize PFP to motivate employees.

**Research Question**
How can organizations and leaders make employees engage in, rather than withdraw from, work—and thus perform well—under pay for individual performance (PFP-I)? Specifically, how do employees appraise and react to perceived PFP-I, and how do leader competence and warmth shape these appraisals and reactions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived PFP-I (perceived strength of the linkage between individual performance and pay)
- DV(s): Employee task (in-role) performance
- Mediators: Challenge appraisal → work engagement (serial); threat appraisal → work withdrawal (serial); Study 2 adds control coping and escapist coping as micro-mediators
- Moderators: Leader competence (strengthens the PFP-I → challenge-appraisal link); leader warmth (attenuates the PFP-I → threat-appraisal link)

Integrating transactional theory of stress and role engagement theory within Park and Sturman's PFP perception framework, the paper treats PFP-I as a job stressor whose meaning is not fixed but appraised. When the leader is competent—signaling reliable evaluation and task-related resources—employees appraise perceived PFP-I as a challenge, creating a positive psychological condition (meaningfulness, availability) that fuels work engagement and higher performance. When the leader is warm—signaling fairness and socioemotional support—employees are less likely to appraise perceived PFP-I as a threat, reducing work withdrawal and protecting performance. The two countervailing pathways operate concurrently, so employees can have clear or ambivalent reactions depending on the leader's competence–warmth profile.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The research makes a threefold contribution to compensation research: (1) it stresses employees' active role in perceiving, appraising, and reacting to PFP-I, showing perceived PFP-I varies across employees and drives motivational mechanisms for in-role performance; (2) it extends Park and Sturman's (2022) PFP perception framework and integrates leadership and motivation research by positioning leader competence and warmth as fundamental moderators of appraisals and role (dis)engagement; and (3) it foregrounds employees' role (dis)engagement—especially work withdrawal as distinct from (not the opposite of) engagement—as a neglected motivational mechanism that helps reconcile the mixed evidence on the incentive effect of PFP.

**Practical Implication**
Designing a sound PFP system is necessary but not sufficient; organizations and leaders must understand how employees perceive and appraise PFP. Because employees care about leaders' competence and warmth when appraising perceived PFP-I, leaders should develop and visibly communicate both characteristics—e.g., accurate performance evaluation and clear paths to goals (competence) and supportive, considerate behaviors and positive emotions such as gratitude (warmth)—to channel PFP toward engagement rather than withdrawal and to prevent harmful outcomes (performance decrements, ill-being).

**Limitations**
Both studies were conducted in a single, collectivistic culture (China), raising generalizability questions across individualistic cultures. The samples skewed relatively young, presumably because younger employees were more willing to complete the online surveys, so it remains untested whether the findings replicate in samples comprising mostly older employees. Although Study 2 added many controls and explored predictors of perceived PFP-I, the research did not have a theoretical focus on the antecedents of perceived PFP-I.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine nonleader contingencies (e.g., job design features such as autonomy and feedback) that may similarly shape employees' challenge and threat appraisals of perceived PFP-I. Researchers could further probe how employee individual differences moderate reactions to perceived PFP-I (rather than to pay design characteristics), and could more directly theorize and test the antecedents of perceived PFP-I within the broader PFP environment.
