# Lay Theories of Instrumental Relations: Explaining Individual Differences in Dispositional Similarity-Attraction

**Abstract**
The growing diversity in today's workplace requires engaging with people who not only look different but also think different. Yet, research on workplace relations has treated similarity-attraction as a human universal and paid limited attention to individual differences in who respects or tolerates different views and values, and why. We address this gap by examining how lay theories people hold about instrumental relations affect dispositional similarity-attraction. Because people who hold a fixed (versus growth) theory of instrumental relations believe that relationships form primarily on the basis of natural compatibility (versus effort), they should be particularly prone to similarity-attraction on the basis of dispositional similarity in values, attitudes, and personality traits. To test our arguments, we first develop a lay theories of instrumental relations scale, which we use to demonstrate that holding a growth theory decreases (moderates) people's tendency to avoid dispositionally dissimilar partners in a naturally occurring network (Field Study) and a dyadic task (Experiment 1). Finally (Experiment 2), we manipulate lay theories to show that inducing growth theories increases people's satisfaction with a task partner who is dispositionally dissimilar. We conclude by discussing theoretical and practical implications for building diverse yet cohesive workplaces.

**Research Question**
Are some individuals more likely than others to choose or get along with people who have different values, attitudes, and personality traits, and why? Specifically, how do lay theories of instrumental relations (LIR) — beliefs about whether interpersonal compatibility is fixed (natural chemistry) versus malleable (effort) — moderate dispositional similarity-attraction in partner choice and partner satisfaction?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Dispositional similarity (in values, personality traits, or attitudes, depending on study); lay theories of instrumental relations (LIR; fixed vs. growth, measured as a continuum or experimentally manipulated in Experiment 2).
- DV(s): Partner choice / tie formation (perceived instrumental ties in Field Study; sign-up decision in Experiment 1) and satisfaction with a task partner (Experiment 2).
- Mediators: Expected compatibility (mediates the LIR × dispositional-similarity effect on partner choice; Hypothesis 1b).
- Moderators: LIR is the focal moderator of similarity-attraction (Hypotheses 1a and 2); the design statistically or experimentally controls demographic similarity.

People with a fixed theory believe relationships form on natural compatibility ("like pieces of a puzzle"), so they treat dispositional similarity as a quick diagnostic of compatibility and avoid dissimilar partners; people with a growth theory believe compatibility is cultivated through effort ("like muscles"), so they are more receptive to dissimilar others. Because lay theories are self-fulfilling, fixed theorists derive satisfaction from effortless clicking while growth theorists tolerate the effort of working with dissimilar partners. The result is a mediated-moderation model: dispositional similarity-attraction is stronger for fixed theorists, and (for partner choice) this is channeled through expected compatibility.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a novel account of individual differences in dispositional (deep-level) similarity-attraction, departing from prior explanations centered on stereotyping and social categorization of surface-level differences by invoking *compatibility* as the key mechanism. It extends lay-theory research from individuals, intimate relations, and episodic exchanges into ongoing workplace instrumental relations, introducing LIR as both a theoretical construct and a validated measure. It also speaks to the problem of agency in networks, framing LIR as a basis of human agency in how people form and shape instrumental ties rather than treating homophily as a universal.

**Practical Implication**
Measuring or changing employees' lay theories may offer a less heavy-handed route to building diverse yet cohesive workplaces — cultivating growth theories to promote cultural and interpersonal adaptability rather than screening employees for cultural fit. Because lay theories are simple and can be taught "on the spot," brief interventions may help employees value dissimilar partners. The authors caution that growth theories are a barrier-lowering tool, not a silver bullet, and warn against a "false growth mindset" that blames individuals and masks systemic barriers.

**Limitations**
Samples were drawn from students or online populations, limiting generalizability, and the student samples are not large by current standards for testing moderation, warranting caution. Experiment 2 was limited by class size to two treatment groups (no neutral control), so firm conclusions about the relative effects of fixed versus growth manipulations cannot be drawn. The Field Study did not involve experimental treatment and conflates partner choice with partner satisfaction, and the studies capture relatively short-term interactions, raising questions about long-term effects.

**Future Research**
Future work should test whether LIR effects endure over time and shape relationships over longer horizons, and whether LIR affect other relational outcomes such as network multiplexity. More research is needed on the antecedents of lay theories — why some people become fixed rather than growth theorists — including how organizational culture and diversity climate shape employees' LIR. Studies could also examine LIR across non-instrumental relations and the limits of how much deep-level dissimilarity growth theories can overcome.
