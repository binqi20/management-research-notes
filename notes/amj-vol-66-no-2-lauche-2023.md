# The Relational Dynamics of Issue-Selling: Enacting Different Genres for Dealing with Discontent

**Abstract**
Actors without formal power can initiate and shape organizational change through issue-selling as a means to address their concerns about organizational practices. The literature has emphasized the need for perspective-taking and found discontent to be associated with inhibiting fear. Based on a longitudinal field study of middle managers trying to instigate strategic renewal in six different manufacturing companies, we find that discontent can also energize issue-selling, particularly if sellers and -recipients also engage in perspective-taking. We identify three different genres of issue-selling and enacting discontent that are jointly shaped by sellers and -recipients: (a) productive confrontation, (b) avoiding escalation, and (c) collective moaning. In a process model of issue-selling as framing, mobilizing, and pursuing, we illustrate how the genres form part of an organization's collective repertoire of initiating and responding to change. Our findings have implications for pursuing change, as well as for managers seeking to create a constructive environment for expressing discontent.

**Research Question**
How do issue-sellers and -recipients jointly shape how discontent is enacted or circumnavigated over time? The study investigates how less powerful actors pursue strategic-change initiatives and whether discontent serves as a motivational driver or a hampering force for issue-selling.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: discontent (cognitive disagreement paired with emotional commitment to an issue tied to one's values or professional identity); perspective-taking; relational history (power distance, prior conflicts, quality of previous encounters)
- Process / phases: framing → mobilizing → pursuing, with recipients' responses shaping each trajectory
- Genres (emergent patterns): (a) productive confrontation — framing as far-future opportunity, mobilizing by explicating/openly challenging, pursuing by escalating concerns, recipients acknowledging; (b) avoiding escalation — framing as near-future technical renewal, mobilizing by foreshadowing, pursuing by implicit fighting, recipients deferring; (c) collective moaning — framing as avoiding past errors, mobilizing by indirect alluding/generic complaints, pursuing by appeasing, recipients leaving open
- Boundary conditions: low power distance and constructive relational history enable productive confrontation; high power distance and fear yield avoiding escalation; accumulated frustration and pseudo-egalitarian distance yield collective moaning

The authors theorize issue-selling as a relational, collectively enacted process rather than an individual or dyadic act. Whether discontent energizes or paralyzes change depends not only on the seller's moves but on the jointly enacted genre and the recipient's response: supportive acknowledgment fosters agency and productive confrontation, whereas double-bind or indifferent responses foster fear, frustration, and collective helplessness. Reframing DeCelles et al.'s (2020) fight/flight/freeze responses as collective patterns, the genres function as an organization's shared repertoire for raising and responding to change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the issue-selling literature by showing that discontent can be a motivational force for change—conditional on a relational history of collective problem-solving and shared repertoires of open conflict resolution—rather than merely a fear-inducing inhibitor. It conceptualizes issue-selling as genres: collectively shared, recognizable patterns of framing, mobilizing, and pursuing that sellers and recipients jointly enact and reenact, demonstrating that the success of issue-selling cannot be understood from the seller's perspective alone. Collective moaning is identified as a previously unreported phenomenon.

**Practical Implication**
Issue-sellers can diagnose their context: those in low-power-distance settings are best served by framing issues as future opportunities, articulating explicitly, and daring to escalate; those facing high power distance should "read the wind," engage more actively in perspective-taking, and avoid self-depreciation. Managers should recognize that their reactions shape outcomes—acknowledging concerns and following up with clear decisions retains valuable staff, whereas deferring and leaving things open alienates issue-sellers. Facilitators can help teams focus on the shared object and address implicit fighting and moaning.

**Limitations**
Given the inductive design, the researchers could not know in advance how trajectories would unfold or what to look for, which they compensated for by returning to rich video recordings. Interviews focused on issue-sellers, so the subjective experience of recipients—who also play a key role—was underexamined. Spreading data collection across several companies strengthens generalization but means empirical differences between companies can have diverse causes, since the firms differed in innovation practices, scope, team composition, and senior-management roles.

**Future Research**
Future work should include recipients' subjective experience and emotional involvement in issue-selling trajectories. Researchers should examine whether the selling strategies and responses of the three genres are always empirically confounded, and test in experimental or intervention studies whether more successful strategies and responses can improve collective outcomes (e.g., counteracting moaning by explicating content and resource issues). Because the study focused on new product development, future research should further investigate the temporal dynamics of issue-selling in different contexts.
