# With Head and Heart: How Emotions Shape Paradox Navigation in Veterinary Work

**Abstract**
Emotions are part of everyday life, and paradox scholars have long acknowledged that navigating paradox—that is, opposite, interrelated, and enduring tensions—is inherently emotional. Yet, how such emotions shape the ways in which individuals navigate paradoxes remains unclear. Drawing on an in-depth qualitative study of 63 veterinarians facing the business–care paradox, I develop an emotionally informed model of how individuals navigate paradox at work. I find that emotions: (a) make paradox salient by surfacing a personal connection to paradoxical tensions, (b) trigger flexible emotional labor while responding to paradox, and (c) leave emotional traces that foster ongoing learning. In so doing, I surface how emotions shape the way paradoxes are recognized and experienced. My model invites paradox scholars to consider the centrality of emotions in paradox navigation, thus moving beyond emotions as something to be avoided and instead embracing their generative potential. Foregrounding emotions offers rich theoretical opportunities for future work.

**Research Question**
How do emotions shape the ways in which individuals navigate paradox? The study examines this through veterinarians confronting the business–care paradox—the opposing yet interrelated business and care goals embedded in their work.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: The business–care paradox, made salient each time a client interaction pits profitability against the care of a pet.
- Phase 1 — Emotional salience of paradox: Social bonds (perceived deservingness of pet owners) surface social emotions (empathy, compassion, guilt), creating a personal connection to paradoxical tensions and an impetus to consider both sides.
- Phase 2 — Flexible emotional labor between demands: Vets intuit "room to maneuver" and either justify downplaying or justify following emotions, opting for cost or care; organizational billing norms influence the direction.
- Phase 3 — Emotional traces following response: Decisions leave emotional fatigue (focus on cost) or emotional energy (focus on care) that linger after the consultation.
- Phase 4 — Ongoing learning from emotions: Traces drive vets to construct "workable barriers" that block fatigue while preserving exceptions, enabling learning over repeated exposure.
- Influencing factors / boundary conditions: Social bond between vet and client; perceived strictness of organizational billing norms.

Pradies theorizes paradox navigation as an emotionally fueled process rather than a purely cognitive or rational act. Emotions make paradox personally felt (not merely "seen" cognitively), and intuition—a swift, unconscious process—drives flexible iteration between competing demands. The emotional traces left by each response accumulate into learning, so that over time individuals adjust their workable barriers to balance energy and fatigue while sustaining engagement with both poles.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study enriches the microfoundations of paradox theory by explaining how emotions shape both the salience of paradox and individuals' responses to it. It theorizes an affective route to salience (driven by a desire to sustain social bonds), positions intuition as the key driver of flexible responses, and extends Smith's (2014) dynamic-equilibrium model to show that learning to navigate paradox entails balancing emotional traces, not just responses. More broadly it answers calls to address the dominant rational focus of paradox scholarship by foregrounding emotionality alongside rationality.

**Practical Implication**
Practitioners benefit from being attuned to their emotions when confronting paradoxical tensions, since learning to navigate paradox involves reaching a dynamic equilibrium between emotional traces (energy and fatigue), and building a "workable barrier" should be a conscious act. Management educators should highlight emotional awareness and intelligence as key skills in paradox navigation.

**Limitations**
As with other qualitative studies, the single French veterinary context raises questions about generalizability, though the dynamics may transfer to other professional settings where occupational values are pitted against business imperatives. Findings are likely most applicable to organizational members with high autonomy. It is also difficult to verify whether consultations categorized as lacking social emotions were truly devoid of emotion or whether emotions were repressed.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how varying levels of emotional intensity produce different levels of salience over time, explore emotions and paradox at the organizational level (e.g., via multiple case studies of emotion norms), and investigate how individual backgrounds, work orientations, or affective commitments shape emotional experiences of paradox.
