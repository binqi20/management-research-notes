# Inside-Out, Outside-In, or All-in-One? The Role of Network Sequencing in the Elaboration of Ideas

**Abstract**
The structure of advice and support networks within organizations has a profound impact on the elaboration of novel ideas. We explore how the sequence in which individuals expose ideas to their network contacts affects their innovation performance. We argue that, during idea elaboration, inside-out network sequencing—that is, mobilizing input and support from inner-circle ties before outer-circle ones—yields an innovation performance advantage over outside-in network sequencing and all-in-one mobilization of network contacts. Inside-out network sequencing generates valuable early feedback and support from inner-circle ties that actively engage with ill-defined, ill-structured, and uncertain ideas, and delays exposure to outer-circle ties until ideas can better withstand criticism from beyond the social circle where they emerged. We further contend that the benefits of inside-out network sequencing are amplified in environments that lack support for innovation. Using an analysis of survey data and archival innovation performance records for 301 research and development scientists and engineers in a large multinational firm, we find support for our predictions.

**Research Question**
How does the sequence in which R&D scientists and engineers mobilize feedback and support from colleagues for the elaboration of novel ideas affect their innovation performance? In particular, does mobilizing inner-circle ties before outer-circle ones ("inside-out" sequencing) outperform "outside-in" or "all-in-one" approaches, and is this advantage stronger in less supportive work environments?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Degree of inside-out network sequencing (IONS), an index combining the dispersion of tie mobilization across the four idea-elaboration stages and the consistency of mobilizing inner-circle (high shared-third-party) ties before outer-circle (low shared-third-party) ties.
- DV(s): Individual innovation performance rating (three ordered bands from Neptune's annual review); robustness uses a self-reported count of projects reaching the implementation stage-gate.
- Mediators: None tested (the paper notes it does not directly examine why sequencing differences arise).
- Moderators: Four indicators of a less supportive innovation environment — emphasis on accountability (over autonomy), leaders' lack of innovation support, lack of organizational slack, and reluctance to cannibalize existing assets.

Inner-circle ties (those sharing many third-party connections with the idea creator) bring greater commitment and information redundancy, so they patiently digest ill-defined, ill-structured early-stage ideas, supply candid constructive feedback, and surface logical inconsistencies before creators grow wedded to flawed directions. Exposing immature ideas to outer-circle ties too early risks dismissive reactions and "false negatives," whereas these nonredundant outsiders become valuable later, once ideas are robust enough to withstand external scrutiny and to validate fit with the wider organization. Sequencing inner-before-outer therefore lets individuals secure early feedback while shielding fragile ideas, mitigating the dual risk of dropping good ideas or persisting with bad ones. The contrast between circles—and hence the payoff to careful sequencing—sharpens in unsupportive contexts where outer-circle skepticism is most damaging.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces "network sequencing" as a temporal, behavioral dimension of networks distinct from network structure and position, showing that *when* individuals involve which contacts—not just *whom* they are connected to—explains differences in innovation performance over and above network constraint. It connects to and extends research on strategic networking behaviors by demonstrating that creative individuals outperform peers by making "savvy choices of who to involve when." Third, it shows that network mechanisms such as feedback and support are time- and alter-dependent and may backfire if mobilized at the wrong time, unpacking idea elaboration into substages in which strong/inner and weak/outer ties play different roles.

**Practical Implication**
Because network sequencing matters yet many individuals make poor mobilization choices, organizations should inform and train R&D talent to deliberately sequence their networks—seeking early input from committed inner-circle ties to nurture ideas in "safe spaces" before exposing them to outer-circle scrutiny, while avoiding cleaving too closely to the inner circle and engaging outer-circle ties once ideas mature. Such training is especially valuable in units lacking innovation support, where engaging outsiders too early is costly; careful management of slack, autonomy/accountability balance, cannibalization concerns, and supportive leadership can each reduce the risk of squandering creative talent.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a single organization with relatively homogeneous HR practices, limiting generalizability, and it examines only intra-organizational networks. The scenario-based measure of mobilization timing captures expected rather than actual behavior and cannot establish whether the reported approach generalizes across different ideas, contexts, or situations. The innovation-performance measure has a short (single-year) time horizon, and inner/outer-circle ties are operationalized structurally (shared third parties) without directly observing tie strength; the paper also does not explore why sequencing differences arise.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine network sequencing in other organizational contexts and across organizational boundaries, and use more direct methods (e.g., diary-based monitoring) to capture how individuals seek feedback for different ideas. Researchers could disentangle tie strength from social cohesion in sequencing effects, study how idea radicalness and longer-horizon (radical) innovation call for different sequencing strategies, and investigate antecedents of sequencing choices such as confidence, political skill, personality, and cognition.
