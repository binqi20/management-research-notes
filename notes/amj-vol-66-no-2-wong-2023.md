# Pay Transparency as a Moving Target: A Multistep Model of Pay Compression, I-Deals, and Collectivist Shared Values

**Abstract**
Drawing from research on the transparency-privacy dilemma in management, we theorize that firm-level pay transparency elicits a multistep process involving managers and employees that shifts the dispersion in remuneration from more to less observable forms, thus making pay transparency a “moving target.” We posit a serial indirect effect of pay transparency on firm-level rates of i-deal grants (a less observable form of remuneration) via variable pay compression and heightened rates of employee i-deal requests, with this indirect effect amplified in firms characterized by collectivist shared values. First examining the role of managerial agency and collectivist shared values in the pay transparency–compression relationship in a simulation-based experiment, we test the overall model in a multisource field study using a sample of 111 medical device distribution firms. Our findings demonstrate that: (a) firm-level pay transparency is predictive of greater pay compression, (b) firm-level rates of i-deal grants are largely explained by this pay compression via its effects on employee i-deal requests, and (c) this sequential effect is amplified in firms with more collectivist shared values. Accordingly, we explicate how transparency triggers unintentional hiding, and suggest that accompanying more transparent pay may be an increased reliance upon rewards that, by their very nature, are less transparent.

**Research Question**
Whether, how, and when does firm-level pay transparency become a "moving target" — that is, through what multistep process does transparency lead managers and employees to shift remuneration from more observable forms (variable pay) to less observable ones (nonmonetary i-deals), and under what conditions is this process amplified?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm-level pay transparency
- DV(s): Firm-level rates of nonmonetary i-deal grants (developmental and benefit)
- Mediators: Variable pay compression (serial step 1) → firm-level rates of employee i-deal requests (serial step 2)
- Moderators: Firm-level collectivist shared values

Building on Bernstein's (2017) transparency-privacy model, the authors argue that making pay more observable pressures managers to avoid controversy over visible pay differentials by compressing variable pay. Consistent with compensating wage differentials theory, this compression motivates employees to seek redress through less observable, particularistic rewards (i-deals), and those requests are in turn granted — thereby "hiding" reward dispersion in discreet forms. Drawing on social comparison theory, firms with more collectivist shared values heighten the salience of observability and social monitoring, amplifying the compression-to-i-deal-request stage of the serial indirect effect. The field study supported Hypotheses 1–4 and 6 (and 5b), while Hypotheses 5a was not supported.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Bernstein's (2017) transparency-privacy theory from the individual to the firm level, explicating a previously undescribed multistep mechanism by which transparency indirectly (rather than intentionally) produces hiding of reward differentials. It contributes to i-deals research by identifying pay compression as a firm-level antecedent of i-deal requests and grants and by isolating collectivist shared values as a boundary condition that counterintuitively amplifies the transparency–privacy paradox.

**Practical Implication**
Because pay transparency can trigger unintended pay compression and a downstream rise in nonmonetary i-deal grants, leaders should anticipate these externalities — e.g., emphasizing to managers that justifiable pay differences benefit their own performance, and monitoring i-deal allocation so hidden rewards are not used as a "back door" for gender- and ethnicity-based disparities. Effects are especially pronounced in collectivist contexts, where managers should make pay policies more transparent and procedurally clear.

**Limitations**
Study 1's experimental design cannot rule out reciprocal causation between compression and transparency, and its short-term managerial role may not generalize to real-life decisions. Study 2 used field data from small firms in a single industry (medical device distribution) and a single occupation (salespeople) in one country, limiting generalizability to larger firms, other industries, and contexts where vertical/tournament pay dynamics matter.

**Future Research**
Future work should test the causal link between pay compression and i-deals via experimental or quasi-experimental designs, examine pay compression as a mechanism linking transparency to outcomes like collective performance and human-capital sorting, and pursue cross-industry and cross-cultural samples. Researchers could also unpack the dominant mechanism behind the collectivist amplification effect and study pay "noticeability" at the individual level.
