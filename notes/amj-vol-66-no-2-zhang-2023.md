# Learning Down to Train Up: Mentors Are More Effective When They Value Insights from Below

**Abstract**
Although mentorship is vital for individual success, potential mentors often view it as a costly burden. To understand what motivates mentors to overcome this barrier and more fully engage with their mentees, we introduce a new construct, “learning direction,” which captures the beliefs people have about which individuals within a hierarchy— upward, lateral, or downward—are valuable sources of knowledge. Although most mentors believe knowledge resides higher up in hierarchies, the current research demonstrates mentors are more engaged and effective when they value insights from below. Our first three studies found that downward learning predicted mentor engagement (Study 1) because downward learning-directed mentors viewed mentoring as a worthwhile opportunity for their own learning (Studies 2A and 2B). A field study examined downstream consequences on mentees’ learning outcomes, finding that downward learning-directed mentors trained more hireable mentees (Study 3). A final experiment established causality while also demonstrating that learning direction is open to intervention: reflecting on a downward learning experience increased mentor engagement, which enhanced the mentorship experience for their mentees (Study 4). We extend theories on learning orientation to learning direction and demonstrate that beliefs about the hierarchical sources of learning are critical to successful mentoring.

**Research Question**
What motivates potential mentors to engage with and be effective for their mentees? The paper asks whether mentors' "learning direction"—specifically the belief that those lower in a hierarchy are valuable sources of knowledge—predicts mentor engagement and mentoring effectiveness, above and beyond related constructs like learning orientation and growth mindset.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Mentor downward learning direction (measured in Studies 1-3; manipulated downward vs. upward in Study 4), with lateral and upward learning direction as comparison directions
- DV(s): Mentor engagement (time/questions answered, advice word count); mentee learning outcomes (technical skill, hireability in Study 3); mentee learning experience (perceived quality of received advice and support in Study 4)
- Mediators: Perceiving mentoring as a worthwhile opportunity to learn knowledge and skills (Studies 2A-2B); mentor engagement (Studies 2B, 4)
- Moderators: Mentor hierarchical position / positional power (Study 4)

Believing that people below oneself in a social hierarchy hold valuable knowledge reframes mentoring from a costly obligation into a two-way learning opportunity. This reframing raises mentors' engagement (the time and effort they invest), which in turn improves mentees' learning outcomes and their perceptions of advice and support. Because power tends to reduce perspective-taking, the downward-learning manipulation has its strongest effects for mentors high in positional power.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces "learning direction" as a new construct that shifts learning from a task-related construct (learning orientation) to a relational one—who one believes one can learn from across a hierarchy. It extends work on learning orientation and growth mindset (which focus on the self) by showing that downward learning predicts mentor engagement and effectiveness independent of those constructs, and integrates research on social hierarchy, mentoring, and advice to explain what makes mentors effective.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations typically motivate mentors by emphasizing benefits to mentees, which is insufficient because mentorship still feels like a costly obligation. Instead, they should highlight the learning value mentees offer to mentors. The research identifies an easy-to-implement intervention: having mentors reflect on times they learned from someone below them in a hierarchy shifts their learning direction and boosts engagement, especially for those in positions of power.

**Limitations**
In the field study (Study 3), although mentor-mentee matches were based only on scheduling availability, the authors acknowledge the relationship between mentors' downward learning and student outcomes could partly run in reverse, with mentee performance shaping mentors' reported learning direction. The field sample of matched mentors was small and predominantly male, motivating the controlled, gender-diverse experiment in Study 4.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine downward learning beyond mentoring (e.g., how leaders' downward learning affects their own and their team's productivity and innovation), and explore optimal learning directions across career stages and roles. Researchers are also encouraged to study the conversational-level process through which downward learning improves mentee outcomes—disentangling content of knowledge exchanged from relational rapport—and to extend learning direction to nontraditional sources of learning.
