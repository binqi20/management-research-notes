# May I Please Go the Extra Mile? Citizenship Communication Strategies and Their Effect on Individual Initiative OCB, Work–Family Conflict, and Partner Satisfaction

**Abstract**
Organizational citizenship behavior (OCB) contributes to organizational effectiveness, but it can also create a burden for employees and their partners. In three studies, we examine how employees communicate with their partner about engaging in OCB and how this affects employees' OCB, their work–family conflict, and their partner's satisfaction. In Study 1, we use qualitative data to identify five ways employees communicate with their partner in order to engage in individual initiative OCB (e.g., staying late at work, volunteering for special assignments). In Study 2, using dual-concern theory, we hypothesize that self-concerned citizenship communication strategies will not only increase OCB, but also increase work–family conflict, and we predict that other-concerned communication strategies will reduce work–family conflict and increase partner satisfaction. Using survey data from employees and their partners, these hypotheses were partially supported. Finally, in Study 3, we use experimental vignette methodology to provide causal evidence that citizenship communication strategies influence partner satisfaction. Together, these studies increase our understanding of how employees communicate with their partner about OCB, indicate that there is no single communication strategy that has positive implications for both OCB and home life, and suggest that employees and their partners tend to think of OCB as involuntary for achieving family goals.

**Research Question**
How do employees communicate with their partner at home when engaging in individual initiative OCB, and how do these citizenship communication strategies affect the employee's OCB, their work–family conflict, and their partner's satisfaction?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Five citizenship communication strategies, classified by dual concern orientation. Self-concerned: projecting payoffs, invoking prior conversations. Other-concerned: providing early notice, seeking permission, negotiating logistics.
- DV(s): Individual initiative OCB (partner-rated); work–family conflict (employee + partner averaged); partner satisfaction (relationship satisfaction in Study 2; communication satisfaction in Study 3).
- Mediators: None modeled (the conceptual model focuses on main effects; the authors did not find evidence that strategies are used in combination).
- Moderators: None supported; gender was tested as a moderator in Studies 2 and 3 and OCB type (depleting vs. enriching) in Study 3, but no significant interactions emerged.

Drawing on dual concern theory from the conflict-management literature, the authors argue that self-concerned strategies emphasize the employee's own career goals and the rewards of OCB, whereas other-concerned strategies prioritize accommodating the partner. Self-concerned strategies should facilitate OCB but strain home life, while other-concerned strategies should reduce work–family conflict and raise partner satisfaction. Empirically, the trade-off is asymmetric: invoking prior conversations and projecting payoffs raise OCB (and invoking prior conversations raises work–family conflict; projecting payoffs lowers partner satisfaction), while seeking permission and providing early notice raise partner satisfaction but do not reduce work–family conflict and (for seeking permission) lower OCB.

**Theoretical Contribution**
By identifying the *content* of partner communication specific to OCB, the paper extends partner-communication and work–family research—previously focused on how communication is enacted (e.g., demand–withdraw, assertive–passive)—into the citizenship domain. It demonstrates that there is no single communication strategy beneficial for both work (OCB) and home (reduced conflict, higher partner satisfaction), and shows that employees and their partners conceptualize OCB as involuntary for achieving family goals, deepening the literature on felt pressure to engage in citizenship.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations and managers should recognize that "extra-role" behavior is often experienced as non-discretionary by employees and their partners, raising justice concerns. Employees can strategically match communication to their priorities: emphasizing seeking permission and providing early notice to protect home life, or projecting payoffs and invoking prior conversations to enable OCB—while being mindful of each strategy's costs. Organizations should encourage, but not compel, good citizenship.

**Limitations**
The Study 1 qualitative sample was relatively young, well educated, predominantly White, and seemingly happily married, possibly surfacing mainly constructive strategies and limiting transferability. Study 2 data were cross-sectional, precluding causal inference, and OCB was partner-rated rather than fully objective. The research focused on one resource-consuming OCB type (individual initiative) and on one-way (employee) communication rather than dyadic exchange.

**Future Research**
Scholars could examine dysfunctional or post-hoc communication strategies, collect reciprocal dyadic data using the actor–partner interdependence model, and study how OCB may generate work–family enrichment rather than only conflict. Additional work could investigate antecedents of citizenship communication (e.g., personality, organizational norms) and whether these strategies extend to other OCB types and targets such as coworkers and managers.
