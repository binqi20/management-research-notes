# Double Weaving: A Bottom-Up Process of Connecting Locations and Scales to Mitigate Grand Challenges

**Abstract**
Grand challenges are system-wide problems. While top-down approaches to mitigating grand challenges may have potentially far-reaching impact, such initiatives from the higher scales can fail because of limited knowledge of ground realities. At the same time, resilient local efforts to address societal challenges can have limited reach. How can a grassroots organization work across locations and scales to address a grand challenge? We report the findings of a longitudinal case study of the Child In Need Institute, a nonprofit organization that successfully scaled its impact to reach over seven million beneficiaries. Based on 40 years of archival data and four years of intensive fieldwork, our findings reveal “double weaving” as a recursive process of diagnosing and addressing problems by connecting actors and resources across locations and scales. The double weaving process gives researchers a framework to explore the complex interplay between locations and scales that is necessary to address grand challenges. Our study contributes to research on grand challenges and scaling, advancing the spatial turn in organizational studies by developing the scalar dimension, and expanding research that has primarily focused on locations.

**Research Question**
How can a grassroots organization work within and across locations and scales to mitigate a grand challenge? The study uses a spatial lens that treats space as both horizontal (locations) and vertical (scales), asking how a resource-poor local organization can connect dispersed actors and resources to address a system-wide, "wicked" problem.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / dimensions: Scaling out, scaling up, scaling down (three directional components) plus reinforcing (a process driver) — the four broad activity types that comprise double weaving.
- Process / phases: Scaling out (local intervening, disseminating) → scaling up (synchronizing, strategic linking) → scaling down (collective capacity building, anchoring) → reinforcing (diagnosing, consolidating), iterating recursively through multiple cycles.
- Triggering conditions: Grassroots interventions lack financial resources and access to policy-making, which triggers a need to scale up to higher-scale actors; successful scaling up builds on credible disseminated outcomes of local interventions.
- Boundary conditions: A cooperative case with no contestation/conflict; norms of freely shared knowledge; and a long, nested four-decade scaling path from community to government.

Double weaving is theorized as a multidirectional, recursive process in which a focal organization "weaves" actors and resources horizontally across locations and vertically across scales at the same time, analogous to two fabrics woven and interwoven for strength. Reinforcing (continuous diagnosing of root causes and consolidating of what works while discarding what fails) drives and sustains scaling out, up, and down, giving the process continuity and durability. Rather than seeking shared consensus among all stakeholders, the organization weaves actors around specific problems on which agreement can be more easily managed, navigating scalar asymmetries of power and contextual familiarity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to grand-challenges research by showing that identifying a grand challenge and its root causes is inseparable from engaging with it, and by treating diagnosing and mitigating as concomitant rather than sequential. It advances the "spatial turn" in organizational research by developing the under-theorized scalar (vertical) dimension alongside locations (horizontal), and extends scaling research—which has treated scaling as a unidirectional process—by theorizing scaling down as a complement to scaling out and up and by foregrounding scalar asymmetry.

**Practical Implication**
For grassroots organizations with limited resources, the case offers lessons on scaling social impact: pair local interventions with disseminating credible outcomes to attract higher-scale actors, synchronize with the mandates and expectations of those actors (rather than lecturing them), and use scaling down to channel resources and policy into superior grassroots interventions. Lower-scale actors can exploit asymmetries—using higher-scale actors' resources and policy access while supplying the local contextual familiarity those actors lack.

**Limitations**
The authors flag three boundary conditions of a single-case study. First, the case features collaboration and cooperation without the contestation or conflict often inherent in grand challenges. Second, CINI members freely shared knowledge, whereas many settings involve closely guarded ideas and inter-firm rivalry. Third, the nested, four-decade scaling path highlights how slow the process can be and may not generalize to contexts with faster, politically accountable pathways.

**Future Research**
Future work could compare less emotionally intense and vivid issues (e.g., preventable diseases such as malaria) with child malnutrition; examine the conditions under which knowledge diffuses widely given the tension between sparse networks for idea brokering and dense networks for implementation; and study grand challenges from a higher-scale actor's perspective. Researchers could also examine more closely how actors exploit scalar asymmetries across scales.
