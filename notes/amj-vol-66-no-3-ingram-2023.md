# Identity Multiplicity and the Formation of Professional Network Ties

**Abstract**
How does personal identity multiplicity—a self-perception with more elements—help an individual connect with others? I argue that identity multiplicity increases the likelihood of establishing professional network ties through two mechanisms: individuation, or the tendency of a potential network partner to view one as an individual as opposed to a representative of a category; and identity homophily, or the attraction and interaction opportunities that emerge as a function of shared identity elements with a potential partner. The benefits of identity multiplicity are particularly strong for promoting interracial ties as individuation reduces racial stereotyping. I find support for these ideas in an analysis of the creation of network ties of mid-career professionals.

**Research Question**
How does identity multiplicity—the extent to which personal identities draw on multiple elements—predict the incidence of professional network ties, and does it particularly promote ties between individuals of different races?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Dyadic Identity Multiplicity (count of salient identity elements across ego and alter, from "identity maps")
- DV(s): Likelihood of a professional network tie within a dyad (sent and reciprocated ties; logit)
- Mediators: Dyadic Individuation (uniqueness of self-description via LSA); Identity Homophily (LSA cosine similarity of ego and alter identity-map text)
- Moderators: Different Race (identity multiplicity moderates the negative racial-difference effect on tie formation)

Identity multiplicity makes a person harder to categorize, which prompts interlocutors to individuate them—perceiving them as a unique person rather than a category representative—and individuation drives attraction, engagement, and the initiation of ties. Simultaneously, more identity elements raise the odds of sharing salient elements with an alter (identity homophily), creating bases of attraction and interaction. KHB tests indicate individuation and identity homophily jointly mediate ~82% of the identity-multiplicity effect (full mediation). Because individuation reduces negative racial stereotyping, identity multiplicity is especially potent for forging interracial ties—what the author labels the "Grunitzky effect."

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper brings personal identity directly into network-formation theory, advancing the "bring the individual back in" agenda by treating self-articulated identity (not demographic proxies) as a determinant of ties. It introduces "identity homophily," extending value homophily beyond race and gender to any self-identified element, and theorizes individuation as an original, tested mechanism linking identity to tie formation and to the transcendence of racial categories.

**Practical Implication**
Individuals who want richer, more diverse professional networks should cultivate identity multiplicity—e.g., by joining multiple affinity groups rather than focusing on one, and by experimenting with and refining authentic identity narratives. Organizations can leverage existing identity-based affinity groups to build cross-demographic ties that enhance creativity, decision-making, collaboration, and inclusion.

**Limitations**
The data do not capture off-campus socializing, so the exact location where each tie formed is unobserved (though the author argues this does not threaten the hypotheses). The reverse-causality concern—that ties reshape identity salience—is addressed through several robustness analyses but cannot be fully ruled out, particularly over longer time periods. The setting is a single part-time MBA program, and data collection occurred under COVID-19 constraints.

**Future Research**
Future work could analyze the structure of identity maps (e.g., path distance or eigenvector centrality of elements) to study how the importance and interconnection of identity elements shape networking. Researchers could examine networking agency and goal achievement as a function of identity multiplicity, apply text analysis of large identity corpora (e.g., college essays) to study identity homophily at scale, and explore implications for tie efficacy and overall network structure (e.g., modularity).
