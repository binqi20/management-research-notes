# Structuring the Start-Up: How Coordination Emerges in Start-Ups through Learning Sequencing

**Abstract**
To succeed in growing and scaling their organization, start-ups must establish roles, routines, rules, and plans that coordinate organizational activities. However, early-stage start-ups often lack such coordination mechanisms. Through a longitudinal qualitative multiple-case study of five start-ups, we develop a theoretical framework for how start-ups develop and improve coordination over time. We find that start-ups establish coordination through a learning sequence consisting of four distinct learning styles. To develop coordination successfully, start-ups anticipate coordination problems before they escalate, steal ideas and frameworks from others, experimentally implement coordination, and combine and simplify coordination mechanisms. By providing a processual understanding of how start-ups develop coordination, we contribute to the literature on coordination in start-ups, which has tended toward static explanations. We also add to the literatures on emergent coordination and organizational learning, as we highlight the role of deliberate learning in developing coordination, and how different learning styles link together to create learning sequences.

**Research Question**
The paper asks: (a) How do start-ups develop coordination over time? and (b) How does successful coordination affect organizational performance?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Early-stage start-ups lack the institutionalized scaffolding (roles, routines, rules, plans) that the emergent-coordination literature assumes is already present, so they must learn to coordinate from a rudimentary starting point.
- Process / phases (the "best practice" learning sequence): (1) *Anticipating coordination* (anticipatory learning) — foreseeing coordination needs before crises emerge; (2) *Steal with pride* (vicarious learning) — importing ready-made frameworks and practices from peers and competitors; (3) *Experimental implementation* (experimental learning) — minimal roll-outs, then scaling mechanisms that prove functional; (4) *Combine and simplify* (trial-and-error learning) — layering and simplifying mechanisms to fit the start-up and avoid over-formalization.
- Key constructs (coordination mechanisms scored per case): rules and plans, routines, and roles (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009).
- Emergent outcomes: Coordination mechanisms that improve performance, indexed by market growth, received funding, and employee job satisfaction.
- Boundary conditions: Learning styles complement and prompt one another (path-dependent sequencing); start-ups may restart the sequence at junctures such as rapid scaling or pivots.

Across the five cases, the high performer (Freya) executed the full anticipatory→vicarious→experimental→trial-and-error sequence, while lower performers stalled by relying on improvised, unstructured solutions or by failing to combine and simplify. The central process insight is that, lacking pre-existing scaffolds, start-ups must learn coordination *deliberately*: each learning style makes a distinct contribution, and styles feed into one another (e.g., anticipatory learning motivates vicarious search; vicarious learning supplies frameworks for experimentation). Coordination is thus reframed as a state reached through learning rather than something that emerges from institutionalized scaffolds.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper's core contribution is a processual theoretical framework explaining how start-ups learn to coordinate by sequencing complementary learning styles, addressing a literature on start-up coordination that has been dominated by static, top-down "blueprint" explanations. It extends the emergent-coordination literature by showing how coordination emerges from an immature state with underdeveloped routines and roles—through deliberate learning rather than from pre-existing scaffolds—and adds to the organizational learning literature by demonstrating how different learning styles interact in learning sequences, with one style compensating for shortcomings in another.

**Practical Implication**
Start-ups should not fear having clear roles, rules, plans, and routines and should deliberately seek to implement them rather than staying unstructured. Entrepreneurs are advised to anticipate coordination needs early, learn from peers, and continuously combine and simplify coordination mechanisms to fit their current situation while avoiding bureaucratic rigidity.

**Limitations**
As an inductive multiple-case study, the work is limited in its knowledge claims, especially regarding performance outcomes, and the authors cannot fully rule out alternative explanations (e.g., market volatility, team-formation effects, founder experience). Because only the high performer restarted its learning sequence within the study window, the authors' ability to theorize iterative sequences and recovery from coordination crises is constrained, and the data are limited on how start-ups recover from such crises.

**Future Research**
The authors call for larger-scale, quantitative work to test whether the learning styles are additive and sequential (does order matter, or can styles be reordered?) and whether the findings replicate in a larger population of start-ups. They also encourage research on how specific learning styles contribute to distinct aspects of coordination, how start-ups restart learning sequences as they scale, and how start-ups recover from coordination crises.
