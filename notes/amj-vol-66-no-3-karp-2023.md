# Gaining Organizational Adoption: Strategically Pacing the Position of Digital Innovations

**Abstract**
Scholars have long suggested that, to foster adoption for their innovations, entrepreneurs should engage with customers to better understand their unmet needs. Yet, customers frequently reside in organizations, and organizational members may not be aligned in their views regarding the benefits an innovation offers. Decision-making within organizations is rarely under the purview of a single executive and various members can wield influence. Given this plurality, how do entrepreneurial firms gain organizational adoption for their innovations? I explore how 54 entrepreneurial firms, all participating in a digital health accelerator, attempted to gain organizational adoption for their innovations. I show that, when entrepreneurs pursued an embedded process, they developed ways to expand the relevance of their innovations for new customers through new use cases, but strategically paced and withheld disruptive aspects of their innovations to appease different audiences within customer organizations. Firms that leveraged a market-centric process also expanded the relevance of their innovations. Despite sparking the interest of executives, firms using a market-centric approach did not gain adoption. By unpacking the process by which some entrepreneurs discovered how to strategically pace the deployment of their digital innovations, I contribute a mechanism that explains how digital innovations gain organizational adoption.

**Research Question**
Given that adoption decisions inside customer organizations involve a plurality of members with divergent views rather than a single executive, how do entrepreneurial firms gain organizational adoption for their (often disruptive) digital innovations?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: "new use cases" (extensions that expand an innovation's relevance); the single-versus-multiparty difference in organizational adoption; the tension between revealing value-adding disruptive functionality and threatening members' expertise, roles, and jurisdiction.
- Two approaches: an *embedded approach* (deep, on-site engagement with customers to co-develop use cases) versus a *market-centric approach* (scanning competitors and the market, demonstrating expertise, magnifying claims, comprehensively rolling out functionality).
- Practices of strategic pacing (embedded firms only): (i) concealing disruptive functionality, (ii) restraining claims that an innovation displaces work, and (iii) shifting the velocity of deployment based on impact to members' expertise.
- Triggering conditions: deep engagement surfaces how an innovation threatens members' jurisdiction, prompting firms to withhold or slow disruptive elements; threats from *outside* a focal business unit require concealing functionality, whereas threats from *within* a unit can be managed by restraining claims alone.
- Emergent outcome: firms using the embedded approach with strategic pacing generally gained adoption (paid pilots); market-centric firms sparked executive interest but generally failed to gain adoption.

Across 54 firms, identifying unmet customer needs was necessary but insufficient for adoption. The embedded approach gave entrepreneurs contextual insight into how diverse organizational members would receive an innovation, enabling them to strategically pace deployment—temporarily concealing disruptive, value-adding features and restraining claims that the innovation would automate or replace members' work—so as to appease audiences whose expertise or jurisdiction the innovation threatened. The digital nature of the innovations made this selective, reversible "switching on and off" of functionality feasible.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The core contribution is a theoretically grounded process model explaining how entrepreneurial firms gain organizational adoption for digital innovations through strategic pacing, extending research that typically picks up after the adoption decision or assumes adoption is made by a few individuals. It reconceives pacing as a *strategic* tool (slowing the introduction of disruptive elements to manage internal stakeholders) rather than only a reflective device for managing market uncertainty, and shows that threats to members' expertise and jurisdiction from outside versus inside a focal business unit call for different tactics (concealing functionality vs. positioning alone).

**Practical Implication**
Entrepreneurs seeking organizational adoption should engage deeply with customers not merely to identify unmet needs but to gain contextual awareness of how diverse organizational members will react, and should strategically pace deployment—building disruptive functionality robustly but withholding or slowly releasing it, and positioning innovations as complements or aids—until affected members can demonstrate expertise in new ways.

**Limitations**
The study examines digital health and does not test whether the observations generalize to other contexts (e.g., financial services or fields further afield), nor whether strategic pacing works for non-digital innovations whose features cannot be flexibly turned off. It also does not address the long-term effects of an embedded process and strategic pacing.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how these dynamics transition over time as firms gain scale or market dominance and may shift from an embedded to a market-centric process, whether an embedded process and strategic pacing enable other forms of entrepreneurial growth such as funding, and how entrepreneurs foster adoption of disruptive innovations in other regulatory and industry settings.
