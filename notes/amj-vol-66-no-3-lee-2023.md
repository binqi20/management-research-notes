# Betwixt and Between: National and Organizational Identification of Host Country Managers Working in MNE Subsidiaries

**Abstract**
This study investigates the patterns of dual identities of host country managers working in multinational enterprise (MNE) subsidiaries and how these patterns relate to their feelings of identity-related stress within the foreign-owned firms. Drawing on the acculturation literature, we classify the patterns of dual identities based on membership in nation and organization into four identity profiles: (a) diffuse (weak national and organizational identification), (b) national (strong national and weak organizational identification), (c) organizational (weak national and strong organizational identification), and (d) integrated (strong national and organizational identification). We submit that perceived conflict between the dual identities renders MNEs' host country managers less likely to present the integrated and the organizational identity profiles and more likely to present the national identity profile than domestic firm managers. MNEs' host country managers also identify more strongly with their nation and less strongly with their organization than domestic firm managers do. The combination of strong national identification and weak organizational identification among MNEs' host country managers exposes them to greater acculturative stress within foreign organizations. The results of an analysis of a sample of 843 Korean managers working in 19 Korean firms and 60 MNE subsidiaries in Korea corroborate our hypotheses.

**Research Question**
How do the dual identity patterns of host country managers working in foreign MNE subsidiaries—in terms of the interrelationship and relative strength of their national and organizational identities—differ from those of domestic firm managers, and how do these distinctive patterns relate to the identity-related (acculturative) stress they experience within the foreign-owned firm?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Foreign-owned firm (dummy: 1 = managers in MNE subsidiaries, 0 = domestic firm managers); national identification and organizational identification (for the acculturative-stress model).
- DV(s): Dual identity profile membership (diffuse / national / organizational / integrated, via LPA); level of national identification; level of organizational identification; acculturative stress.
- Mediators: None formally modeled.
- Moderators: Perceived career prospects (moderates the foreignness → national/organizational identification links, H5-H6); organizational identification × national identification interaction predicting acculturative stress (H7).

The paper argues that because host country managers' national and organizational identities cross-cut each other (whereas domestic managers' identities are nested), foreign ownership and control heighten the salience of national identity and pose distinctiveness/value threats to it. These threats promote subgroup (national) identification and inhibit superordinate (organizational) identification, making the national profile more likely and the integrated/organizational profiles less likely than for domestic managers. Strong national plus weak organizational identification then exposes managers to greater acculturative stress; high organizational identification neutralizes the positive national-identification–stress link, while more positive perceived career prospects attenuate the foreignness effects.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to international management in three ways: it uses a counterfactual framework to show how host country managers' dual-identity patterns differ from domestic managers' (rather than studying host country managers in isolation); it identifies an internal, human-resource source of the liability of foreignness (low organizational identification and acculturative stress) overlooked by prior work focused on external-environment sources; and it shows that host country managers' persistent strong national identity implies the MNE is a multicultural "mosaic" rather than a "melting pot," so imposing a common corporate identity may backfire.

**Practical Implication**
Rather than substituting host country managers with parent country nationals, MNEs should adopt measures that raise host country managers' organizational identification and reduce acculturative stress—notably well-developed global talent-management programs that provide clear, credible career development paths across the worldwide organization. A dual identity approach that acknowledges distinct national affiliate identities while cultivating a common ingroup identity is recommended over a "one-company" global identity approach.

**Limitations**
The theoretical premise that nationality is a subjectively important identity may not hold where perceived "nation-ness" is low or in less-developed host countries, so the main thesis may not generalize beyond a relatively developed, collectivist setting like Korea. The single-source, cross-sectional data likely deflate the interaction effect (common method variance) and make it difficult to verify the causal direction between identification patterns and acculturative stress.

**Future Research**
Future work should explore how perceived nation-ness and sociocultural divides shape attitudes toward foreignness, and test whether the main thesis holds in more individualistic and in considerably less-developed host countries. Longitudinal designs collecting identification and acculturative-stress data at different time points are needed to address common method variance and establish causality in MNE subsidiary contexts.
