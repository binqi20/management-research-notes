# Creative Jolts: Exploring How Entrepreneurs Let Go of Ideas During Creative Revision

**Abstract**
Creative revision involves sharing nascent ideas and soliciting feedback. This process has been portrayed as providing instrumental feedback that enables autonomous creators to shape and progress their ideas in a mostly positive, developmental way. However, creative revision also entails destruction—letting go of some ideas to make way for the new. Research has suggested that abandoning some aspects of ideas will be affectively challenging for creators, but has yet to elaborate how they navigate that challenge. In a longitudinal qualitative study of entrepreneurial ventures over a one-year period, we uncovered a novel phenomenon: the creative jolt—an episode of cognitive and emotional upheaval through which creators let go of their original ideas and allowed new ones to emerge, so that their ideas shifted in dramatic ways. We elaborate the processes through which creative jolts shape the trajectories of creative ideas. Our study captures the rollercoaster of heightened affect and cognitive disorientation experienced by autonomous creators as they try to bring nascent ideas to life.

**Research Question**
How do creators let go of ideas during creative revision (in order to allow new ideas to emerge and develop)? The paper studies how autonomous creators—early-stage entrepreneurs—navigate the disruptive episodes through which they relinquish ideas.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: *Trigger feedback* — negative, subtractive, core-directed feedback from a credible source (trusted/high-status mentor) that is perceived as threatening the idea or the venture's core mission.
- Key constructs / tensions: the *creative jolt* (an episode of cognitive and emotional upheaval); *assumptive worlds* that must be broken down before letting go; the persistency paradox between flexible revision and rigid commitment.
- Process / phases: (1) *existential crisis* — shattered assumptions plus a heightened emotional state, branching into *existential dread* (threat to idea) or *existential awakening* (threat to mission); (2) *reappraisal* — sensemaking via *reaching out* (diversifying through new networks) or *reaching in* (reflecting on core values with close peers); (3) *resolution* — discarding core aspects and reorienting via *recreation* (a fundamentally new idea) or *restoration* (return to the original mission).
- Emergent outcomes: a dramatic, generative shift onto a new idea trajectory, accompanied by emotional repair.

Creative jolts loosen creators' attachment to ideas by breaking down the assumptive worlds underpinning them, opening a "gap in meaning" that motivates a sensemaking search through which a new idea emerges. Negative affect (dread) and emotional ambivalence (awakening) supply the motivation, while reaching out and reaching in supply the cognitive raw material for reconstruction—so that the remains of the old idea define the opening into which the new one grows.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper conceptualizes the previously unexamined *destructive* counterpart of idea generation—how letting go of ideas unfolds through creative revision—and introduces the creative jolt as a distinct, affectively charged, multi-phase episode that pushes ideas onto radically new trajectories. It shifts research attention from how ideas are generated and selected toward the processes by which "openings" for new ideas are constructed, arguing that ideas alone are not enough and that commitment/rigidity can paradoxically enable radical change once shaken.

**Practical Implication**
Autonomous creators and the mentors, accelerators, and coworking communities supporting them should recognize that highly critical, core-directed feedback can trigger an agonizing but generative period rather than mere failure. Supporting creators through the emotional upheaval—via opportunities to reach out to diverse networks and reach in to core values—can help them let go of unworkable ideas and reorient productively.

**Limitations**
The analysis deliberately focused on cases where creators let go of ideas, so it cannot establish whether letting go improved outcomes or whether persistence would have succeeded. The study is confined to autonomous creators (individual founders or primary cofounders), so collective- or team-level jolt dynamics could not be elaborated. Findings rely partly on retrospective interviewing, mitigated but not eliminated by the longitudinal, near-real-time design.

**Future Research**
Future work should map creators' emotional journeys more completely and examine how upheaval is prompted across different creators and contexts (e.g., organizational settings where support is withdrawn after a project is greenlit). Scholars could probe the nature of assumptive worlds and how creators emotionally prepare for their destruction, whether jolts can arise from positive triggers, and how jolts unfold at the team or collective level.
