# Rekindling the Fire and Stoking the Flames: How and When Workplace Interpersonal Capitalization Facilitates Pride and Knowledge Sharing at Work

**Abstract**
Sharing positive events with others is a phenomenon referred to as “capitalization.” Extant theory on capitalization considers the process of disclosing positive events as socioemotional in nature. However, individuals capitalize in the context of workplace relationships that serve instrumental functions, pointing to the possibility that the process of capitalization may at times be instrumental as well. In this paper, we integrate theory on capitalization with the social-functionalist model of emotions to develop a model that explains how workplace interpersonal capitalization serves the instrumental function of spurring knowledge sharing. We identify pride as a linking mechanism between workplace interpersonal capitalization and knowledge sharing, and we argue that experiencing pride is contingent on employees disclosing positive events that they attribute to their own efforts. We also identify perceived coworker responsiveness as a boundary condition qualifying the association between pride and knowledge sharing. A sourceand time-separated field study and an experience sampling field experiment with a daily intervention provide support for our hypothesized model. Overall, our work broadens the concept of capitalization and contributes to the social-functionalist model of emotions.

**Research Question**
How and when does workplace interpersonal capitalization (sharing personal work-related positive events with coworkers) serve the instrumental function of spurring disclosers' knowledge sharing? The paper asks whether capitalization is not only socioemotional but also instrumental, with pride as the linking mechanism, effort attribution as a first-stage boundary condition, and perceived coworker responsiveness as a second-stage boundary condition.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Workplace interpersonal capitalization (Study 1: 5-item self-report propensity; Study 2: daily randomized capitalization manipulation)
- DV(s): Knowledge sharing (the sharing of expertise or know-how)
- Mediators: Pride (authentic, achievement-oriented self-conscious emotion)
- Moderators: Effort attribution (first stage: capitalization → pride); perceived coworker responsiveness (second stage: pride → knowledge sharing)

Integrating capitalization theory with the social-functionalist model of emotions, the authors argue that disclosing a positive work event "rekindles the fire" of past achievement, drawing attention to the self and activating self-enhancement that elicits pride—but only when the event is attributed to one's own effort, a marker of work-goal progress. Pride in turn motivates disclosers to "stoke the flames" by sharing knowledge, a prototypical prestige-seeking behavior through which individuals convert valued achievements into higher social status. This prestige-seeking pathway holds only when disclosers perceive coworkers as responsive, because responsiveness signals that knowledge sharing will be rewarded with status. The full model is a two-stage moderated mediation in which the indirect effect is strongest when both effort attribution and responsiveness are high.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper broadens the concept of capitalization by theorizing its instrumental potential, challenging the long-standing assumption that capitalization is a purely socioemotional process and that the characteristics of the disclosed event are inconsequential. By positioning effort attribution as a boundary condition, it shows that only effort-attributed events activate the instrumental process. It also contributes to the social-functionalist model of emotions by qualifying the assumed evolutionary, universal pride–knowledge sharing link with the boundary condition of responsiveness, demonstrating that the link is not as unbounded as previously theorized.

**Practical Implication**
Because sharing good news at work can indirectly promote knowledge sharing, organizational leaders—especially in knowledge-intensive and innovation-oriented organizations—should encourage employees to share positive events (e.g., in meetings or via workspace design) and coach employees to respond responsively when they are targets of capitalization. Leaders should be cautious, however, since capitalization can backfire through unfavorable social comparison or undermining, and overly controlling reactions (e.g., financial rewards) can reduce intrinsic motivation.

**Limitations**
Study 1 is correlational and cannot fully establish causality; it is set in a single manufacturing organization in China, limiting generalizability, and cannot fully assess whether a given team member was both a capitalization and a knowledge-sharing target. The model does not explain why or when disclosers come to perceive responsiveness in coworkers, and the authors did not directly theorize the instrumental downsides of workplace interpersonal capitalization.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine other characteristics of the disclosed positive event beyond effort attribution, explore the "half-life" of capitalization-elicited pride over time, develop theory on the antecedents of perceived responsiveness, and test whether the instrumental capitalization process generalizes to non-work contexts such as romantic relationships, religious groups, schools, or families. Researchers are also encouraged to study the potential costs and net effects of sharing good news at work.
