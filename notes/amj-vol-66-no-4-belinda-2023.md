# A Spillover Model of Dreams and Work Behavior: How Dream Meaning Ascription Promotes Awe and Employee Resilience

**Abstract**
Sleep and employee behavior are linked. While the dominant explanations for this link involve physiology and cognitive resources, we offer a different perspective. We suggest that dreams, or psychological experiences during sleep, spill over to affect employee behavior. This spillover effect, we argue, results from the interplay between dreaming, meaning creation, and emotion. Taking a morning-of perspective, we theorize that recalling and ascribing meaning to dream experiences elicits awe—an epistemic emotion produced by appraisals of vastness and a need for cognitive accommodation. In turn, because awe reduces individuals’ focus on themselves and their concerns, we argue that experiencing awe upon awakening increases employee resilience, and ultimately goal progress, throughout the workday. However, because awe entails a felt need to expand one’s existing ways of knowing, employees may vary in their receptivity to awe. We therefore argue that the link between awe and resilience hinges on trait epistemic curiosity, or employees’ inherent desire to seek and acquire new knowledge. Across three studies—including a morning-of field study, a single-day morning–afternoon study, and a two-week experience sampling study—we find that ascribing positive meaning to dreams elicits awe, which in turn promotes resilience, and ultimately goal progress, throughout the workday.

**Research Question**
How and for whom do dreams spill over to shape daily work behavior? Specifically, the authors ask how ascribing meaning to recalled dreams elicits waking awe that promotes employee resilience and goal progress throughout the workday, and whether trait epistemic curiosity moderates the awe-resilience link.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Positive (and negative) dream meaning ascription upon awakening
- DV(s): Employee resilience (adaptive appraisal of work stressors) and, ultimately, daily work goal progress
- Mediators: Waking awe (sequential mediation: dream meaning ascription → awe → resilience → goal progress)
- Moderators: Trait epistemic curiosity (amplifies the awe → resilience relationship)

Integrating research on dream meaning ascription with appraisal theories of emotion, the authors argue that forming the belief that a dream carries meaning triggers core awe appraisals—perceptions of vastness and a need for cognitive accommodation—giving rise to awe. Through an appraisal-tendency mechanism, awe induces a "small-self" frame that makes subsequent work stressors feel less threatening, increasing resilience and downstream goal progress. Because awe requires expanding one's existing ways of knowing, its benefits accrue mainly to employees high in epistemic curiosity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper augments the organizational sleep literature by delineating a psychological (rather than purely biophysiological) pathway—dream meaning ascription—through which sleep spills over into waking behavior. It advances research on emotions by positioning awe as a linchpin between dreams and behavior, and on resilience by showing that resilience can result from emotions occurring prior to stressful encounters, challenging the directional assumption of transactional appraisal theories that appraisal precedes emotion.

**Practical Implication**
Because roughly 40% of working people recall dreams on a given morning and tend to make sense of them, managers and employees benefit from treating sleep as a meaning-laden process. Low-effort practices such as keeping a dream journal or imagery-rehearsal techniques can elicit meaningful dreams and awe; organizations can also cultivate awe directly (e.g., biophilic job design, leadership storytelling).

**Limitations**
Convenience sampling in the time-separated studies (Studies 2-3) may have increased attrition, and analyses rely primarily on self-report data. The time-separated designs lacked a third measurement period, and dream meaning ascription was measured with single items; focusing on dream-recall days created potential selection bias addressed through multiple robustness checks.

**Future Research**
Future work could combine biophysiological and dream-spillover perspectives—e.g., examining dreams as a moderator that offsets or exaggerates the effects of sleep deprivation, or parsing other aspects of sleep. Researchers might use probability sampling, objective and other-report outcome measures, and additional measurement periods, and examine how work outcomes vary solely as a function of dream recall.
