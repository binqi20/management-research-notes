# Communication Miscalibration: The Price Leaders Pay for Not Sharing Enough

**Abstract**
Leaders may be seen by their followers as miscalibrating the quantity of their communication—sharing too much or too little. We propose that leaders are more likely to be seen as under-communicating than over-communicating, even though under-communication is more heavily penalized. In Study 1a, we examine 2,717 qualitative comments from archived leadership assessments and find that leaders are nearly 10 times as likely to be criticized for under-communicating than for over-communicating. In Study 1b, we obtain further evidence of this bias using a representative sample of U.S. adults. In Study 2, we manipulate communication (mis)calibration, showing that leaders who under-communicate are viewed as less qualified for a leadership role because they are viewed as less empathic. In Study 3, we use separate measures of employee perceptions of their manager's communication as well as their preferences. When there is a lack of congruence between perceived and preferred communication, employees judge their leaders as lacking empathy and, in turn, leadership ability.

**Research Question**
How does the perceived quantity of a leader's downward communication—specifically the (mis)calibration between how much employees perceive and prefer their leader to communicate—shape followers' evaluations of that leader's ability, and why is under-communication penalized more severely than over-communication?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived communication (mis)calibration / quantity of downward communication (under-communicating vs. well-calibrated vs. over-communicating), operationalized via archival coding (1a), self-report scales (1b), experimental vignette manipulation (2), and perceived–preferred congruence via response-surface analysis (3).
- DV(s): Perceived leadership ability/effectiveness.
- Mediators: Perceived leader empathic concern.
- Moderators: None focal (gender and demographic characteristics tested as robustness controls).

Drawing on social judgment and helping-behavior research, the authors argue that employees read communication quantity as a prosocial signal of a leader's empathic concern. Because of the "curse of knowledge," leaders systematically underestimate how much their followers want and are therefore seen as under-communicating more often than over-communicating. Under-communication sends a stronger negative signal—that the leader neither helped nor even engaged the employee—whereas over-communication signals a misguided but well-intentioned effort. Consequently, perceived under-communication lowers perceived empathy, which in turn lowers perceived leadership ability, producing an asymmetric penalty.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shows that the trade-offs of communication miscalibration are asymmetric in leadership evaluations: under-communication "looms larger" than over-communication and is judged as a more significant failing, so not all forms of miscalibration are equal. It documents a robust perceptual bias whereby leaders are seen as under-communicating far more often than over-communicating, and it identifies perceived empathic concern as the psychological mechanism linking communication quantity to leadership evaluations—remedying prior downward-communication research that offered no account of how much communication is "the right amount."

**Practical Implication**
Leaders cannot trust their intuition when calibrating the quantity of downward communication; they should adopt independent mechanisms—soliciting employees' idiosyncratic preferences, establishing routine rather than ad hoc communication patterns, and "pretesting" messages for calibration. In most cases leaders should err toward communicating more, accepting the relatively minor risk of being seen as over-communicating to send a stronger signal of empathy and concern.

**Limitations**
The samples were primarily American, so the findings cannot account for cultural differences (e.g., high-context or high-power-distance cultures) that might shape judgments of communication calibration. The theorizing and measures focus exclusively on leadership evaluations as the outcome, leaving aside other objectives such as supporting employee autonomy, and the studies isolate communication quantity while not modeling the qualitative dimension (content, style, delivery) of communication.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how the quantitative and qualitative dimensions of leader communication interrelate and which matters more in particular cases, including whether qualitative miscalibration signals incompetence rather than low empathy. Researchers should also test whether under-communication can function as deliberate "tough love" that serves employees' developmental or autonomy needs, and how communication expectations vary with hierarchical distance between leaders and direct versus indirect reports.
