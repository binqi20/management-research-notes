# The Technological Entrainment of Moral Issues: The Case of Genomic Data Markets

**Abstract**
This research considers the role of technology in shaping the moral foundations of emerging digital markets. Through an inductive process study, we examine the development of the market for genomic (DNA) data—an emergent digital market plagued by moral conflicts over privacy and ownership. Drawing on interview, archival, and observational data, we analyze how rules for exchanging genomic data emerged, and how entrepreneurial technology-development moves influenced those rules. Our findings reveal a four-step process, which we call the technological entrainment of moral issues, involving the progressive synchronization of framings, technical policies, technologies (software), exchange platforms, and market participant behaviors. This process baked particular moral stances on privacy and ownership into technological designs, which then dictated the rules market participants were required to follow to engage in exchange. We conclude that when markets are digital, technology processes central to exchange end up controlling many moral aspects of exchange. Our study contributes to theories of morally contentious markets and cultural or institutional entrepreneurship by illuminating how those theories can incorporate material (not only symbolic) aspects of technology processes and better explain the emerging moral order within the rapidly expanding realm of digital markets.

**Research Question**
What role does technology play in shaping the rules of exchange in digital markets fraught with moral conflicts? The authors investigate this in the emerging U.S. market for human genomic data, where exchange generates moral conflicts over privacy and ownership.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: technological entrainment of moral issues; moral conflicts over privacy and ownership of personal (genomic) data; exchange rules; technological framing (designers encoding values into technologies); competing actor clusters (Right-to-Science, Trusted Brokers, Walled Gardens).
- Process / phases: a four-step entrainment process involving the progressive synchronization of (1) framings, (2) technical policies, (3) technologies/software, (4) exchange platforms, and ultimately market participant behaviors.
- Triggering conditions: entrepreneurial technology-development "moves" by actors seeking to advance their preferred exchange rules in an emergent digital market.
- Emergent outcomes: particular moral stances become "baked into" technological designs, which then dictate the rules participants must follow; a technologically mediated coercive isomorphism that locks in some moral stances and forecloses others.

Through inductive grounded theorizing, the authors show that as actors develop technical policies, software, and exchange platforms, their preferred moral stances on privacy and ownership become progressively synchronized and embedded in the material technology itself. Once these interlocking technologies are widely distributed, they impose de facto exchange rules behind the scenes, rendering discursive or rhetorical contestation ineffectual and making the entrained moral order path-dependent and hard to reverse.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces "technological entrainment of moral issues" to explain how the material (not merely symbolic) aspects of technology processes constitute the moral order of digital markets. It extends theories of morally contentious markets and cultural/institutional entrepreneurship by showing technology as a constitutive force—rather than only a cultural resource imbued with meaning—through which exchange rules and moral legitimacy are determined, and it surfaces exchange-platform organizations and technology developers as central (if less visible) moral entrepreneurs.

**Practical Implication**
Because exchange rules are set implicitly through upstream technology development, policy attention and early regulatory action should focus on the exchange-technology-development process before institutionalization, opening it to broader, inclusive, multiparty societal dialogue rather than leaving it to actors with vested interests and technical capabilities. Civil society and collective-action efforts (e.g., data trusts) could champion alternative exchange technologies that protect collective privacy and ownership interests.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on a single emerging market (U.S. genomic data) and could observe only the Right-to-Science and Trusted Brokers clusters in detail; the secretive "Walled Gardens" cluster (e.g., direct-to-consumer testing firms like 23andMe) was excluded from the analysis because of limited access to their exchange technology and its development.

**Future Research**
Future work could compare technology-centered entrainment with sociocultural models of moral and institutional entrepreneurship, examine how the two relate, combine, or compete to produce market- or field-level settlements, and explore whether entrainment constitutes a fourth form of moral-controversy settlement. Researchers could also investigate the role of platform organizations, the legitimacy and exclusionary implications of converting moral questions into technical ones, and the dynamics of Walled Gardens in market evolution.
