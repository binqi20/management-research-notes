# Signaling Legitimacy: Why Mixed-Gender Coalitions Outperform Single-Gender Coalitions in Advocating for Gender Equity

**Abstract**
Employees often engage in collective grassroots efforts to bring about gender equity in the workplace. Such coalition-based advocacy is largely driven by women, which has led to debate about whether men's involvement as allies can help. Integrating literatures on signaling and legitimacy, we propose that the demographic composition of a gender equity advocacy coalition matters: men-only groups lack "coalition legitimacy," or the perception that they are the "right" spokespersons for gender equity issues, whereas women-only groups struggle to convey "issue legitimacy," or the perception that gender equity is of strategic importance within business organizations. By contrast, mixed-gender coalitions signal both forms of legitimacy, and are thus uniquely effective. We demonstrate these effects over three studies: managers reporting on advocacy coalitions comprising their colleagues (Study 1), an audio-based study showing that the proposed effects are unique for gender equity issues (Study 2), and a stimulus sampling study involving multiple policy proposals (Study 3). Our findings highlight the different forms of legitimacy that women and men bring to the table when selling gender equity issues. We discuss implications for who should be recruited to gender equity advocacy coalitions, and why.

**Research Question**
Does the gender composition of an employee advocacy coalition affect managers' support for gender equity issues, and why are mixed-gender coalitions more persuasive than women-only or men-only coalitions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Coalition gender composition (women-only vs. men-only vs. mixed-gender); type of issue (control vs. gender equity, manipulated in Study 2)
- DV(s): Issue support (managerial support for the advocated gender equity issue)
- Mediators: Coalition legitimacy (the coalition is the "right" messenger) and issue legitimacy (the issue is strategically important)
- Moderators: Type of issue (gender equity vs. nongender equity); exploratory: managerial sexism

The paper integrates signaling theory and legitimacy theory to argue that a coalition's gender composition signals two distinct forms of legitimacy. The presence of women signals coalition legitimacy (the messengers have material stake and are the appropriate spokespersons), while the presence of men signals issue legitimacy (the issue is strategically important rather than a niche "women's" concern). Because women-only coalitions convey the former but not the latter and men-only coalitions the reverse, only mixed-gender coalitions signal both, making them uniquely effective at winning managerial endorsement for gender equity issues.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends signaling and legitimacy theories by distinguishing messenger legitimacy (coalition legitimacy) from message legitimacy (issue legitimacy) and showing that the characteristics conferring one need not confer the other, so advocacy coalitions must be composed to convey both simultaneously. It contributes to research on employee coalitions by specifying who should be part of them, and to work on men's allyship by moving beyond individual confrontation of sexism to the collective, coalition-based context.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations and employees seeking to advance gender equity should deliberately recruit mixed-gender coalitions rather than relying on gender-homophilous (women-only) groups, because including men alongside women helps the coalition signal both that it is a credible messenger and that the issue is strategically important to the business.

**Limitations**
The studies were experimental and thus limited by their simulated nature despite using immersive paradigms. The threshold of male representation required for a coalition to appear sufficiently "mixed" remains an open empirical question, and the main effect of coalition composition on managerial support was not significant across studies because the two mediators operated in opposing (suppressing) directions.

**Future Research**
Future studies could use qualitative comparative case study approaches to examine how coalition gender composition shapes legitimacy and support over time, investigate the threshold at which a social group is viewed as properly represented rather than tokenistic, and explore additional boundary conditions of the theory.
