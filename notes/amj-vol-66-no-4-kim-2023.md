# Corporate Proximity and Product Market Reentry: The Role of Corporate Headquarters in Business Unit Response to Product Failure

**Abstract**
Understanding how organizations respond to failure is important to management research, yet prior studies have offered contrasting findings for whether, in a multiunit hierarchical organization, a corporate office improves business unit search following product failure. To better understand how a corporate office affects business unit search, we focus on the role of corporate proximity (hierarchical, geographic, and cognitive) between the corporate office and constituent units. We argue that corporate proximity improves a business unit’s local search process through two mechanisms—vertical linkages and corporate attention—that positively condition the likelihood of persisting; that is, reentering a product market after having experienced a prior product failure in that market. We find support for our theory using data on reentry in the U.S. medical device industry following exit from the market due to product failure. We also explore how age of the product market and characteristics of the failure—cause and severity— further moderate corporate proximity’s role in business unit reentry. Overall, our study offers a better understanding of how complex organizations respond to failure, thereby contributing to literatures on search, corporate headquarters, and product entry.

**Research Question**
Whether and how a corporate office shapes business unit search following product failure in multiunit hierarchical organizations—specifically, how the hierarchical, geographic, and cognitive proximity of business units to the corporate headquarters conditions a unit's likelihood of reentering a product market after a prior product failure.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Corporate proximity (composite of hierarchical, geographic, and cognitive proximity between corporate office and business unit)
- DV(s): Product market reentry following a product failure-related exit (measure of persistence in local search)
- Mediators: Vertical linkages; corporate attention
- Moderators: Product market age; cause of failure (technical error-driven); severity of failure (FDA severity categorization)

The paper draws on the behavioral theory of the firm, theories of search, strategic human capital, and the attention-based view to argue that variation in failure response stems partly from how proximate business units are to corporate headquarters. Corporate proximity improves a unit's local search process through two channels—vertical linkages that transfer corporate resources and assistance, and corporate attention directed at the unit's problem—thereby increasing the likelihood the unit persists and reenters the failed product market. These effects are amplified when the product market is established and when failures are technical and severe, conditions that increase the corporate office's ability and motivation to support problem-solving.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to the behavioral theory of the firm and organizational learning by drawing renewed attention to corporate proximity as a determinant of business unit response to failure, theorizing and empirically demonstrating two operative mechanisms (vertical linkages and corporate attention). It advances the corporate headquarters literature by treating proximity as a multidimensional construct (beyond geographic distance) and by showing corporate influence as a contingent phenomenon shaped by failure characteristics. It also contributes to the literature on determinants of persistence in product entry by positioning corporate proximity as a driver of persistence.

**Practical Implication**
Understanding how structural variation shapes reentry is of value to managers making decisions about proximity via structural design, location, and managerial hiring across units. Although executives rarely relocate corporate headquarters or units, they may channel and redirect attention through changes to structure or cognitive ties, and may configure proximity levers to support performance-enhancing reentry following failure.

**Limitations**
Because the authors only have access to annual data on entry and exit, they cannot capture more granular reentry events (e.g., reentry in the same year as exit) that may also reflect learning. While product failures may cause exits, they do not always do so, so the authors cannot claim exits were caused by a preceding failure in every case (though such "false positives" make the test more conservative). The empirical setting is a single industry over a historical window.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine conditions under which the effects of hierarchical, geographic, and cognitive proximity might diverge (e.g., successful exploration may require close hierarchical but distant cognitive proximity). More work is needed on the performance consequences of proximity's effects on failure response, how and when firms should adjust proximity choices, and how different configurations of proximity levers can create performance-enhancing levels of reentry.
