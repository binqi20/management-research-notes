# The Benefits and Burdens of Work Moralization on Creativity

**Abstract**
Although morality has long been considered an important and integral element of organizational life for employees, research has suggested that moral considerations reduce creativity. However, evidence has hinted that morality may not always be a burden and instead could spark creativity. I develop a model that investigates how work moralization—or the degree to which individuals integrate moral considerations into their work tasks—influences employee creativity. Drawing on regulatory focus theory, I argue that work moralization can lead to prevention-focused cognitions (i.e., moral rumination), which impair and redirect the cognitive resources needed for creativity. However, I also posit that work moralization can lead to promotion-focused cognitions (i.e., cognitive flexibility), leading employees to adopt cognitive approaches that increase creativity. Moreover, integrating research on value congruence, I suggest that work moralizers are the least likely to ruminate—and the most likely to become cognitively flexible—when they perceive that their values are aligned with those of their organization, which ultimately fosters higher levels of creativity. I test my theoretical model with two field studies and a preregistered experiment. This research challenges the consensus that moral considerations generally have a negative impact on creativity, and contributes to research on organizational ethics and creativity.

**Research Question**
How does work moralization—the degree to which individuals integrate moral considerations into their work tasks—influence employee creativity, and under what conditions (value congruence) is its effect a benefit versus a burden?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Work moralization
- DV(s): Employee creativity
- Mediators: Moral rumination (prevention-focused cognition); cognitive flexibility (promotion-focused cognition)
- Moderators: Value congruence (alignment between employee and organizational values)

Grounded in regulatory focus theory, work moralization is theorized as a double-edged sword: it can trigger prevention-focused moral rumination that drains the cognitive resources creativity requires, while simultaneously prompting promotion-focused cognitive flexibility that enables creative associations. Value congruence is the critical moderator that tips the balance—when employees perceive their values align with the organization's, they are least likely to ruminate and most likely to become cognitively flexible, so work moralization yields higher creativity; when values are misaligned, the burden side dominates.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the largely held assumption that moralizing work generally harms creativity, showing instead that work moralization can—and often does—positively influence creativity through offsetting cognitive pathways. It contributes to regulatory focus theory by demonstrating that morality is itself a key predictor of promotion- and prevention-focused cognitions, and it introduces a conceptualization of work moralization that advances research on organizational ethics.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should not assume that encouraging employees to view their work in moral terms will dampen creativity. Because value congruence determines whether moralization sparks cognitive flexibility or rumination, cultivating alignment between employee and organizational values can help employees who moralize their work translate that orientation into creative, rather than draining, cognitive outcomes.

**Limitations**
The author notes that the field studies could not establish causal inference, motivating the preregistered experiment as stronger causal evidence. Further evidence is needed to investigate the effects of work moralization on creativity and the role of the proposed moderator across additional contexts.

**Future Research**
Future work should further investigate when and why work moralization benefits versus burdens creativity, examine additional moderators and boundary conditions beyond value congruence, and extend the model across varied organizational contexts and types of work.
