# The Glass Wall and the Gendered Evaluation of Role Expansion in Freelancing Careers

**Abstract**
People pursuing careers outside organizations often face a career progression paradox: they must develop expertise to find new work opportunities, but such expertise is largely acquired through accumulating work experience to which they seldom have access. Research has proposed that progressive role expansion—starting one's career specialized in a single work role and later expanding into new work roles—would be helpful to navigate this challenge. However, we propose that when women engage in lateral role expansion within professional careers, they face a "glass wall"—an invisible barrier that limits their work opportunities due to a gendered evaluation of role expansion. Specifically, role expansion by female (vs. male) freelancers is seen as less agentic, and such difference undermines female freelancers' perceived competence and commitment. Three studies using different methods (archival data and experiment), industries (music and film), and samples (Korean and American) show that female (vs. male) freelancers who expand into new work roles are seen as less agentic and have less chance of finding new work opportunities. Although women who pursue careers outside of organizations may become unshackled from gender biases embedded within traditional organizational hierarchies, we suggest that they still face obstacles in the new forms of work.

**Research Question**
How is gender bias manifested in freelancing careers that advance outside formal organizations, and specifically, does progressive role expansion (starting as a specialist and later expanding into new work roles) yield the same career benefits for women as for men, or do women face a "glass wall"?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Role expansion (specialist who later expanded into a new work role vs. remained a specialist); freelancer gender (female vs. male)
- DV(s): Hazard of having a new work opportunity / new release (Study 1A); perceived agency, perceived competence, and perceived commitment (Studies 1B, 2)
- Mediators: Perceived agency (mediates the effect of role expansion on perceived competence and commitment)
- Moderators: Freelancer gender (moderates the role-expansion → perceived-agency relationship)

The paper argues that progressive role expansion can signal agency—taking control of one's career—but that gender stereotypes act as an evaluative anchor on how that expansion is read. Because women are stereotyped as communal, emotional, and lacking self-control, their role expansion is attributed to situational pressures or impulsiveness rather than purposeful career-crafting, so it is perceived as less agentic than men's. Reduced perceived agency in turn lowers perceived competence and commitment—the cues clients use to award work—producing an invisible "glass wall" that blocks women from converting role expansion into new opportunities. The effect is conditioned by situational ambiguity, which is endemic to freelancing.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces the "glass wall"—an invisible barrier disadvantaging women who pursue lateral role expansion within professional careers—as a complement to the glass ceiling, glass cliff, and glass escalator that operate inside organizational hierarchies, extending gender-bias theory to nonstandard, organization-free work. It identifies the gendered evaluation of role expansion via perceived agency as the underlying mechanism, and contributes to the generalist-discount/advantage debate by showing that gender stereotypes anchor whether generalism is read as competent agency or as erratic situational drift.

**Practical Implication**
Freelancers (especially women) may mitigate the bias by presenting their practice under an incorporated business name to shift client attention from demographic cues to work portfolios. Clients are cautioned that stereotype-tainted evaluations lead to suboptimal hiring and that female generalists may be an undervalued labor pool; policymakers could accredit licensing schemes that let women credibly signal agentic investment in acquiring new work roles.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting is confined to high-skill creative freelancing (music and film), so generalizability to low-skill platform work (e.g., Uber, Mechanical Turk) and to part-time or transitional freelancing remains untested. Both focal industries are male-dominated, which heightens the salience of gender and may inflate the observed bias, leaving open whether the glass wall appears in female-dominated industries.

**Future Research**
Future work should test whether the glass wall extends beyond high-skill creative freelancing to low-skill gig work and to traditional careers being reformed toward greater worker autonomy, and whether it appears in female-dominated industries. Scholars are also encouraged to examine additional parallel paths to the glass wall—including demand-side algorithmic matching and supply-side self-selection into specialization—and how they may reinforce one another.
