# How Organizations Influence Interpersonal Trust Repair: The Case of a French Antiterrorist Unit

**Abstract**
Organizations that increasingly rely on interdependent teams, such as project teams, often imply work that is important, improvisational, and interdependent. However, these are the very same task conditions where trust is most fragile and in need of repair. As a result, organizations may wish to intervene in repairing interpersonal trust among members. Through an inductive qualitative study of a French antiterrorist unit, we move beyond dyadic treatments of interpersonal trust repair to theorize a model that elucidates the organizational practices that can be used to guide members through the trust repair process. We also induce core mechanisms, such as establishing trust violation rules and providing trust templates, that explain why these organizational practices work to repair interpersonal trust. We suggest how this organizationally directed trust repair process can lead to full, partial, or failed trust repair for trust violators, with consequences on whether and how these violators may be reintegrated into the organization. We conclude by discussing the contributions of our research on trust repair, as well as on how organizations treat organizational wrongdoers, and suggest several avenues for future research.

**Research Question**
What role, if any, does an organization play in facilitating interpersonal trust repair among its members — that is, how might an organization broker the relationship between a wrongdoer and the person(s) whose trust has been violated? The authors examine this in an elite antiterrorism organization, the GIGN.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: trust as "capital" eroded by mistakes; the dilemma that high-stakes interdependent work both requires trust and makes it fragile; trust violations versus trust repair.
- Organizational practices: communicating trust standards through selection and socialization; conducting debriefing sessions about violations; using practice, drills, and simulations; team-leader assessments of trust repair; and role assignments on missions.
- Core mechanisms (the "why"): (a) establishing trust violation rules based on trust standards, (b) detecting violations and assigning blame, (c) providing a trust repair template and opportunities, and (d) rendering and communicating an organizational trust judgment (first by the mission team leader, then by all team leaders).
- Emergent outcomes: full, partial, or failed interpersonal trust repair, with downstream consequences for whether and how violators are reintegrated into mission teams.
- Boundary conditions: the "mesh size" (tightness) of trust violation rules and the rigidity of the repair template vary with the organization's task requirements (e.g., how consequential, complex, and interdependent the work is).

The paper theorizes a "Big O" (organizational) process model in which the organization, not just the trustor–trustee dyad, structures interpersonal trust repair. Because all GIGN mistakes are treated as trust violations and repaired through a single standardized process that mirrors selection and socialization (fix character before competence), the organization can repair and reintegrate scarce expert operatives relatively quickly and unambiguously, signaling restored trust to future teammates via mission assignments.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study develops a process model of how organizations influence interpersonal trust repair, contributing to the "third wave" of trust research that looks beyond the trustor–trustee dyad to third parties such as the organization. It shows that what is usually treated as a dyadic, idiosyncratic phenomenon can in fact be collective and standardized, and it bridges the trust-repair literature with research on how organizations deal with wrongdoers (discipline, reinstatement, reintegration).

**Practical Implication**
Coworkers are unlikely to fully trust reinstated violators on the strength of an organizational "stamp of approval" alone; managers should attend to reintegration, not just punishment. The authors suggest that shared agreement on what counts as a violation and how it is repaired, transparency in detection and the remediation process, and trust in the organization and its practices are conditions that make organizationally directed trust repair effective.

**Limitations**
As a theory-elaborating qualitative study of a single antiterrorist unit, transferability and generalizability are constrained; findings apply most directly to similar elite, paramilitary, or project-team organizations that must retain experts after trust violations. The sensitive nature of the missions prevented the researchers from attending violation-detection and blame-assignment meetings or tracking specific incidents in real time, and the studied organization already had functional trust standards, rules, and templates in place.

**Future Research**
Future work could track individuals longitudinally to identify additional ways organizations become involved at different points in the repair process, follow a comparable organization to see how trust standards, rules, and templates form, and examine cases where organizational attempts to repair interpersonal trust fail. Researchers might also probe boundary conditions such as the "mesh size" of violation rules and the rigidity of repair templates across contexts.
