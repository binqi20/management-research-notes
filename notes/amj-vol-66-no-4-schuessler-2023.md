# “We Can’t Compete on Human Rights”: Creating Market-Protected Spaces to Institutionalize the Emerging Logic of Responsible Management

**Abstract**
To what extent are multinational corporations able to address societal challenges through voluntary corporate social responsibility (CSR)? Based on an in-depth qualitative study of how apparel multinational corporations have addressed labor standards violations since the deadly 2013 Rana Plaza factory collapse, we show how CSR managers navigate the tension between the emerging responsible management logic and the highly institutionalized market logic, revealing how some go beyond accommodating responsible management within the market by prioritizing responsible management in market-protected spaces. We theorize the construction of market-protected spaces as a multilevel mechanism for institutionalizing an emerging logic in the context of a field dominated by the market logic via three forms of institutional work: restraining the jurisdiction of the market logic, infusing the responsible management logic with nonmarket elements, and maintaining market-protected spaces against resistance. A “marketprotected space” is an institutionally bound space that suspends the dominance of the market logic on selected issues based on a binding institutional infrastructure that allows prioritizing responsible management—unlike voluntary CSR, which still prioritizes the market logic. The concept of a market-protected space maps a path for policy-makers, managers, and other actors interested in institutionalizing responsible management in the global economy.

**Research Question**
To what extent can multinational corporations address societal challenges through voluntary CSR, and how can firm- and field-level actors overcome the systemic constraints of the dominant market logic to institutionalize the emerging logic of responsible management?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: tension between the emerging responsible management logic and the highly institutionalized market logic; the "market-protected space" as an institutionally bound space that suspends the dominance of the market logic on selected issues.
- Approaches identified: (a) prioritizing the market logic, (b) accommodating responsible management within the market logic, and (c) prioritizing responsible management in market-protected spaces.
- Institutional work (process): restraining the jurisdiction of the market logic; infusing the responsible management logic with nonmarket elements; maintaining market-protected spaces against resistance.
- Boundary conditions: construction relies on pre-institutionalized market-constraining actors (e.g., trade unions) to act as legal representatives of rights holders; spaces are fragile, contested, and require continuous maintenance.

Through three interlocking forms of institutional work, firm- and field-level actors jointly construct a binding institutional infrastructure that brackets the market logic on selected issues (e.g., fire/building safety, living wages) so that responsible management can be prioritized rather than merely accommodated. The authors theorize this as a multilevel mechanism for institutionalizing an emerging logic within a field dominated by a highly institutionalized rival logic, framed as cyclical embedding and disembedding of the market (Polanyi) rather than linear progress.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to institutional theory by specifying an alternative way to address tensions between conflicting institutional logics when one logic (the market) is highly institutionalized and dominant. It advances the concept of a "market-protected space" held in place by a binding institutional infrastructure as a distinct alternative to voluntary CSR, and theorizes the multilevel institutional work required to construct and maintain such spaces, informing debates on the limits of CSR.

**Practical Implication**
Binding sectoral agreements—rather than the proliferation of small-scale voluntary projects—can restrain the market in ways that materially benefit peripheral stakeholders such as workers. Firms must align internal incentives (e.g., purchasing managers' KPIs) with responsible management demands, and policy-makers should strengthen market-restraining actors and provide binding regulation, since catastrophic events alone do not shift dominant logics.

**Limitations**
The study examines only one issue (labor standards) in one industry (apparel), so the observed dynamics may be context-specific and not immediately transferable to other grand challenges. The market-protected spaces studied were temporary and fragile, leaving open how the institutional work can scale and stabilize beyond these spaces.

**Future Research**
The authors call for comparative studies of market-protected spaces across grand challenges (e.g., environmental issues), examining which stakeholders can become key allies in restraining the market. Further work could analyze the multilevel political struggles around creating and maintaining such spaces from an open-polity perspective, how practices diffuse from these spaces into organizations, and why firms adopt different approaches cross-nationally.
