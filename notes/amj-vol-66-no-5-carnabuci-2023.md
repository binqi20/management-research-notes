# When People Build Networks That Hurt Their Performance: Structural Holes, Cognitive Style, and the Unintended Consequences of Person–Network Fit

**Abstract**
Extant research shows that individual cognitive style affects whether employees benefit most from a brokering or a dense network. But do people build the network structure in which they perform best? We address this question by advancing a novel two-stage explanatory model that explicitly disentangles the network formation process from its performance effects. We hypothesize that "adaptors" (i.e., individuals inclined to focus on implementable solutions through commonly accepted and well-defined approaches) perform best when their network spans structural holes. Yet, these same individuals systematically forego opportunities to build relations across structural holes. By contrast, "innovators" (i.e., individuals inclined to focus on envisioning creative solutions that break away with established approaches) draw no or even negative performance returns from structural holes. Nevertheless, their inclination is to build ever-new bridging relations. We test and find support for this counterintuitive hypothesis through a randomized longitudinal field experiment enabling us to disentangle empirically both stages of our theorized process model. Our findings help illuminate why people may build networks that hurt their performance, shed a new light on the role of individual cognitive style in shaping network advantage, and bear concrete implications for organizations aiming to leverage networks to enhance employees' performance.

**Research Question**
Do people build the network structure in which they perform best? Specifically, are adaptors more likely than are innovators to build bridging relationships across structural holes, even though brokerage benefits adaptors' performance more than innovators'?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Individual cognitive style on the adaptor–innovator continuum (Kirton adaption–innovation inventory); a randomized executive-education treatment providing exogenous bridging opportunities
- DV(s): Stage 1 — propensity to build bridging ties across structural holes (network constraint); Stage 2 — supervisor-rated innovative performance
- Mediators: Network constraint / brokerage position (treatment-induced bridging ties, instrumented, carry the performance effect in Stage 2)
- Moderators: Cognitive style moderates the brokerage–performance relationship (adaptors benefit from brokerage; innovators do not)

The paper advances a two-stage person–network fit model. People perform best in networks that "complement" (combine productively with) their cognitive style, but they tend to build networks that "supplement" (align with and reinforce) their cognitive style. Because adaptors prefer well-defined, consensus-based, stable interactions, they avoid forming the bridging ties across structural holes that would most benefit their idea-implementation strengths; innovators, who gain little from brokerage, nonetheless keep building ever-new bridging relations. When complementary and supplementary fit are misaligned, people build networks that detract from rather than enhance their performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the cognitive-microfoundations stream of network theory by showing that cognitive style shapes the brokerage–performance relationship in a more complex, counterintuitive way than prior contingency accounts: it governs not only who benefits from structural holes but also who builds them, and the two are misaligned. By integrating network formation and network performance within a single explanatory framework, it explains why large numbers of employees build networks that are not good for their performance, contributing to both structural-holes and person–environment fit research.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations investing in network-building interventions should tailor them to employees' cognitive styles rather than encouraging brokerage uniformly. Adaptors should be helped to bridge structural holes (which they avoid but benefit from), whereas innovators are better served by deepening cohesive networks optimized for idea implementation; even a single day of executive education can shift employees' network positions.

**Limitations**
Evidence is from a single business unit of one semiconductor company in Italy, which may limit generalizability across settings and industries. Individual-level fixed effects could not be modeled because treatment varied between rather than within employees, so the design relies on random assignment and instrumental-variable estimation; noncompliance and always-takers required an intention-to-treat correction.

**Future Research**
Future work could test the two-stage complementary-versus-supplementary fit model in other organizational and cultural settings and with other dimensions of person–network fit, and examine additional cognitive or dispositional traits that drive how people build networks misaligned with their own performance.
