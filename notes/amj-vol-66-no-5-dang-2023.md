# On the Plurality and Politics of Ally Work: Liberalism and Self, Relational, and Organizational Ally Work

**Abstract**
We theorize that ally work (actions by employees belonging to advantaged social groups to support employees belonging to disadvantaged social groups) can be organized into three modalities: self ally work that is oriented toward developing skills and capabilities, relational ally work that is focused on building positive work relationships, and organizational ally work aimed at changing organizational practices to support disadvantaged coworkers. Drawing from the theory of political ideology as motivated cognition, across multiple pre-registered field studies we found that employees who were more liberal-leaning (vs. conservative-leaning) reported higher engagement in all three modalities. We also examined whether contextual affordances amplified the liberalism effect. Specifically, we tested whether personal accountability for diversity, colleague diversity, and organizational ideology directed liberals toward greater engagement in self, relational, and organizational work, respectively. As hypothesized, more liberal-leaning employees reported higher engagement in organizational ally work in more ideologically liberal organizations. However, the hypothesized effects of personal accountability were not supported. Notably, personal accountability had a positive effect on self ally work among more conservative-leaning employees. The moderating effects of colleague diversity were also mixed across studies. Highlighting both the plurality and politics of ally work, we discuss theoretical and practical implications of our findings.

**Research Question**
What motivates employees in positions of relative social advantage to engage in ally work, and how does political ideology—filtered through contextual affordances—shape the distinct self, relational, and organizational modalities through which ally work is enacted within organizations?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Political ideology (liberalism vs. conservatism, measured along a liberal–conservative continuum)
- DV(s): Engagement in three modalities of ally work — self ally work, relational ally work, and organizational ally work
- Mediators: None formally tested (political ideology is theorized as the motivational mechanism itself)
- Moderators: Perceived contextual affordances — personal accountability for diversity (self path), colleague diversity (relational path), and perceived organizational political ideology (organizational path)

The paper theorizes that political ideology operates as motivated cognition: a liberal (vs. conservative) ideology functions as a "higher-order want" and "perceptual filter" that leads advantaged employees to "see" their organizations as arenas of marginalization where progress requires challenging the status quo, motivating engagement across all three ally-work modalities. Perceived affordances act as a "push" that narrows liberals' choice set toward modality-specific action. Empirically, liberalism showed a consistent positive main effect on all three modalities, and perceived organizational liberalism amplified liberals' organizational ally work; however, the personal-accountability and colleague-diversity moderation hypotheses were not supported, and personal accountability instead promoted self work among more conservative-leaning employees.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a pluralistic tripartite framework that disaggregates ally work into self, relational, and organizational modalities operating at different levels of the social self, and develops a validated measure of these constructs. It identifies political ideology-as-motivated-cognition as a general motivational mechanism for ally work that is portable across multiple intersecting social categories, moving beyond prior identity-specific or social-identity-based accounts. By introducing perceived affordances, it shows how organizational context conditions—and sometimes reverses—the behavioral expression of ideology.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can encourage allyship by creating affordances tailored to specific modalities (e.g., signaling organizational openness to structural change to elicit organizational ally work). Because self work is relatively accessible and self-focused, affording personal accountability can engage even conservative-leaning or politically ambivalent employees in self-directed change. Where employees resist organizational ally work, leaders are advised to move cautiously and use subtler messaging.

**Limitations**
Ally work was assessed only from the focal employee's self-reports, raising questions about whether liberals are "aspirational allies" whose self-reported ally work aligns with observers' reports. The colleague-diversity hypothesis yielded discrepant patterns, suggesting unmeasured moderators (e.g., task interdependence) the data could not test. Several samples were drawn from online panels and student participants, and the design is largely cross-sectional within the U.S. liberal–conservative context.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how organizational factors such as leadership and culture might offset exogenous political influences on discretionary diversity behaviors, and study ally work within specific identity relations and from beneficiaries' perspectives. Researchers could investigate when it is useful to combine versus distinguish the three modalities, the strong self–organizational correlation, and the political ramifications and potential polarization effects that ally work may produce.
