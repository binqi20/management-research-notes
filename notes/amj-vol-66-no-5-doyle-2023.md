# Hierarchical Team Structures Limit Joint Gain in Interteam Negotiations: The Role of Information Elaboration and Value-Claiming Behavior

**Abstract**
Although teams of negotiators are widely assumed to be better at unlocking integrative solutions compared to individual negotiators, the interteam negotiation context is characterized by unique challenges that can make effective collaboration between teams difficult. We extend our theoretical understanding of interteam negotiations by offering novel insights about when and why teams realize their potential in integrative negotiations. Specifically, we propose a theoretical model that explains how hierarchical team structures reduce information elaboration within teams, which reinforces "fixed-pie" assumptions that prompt the reliance on value-claiming behaviors between teams and lower high-quality outcomes such as the joint gain achieved. Across four studies, each involving interactive team-on-team negotiations, we provide support for the hypothesized effects of formal intrateam hierarchies on joint gain, and test a useful intervention to mitigate the harmful effects of hierarchically structured teams at the negotiation table. Contributions to the literatures on team negotiations, interteam collaboration, and hierarchical differences within teams are discussed.

**Research Question**
When and why do formal hierarchical structures within negotiation teams shape a team's ability to maximize joint gain in negotiations between teams that have integrative potential?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Formal intrateam hierarchy (hierarchical vs. egalitarian team structure; plus a "blended" condition where one team is hierarchical and one egalitarian, and a solo/control condition)
- DV(s): Joint gain achieved between the negotiating teams
- Mediators: Intrateam information elaboration (Path A) and interteam value-claiming behavior (Path B), tested as a serial mediation
- Moderators: Information elaboration instructions (manipulated in Study 4 as a moderation-of-process test of the proposed causal mechanism)

Formal intrateam hierarchy restricts the free flow and exchange of information within teams, reducing intrateam information elaboration. Lower elaboration reinforces the "fixed-pie" assumption that one party's gain is the other's loss, which fosters competitive value-claiming behavior between teams and ultimately lowers joint gain. Study 4's manipulation of information elaboration shows that providing elaboration instructions attenuates the harmful effect of hierarchy, supporting the proposed causal role of the elaboration-then-value-claiming pathway.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends team-negotiation research—which had focused predominantly on the benefits of teams over solo negotiators and treated teams as monolithic—by theorizing how within-team hierarchy undermines intrateam information elaboration and interteam joint gain. It bridges the literatures on intrateam hierarchy and interteam collaboration, showing that formal hierarchies can harm value creation across teams (not just within them), and contributes to work on hierarchical dynamics in teams and on multiteam systems by identifying when and why teams leave valuable resources unused.

**Practical Implication**
Contrary to the common advice from negotiation-training organizations that teams should appoint a single leader with decision-making authority, simply assigning members to leader and follower roles (or allowing self-selection) can inhibit information elaboration, promote competitive behavior between teams, and leave resources unused. Teams with leaders should deliberately take time to engage in information elaboration; egalitarian teams do not perform worse than competitive hierarchical counterparts, offering reassurance for sending more egalitarian teams to the table.

**Limitations**
The studies used MBA and undergraduate student samples in simulated business negotiations, raising generalizability concerns relative to more-complex, hostility-laden real-world negotiations. Effects of hierarchical differentiation may be contingent on the leader's negotiation expertise. The team sizes studied were small, and findings may not extend to larger teams, to status (rather than formal) hierarchies, or to negotiations lacking integrative potential.

**Future Research**
Future work could leverage real-world samples or more difficult-to-resolve negotiations with pre-existing hostilities, examine how leader expertise and leadership styles condition the effects, and study informal status hierarchies and alternative hierarchical shapes in larger teams. Researchers could also measure the fixed-pie bias more directly and examine how hierarchically structured teams perform across tasks with different coordination needs, including purely distributive (single-issue) settings.
