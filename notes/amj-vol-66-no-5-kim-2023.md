# Wearing Your Worth at Work: The Consequences of Employees’ Daily Clothing Choices

**Abstract**
Do the clothes worn to work impact employees’ thoughts and behaviors? Despite the universal necessity of wearing clothes and the fact that employees make decisions about this daily, organizational scholars have not yet addressed this question. We integrate sociometer and enclothed cognition theories to propose that aspects of clothing—their aesthetics, conformity, and uniqueness—hold symbolic meanings that have implications for employees’ state self-esteem and subsequent task and relational behaviors (i.e., goal progress, social avoidance). We first provide evidence for the nature of the symbolic meanings associated with these three dimensions of work clothing in a set of within-person experimental studies. The results of a 10-day field study of employees from four organizations generally supported our predictions, showing that daily clothing aesthetics and uniqueness had effects on state self-esteem and downstream behavioral consequences. The effects of daily clothing conformity emerged under the condition of greater interaction frequency with others in the workplace. Our manuscript contributes to both major theories from which we draw, and further offers theoretical and practical contributions to the literature on organizational clothing.

**Research Question**
Whether, why, and when employees’ daily clothing choices have implications for their daily state self-esteem and subsequent task and relational work behaviors.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Daily perceived dress aesthetics; daily perceived dress conformity; daily perceived dress uniqueness
- DV(s): Daily goal progress; daily social avoidance (with state self-esteem as the proximal outcome)
- Mediators: State self-esteem; the symbolic meanings of personal attractiveness (for aesthetics), belongingness (for conformity), and distinctiveness (for uniqueness)
- Moderators: Daily interaction frequency with colleagues

Drawing on enclothed cognition theory, the authors argue that the clothes employees wear "embody" symbolic meanings that activate associated psychological processes. Integrating sociometer theory, those meanings (attractiveness, belongingness, distinctiveness) carry relational value that the sociometer registers, raising or lowering state self-esteem on a given day. Heightened state self-esteem then promotes motivated task behavior (goal progress) and reduces relational withdrawal (social avoidance). Because the sociometer is socially attuned, the conformity effect on self-esteem is amplified on days with more interpersonal interaction, when conformity to organizational dress norms is more readily compared and observed.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends enclothed cognition theory from specific, universally meaningful garments (e.g., a doctor's lab coat) to ordinary, day-to-day work clothing applicable to employees in virtually any workplace, and adopts an actor-centric, within-person lens rather than the observer-centric view dominant in the organizational dress literature. It extends sociometer theory by showing that controllable daily choices—not just external events or stable physical attributes—drive state self-esteem, and by providing empirical support for the idea that both fitting in (belongingness) and standing out (distinctiveness) can foster self-esteem.

**Practical Implication**
Investing modest morning effort to dress in clothing that is aesthetically pleasing and unique—and, when interactions are expected, conforming to organizational norms—can meaningfully improve how employees feel about themselves and behave at work. Managers can offer soft guidance and organizationally branded items that help employees fit in while leaving room for self-expression, rather than imposing rigid dress codes.

**Limitations**
All Study 2 measures were self-reported, raising common-method-variance concerns, though the authors temporally separated variables, lagged mediators/outcomes, and group-mean centered within-individual variables to mitigate this. The field-study effects were somewhat small in magnitude, and the study did not use objective indicators of dress or behavior.

**Future Research**
Future studies could assess work behavior with objective indicators (e.g., sales) or other-source reports, and rate attire through a more objective lens (e.g., trained coders) to further reduce common-method-variance concerns. Researchers could examine the dimensionality of clothing with greater specificity, including how particular or personalized clothing attributes convey relational value differently across organizational and cultural contexts (a possible congruence effect).
