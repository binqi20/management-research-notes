# Tempering Temperance? A Contingency Approach to Social Movements’ Entry Deterrence in Scottish Whisky Distilling, 1823–1921

**Abstract**
What makes social movements successfully deter entry in contested industries? We develop a contingency framework explaining how movements' success depends on the internal fit between their private and public politics strategies with the tactics of mass and elite mobilization. We also highlight the importance of how these tactics fit with external conditions like the cognitive legitimacy of the industry and industry countermobilization. When movements rely on a private politics strategy to condemn an industry in the eyes of the public, social movement mass will be decisive. Alternatively, when movements use a public politics strategy to push for regulatory intervention, mobilization of elites is crucial. We develop our understanding of external contingency factors by exploring how cognitive legitimacy residuals from local ancestral populations affect both mass-driven private politics and elite-driven public politics, and how nationallevel industry countermobilization efforts affect elite-driven public politics strategies. We test these ideas in a historical study of the Scottish whisky distilling industry during the rise of temperance movements (1823–1921). We contribute to the social movements literature by showing how movements' entry deterrence in contested industries depends on the internal fit between their strategies and mobilization tactics, as well as on their engagement with external contingencies.

**Research Question**
How does social movement opposition affect entrepreneurial entry in contested industries, and how does the effectiveness of movements' contestation depend on the fit between their private/public politics strategies, their mass/elite mobilization tactics, and external contingencies (cognitive legitimacy and incumbent countermobilization)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Social movement mass mobilization (STL member proportion, private politics); elite mobilization (SPBTA local elite members and MPs, public politics); local ancestral populations (cognitive legitimacy residual); national-level industry countermobilization (whisky-specific associations).
- DV(s): Whisky distillery foundings (entrepreneurial entry), modeled as an annual county-level count.
- Mediators: None modeled (direct effects of mobilization and contingencies on founding rates).
- Moderators: Ancestral populations (moderating mass-driven private politics and elite-driven public politics); industry countermobilization (moderating elite-driven public politics).

Movements pursuing private politics deter entry by getting the public to internalize a negative frame, so a critical mass of supporters raises the social cost of entry; movements pursuing public politics deter entry by enlisting political elites who can signal forthcoming restrictive regulation. These effects are contingent on context: ancestral populations endow the industry with cognitive legitimacy, while incumbent countermobilization in the national legislative arena blunts elite-driven public politics — and, unexpectedly, can even spur further entry.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper builds a contingency theory of social movements' entry deterrence in established-but-contested industries, distinguishing the constituencies (mass vs. elite) that make private versus public politics strategies effective. It theorizes and tests two external contingencies — ancestral-population cognitive legitimacy and incumbent countermobilization — showing that contestation is a two-way, coevolutionary process whose outcomes depend on the internal fit between strategy and tactics and on engagement with these external conditions.

**Practical Implication**
For activists, the effective tactic depends on strategy: mass mobilization matters most for private politics, while enlisting political elites matters most for public politics, and elite leverage is strongest where an industry is historically well-anchored. For incumbents in contested industries, national-level countermobilization can defend sociopolitical legitimacy and may even encourage new entry by making the regulatory environment appear less hostile.

**Limitations**
The historical data preclude fixed-effects estimation and limit the ability to capture how movements adapted their framing or deployed specific tactics (petitions, boycotts, townhall meetings) over a long period. The authors also could not systematically capture the active lobbying behind incumbent countermobilization or countermobilization efforts aimed specifically at private-politics framing.

**Future Research**
Future work could develop finer-grained understanding of contestation strategies and their supporting constituencies, theorizing about the fit between new combinations of internal and external contingencies. Scholars could adopt a more granular historical approach to trace how movement and countermobilization dynamics, including specific tactical repertoires, unfold over prolonged periods.
