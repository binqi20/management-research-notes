# The Award Goes To… Someone Else: A Natural Quasi-Experiment Examining the Impact of Performance Awards on Nominees’ Workplace Collaboration

**Abstract**
Employee awards programs are prevalent in organizations. This research examines a group of employees who have received relatively less attention in the employee awards literature: those who are nominated for but fail to receive an award. Integrating social comparison theory with the functional theory of counterfactual thinking, we argue that nonwinner nominees go through complex motivational states and affective experiences, which influence their collaboration responsiveness to colleagues. We conducted a field quasi-experiment using 160,830 collaboration records of 494 employees over eight months (Study 1) and a multisource study using survey data and 34,911 collaborative records of 598 employees over two months (Study 2). The results revealed that, compared to non-nominees, nonwinner nominees have lower collaboration responsiveness to winners following the award announcement, especially when they have a higher structural proximity to winners. However, in the long run, nonwinner nominees have higher collaboration responsiveness to others in general. Furthermore, being nominated for but failing to receive an award simultaneously induces nonwinner nominees to experience higher causal-inference counterfactual thinking and negative affect, which exert opposing impacts on nonwinner nominees’ collaboration responsiveness. This research advances the understanding of the impact, influencing mechanisms, boundary conditions, and temporal dynamics of employee awards on workplace collaboration.

**Research Question**
How does being nominated for but failing to receive an award affect "nonwinner nominees'" collaboration responsiveness to colleagues—both to winners in particular and to others in general—compared with non-nominees, and through what mediating mechanisms, boundary conditions, and temporal dynamics?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Being a nonwinner nominee (nominated for but not receiving the award) versus being a non-nominee, following an award announcement.
- DV(s): Collaboration responsiveness (how promptly an individual addresses colleagues' collaboration requests, measured objectively as time to process received requests)—decomposed into responsiveness to winners and to others in general.
- Mediators: (1) Causal-inference counterfactual thinking (a positive, motivational pathway) and (2) negative affect (disappointment, frustration, anger; a negative pathway).
- Moderators: Structural proximity to winners (same department, section, or local office), strengthening the negative effect on responsiveness to winners.

Integrating social comparison theory with the functional theory of counterfactual thinking, the authors argue that a "near-miss" experience triggers two opposing forces in nonwinner nominees. Causal-inference counterfactual thinking ("if I had performed a little better, I would have won") motivates them to raise collaboration responsiveness to improve future winning chances, while the contrast effect generates negative affect that depresses responsiveness, especially toward winners. Because negative affect is short-lived but motivation is durable, the net effect is reduced responsiveness to winners in the short run (amplified by structural proximity) but elevated responsiveness to others in general in the long run.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes a finer distinction within nonrecipients—separating nonwinner nominees from non-nominees—and shows that those who come closer to a desired outcome behave differently than those who do not, extending social comparison theory and the star-performer literature (e.g., how "losing stars" can harm "winning stars"). It introduces collaboration responsiveness as a novel outcome to the employee awards literature, identifies a dual mediating mechanism (causal-inference counterfactual thinking and negative affect) that holds beyond competing mechanisms such as role modeling, rivalry, and benign/malicious envy, establishes structural proximity as a boundary condition, and uses a longitudinal design to reveal the temporal dynamics of awards effects.

**Practical Implication**
Because awards programs act as a double-edged sword in the short term but yield net-positive long-run collaboration effects, managers should actively promote collaboration between nonwinner nominees and winners—especially immediately after announcements and especially when the two are structurally proximate—to protect winners' performance and retention. Managers can also help nonwinner nominees curb episodic negative affect by showing appreciation for their contributions and providing supervisory support, encouragement, feedback, and resources, lessening frustration and disappointment.

**Limitations**
The study examines only one facet of collaboration (responsiveness to received requests) and does not test other facets such as initiating collaborations, value contributed, or tie persistence. Data were collected in China, a highly collectivistic culture, which may limit generalizability to individualistic or non-collaboration-valuing contexts, and the awards combined social recognition, feedback, and financial incentives. The proposed mediators explain a modest share of effects, leaving room for additional mechanisms, and the DID framework cannot rule out individual differences that change after the announcement; the temporal turning-point results are exploratory.

**Future Research**
The authors call for examining additional facets and outcomes of collaboration (e.g., initiating requests, performance, withdrawal, unethical behaviors), additional boundary conditions (e.g., cultural context, award type including purely symbolic awards), and additional mediators (e.g., rivalry, perceived fairness/attributions). They also encourage replicating the quasi-experimental design to test the generalizability of the exploratory temporal pattern, studying "surprise" awards whose nominees are not notified, and exploring employee collaboration during the nomination stage.
