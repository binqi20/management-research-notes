# Distilling Authenticity: Materiality and Narratives in Canadian Distilleries’ Authenticity Work

**Abstract**
Authenticity is increasingly seen as a source of competitive advantage in many industries. Accordingly, authenticity work, the organizational efforts to develop and sustain believable authenticity claims, has emerged as an important organizational practice. We examined the interplay of materiality and narratives underpinning producers’ authenticity work in the context of incumbent and micro-distilleries operating in the Canadian whisky industry. We found that producers’ material endowments, especially central product features, anchored which authenticity claims they could credibly narrate. Other material endowments, such as key people and architectural design, were used to reinforce the integrity of authenticity claims. Our study extends our understanding of authenticity as a valued organizational resource. First, we identify two mechanisms, anchoring and reinforcement, through which materiality both constrains and facilitates organizations’ authenticity narratives. Second, our research brings to the fore how audience members’ experiential closeness to producers colors their perceptions of authenticity, and we show how material artifacts can enhance such closeness. Third, our findings highlight the competitive value of authenticity by unpacking how producers’ material endowments may constitute a resource or a liability.

**Research Question**
How do organizations’ material endowments enable and constrain their authenticity claims, and how does the interplay of materiality and narratives shape the authenticity work through which producers craft and sustain believable authenticity claims?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: authenticity work; authenticity claims (combinations of narratives and material artifacts); material endowments (product features, key people, architectural design); the tension between tradition-based and originality-based bases of authenticity.
- Process / mechanisms: *Anchoring* — central product features tie producers to a prefigured base of authenticity, making some claims natural and others requiring greater narrative effort; *Reinforcement* — other material endowments (human embodiments, architectural design) bolster the integrity and persuasiveness of claims.
- Triggering conditions: differential material endowments (e.g., incumbents’ access to aged liquid vs. micro-distilleries’ lack thereof) prefigure which authenticity claims are credible.
- Emergent outcomes: relatively stable authenticity profiles; experiential closeness between producers and audiences that heightens perceived authenticity; authenticity as a competitive resource or liability.

Materiality is prefigurative: rather than being a blank canvas, an organization’s material endowments inform the selection and audience reception of authenticity narratives. Producers exercise agency by skillfully (re)combining narratives and materiality to overcome the constraints of anchoring or to reinforce an existing base of authenticity, conferring plasticity to authenticity claims and generating audience buy-in.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends understanding of authenticity as a valued organizational resource by identifying anchoring and reinforcement as two mechanisms through which materiality both constrains and facilitates authenticity narratives. It theorizes materiality as prefigurative—shaping which claims are credible rather than merely amplifying chosen claims—and surfaces audience members’ experiential closeness as a route through which material artifacts enhance perceived authenticity. It reframes resource advantage as a matter of how organizations use endowments in authenticity work rather than mere ownership.

**Practical Implication**
For producers in industries where authenticity matters, competitive advantage depends less on owning prestigious material endowments than on skillfully combining materiality and narratives to construct credible claims. Incumbents anchored to tradition may need to demonstrate innovation, while newer entrants can leverage approachable makers and architectural design to build experiential closeness; material endowments can be a resource or a liability depending on the authenticity work performed.

**Limitations**
The study relied on audience members’ accounts of what they deem authentic and deferred to their subjective understandings of how materiality matters, building on an audience-centric view of authenticity. It focuses on a single, tradition-rich industry (Canadian whisky) where a product’s perceived authenticity is tightly tied to that of its producer, which may limit transferability.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine how the challenge of balancing different bases of authenticity plays out across industries, and settings where material bases differ or where the product–organization tie is looser (e.g., high-technology or sharing-economy contexts). Scholars could also study how the meaning of material artifacts is negotiated in situ and how authenticity work and an organization’s primary authenticity claim change over time in response to internal and external factors.
