# Confronting the Contested Past: Sensemaking and Rhetorical History in the Reconstruction of Organizational Identity

**Abstract**
This study explores how organizations experience and respond to identity challenges that arise due to conflicting interpretations of their past. Drawing on a case study of a fintech venture, we offer a process model that illuminates the unfolding of "temporal identity complexity," a sensemaking process that involves different members developing conflicting understandings of how the past undermines the organizational identity. Our model also reveals how leaders can restore members' beliefs in the organizational identity through "temporal synergizing," a sensegiving process that recombines conflicting interpretations of the past to support desired identity claims in the present and future. In contrast with prior research that has emphasized the need to construe a sense of identity continuity over time, we show how organizations can instead capitalize on perceived discontinuity in their past to reaffirm their identity. We discuss this and other contributions to research on organizational identity, focusing on its threads on sensemaking and rhetorical history. This includes exploring the important role that temporality and emotions play in organizational identity reconstruction.

**Research Question**
How do leaders and members reconstruct the organizational identity when members contest the organization's past in conflicting ways?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Accumulation of memory cues from external sources (media, public awareness) and internal experiences (reformed and persisting practices) that keep the contested past salient.
- Key constructs / tensions: "Temporal identity complexity" — different member groups develop conflicting cognitive and emotional understandings in which (a) a "bad past" taints the identity in the present, (b) a "good past" dwarfs the identity in the present, and (c) the contested past frustrates identity in the future.
- Leaders' sensegiving strategies ("temporal synergizing"):
  - Transfusing the conflicting past into the present — articulating how a tempered interpretation of the conflicting past benefits the present (past–present).
  - Grafting the future with the conflicting past — integrating key experiences from both interpretations to re-envision future identity enactment (past–future).
  - Defusing present events vis-à-vis the conflicting past — construing current incidents that resemble past violations as evidence of effective identity enactment (past–present).
- Emergent outcomes: Generative sensemaking by members, replacement of resignation and shame with pride and achievement, and reaffirmation of identity claims by capitalizing on identity discontinuity (Ricoeur's identity as ipse) rather than continuity (idem).

The process model shows that when members hold conflicting interpretations of the same period of the past, the standard approach of construing temporal continuity backfires because creating continuity with one view alienates the other and creating continuity with all conflicting views is internally incoherent. Leaders instead succeed by recombining the conflicting past — invoking perceived inconsistencies in past identity enactment to construe improved enactment in the present and future, thereby restoring members' belief in the organization's identity.

**Data & Measures**
Combines interview and archival data on the fintech venture DigiCo (pseudonym). The core data source is 46 in-depth interviews — 44 with DigiCo leaders and employees across functions (20 leaders, 24 members) and two with consultants — averaging one hour, recorded and transcribed. These are triangulated with extensive archival data: internal texts (presentation slides, videos, memos), DigiCo's website via the Wayback Machine, 77 Glassdoor reviews and 22 CEO replies, internal management presentations, employee statement pictures, 7 press releases, 1,250 press articles, 36 industry reports, and 1,046 TrustPilot reviews. Constructs were operationalized inductively through Gioia-style analysis: sentence- and paragraph-level open coding into 20 first-order codes, axial coding linking first-order codes into 10 second-order themes, and three aggregate theoretical dimensions, with a dedicated pass coding informants' emotions and temporal bracketing to trace how the process unfolded across periods.

**Key Findings**
The study surfaces an inductive process model of temporal identity reconstruction. Members developed "temporal identity complexity" — different groups formed conflicting cognitive and emotional understandings in which a "bad past" tainted the identity in the present, a "good past" dwarfed it in the present, and the contested past frustrated the identity in the future — leading members to lose faith in DigiCo's identity as a customer-focused fintech. Construing temporal continuity did not resolve this challenge, because siding with one view alienated the other and reconciling all conflicting views was internally incoherent. Leaders instead restored members' belief in the identity through "temporal synergizing," a sensegiving process combining transfusing (recasting the conflicting past as benefiting the present), grafting (integrating both interpretations into a re-envisioned future), and defusing (construing present incidents that resemble past violations as evidence of improved enactment). The central finding is that organizations can reaffirm identity by capitalizing on perceived identity discontinuity — invoking inconsistencies in past identity enactment to demonstrate strengthened enactment in the present and future — rather than construing continuity, with members ultimately coming to express pride in place of earlier shame, sadness, and resignation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper shifts the understanding of how leaders create temporal coherence to reaffirm organizational identity from "construing continuity" to "capitalizing on identity discontinuity" — invoking past inconsistencies to demonstrate strengthened identity enactment in the present and future. It articulates "temporal identity complexity" as a novel identity threat in which members develop conflicting cognitive and emotional understandings of the past, and theorizes "temporal synergizing" (transfusing, grafting, defusing) as the sensegiving process by which leaders recombine conflicting pasts into a synergistic basis for present and future identity claims. It also cautions rhetorical-history scholarship against dissociating organizations from problematic pasts or privileging one external stakeholder group, since both approaches can ignite internal identity challenges.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders confronting internal contestation of the organization's past should not rely on selective remembering or on siding with one group's view, because this perpetuates conflict and alienates other members. Instead, they should engage comprehensively with the conflicting memory cues by transfusing, grafting, and defusing — articulating how inconsistencies in past identity enactment have strengthened the organization and inform an improved future. Leaders should also recognize that internal identity contestation may be obscured by simpler external "villain" narratives in the media, and design sensegiving that addresses the distinct emotions (shame, sadness, resignation) underlying each group's view.

**Limitations**
Leaders at DigiCo drove the sensegiving and members provided input but not the formal messaging, leaving open how members themselves engage in sensegiving about identity through less visible channels. The case organization could not remove its negative external image, so the findings privilege contexts in which external image continues to cue a conflicting past. The study examines discursive sensegiving and does not investigate how leaders mobilize organizational practices and materiality (e.g., artifacts that cue the past) to reconstruct identity.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how members rather than leaders engage in sensegiving about identity through informal channels, and how external image affects temporal identity complexity when leaders cannot reshape that image. Researchers could also study how leaders use materiality and artifacts (versus discourse) to reconstruct contested identities, and how different sensegiving strategies might construe identity significance through a dialectic between idem (continuity) and ipse (interrupted continuity). Further work could investigate emotions of different valence underpinning temporal identity complexity, and situations in which both internal and external stakeholders hold conflicting interpretations of the past.
