# Mother’s Reentry: A Relative Contribution Perspective of Dual-Earner Parents’ Roles, Resources, and Outcomes

**Abstract**
The reentry period after maternity leave can be a stressful but important transition for mothers, their significant others, and their organizations. In the present study, we integrate conservation of resources theory, the crossover model, and the relative contribution model to understand the influence of perceived organizational support during reentry on both mothers’ and their significant others’ home stress and, ultimately, workplace experiences. Beyond actor effects, we investigate the relationship between each parent’s perceived organizational support on their partner’s home stress via three crossover mechanisms: (a) direct, (b) indirect, (c) shared stressor. Notably, we offer a theoretically novel lens through which to view the shared-stressor mechanism. Drawing upon the relative contribution model, we conceptualize dual-earner parents as a team and argue that because mothers are the critical members during this period, their resources will directly influence both parents’ home stress. Findings yielded by this multistage, multisource field study demonstrate the importance of supporting mothers as critical members of the family during reentry, as organizational support that is perceived as inadequate directly affects home stress and indirectly influences workplace experiences that can impede successful transitions and threaten career outcomes for both parents.

**Research Question**
How does perceived organizational support (POS) shape dual-earner parents’ home stress and downstream workplace outcomes (family–work conflict and interpersonal deviance) during the mother’s post-maternity-leave reentry, and through what direct, indirect, and shared-stressor crossover mechanisms — given the mother’s role as the couple’s critical member — does each parent’s POS influence their partner’s home stress?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Mother’s POS (Time 1); significant other’s POS (Time 1)
- DV(s): Mother’s and significant other’s family–work conflict (FWC) and interpersonal deviance at work (Time 3)
- Mediators: Mother’s home stress (Time 2); significant other’s home stress (Time 2); support of the partner (Time 2); Time 1 home stress for direct crossover
- Moderators: Tested (and not supported): breadwinner status, perceived contribution to family, masculine/feminine industry

Integrating conservation of resources (COR) theory, the crossover model, and the relative contribution model (RCM), the authors theorize that POS is a boundary-spanning resource whose esteem, status, and positive emotions spill into the home domain and reduce home stress. Because dual-earner parents share the home, stress crosses over directly (emotional contagion) and indirectly (via partner support). Crucially, RCM positions the mother as the couple’s critical member during reentry, so her POS directly attenuates both parents’ home stress (shared-stressor mechanism), which in turn shapes both parents’ FWC and interpersonal deviance at work.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper offers a new theoretical lens on the shared-stressor crossover mechanism by integrating the relative contribution model with COR theory: it shows that during shared stressful family events the critical member’s resources — not the focal individual’s alone — drive both partners’ stress. This reframes COR’s standard interpretation of valuable resources by asking *whose* resources matter, and extends crossover research by demonstrating that mothers’ POS shapes significant others’ home stress, FWC, and interpersonal deviance even when significant others’ own POS does not.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should treat post-maternity-leave reentry as a critical, dyadic family transition rather than a “mother’s issue” and proactively engender POS for returning mothers — e.g., showing appreciation for their goals, checking in on well-being, providing schedule control, and signaling that motherhood will not damage career opportunities. Because the critical family member’s POS spills over to her partner’s stress and workplace behavior, supporting mothers yields collateral benefits for significant others’ workplaces.

**Limitations**
All participants were U.S.-based, so country-level policy and cultural variation in maternity leave was not modeled, and part-time versus full-time status post-leave was not measured. Childcare arrangements were not assessed. Mothers’ contributions may be inflated because the comparison occurred immediately after leave. The mother-deviance scale showed below-cutoff reliability (α = .59), likely because mothers’ deviance was almost exclusively limited to “making fun of someone at work,” reflecting heightened scrutiny on returning mothers.

**Future Research**
Future studies should test the model in different national/cultural contexts, contrast full-time and part-time returners, incorporate childcare arrangements, and examine longer-term consequences across stages of parenting. The COR + RCM integration could extend to other teams where a critical member’s resources may disproportionately shape collective outcomes (e.g., academic teams, medical teams), and to other shared stressors (e.g., COVID-19 caregiving demands) where the locus of the critical member may shift.
