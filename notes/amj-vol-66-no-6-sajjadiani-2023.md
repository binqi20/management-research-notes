# Who Is Leaving and Why? The Dynamics of High-Quality Human Capital Outflows

**Abstract**
This study proposes a unified, dynamic framework based on turnover event theory to evaluate the effects of dismissals, layoff announcements, and voluntary turnover on subsequent work-unit voluntary turnover. Applying our approach to 1,620 retail stores over 22 months, we show that modeling exit events as a dynamic and interdependent system adds to our ability to predict subsequent human capital outflow. Dismissals had the weakest total effect on subsequent voluntary turnover, layoff announcements had the strongest and most immediate effects, and voluntary turnovers had moderate but lasting effects. We also find that these three exit reasons each exhibit a distinct pattern of subsequent turnover intensity and longevity. Based on characteristics of work units in our setting, our results correspond to a cumulative worker-level average effect of 0.17 quits following dismissals, 0.23 quits following quits, and 2.2 quits following a layoff. We find that these "multiplier" effects are concentrated among workers of similar performance: high performer exits beget high performer quits, just as low-performer exits beget low-performer quits. Our findings suggest analyses offering individual-level estimates of turnover will generally underestimate the broader, work-unit level consequences of individual exit events on similar workers.

**Research Question**
How do different types of employee exit events—dismissals, layoff announcements, and voluntary turnover—differentially affect subsequent unit-level voluntary turnover, and how is this dynamic shaped by the quality (performance) of the human capital leaving the work unit?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Unit-month rates of dismissals, layoff announcements, and voluntary turnover (also disaggregated into exits of high- versus low-performing workers); hiring rate included for interdependence.
- DV(s): Subsequent unit-level voluntary turnover rate (also decomposed into voluntary turnover of high vs. low performers).
- Mediators: Not estimated; structural changes in job demands, human capital resources, and social relationships are theorized as transmission channels.
- Moderators: Human capital quality of the leaver (high vs. low performer, indexed against the unit's average supervisor rating).

Drawing on turnover event theory, the authors argue that exit events trigger a disruption-then-recovery phase whose strength is a function of the event's novelty, disruption, and criticality. Layoff announcements signal an uncontrollable, psychological-contract-violating threat, producing strong and immediate spillover quits; voluntary turnover signals viable alternatives and produces moderate but longer-lasting contagion; dismissals largely remove disruptive workers and only weakly (and briefly) depress subsequent quits. The PVAR estimates show these dynamic effects are concentrated within performance strata—high-performer exits beget high-performer quits and low-performer exits beget low-performer quits—revealing a similarity-based "multiplier" structure that individual-level turnover models cannot detect.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends turnover event theory by jointly modeling dismissals, layoff announcements, and voluntary turnover as endogenous, coevolving elements of a dynamic system rather than as independent outcomes, and by separating layoff announcements from layoff events themselves. It generates and tests TET-grounded predictions about how novelty, disruption, and criticality differentiate the magnitude and duration of subsequent voluntary turnover, and it shows that "turnover begets turnover" is not uniform but stratified by performance similarity—a mechanism prior collective-turnover work had not articulated.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat layoff announcements as the single most potent driver of subsequent voluntary turnover, especially when high performers are among those being laid off, and weigh the announcement decision accordingly. Because the consequences of departures multiply among similar workers, organizations underestimate the true human capital cost of losing high performers; retention investments in high performers therefore yield compounding returns. Dismissals targeted at clearly poor performers can be a net stabilizer of the unit.

**Limitations**
Findings are drawn from a single large U.S. retailer with relatively low task complexity and modest human capital investment, which may attenuate the magnitude of disruption effects; generalizability to higher-complexity or higher-tenure settings is uncertain. Human capital quality is operationalized via annual supervisor performance ratings, available only for employees with at least one year of tenure, and the authors lacked objective performance measures or a measure of perceived dismissal fairness. Other plausible sources of heterogeneity (job level, full- vs. part-time status) could not be modeled.

**Future Research**
Replicate the dynamic framework in occupations with higher task complexity, longer training, and greater human capital investment, where event novelty, disruption, and criticality should be larger. Use more objective performance metrics and direct measures of event novelty, disruption, and criticality to test the theorized mechanism more cleanly. Explore why exit events spill over within performance strata—e.g., the role of social-network homophily, social comparison, and signaling among similar workers.
