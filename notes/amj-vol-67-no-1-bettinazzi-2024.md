# Media Coverage of Firms in the Presence of Multiple Signals: A Configurational Approach

**Abstract**
Recent management research suggests that firms try to orchestrate conformity and distinctiveness in their behavior to reconcile opposing demands of norm compliance and competitive distinction in order to appear optimally distinct. Whether they succeed or not depends on how audiences perceive the multiple signals they elicit. Extant literature has not fully uncovered how the media combine these different signals to form a representation of firms' behavior and their subsequent reactions to these combinations. We adopt a configurational approach to address this gap in the environmental sustainability context. Drawing on unique proprietary data on 118 cases in the energy utility industry and using fsQCA, we determine that different equifinal signal configurations are associated with positive media coverage regarding environmental behavior or the lack thereof. We then generate five patterns of signals that explain media reactions and disentangle the underlying logics behind them. Our study adds to the research on the multifaceted nature of signal (in)congruence perception by discussing the role of conformity and distinctiveness and highlighting that this perception depends on the signals' credibility. Delivering a balanced orchestration of conformity and distinctiveness signals is possible, but complex, because audiences tend to penalize incongruent postures they cannot associate with a given category.

**Research Question**
How do the media combine the various conformity and distinctiveness signals that firms emit about their environmental behavior to construct an overall representation, and which signal configurations are associated with positive (versus the absence of positive) media coverage?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: Six signal types crossing conformity vs. distinctiveness with three levels of credibility — firm-generated (donations, transformative actions), relationship-generated (associations, interfirm partnerships), and third-party-generated (certifications such as ISO 14001, ratings such as DJSI inclusion).
- Outcome: Presence vs. absence of positive media coverage of firms' environmental behavior (Janis–Fadner-based tenor measure).
- Patterns producing positive coverage: (1) Congruent signalers (congruent conformity or distinctiveness around a credible third-party signal); (2) Balancing signalers (a credible third-party signal paired with a lower-credibility opposing signal); (3) Muddling-through signalers (combinations of lower-credibility signals, granted temporary "benefit of the doubt").
- Patterns producing absence of positive coverage: (4) Minimal signalers (insufficient signaling, no anchor); (5) Credible incongruent signalers (simultaneous presence of opposing highly credible third-party signals that prevent media from anchoring on a dominant message).
- Boundary conditions: Causal complexity, equifinality, and conjunctural causation; underlying perception depends jointly on signal credibility and congruence.

The process by which media form environmental assessments is configurational rather than additive: they triangulate multiple noisy signals through a sensemaking process anchored on the most credible signals available. Incongruence is only problematic when it occurs at the level of highly credible third-party signals because the media cannot identify a dominant message; lower-credibility signals can rebalance a dominant credible message or, in the absence of credible signals, temporarily compensate. Optimal distinctiveness via simultaneous high-credibility conformity and distinctiveness signaling is therefore difficult — the media tend to read it as greenwashing-like incongruence.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper cross-fertilizes signaling theory and optimal distinctiveness literature by showing that signal (in)congruence and signal credibility are interdependent drivers of audience evaluation. It identifies signal credibility as a deeper-level determinant of incongruence perception: incongruence at the level of highly credible signals undermines audience approval, whereas balanced or congruent credible anchors enable positive assessments even when lower-credibility opposing signals are present. The paper also reframes optimal distinctiveness as harder to achieve than prior literature suggests because audiences penalize postures they cannot categorize.

**Practical Implication**
Firms should focus on obtaining third-party endorsement of either their conformity (e.g., ISO 14001) or their distinctiveness (e.g., DJSI inclusion), but not both simultaneously, because over-endorsement with opposing credible signals invites media scrutiny and is read as incongruent. Firms without credible third-party recognition can use combinations of lower-credibility signals as a temporary buffer, but must move toward acquiring a credible anchor to sustain positive coverage.

**Limitations**
The findings rest on the assumption that firms have agency in deciding which signals to emit, which may not hold equally across contexts (e.g., financial disclosures vs. marketing campaigns). The setting (environmental sustainability in electric utilities) is characterized by high causal ambiguity and may not generalize to more standardized contexts where signal credibility and congruence may matter less. The interpretation of configurations and patterns is abductive and primarily exploratory.

**Future Research**
Future studies could examine (in)congruence in other behavioral dimensions beyond conformity–distinctiveness and in contexts where highly credible third-party signals are absent or less relevant. Heterogeneity among media (including social media and other arbiters), context ambiguity as a moderator, and the cross-influence between media reactions and other stakeholder reactions (e.g., activists) are promising avenues. Researchers could also disentangle the sensemaking process audiences use when facing incongruent signals of differing credibility.
