# Firm–Stakeholder Dialogue and the Media: The Evolution of Stakeholder Evaluations in Different Informational Environments

**Abstract**
We propose that the informational environment in which firm–stakeholder interactions are embedded shapes the effect of firm–stakeholder dialogue on a stakeholder's evaluation of a firm. How much stakeholders know about a firm (information availability), what stakeholders know about a firm (information content), and how much they trust the media that informs them (information reliability) vary across countries, firms, and time. We theorize and assess how these three dimensions of the informational environment shape the effects of firm–stakeholder dialogue on the evolution of a stakeholder's evaluation of a firm. We illustrate our arguments with insights from field interviews conducted to understand stakeholders' evaluations of the Rosia Montana gold mine in Romania, and we analyze them using original, manually coded stakeholder-level data that capture 2,454 stakeholders' evaluations of 26 gold-mining operations around the world.

**Research Question**
How does the informational environment — specifically the availability, ESG thematic content, and reliability of media information about a firm — moderate the effect of proactive firm–stakeholder dialogue on the evolution of a stakeholder's evaluation of the firm?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Stakeholder dialogue (continuous percentage of firm-initiated interactions and dichotomous high-dialogue indicator)
- DV(s): Improvement in a stakeholder's evaluation of a firm (binary, coded from change in Goldstein-scale conflict–cooperation scores of stakeholder-initiated events)
- Moderators: Information availability (firm-level share of media coverage), information content (ESG-theme intensity scored by EMPATH across 11 themes), information reliability (V-Dem government censorship, V-Dem media-critical, Freedom House press freedom)
- Controls: Stakeholder type (political, social, economic), stakeholder location (local, national, international), prior stakeholder evaluations, inclusive stakeholder strategy, media sentiment, country and year fixed effects, inverse Mills ratio from a first-stage selection model

Building on Akerlof's lemons logic and stakeholder theory, the paper argues that firm–stakeholder dialogue and media information are alternative channels for reducing information asymmetries between a firm and its stakeholders. Dialogue most improves stakeholder evaluations precisely when media information is scarce, ESG-focused content is low, and the media is trusted; conversely, when the media is censored or unreliable, stakeholders' distrust spills over to firm-provided information, dampening dialogue's effect. Dense ESG-laden coverage shifts the agenda of firm–stakeholder interactions and crowds out the firm's ability to shape evaluations through proactive dialogue.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances stakeholder, reputation, and impression-management research by theorizing the informational environment as a critical moderator of firm–stakeholder dialogue, shifting the analytic level from the firm to the firm–stakeholder dyad and integrating proactive dialogue with reactive impression management on a single proactive-to-reactive continuum. It also extends attention to nonmarket stakeholders — communities, NGOs, governments, religious leaders — whose evaluations form a key part of a firm's social approval but who are absent from conventional governance mechanisms. Counterintuitively, it shows that low media reliability undermines rather than amplifies the value of direct firm–stakeholder communication, because distrust in the broader information environment spills over.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should match stakeholder strategy to the informational environment: proactive dialogue pays off most when media coverage of the firm is sparse, ESG salience is low, and stakeholders trust the media; under heavy ESG-focused or censored coverage, dialogue alone cannot offset the agenda set by the media and may need to be paired with other responses. Investments in stakeholder dialogue — the basis of a firm's "social license to operate" — are particularly valuable for projects in environments where independent media is weak but where direct, trusted relationships can still be built.

**Limitations**
The study observes only stakeholder evaluations reported in English-language media and therefore relies on a first-stage selection model with an inverse Mills ratio to mitigate sample-selection bias. The empirical setting is restricted to gold-mining firms listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange operating outside the U.S., Canada, and Australia, which improves comparability but limits generalizability to other industries and to interactions not visible to the media. The Rosia Montana fieldwork is presented as illustration rather than as theory-building qualitative data.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how signaling and impression-management tactics play out in different institutional environments, including under political openness, executive constraints, and varying stakeholder network structures. Scholars should further investigate how the deteriorating reliability of media — including "fake news" and disinformation — reshapes the value of direct firm disclosure, third-party certification, and firm–stakeholder dialogue across industries. Closer attention is needed to how reactive impression management interacts with proactive dialogue along the continuum theorized here.
