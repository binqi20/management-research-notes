# Coherence within and across Categories: The Dynamic Viability of Product Categories on Kickstarter

**Abstract**
How does the viability of a product category shift over time? Studies abound on how categories emerge and become established, or fall out of use. Yet, extant research has often examined the evolution of categories one at a time, leaving open the question of how related categories affect a focal category's viability. In contrast, we consider both intra- and inter-category dynamics. Viewing categories as continuously shaped by actors' efforts to position their products, we argue that these efforts alter the coherence that products exhibit not only within a category (a category's heterogeneity), but also across related categories (a category's distinctiveness). We theorize how the interaction between a category's heterogeneity and distinctiveness shapes its subsequent viability. When a focal category's distinctiveness is low, the heterogeneity–viability relationship takes an inverted U-shape. However, as distinctiveness grows, the relationship flattens and eventually flips to a U-shape. We explain this by considering the trade-off between the "classification" and "valuation" benefits that a category affords. We find support for our argument by tracking 170 categories over an 11-year period on Kickstarter, one of the largest crowdfunding platforms. By providing a nuanced understanding of category dynamics, we shed new light onto the fluctuating viability of categories.

**Research Question**
How does the viability of a product category shift over time, and specifically, how do intra-category heterogeneity and inter-category distinctiveness — both of which are dynamically reshaped by actors' product-positioning efforts — jointly determine whether a category remains in active use?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: When a focal category's distinctiveness is low, there is an inverted U-shaped relationship between the category's heterogeneity and its viability (too little or too much within-category heterogeneity lowers viability).
- H2: As a focal category's distinctiveness grows, the inverted U-shaped heterogeneity–viability relationship flattens and eventually flips to a U-shape (for highly distinctive categories, both low and high heterogeneity raise viability).

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Category heterogeneity (intra-category coherence, measured as mean pairwise Jensen–Shannon divergence among campaign topic distributions within a focal category over a trailing 90-day window) and category distinctiveness (inter-category coherence, measured as mean pairwise JSD between focal- and related-category campaign topic distributions over the same window). Quadratic terms in heterogeneity and a heterogeneity × distinctiveness interaction (including a squared-heterogeneity × distinctiveness term).
- DV(s): Category viability, operationalized as the daily count of campaign launches (with at least five backers) over a forward 30-day window in a given category.
- Mediators: Latent classification and valuation benefits — both move with heterogeneity but in opposite directions (classification rewards low heterogeneity; valuation rewards moderate-to-high heterogeneity).
- Moderators: Distinctiveness moderates the heterogeneity–viability curvature, flipping it from inverted-U at low distinctiveness toward a U-shape at high distinctiveness.
- Controls: Category-level (past performance, established audience base), category-set-level (set fixed effects, number of related categories, external Google Trends interest), and platform-level (Kickstarter popularity, seasonal cycles, post-emergence dummy, post-algorithm-change dummy, potato-salad-incident dummy).

The mechanism rests on two competing benefits that categories provide: classification benefits (low within-category heterogeneity helps actors and audiences group comparable products together) and valuation benefits (greater within-category variety lets actors carve out distinct positions and meet diverse audience preferences). When a focal category is not very distinctive from its neighbors, classification benefits dominate at the extremes and valuation benefits dominate in the middle, yielding the canonical inverted-U. As the focal category becomes more distinctive, classification benefits weaken (the label already signals separation), valuation benefits at the extremes strengthen, and the curve flattens and then flips to a U-shape — in which both very tight and very loose categories outperform middling ones.

**Data & Measures**
- Data: publicly retrieved records of 425,525 Kickstarter campaigns (each campaign's "Story" narrative) launched April 2009–December 2019, aggregated into 428,873 category-day observations across 170 categories nested in 15 superordinate sets.
- Text model: a 750-topic structural topic model (STM) over cleaned, English-language narratives yields each campaign's topic distribution, read as the cultural resources actors mobilize.
- DV — viability: the daily number of campaign launches (each with at least five backers) over a forward 30-day window (t to t+29) in a category.
- IV — heterogeneity (intra-category coherence): mean Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD) between the topic distributions of all campaign pairs ending within the focal category over the prior 90 days (t−90 to t−1); entered with a quadratic term.
- IV — distinctiveness (inter-category coherence): mean JSD between focal-category and related-category campaign topic distributions over the same 90-day window; interacted with heterogeneity and heterogeneity².
- Controls: category-level (past performance, established audience base), set-level (15 set fixed effects, number of related categories, external Google Trends interest), and platform-level (Kickstarter popularity, seasonal sine–cosine cycles, and post-emergence, post-algorithm-change, and potato-salad-incident dummies).
- Identification: main models are generalized least squares (GLS) on the observational (non-randomized) category-day panel, with turning-point and flipping-point tests following Haans et al. (2016). The paper adds coarsened exact matching, frames Kickstarter's category additions as a quasi-natural experiment, and runs a sensitivity analysis to buttress its causal claims.

**Key Findings**
- Direct effect (Model 2): the heterogeneity² coefficient is positive and significant (+17.691, p < .001), indicating a curvilinear (on average flat, U-shaped) heterogeneity–viability relationship before conditioning on distinctiveness.
- Distinctiveness main effect (Model 3): negative and significant (−24.164, p < .001) — on average, categories that fit poorly with related categories are less viable.
- H1 supported (Model 4): at low distinctiveness the heterogeneity² coefficient is negative and significant (−367.875, p < .001), i.e., an inverted U-shape in which too little or too much heterogeneity depresses viability.
- H2 supported (Model 4): the heterogeneity² × distinctiveness coefficient is positive and significant (+1,107.230, p < .001), flattening the inverted U as distinctiveness rises and flipping it to a U-shape past an estimated flipping point of distinctiveness ≈ 0.332 (within the observed range).
- Substantive magnitude: at low distinctiveness a two-SD shift of heterogeneity from the turning point yields 4.39 fewer launches/day (≈23% below the mean of 19.13); at high distinctiveness the same shift yields 3.42 more launches/day (≈18% above the mean). The authors report "considerable support for Hypotheses 1 and 2."
- Robustness: results hold under OLS, Poisson, negative binomial, and hurdle models; alternative viability proxies (average backers, success rate); varied topic counts and 7/30/60/90/180-day windows (only the 7-day window weakened some independent variables); category-level and no-fixed-effects specifications; coarsened exact matching; and 10,000-run power resampling.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconciles competing strands in the category-dynamics literature (life-cycle vs. fluid views; negative, positive, and inverted-U findings on heterogeneity) by jointly modeling intra-category heterogeneity and inter-category distinctiveness, showing that the heterogeneity–viability relationship is not a single fixed shape but a family of shapes contingent on distinctiveness. It extends Lo et al. (2020) by formalizing the classification/valuation trade-off and demonstrating that what appears "optimal" is dynamically accomplished and may even invert when categories become highly distinctive. It also advances cultural entrepreneurship research by shifting attention from individual product symbolic actions to how those actions, taken together across a classification system, shape the viability of entire categories.

**Practical Implication**
For entrepreneurs deciding how to position their crowdfunding campaigns, the results imply that "healthy" levels of within-category variety are not absolute but should be evaluated against how distinctive the category already is from its neighbors. Introducing products that increase within-category heterogeneity has different implications depending on the focal category's relation to adjacent categories — sometimes broadening the category helps viability, sometimes sharpening it does.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting is bounded to relatively simple classification systems (Kickstarter's 15 superordinate sets containing 170 categories), and more complex nested systems (e.g., Québec terroir, B Corp) may exhibit different dynamics. The language-based measures capture symbolic positioning but not material aspects of products or the evolution of individual entrepreneurs' claims. Operationalizing viability via the count of new entries is appropriate to crowdfunding but is an imperfect proxy for viability more broadly (which may also reflect popularity, attractiveness, or demand).

**Future Research**
Future studies could test the theorization in more complex classification systems and in non-crowdfunding contexts, using longitudinal or experimental designs to capture actors' propensity to claim or forgo category membership. Other promising avenues include unpacking when specific cultural resources contribute primarily to classification vs. valuation benefits, examining the codes that encourage or discourage variety, integrating material and symbolic dimensions of products, and exploring richer forms of inter-category relatedness beyond "A is not B" (e.g., A as B, A with B, A for B, A against B).
