# Left Behind? Understanding the Career Consequences of Collaborator Exits

**Abstract**
Prior research on career interdependence has focused on how colleague exits shape employees' intraorganizational careers through the creation of job vacancies and vacancy-driven promotion. In this paper, I propose that colleague exits can shape employees' careers through the creation of valuable relational "vacancies." Focusing on employees engaged in collaborative research, and drawing on the vacancy chains literature, I argue that colleague exits create new collaboration opportunities for remaining employees that can enhance learning and facilitate competency-driven promotion. Yet, employees will benefit only when these opportunities arise as a result of their own collaborators leaving—that is, employees must lose to gain. Using longitudinal data on employees within a single research organization, I show that collaborator exits increase the likelihood of employees' competency-driven promotion in a way that noncollaborator exits do not. Furthermore, it is the exit of higher-level collaborators that is most beneficial. These findings highlight a different type of career interdependence and the role of collaborator mobility in shaping the intraorganizational careers of knowledge workers.

**Research Question**
How do collaborator exits—as opposed to noncollaborator exits—shape the competency-driven promotion of remaining employees in knowledge-intensive organizations, and to what extent does this depend on the relative job grade of the exiting collaborators?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Collaborator exits in prior 12 months (count); higher-grade collaborator exits; similar-or-lower-grade collaborator exits; noncollaborator exits.
- DV(s): Competency-driven promotion (binary, monthly).
- Mediators: Formation of new collaborative relationships (especially with colleagues who had also collaborated with those who left); year-on-year change in number of higher-grade collaborators.
- Moderators: Employee age (proxy for career stage); employee role/grade group (high-grade scientist, low-grade scientist, high-/low-grade technician).

When collaborators leave, they vacate "relational vacancies" in the collaboration structure that connected employees can fill by forming new collaborative ties—particularly with colleagues who shared the same departing collaborators. These new ties expose remaining employees to different human capital and stretch projects, accelerating learning and human-capital development that managers recognize through competency-driven promotion. The effect is strongest when the exiting collaborators were in higher job grades (offering richer learning) and when the remaining employee is early in their career, when there is more competency still to be developed.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends career interdependence theory by applying the vacancy-chain perspective to relational structures (the collaboration network) rather than only the job structure, showing that colleague exits create not just job vacancies but multiple relational vacancies with implications for competency-driven—not just vacancy-driven—promotion. It also broadens the literature on collaborator loss beyond star deaths, highlighting positive learning consequences of routine collaborator exits in a bounded organizational setting.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should map collaboration structures to anticipate which employees will be affected by exits and where new collaboration opportunities will arise, and may need to actively facilitate the formation of new collaborative ties—possibly through "collaborator rotation" programs analogous to job rotation—because seminars and infrastructure alone may be insufficient to dislodge employees from over-long collaborative relationships that limit learning.

**Limitations**
Results are correlational rather than causal, and use of journal coauthorship to identify collaborations means non-publishing collaborations go undetected, restricting findings to "successful" research collaborations. Focus on a single organizationally bounded research institute limits generalizability to settings where collaborations span organizational boundaries (e.g., academia) or where competency-driven promotion is uncommon.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine longer collaboration vacancy chains as decaying ties redirect attention to newer collaborators, factors that facilitate or hinder vacancy chains in collaboration structures (e.g., scarcity, external hiring, prior network size), and whether the same dynamics apply to friendship and advice networks or to non-research collaborations such as project-based work in professional service firms.
