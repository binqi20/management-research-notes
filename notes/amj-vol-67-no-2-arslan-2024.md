# Public–Private and Private–Private Collaboration as Pathways for Socially Beneficial Innovation: Evidence from Antimicrobial Drug-Development Tasks

**Abstract**
Although public–private collaboration abounds in addressing societal grand challenges, little is known about its performance relative to private-sector collaboration. Drawing from the innovation collaboration literature, and qualitative insights from interviews and contracts, we argue that collaboration risk impairs task performance of public– private versus private–private collaboration. We attribute this to two collaboration risk mechanisms: cooperation problems arising from misaligned social versus economic incentives, and coordination problems due to differences in organizational governance and processes. We test our hypotheses using the development history of 2,496 antimicrobial drugs from 1995–2019. After accounting for endogenous selection into collaboration, we find that public–private collaboration is less effective than private–private collaboration. However, this performance gap reduces for innovation tasks under high technological uncertainty (i.e., in the discovery stage and for developing new drug action mechanisms). To probe the distinctive cooperation and coordination mechanisms, we also examine innovation task efficiency. Findings reveal a wider gap in task effectiveness but not efficiency between public–private and private–private collaboration, suggesting more significant cooperation than coordination problems. Our theoretical and practical insights pertain to whether, when, and how public–private and private–private collaboration offer viable pathways for socially beneficial innovation tasks.

**Research Question**
Whether and when public–private collaboration contributes to the success of socially beneficial innovation tasks, relative not only to firms' solo efforts but also to collaboration involving only private-sector actors, and how cooperation and coordination mechanisms drive any performance differences.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: Public–private collaboration is (a) less effective and (b) less efficient for an innovation task such as drug development, relative to private–private collaboration.
- H2: The lower (a) effectiveness and (b) efficiency of public–private collaboration relative to private–private collaboration is mitigated in the early discovery stage but amplified in the commercialization stage of innovation.
- H3: The lower (a) effectiveness and (b) efficiency of public–private collaboration relative to private–private collaboration is mitigated for novel drug technologies but amplified for established (existing-mechanism) drug technologies.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Collaboration type (solo development = 0; private–private collaboration = 1; public–private collaboration = 2)
- DV(s): Task effectiveness (binary—drug progresses to next development phase) and task efficiency (count of days to progress to next stage)
- Moderators: Technological uncertainty operationalized two ways—(a) drug-development stage (discovery vs. clinical), and (b) technological novelty of the drug's mechanism of action (new vs. existing mechanism, split at the median)
- Theoretical mechanisms (collaboration risk): cooperation problems from relational risk (misaligned social vs. economic incentives, weaker governance provisions in public–private contracts) and coordination problems from operational risk (differences in organizational structures, processes, and special government regulations such as the Buy American Act)
- Estimation: IPWRA addressing endogenous partner selection; ATET estimates compare collaborative outcomes to a synthetic counterfactual of solo development with similar covariate distribution

The central mechanism is that collaboration risk—comprising cooperation problems from relational risk and coordination problems from operational risk—is higher in public–private than in private–private collaboration because public partners pursue social value/knowledge dissemination goals while private firms pursue commercial returns, and public–private contracts lack governance provisions (development milestones, joint patenting, joint steering committees, termination clauses) that private–private contracts use to mitigate these tensions. Heightened cooperation problems primarily depress effectiveness (whether a task moves to the next stage), while coordination problems primarily depress efficiency (time to progress). Technological uncertainty attenuates this gap: in the discovery stage and for novel drug mechanisms, partners' knowledge-creation interests are more aligned and there is greater tolerance for trial-and-error, so public–private collaboration performs comparably to private–private collaboration; in clinical/commercialization stages and for established mechanisms, incentive misalignment and coordination demands amplify the gap.

**Data & Measures**
- Data: Development history of antimicrobial drugs from the Cortellis database, 1995–2019 (progress observed through July 2022)—7,388 drug–indication–organization–country–phase pentads (2,496 unique drugs), of which 736 are collaborative tasks (367 public–private, 369 private–private); complemented by six semi-structured expert interviews and text analysis of 176 drug-development contracts.
- IV — Collaboration type: categorical (0 = solo development; 1 = private–private, both partners for-profit; 2 = public–private, one for-profit and one public-mission partner; mixed multi-partner deals coded public–private).
- DV — Task effectiveness: binary, coded 1 if the drug progresses to the consecutive development phase for the indication.
- DV — Task efficiency: count of the number of days a drug takes to progress from one development stage to the next.
- Moderators — Technological uncertainty operationalized two ways: (a) drug-development stage (early discovery/preclinical vs. clinical) and (b) technological novelty (count of prior industry uses of the drug's target mechanism of action; sample split at the median into "new mechanism" vs. "existing mechanism").
- Estimation: inverse probability weighting regression adjustment (IPWRA) on observational data to address endogenous selection into collaboration—multinomial logistic treatment model, logistic outcome model for effectiveness, Poisson for efficiency—reporting the average treatment effect on the treated (ATET) relative to a synthetic solo-development counterfactual; robustness via IPW and coarsened exact matching (CEM), plus a supplementary Poisson patenting analysis. The design is observational (selection-on-observables with a double-robustness property), not a randomized or quasi-experimental one.

**Key Findings**
Dominant (quantitative IPWRA) results, reported per hypothesis:
- H1 partially supported. H1a supported—in the full sample public–private collaboration is less effective than private–private (effectiveness ATET −0.041, p = .050, vs. +0.038, p = .075; the private–private-minus-public–private difference is positive and statistically significant, diff = .080, p = .004). H1b not supported—public–private takes roughly 458 days longer than private–private, but the efficiency difference is not statistically significant (p = .441).
- H2 supported. H2a: the effectiveness gap is not significant in the discovery stage (diff = .043, p = .321) but significant in the clinical stage (diff = .119, p = .000). H2b: the efficiency gap is not significant in discovery (diff = 277.4 days, p = .315) but significant in clinical (diff = 982.0 days, p = .012)—reduced early, amplified at commercialization.
- H3 partially supported. H3a supported—the effectiveness gap is not significant for new mechanisms (diff = .046, p = .283) but significant for existing mechanisms (diff = .140, p = .001). H3b not supported—new-versus-existing-mechanism efficiency differences are not statistically significant.
- Net pattern: a wider gap in task effectiveness than in efficiency, which the authors read as more significant cooperation than coordination problems. Robustness checks (IPW, CEM) are broadly consistent; a supplementary patenting analysis parallels the effectiveness finding (public–private lower; b = −.221, p = .009); and the qualitative interview and 176-contract evidence converges, showing public–private contracts lack the milestone, joint-patenting, joint-committee, and termination provisions common in private–private contracts.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances the innovation collaboration literature in three primary ways: it offers an explicit counterfactual comparison between public–private and private–private collaboration that prior public–private work typically lacks; it disentangles cooperation and coordination mechanisms—often examined in separate literature streams—by simultaneously modeling task effectiveness and task efficiency outcomes; and it incorporates the contingent role of technological uncertainty (drug-development stage and technological novelty) and uses "holistic triangulation" combining IPWRA estimation with qualitative interview and contractual evidence to elaborate the relational and operational processes underpinning collaboration risk in public–private versus private–private innovation tasks for societal grand challenges.

**Practical Implication**
Public–private collaboration absorbs roughly USD 660 million in additional clinical-phase investment to advance one drug to the next stage relative to private–private collaboration; managers can reduce this performance gap by drafting public–private contracts that include governance provisions presently rare in such arrangements—development milestones (only 22% of public–private vs. 71% of private–private contracts include them), joint patenting, joint steering committees with equal representation, and conflict-resolution procedures—so that cooperation and coordination problems are mitigated even where public-disclosure and global-access requirements remain.

**Limitations**
The cooperation and coordination mechanisms—incentive alignment, governance structures, and operational processes—cannot be observed directly and are inferred from task-level outcomes supplemented by interviews and contractual text analysis. The study examines a single industry (antimicrobial drug development) and does not capture variation across cross-national institutional settings or across different types of public agencies within the same national innovation system. The dyadic-factor models incorporating complementarity, previous ties, and collaboration duration have fewer observations and findings should be interpreted with caution.

**Future Research**
Future studies could examine how cross-national institutional settings and characteristics of public agencies (e.g., centralized vs. decentralized structures such as DARPA's flat organization) affect the alignment of public- and private-sector innovation goals; investigate collaboration outcomes with different types of public partners (governmental, academic, and nonprofit organizations) given supplementary findings that academic partners excel in discovery while nonprofits perform well in clinical stages; and further disentangle the factors leading to task-level cooperation versus coordination problems, including the role of nonprofit organizations in addressing societal grand challenges.
