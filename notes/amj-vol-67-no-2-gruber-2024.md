# How Founder Human Capital and Founding Conditions Shape New Firm Performance: A Study of Necessity Entrepreneurship during Times of Economic Crisis

**Abstract**
Economic crises have profound effects on societies, motivating many individuals to launch their own firms in order to make a living. Although these firms created "out of necessity" possess few resources besides their founder's human capital, the role that this critical endowment plays in establishing a successful firm during a crisis is unclear, as existing knowledge offers diverging predictions about the value of general and specific human capital. We argue that this debate remains unresolved because we lack a holistic understanding of how each human capital type influences performance when founding conditions vary, and aim to reconcile the contrasting claims by considering how "hard" a crisis hits a given industry. Analyzing data collected from 500 founders who created firms in Greece during the Great Recession, combined with data from the Greek Statistical Office, we find that general human capital provides the greatest benefits, on average, during a crisis; yet specific human capital is more valuable in both the most favorable and the most unfavorable industry contexts. These results reveal how the value of human capital in entrepreneurship is contingent on founding conditions, and call into question existing notions of what it means to be resilient in a crisis.

**Research Question**
How do founders' general versus specific human capital endowments affect the performance of newly launched firms during an economic crisis, and how does this relationship vary across favorable and unfavorable industry founding conditions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Founder human capital type (specific vs. general); founder education type (vocational vs. general) in robustness tests
- DV(s): Yearly sales revenues generated by the new firm (in EUR)
- Moderators: Favorable industry founding conditions (top 10th percentile growth); unfavorable industry founding conditions (bottom 10th percentile decline); industry volatility (post-founding shifts)
- Controls: Founder demographics (gender, age, marital status, children), years of education, years of industry-specific experience, entrepreneurial experience, managerial experience, long-term unemployment, self-efficacy, initial investment, partner, target market/customers, alternatives considered, mainland location, industry fixed effects, firm duration

The mechanism reconciles competing predictions from human capital theory. On average, general human capital wins because conceptual breadth, abstract problem-solving, and a wider knowledge repertoire help founders navigate the high uncertainty, ambiguity, and rapidly shifting demand of a crisis—Lazear's "jack-of-all-trades" advantage. But this average masks contingency: in favorable industries, where crisis features are attenuated, specialists' ready-to-use skills, deeper supplier/distributor networks, and customer trust as domain experts let them outperform generalists. In the most unfavorable (sharply declining) industries, the authors predicted generalists would dominate—yet specialists again outperform, apparently because their narrow focus and pattern recognition let them act faster under extreme time pressure even when adaptation is needed. Resilience, the authors conclude, is also a matter of "entrepreneurial luck"—being the right person (with the right human capital) at the right place (industry) at the right time.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper resolves a long-standing specialist–generalist debate in entrepreneurship by showing that the value of general versus specific human capital is contingent on industry founding conditions during a crisis, rather than uniformly favoring one type. It urges scholars to move beyond treating economic crises as homogeneous, economy-wide shocks and to instead theorize crises at a granular industry level. It also reframes "resilience" in entrepreneurship as a person–context match contingent on luck, extending necessity-entrepreneurship theory and human capital theory (Becker, 1964; Mincer, 1974; Lazear, 2005).

**Practical Implication**
Policy-makers should expand support for unemployed individuals possessing general human capital who wish to start firms, since current government programs in Austria, Germany, and Greece tend to favor specialists with formal professional qualifications. Necessity entrepreneurs should attend closely to whether their own industry is more or less affected than the average economy, question whether prior knowledge and networks remain meaningful, and be prepared to adjust plans swiftly. Governments should also counteract negative crisis narratives and provide dedicated training on operating businesses under crisis conditions.

**Limitations**
The study examines a single geographic context (Greece) during a specific crisis (the Great Recession), which raises generalizability questions—though the authors note similarities to founders elsewhere. The empirical study benefited from the severity of the crisis, leaving the human capital–performance relationship in strongly booming economic contexts open. Despite extensive controls, results may still be subject to unobserved heterogeneity at the individual (e.g., personality, general mental ability) and firm (e.g., firm type) levels.

**Future Research**
Researchers should extend the study to other settings to assess whether contextual variation matters for entrepreneurial outcomes and investigate how strongly booming industries shape the human capital–new firm performance relationship. Future work should also further integrate notions of crisis into research on necessity entrepreneurship, since dramatic downturns suddenly create large pools of necessity entrepreneurs whose process and performance dynamics remain underexplored.
