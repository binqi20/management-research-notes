# Erring Professionals as Second Victims: Grappling with Guilt and Identity in the Aftermath of Error

**Abstract**
Applying a grounded theory approach, we investigate how professionals cope with making errors that unintentionally harm others. We purposively sampled medical clinicians to study this phenomenon, as these professionals understand themselves as "enablers of patient health." When unintentionally harming others, clinicians experienced initial feelings of guilt. Triggered by the error experience, these professionals engaged in a cognitive sensemaking process, aiming to determine the root causes of the error and evaluate their level of responsibility. In this process, clinicians considered aspects of themselves and their social environment (i.e., supervisors, organization, colleagues, and clients) as either sensemaking assets or liabilities. The self and others became sensemaking assets when helping to alleviate initial feelings of guilt, and sensemaking liabilities when aggravating them. Depending on the outcome of their sensemaking process, clinicians engaged in control-focused or escape-focused identity work. This identity work, in turn, influenced the thriving or deterioration of their future professional identity.

**Research Question**
(a) How do professionals experience and make sense of errors? (b) What is the effect, if any, of errors on professional identity?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Initial feelings of guilt (intuitive emotional reaction to unintentional harm) versus ultimate feelings of guilt (post-sensemaking judgment of responsibility); sensemaking assets versus sensemaking liabilities; control-focused versus escape-focused identity work; identity thriving versus identity deterioration.
- Triggering conditions: Error situation characterized by severity of harm (no harm, near miss, minor, major) and causality (equivocal vs. unequivocal); professional identity content (helper, service provider, problem-fixer) and centrality.
- Sensegivers / dualities: Five sources—self, supervisors, organization, colleagues, clients—each functioning as either an asset (alleviating guilt via self-compassion, support, learning culture, empathy, forgiveness) or a liability (aggravating guilt via perfectionism, depreciation, blame culture, sneer, accusation).
- Process / phases: (1) Error event; (2) Initial intuitive feelings of guilt; (3) Cognitive sensemaking with stakeholders' input shaping ultimate judgment of responsibility; (4) Identity work strategy adopted; (5) Identity thriving or deterioration outcome.
- Four identity work types (Categories A–D): A) Control-focused behavioral (learn from errors, severe harm with unequivocal causality), B) Control-focused cognitive (reject image of omnipotence, severe harm with equivocal causality), C) Escape-focused behavioral (engage in self-defense, near-miss with equivocal causality), D) Escape-focused cognitive (distance self from professional identity, minor harm with unequivocal causality).
- Emergent outcomes: Professional identity thriving (strengthening or flourishing) when assets dominate, or identity deterioration (diminishment or deprecation) when liabilities dominate.

The process unfolds when professionals unintentionally harm others, triggering guilt as an other-centered, morally laden emotion. The intensity of guilt depends on harm severity, but is then reshaped through a sensemaking process in which the self and four stakeholder groups can either alleviate (assets) or aggravate (liabilities) feelings of guilt. The configuration of error severity, causality, and the asset/liability profile of self and others channels professionals into one of four identity work strategies, which in turn drive whether the professional's future identity thrives or deteriorates.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions: (1) it elaborates a new perspective on error and trauma theory by introducing "second victims" as professionals traumatized by the unintentional harm they cause others, distinguishing guilt (other-centered) from grief (self-centered); (2) it advances sensemaking theory by demonstrating dualities in sensegiving—how the self and stakeholders can serve as either sensemaking assets or sensemaking liabilities depending on whether they alleviate or aggravate guilt; and (3) it contributes to professional identity theory by identifying four distinct identity work strategies (control-focused behavioral/cognitive and escape-focused behavioral/cognitive) that determine whether errors lead to identity thriving or deterioration.

**Practical Implication**
Supervisors play the most pivotal role in alleviating or aggravating clinicians' feelings of guilt; when supervisors react destructively, no other stakeholder can compensate for this sensemaking liability. Supervisors should therefore be trained to recognize and refrain from destructive behaviors and instead practice active listening, perspective-taking, empathy, and constructive guidance. Organizations should foster supportive learning cultures that allow professionals to make sense of errors rather than fear blame.

**Limitations**
First, some data are retrospective, though triangulation across waves and member-checking enhanced trustworthiness; direct observation would have strengthened the design. Second, the study examines single critical incidents rather than cumulative cycles of errors that may produce more self-destructive identity trajectories over time. Third, the clinician sample saw helping as a moral duty, so findings may differ for self-interested professionals or industries where status-seeking dominates.

**Future Research**
Future research should explore stakeholder reactions over time and across error magnitudes (e.g., why supervisors react empathetically to severe but destructively to minor errors), examine optimal levels of blame, and investigate how multiple sensegiving sources interact within identity networks when they diverge. Longitudinal designs could capture cumulative identity deterioration after repeated errors, and studies in non-medical industries could test whether self-interested "glory seekers" engage in different identity work tactics.
