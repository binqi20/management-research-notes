# Balancing Professional Autonomy and Managerial Goals amid Broad Technology Adoption Pressures: Intraprofessional Segmentation at a Finnish School

**Abstract**
Managers often desire to see their organization adopt new technologies but depend on autonomous professionals to use such technologies in practice. In seeking technology adoption, organizations thus must find a middle ground between professional autonomy and managerial goals. Extant scholarship has examined such middle grounds but has focused on adoptions of specific technologies. However, amid broad technology adoption pressures—expectations on organizations lacking precise prescriptions, success criteria, and sanctions—novel middle-ground approaches might emerge. Our 16-month ethnography of a Finnish school indicates that intraprofessional segmentation—specialization of work tasks within a professional community—can allow organizations to navigate technology adoption while balancing professional autonomy and managerial goals. This segmentation supported managers' and certain teachers' enthusiasm about new technologies at work, without undermining other teachers' autonomy. This segmentation emerged, persisted, and intensified through two processes. First, it emerged through differentiating practices, which allowed a divergence of what teachers said and did regarding technology. Second, this segmentation persisted and intensified through reinforcing practices, which furthered differentiation by formalizing, reorganizing, and strengthening it. Our study contributes to scholarship on occupations and technology by showing how intraprofessional segmentation can help organizations align technology with professional work amid broad technology adoption pressures.

**Research Question**
How is the tension between professional autonomy and managerial interests navigated in an organization facing broad technology adoption pressures—that is, expectations that lack precise prescriptions, concrete success criteria, and formalized sanctions?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: broad technology adoption pressures (here, Finnish national curricula reforms championing "digitalization" and the "digital leap" without specifying technologies, success criteria, or sanctions) that managers nonetheless perceive as consequential for organizational standing
- Key tension: managers' goal of adopting technology vs. teachers' protected professional autonomy in a high-status school
- Two divergent technology-related approaches within one profession: (1) reconfiguring practices = positive technological frames + additive technology use; (2) restraining practices = critical technological frames + substitutional technology use
- Differentiating practices (allowing divergence): permitting practices, including managers' emphatic framing, encouraging discretion, and peers' accepting differentiation
- Reinforcing practices (furthering and stabilizing divergence): formalizing practices (managers allocating technology-related roles and resources, e.g., digital tutors); deepening practices (external engagement by enthusiasts and hiving-off of technology-related tasks from restrainers to reconfigurers); legitimating practices (managers and peers affirming the role specialization)
- Emergent outcome: intraprofessional segmentation as an organizational middle ground that permits some adoption while preserving the autonomy of skeptical professionals; relations between differentiating and reinforcing practices are temporally overlapping, iterative, and recursive

The process model proposes that under broad technology adoption pressures, managers and professionals together generate intraprofessional segmentation through two intertwined bundles of practices. Differentiating practices first allow the open divergence of technological frames and uses within one occupational community: managers emphatically frame technology adoption as important while encouraging professional discretion, and peers explicitly accept the resulting heterogeneity rather than treating it as deviance or conflict. Reinforcing practices then formalize, reorganize, and strengthen this differentiation: managers create specialized technology roles and channel resources to enthusiastic professionals, those professionals take on external engagement and absorb hived-off technology tasks from skeptical colleagues, and both managers and peers legitimate the resulting division of labor. The two bundles run in parallel and feed back on each other, producing a relatively stable workplace settlement in which technological adoption proceeds through some professionals without coercing others.

**Data & Measures**
Design: inductive, single-case ethnography of one high-status Finnish "teaching school" (pseudonym "Mainland"). The first author conducted 16 months of fieldwork (~10 hours/week across the 2018-2019 academic year and ~5 hours/week in fall 2019), taking real-time field notes across classroom observations, teachers' meetings, field trips, and school events. The corpus is complemented by 22 ethnographic interviews at Mainland, 16 additional interviews with teachers and managers at other Finnish schools, and archival materials (national core curricula, school documents and website) used for generalizability checks. A "practice" was defined as the unit of analysis. Coding proceeded iteratively: open coding of field notes and interviews, then focused coding that coded technology use as "substitutional" versus "additive" and technology frames as "positive" versus "critical," clustering these into reconfiguring, restraining, and permitting (differentiating) practices and formalizing, deepening, and legitimating (reinforcing) practices. Analytic induction (Becker, 1998) and grounded-theory principles (Glaser & Strauss, 1967) guided theory building, with descriptive and interpretive validity checked via member checks (Lincoln & Guba, 1985). The design is interpretive rather than causal-identifying; no statistical estimation or identification strategy is claimed.

**Key Findings**
Amid broad technology adoption pressures, intraprofessional segmentation—the specialization of work tasks within one professional community—emerged as an organizational middle ground that let managers and some teachers pursue new technologies without undermining other teachers' autonomy. This segmentation emerged, persisted, and intensified through two intertwined bundles of practices. First, differentiating practices—reconfiguring and restraining, held together by permitting—allowed an openly accepted divergence in teachers' technological frames and uses. Second, reinforcing practices—formalizing (role and resource allocation, e.g., "digital tutors"), deepening (external engagement and hiving-off of technology tasks from restrainers to reconfigurers), and legitimating (affirming role specialization)—formalized, reorganized, and strengthened that differentiation into a persistent segmentation. The two bundles were temporally overlapping, iterative, and recursive rather than strictly sequential. Most participants—managers, reconfiguring teachers, and restraining teachers—were largely satisfied, and managers viewed the resulting heterogeneity as beneficial rather than as failed implementation, though the arrangement was not frictionless (e.g., unused laptops and mild annoyance among colleagues hived-off to). Findings are interpretive and specific to this single high-status case; the paper claims no statistical generalization.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces broad technology adoption pressures as a distinct organizational condition (lacking precise prescriptions, success criteria, and formalized sanctions) and theorizes intraprofessional segmentation as a novel middle-ground response that prior literature has overlooked because it focused on the implementation of specific technologies and on cross-occupational rather than within-profession variation. By unpacking differentiating and reinforcing practices, the paper extends the sociology of professions (Abbott, 1988; Bucher & Strauss, 1961) by showing that intraprofessional segments need not be a source of conflict but can be actively permitted, formalized, and legitimated by managers and peers, repositioning intraoccupational variation as a supportive rather than adversarial feature of professional organization.

**Practical Implication**
Managers facing broad pressures to adopt expansive technological categories (e.g., digital transformation, AI) need not pursue uniform adoption across all professionals. Instead, they can permit and reinforce specialization within a single profession—creating dedicated roles and resources for enthusiastic professionals, accepting that others will continue more conservative practices, and legitimating both choices—to achieve meaningful technology uptake without triggering the resistance and demoralization that come with coercive implementation in autonomy-protective settings such as schools, hospitals, and other professional organizations.

**Limitations**
The findings come from a single high-status Finnish "teaching school" run privately under a university, which may make intraprofessional segmentation more available than in lower-status or more tightly regulated organizations and may bound generalizability. The school setting also features less interprofessional competition than hospitals or engineering firms, and the relatively similar status of teachers within the school removed the kind of status-based dynamics that drive hiving-off in other studies. The 16-month observation window cannot speak to whether the apparently functional segmentation remains supportive over the longer term or eventually becomes adversarial.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine the relationship between organizational status and the likelihood of intraprofessional segmentation emerging as a response to broad technology adoption pressures, and study how segmentation evolves where higher- and lower-status professional groups work together on new technologies. Researchers could also follow intraprofessional segmentation longitudinally to see whether it stabilizes, becomes adversarial, or produces broader occupational change such as jurisdictional battles or spin-offs of new occupations. Finally, scholars should pay closer attention to within-profession variance in technology use and to the explicit permitting practices through which such divergence is legitimated.
