# Job Insecurity and Well-Being: Integrating Life History and Transactional Stress Theories

**Abstract**
The current research proposes and tests a novel model explaining how job insecurity shapes well-being and has consequences for stratification and inequality. I draw on evolutionary life history theory, which proposes that growing up in a poorer versus wealthier environment impacts the sense of control people feel when exposed to threat in adulthood. I integrate this perspective with transactional stress theory to propose that job insecurity has a disproportionately negative effect on employees from poorer backgrounds, leading to lower engagement and higher emotional exhaustion among such employees, while those from wealthier backgrounds are buffered against these effects. These responses to job insecurity, in turn, amplify job loss risk for employees from poorer backgrounds, regardless of employees' current job or financial situation. A preregistered, multisource, five-wave longitudinal study conducted at the height of the COVID-19 crisis in India found support for these predictions. A follow-up quasi-experiment conducted in India and the United States replicated the effects on engagement and exhaustion. The impact of job insecurity on well-being is stratified and acts as a mechanism that reproduces childhood inequalities.

**Research Question**
How does perceived job insecurity differentially shape well-being (engagement and emotional exhaustion) and downstream job loss risk as a function of employees' childhood financial standing, and through what psychological mechanism (sense of control)? Can job insecurity act as an invisible mechanism that reproduces childhood inequalities in adulthood, even when employees from poorer and wealthier backgrounds occupy comparable jobs and current financial situations?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: The effect of perceived job insecurity on engagement is moderated by employee childhood background, such that job insecurity more strongly undermines engagement among workers from poorer (compared to wealthier) backgrounds.
- H2: The moderated effect of perceived job insecurity on engagement is mediated by sense of control.
- H3: The effect of perceived job insecurity on emotional exhaustion is moderated by employee childhood background, such that job insecurity more strongly promotes emotional exhaustion among workers from poorer (compared to wealthier) backgrounds.
- H4: The moderated effect of perceived job insecurity on emotional exhaustion is mediated by sense of control.
- H5: Perceived job insecurity more strongly amplifies leader-rated job loss risk among workers from poorer (compared to wealthier) backgrounds, via its effect on sense of control and, in turn, engagement.
- H6: Perceived job insecurity more strongly amplifies leader-rated job loss risk among workers from poorer (compared to wealthier) backgrounds, via its effect on sense of control and, in turn, emotional exhaustion.
- Preregistered exploratory prediction (not a formal hypothesis): at the high end of childhood financial standing, job insecurity may instead raise sense of control and well-being (higher engagement, lower exhaustion), producing an energizing response.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived job insecurity (Study 1: longitudinal time-variant measure combining Vander Elst et al. (2014) and Hellgren et al. (1999) scales; Study 2: manipulated via guided reflection task contrasting job insecurity vs. job security conditions)
- DV(s): Engagement (Schaufeli, Bakker, & Salanova, 2006); emotional exhaustion (Wharton, 1993); leader-rated subordinate job loss risk (Study 1 only; adapted from Vander Elst et al., 2014)
- Mediators: Sense of control (Lachman & Weaver, 1998 plus Mirowsky & Ross, 1991)
- Moderators: Employee childhood financial standing (Griskevicius, Delton et al., 2011; Griskevicius, Tybur et al., 2011)
- Controls: Employee current financial standing (and its interaction with job insecurity), years of leadership experience, number of employees fired due to crisis, wave

The integrated mechanism predicts that the early-life programming of the stress response system (sensitized by harshness during childhood, proxied by family financial standing) conditions how individuals appraise threat in adulthood. Under salient job insecurity, employees from poorer backgrounds experience a drop in sense of control, producing lower engagement and higher emotional exhaustion; these well-being deficits are then read by supervisors as signs of declining contributions, amplifying job loss risk. Employees from wealthier backgrounds are buffered, and at the very high end of childhood financial standing job insecurity even raises sense of control, producing an energizing response (higher engagement, lower exhaustion) that lowers job loss risk. Differences only emerge when job insecurity is salient, consistent with the conditioned-stress-response logic.

**Data & Measures**
- Data: Two preregistered studies. Study 1 is a multisource, five-wave longitudinal field survey of 449 white-collar employees nested under 147 supervisors (2,207 observations) in small and medium-sized Indian firms during the COVID-19 crisis, surveyed bi-weekly over 2.5 months. Study 2 is a preregistered quasi-experiment with 1,651 Qualtrics Panel employees (818 United States; 833 India).
- Job insecurity (IV): Study 1 combined a four-item Vander Elst, De Witte, & De Cuyper (2014) scale and a three-item Hellgren, Sverke, & Isaksson (1999) scale (a = .79); Study 2 manipulated it via a guided reflection / episodic-recall task (writing about factors making one's job insecure vs. secure), with the same scale used as a manipulation check.
- Sense of control (mediator): Lachman & Weaver (1998) four items plus the positively worded Mirowsky & Ross (1991) items (a = .87 in Study 1).
- Engagement (DV): nine-item Schaufeli, Bakker, & Salanova (2006) scale (a = .91).
- Emotional exhaustion (DV): six-item Wharton (1993) scale (a = .91).
- Leader-rated subordinate job loss risk (DV, Study 1 only): supervisors rated their willingness to let go each subordinate if the situation demanded it, adapted from Vander Elst et al. (2014) (a = .85).
- Employee childhood (and current) financial standing (moderator / control): established life-history measures (Griskevicius, Delton et al., 2011; Griskevicius, Tybur et al., 2011).
- Identification: Study 1 estimates three-level multilevel linear regressions (cases within employees within supervisors) with person-centered time-variant predictors and grand-mean-centered moderator and controls, plus a moderated path analysis with 5,000 bootstrap resamples for moderated indirect effects; the design is longitudinal and largely associational (the author reports reciprocal-effect tests suggesting job insecurity was exogenously shaped in this sample). Study 2 is a quasi-experiment that manipulates job insecurity (random assignment to insecurity vs. security condition) while childhood financial standing is measured, estimated with structural equation modeling and 5,000 bootstrap resamples, conducted to enhance internal validity.

**Key Findings**
Study 1 (longitudinal field study):
- At the sample mean of childhood financial standing, job insecurity had a negative main effect on engagement (b = -0.14, p < .001) and a positive effect on exhaustion (b = 0.20, p < .001).
- Job insecurity interacted with childhood financial standing on engagement (b = 0.14, p < .001) and exhaustion (b = -0.19, p < .001): among workers from poorer backgrounds (1 SD below mean) job insecurity lowered engagement (b = -0.26, p < .001) and raised exhaustion (b = 0.36, p < .001), whereas at higher childhood financial standing (1 SD above mean) both effects were non-significant (engagement b = -0.01, p = .726; exhaustion b = 0.03, p = .392). Supports H1 and H3.
- These effects ran through sense of control: job insecurity lowered sense of control among poorer workers (b = -0.29, p < .001) but not wealthier workers (b = -0.05, p = .158); sense of control in turn raised engagement (b = 0.20) and lowered exhaustion (b = -0.29), and the indirect effects were significant only among poorer workers. Supports H2 and H4 (H1-H4 supported).
- Engagement (b = -0.13, p < .001) and exhaustion (b = 0.11, p < .001) predicted leader-rated job loss risk; the moderated indirect effect of job insecurity on job loss risk (via sense of control, then engagement and exhaustion) was significant among poorer workers but not wealthier workers. Supports H5 and H6.
- Preregistered exploratory analyses: above the top of the childhood-financial-standing range, job insecurity's effects reversed (raising engagement, lowering exhaustion, and reducing job loss risk) — an energizing response among the most affluent.

Study 2 (quasi-experiment; job loss risk not measured):
- The manipulation raised felt job insecurity (b = 0.45, p < .001).
- Replicating Study 1, the manipulation interacted with childhood financial standing on engagement (b = 0.13, p < .001) and exhaustion (b = -0.14, p = .003): among poorer workers job insecurity lowered engagement (b = -0.19, p = .001) and raised exhaustion (b = 0.19, p = .009), while among wealthier workers (1 SD above mean) the engagement effect turned positive (b = 0.11, p = .039) and the exhaustion effect was non-significant (b = -0.13, p = .069). Mediation via sense of control replicated, supporting H1-H4. Participant country (India vs. United States) was a non-significant moderator.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces a novel evolutionary life history perspective to organizational research on job insecurity and well-being, identifying childhood financial standing as a major individual difference that stratifies how job insecurity impacts well-being and, through it, job loss risk — even among employees in comparable current jobs and financial situations. By integrating life history theory with transactional stress theory, it expands the explanatory power of extant job insecurity models and reframes job insecurity as an invisible mechanism that reproduces childhood inequalities. The integration also opens organizational stress research more broadly to life history theory, beyond its prior focus on temporal-discounting behaviors, by showing that conditioned stress responses shape reactions to a key work-related stressor.

**Practical Implication**
The findings highlight a hitherto invisible and likely large-scale cost of job insecurity for public policy: rising precariousness systematically disadvantages employees born into poorer families, undermining performance-relevant well-being and amplifying their job loss risk in ways that conflict with core values of meritocracy and socioeconomic mobility. Within organizations, the supplementary analyses suggest that managerial "opportunity framing" — emphasizing opportunities arising from contextual adversity — can attenuate the problematic effects of perceived job insecurity on engagement among workers from poorer backgrounds, although its buffering effect on emotional exhaustion was not significant.

**Limitations**
The study uses self-reported measures of childhood financial standing as a proxy for childhood environmental harshness, and Study 1 was conducted in the specific context of the COVID-19 crisis among small and medium-sized firms in India, raising questions about generalizability. Although Study 2 replicated effects in India and the United States, broader cross-cultural validation in non-Western contexts is limited, and life history theory has historically relied on relatively elite samples. Multilevel structural equation modeling could not converge for Study 1 given the complexity of the data, and the leader-rated job loss risk measure captures supervisors' willingness to let employees go rather than realized terminations.

**Future Research**
The author calls for broader application of life history theory to other workplace stressors beyond job insecurity, to identify employees who may be disproportionately burdened and to surface attenuating factors. Cross-cultural research outside Western contexts is highlighted as an important next step, given limited data on life history processes in non-Western settings. Further work should examine specific life events that may modify or reinforce the early-life imprint of childhood financial standing, and develop additional managerial and policy-level interventions (beyond opportunity framing) that strengthen sense of control under stress, especially for workers from poorer backgrounds.
