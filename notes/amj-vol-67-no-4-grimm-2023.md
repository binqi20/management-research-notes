# Collaborating on the Edge of Failure: Frame Alignment across Multiple Interaction Arenas in Multi-Stakeholder Partnerships

**Abstract**
One of the greatest challenges of multi-stakeholder partnerships lies in forging a shared understanding and obtaining and sustaining commitment among parties representing different interests and goals. While studies have emphasized the importance of developing shared frames for enabling collaboration and collective action through frame alignment, scant attention has been paid to how stakeholder representatives can attain commitment from their constituents "back home" to frames negotiated on their behalf. Our longitudinal process study explores how participants in the German Partnership for Sustainable Textiles successfully confronted the challenge of aligning frames across multiple interaction arenas, highlighting how failing to tackle this "two-table problem" can risk partnership collapse. Our process model captures how back-and-forth interactions enabled the stretching of shared frames across interaction arenas, thereby propelling the partnership from near collapse to deepened commitments. While stretching frames heightens the risk of frame break, our analysis shows how such iterative ongoing efforts are essential for deepening commitments and advancing collaboration. We thus contribute to framing theory by highlighting how frame alignment can be achieved across multiple interaction arenas by "collaborating on the edge of failure." We further contribute to scholarship on multiparty collaboration by unpacking the multi-table negotiation dynamics that help explain collaborative outcomes.

**Research Question**
How do multiple interaction arenas affect frame alignment, and how can frame alignment be navigated across multiple interaction arenas in multi-stakeholder partnerships?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper. This is an inductive, longitudinal process study (Langley, 1999); the authors develop an emergent process model of frame alignment across multiple interaction arenas rather than testing a priori hypotheses, and state no numbered formal propositions.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: shared interactional frame; two-table problem; red lines; frame break; frame stretching; collaboration on the edge of failure
- Three within-arena framing laminations: relational framing (inquiring, re-evaluating, identifying — shifting attributional orientation from "enemies" to allies); issue framing (extension of interpretive scope from subjective to intersubjective understanding); process framing (reframing the ongoing interaction from antagonistic to collaborative)
- Three cross-arena framing processes: reciprocal frame stretching (through reciprocal concessions between negotiators); integrative frame stretching (through integrating constituents/experts into expert working groups); bilateral frame stretching (through cross-table scanning by representatives)
- Triggering condition: near-collapse / repeated frame breaks at "homecoming" when constituents reject negotiated frames
- Phases: six topic-framing cycles spanning Partnership phase (2014) through Action Plan 1.0, Action Plan 2.0, three re-adjustments, and COVID-related adjustments
- Emergent outcome: deepened commitments to ambitious goals previously rejected, despite waning external pressure

The process model shows that shared frames negotiated in one interaction arena (e.g., the Steering Committee) cannot simply be "transported" to constituent group arenas. Instead, frame alignment requires iterative back-and-forth interactions in which negotiators progressively laminate relational, issue, and process meanings onto a shared frame and then "stretch" that frame across arenas through reciprocal, integrative, and bilateral processes. Collective recognition that the partnership is "on the edge of failure" generates willingness to cross red lines and reach the highest common denominator rather than the lowest. Frame stretching always carries the risk of frame break, but iterative ongoing efforts are what propel collaboration from near-collapse to deepened, more substantive commitment.

**Data & Measures**
Data corpus: a six-year longitudinal process study (Langley, 1999) of the German Partnership for Sustainable Textiles, with multiphase data collection from November 2015 to November 2021 and the partnership's evolution "traced backward" to its establishment in early 2014. The primary data source comprises 92 interviews (81 semi-structured formal and 11 informal) with 48 individuals, up to five times each; most were audio-recorded and lasted between 18 and 102 minutes. Respondents spanned NGOs (n = 22), the federal government (n = 11), trade associations (n = 9), standards organizations (n = 5), trade unions (n = 3), MNC (n = 24) and SME (n = 9) textile-industry representatives, and advisory members from academia (n = 7) and the media (n = 2); the 12 Steering Committee members were interviewed repeatedly, complemented by interviews with key regular members at the stakeholder-group level. These were supplemented by non-participant observations (roughly 33 hours, including a November 2015 "Rana Plaza—Never Again!" workshop in Dortmund) and 3,120 pages of publicly available documents and archival records (published August 2010 to November 2021), used to construct an event chronology and to cross-check interview accounts. Analysis applied a combination of inductive process-analysis strategies (Langley, 1999) in four steps: (1) constructing a process chronology and visual map from an initial round of open coding in NVivo, yielding six interconnected "topic-framing cycles" over six phases; (2) mapping interaction arenas and interactants, relating the case to the negotiation literature's "two-table problem" and to the informants' own terms "homecoming" and "red lines"; (3) tracing frame alignment within and across arenas by coding first-order codes (e.g., "getting to know one another") as indications of relational, issue, and process framing, taking the "interaction traces" between representatives as the unit of data, and identifying three cross-arena "frame stretching" processes; and (4) building the theoretical process model, illustrated with "composite vignettes." The design is interpretive and inductive; no measurement scales, statistical estimation, or causal-identification strategy is claimed.

**Key Findings**
The study surfaces a process model of how frame alignment is navigated across multiple interaction arenas, explaining how the Textiles Partnership was maintained after near collapse and how the parties eventually committed to sustainability goals they had previously rejected, notably after public pressure following the 2013 Rana Plaza disaster had largely subsided. The turnaround unfolded in four parts. First, the partnership's near collapse arose from the difficulty of aligning interactional frames across multiple arenas: Steering Committee representatives failed to recruit their "back home" constituents to the shared frame negotiated on their behalf. Second, achieving frame alignment within an arena entails cumulative "laminations" not only of issue framing but also of relational framing (shifting attributional orientation from treating counterparts as "enemies" toward seeing "a person just like me") and process framing (reframing the ongoing interaction from antagonistic to collaborative). Third, "frame break" can ensue at "homecoming" when these laminations are not built up among constituents, who then reject the compromises and crossings of "red lines" made on their behalf. Fourth, alignment is sustained across arenas through three interrelated cross-arena "frame stretching" processes: reciprocal (mutual concessions between negotiators), integrative (drawing constituents and experts into expert working groups, where they become recognized "frame makers"), and bilateral (cross-table scanning for where frames already overlap), which render frames more "elastic" so that meanings can be carried into new interaction arenas. The central directional finding is that back-and-forth interactions enabled the stretching of shared frames across interaction arenas, propelling the partnership from near collapse to deepened commitments: although stretching heightens the risk of frame break, such iterative ongoing efforts are essential for deepening commitments and advancing collaboration. When negotiating parties collectively recognized that they were "collaborating on the edge of failure," this awareness of risk helped move them from rejecting compromises toward agreeing on the "highest common denominator." The authors conclude that external societal and political pressures alone are not sufficient to sustain such commitment; the negotiation dynamics unfolding across interaction arenas are what preserved and advanced the collaboration.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to framing theory by conceptualizing frame alignment as an ongoing, iterative, multi-arena process in which frames are not transported but stretched, becoming more "elastic" through cumulative laminations of relational, issue, and process framing. It foregrounds the agency of audiences and the relational dynamics among those who "do" the framing, showing that frames are grounded in interpersonal relationships rather than free-floating cognitive packages. It also contributes to scholarship on multi-stakeholder partnerships and inter-organizational collaboration by unpacking the previously underexplored "two-table problem" and revealing how negotiation dynamics, rather than external pressures alone, sustain collaboration "on the edge of failure."

**Practical Implication**
For conveners and participants of multi-stakeholder partnerships, sustaining collaboration requires deliberate investment in informal relational interactions (e.g., off-table meals, "time outs") that build personal bonds enabling representatives to move beyond antagonistic framings. Negotiators must continuously cultivate buy-in from their "back home" constituents through integrative working groups and cross-table scanning rather than treating partnership agreements as one-shot deals. Conveners should expect non-linear progress: maintaining a partnership requires accepting the risk of operating "on the edge of failure," because the willingness to cross red lines often emerges only when collapse appears imminent.

**Limitations**
The authors could not directly observe Steering Committee meetings due to confidentiality, relying instead on interpretive interviewing that may be subject to hindsight bias and may miss interactional dynamics that ethnographic methods could capture. The study examines only two main interaction arenas (partnership and stakeholder group), leaving the intra-organizational arena (e.g., conflicts between CSR and procurement within firms) outside scope. The Textiles Partnership was government-convened, so the role of government as a convener is a boundary condition limiting generalizability to fully private, non-state-sponsored partnerships.

**Future Research**
Future work should apply close-up observational methods or conversation analysis to capture additional cross-arena framing dynamics. Researchers could extend the model to a third negotiation table inside participants' home organizations, examining how CSR managers function as internal activists who "sell" frames to colleagues. Comparative studies of MSPs with and without government sponsorship and of varying degrees of representative authorization and accountability could illuminate how external context shapes framing strategies. Further work could probe the emotional and identity challenges faced by individuals navigating between arenas and how representatives cope with frame shifts at a personal level while remaining "true" to their constituents.
