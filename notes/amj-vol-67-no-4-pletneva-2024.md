# Turning Work into a Refuge: Job Crafting as Coping with Personal, Grief-Inducing Events

**Abstract**
Building on an inductive, qualitative study of employees who experienced grief-inducing events such as bereavement or breakup, this paper explores how and with what consequences work becomes a refuge from grief-inducing experiences in people's lives. Using the results of 68 in-depth interviews, I develop an inductive model that shows that grieving employees not only find refuge in work but can also turn their work into a refuge for themselves. In particular, the model explicates how people turn work into a refuge by engaging in job crafting behavior. I also show perceived consequences of this process that occur in both work and life domains. Such consequences tend to be positive, yet those who work significantly harder also experience negative consequences. This paper advances theorizing on grief in organizations and job crafting.

**Research Question**
How, and with what consequences, can work become a refuge from grief-inducing experiences in people's lives?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Personal grief-inducing event (bereavement or breakup) that causes routine disruption, event-related feelings, and vulnerability
- Unmet needs generated: Need for a mental break, need for emotional balance, need for support
- Facilitating factors: Lack of well-established coping mechanisms; high importance of work; positive prior work experiences
- Job crafting tactics (promotion-oriented):
  - Job expanding (broadening tasks, volunteering, learning new skills)
  - Task fulfillment adjusting (more effort, reprioritizing, inventing new methods)
  - Interactions expanding (more interactions, more social events with coworkers)
  - Interactions enriching (opening up and sharing personal information, deepening interaction quality)
- Mechanisms by which job crafting transforms work into a refuge:
  - Getting distracted from event-related thoughts
  - Experiencing emotional counterpoint to event-generated feelings
  - Feeling supported
- Emergent outcome: Cognitive reframing of work as refuge ("anchor," "crutch," "vacation")
- Consequences (in work and life domains, positive and negative)

Grieving employees become active actors who turn work into a refuge by engaging in promotion-oriented job crafting behavior to address event-generated needs. Through expanding tasks, adjusting task fulfillment, and expanding and enriching interactions, work begins to function as a distraction, an emotional counterpoint, and a source of support. This process eventually leads to a cognitive reframing of work itself, with consequences spanning both domains: positive (professional growth, organizational commitment, avoidance of depression and substance abuse, expanded friendship network, personal growth) and, when job crafting is overly intense and prolonged beyond three months, negative (decreased quality of life and delayed processing of the event).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions. First, it returns agency to grieving employees in the literature on grief in organizations by showing they not only find refuge in work passively but can actively turn work into a refuge through job crafting. Second, it expands job crafting theory by introducing a nonwork-related antecedent (personal grief-inducing events) that crosses the work-life boundary, refining the assumption that job crafting is triggered solely by work-related stimuli. Third, it adds balance to job crafting scholarship by uncovering negative consequences of promotion-oriented job crafting (decreased quality of life, delayed event processing) alongside the typically emphasized positive ones.

**Practical Implication**
Employees can use job crafting tactics to cope with grief-inducing events and may even benefit from doing so, but should be aware that intense, prolonged immersion in work can have harmful consequences. Managers can facilitate the work-as-refuge experience by creating positive work environments before crises occur, ensuring employees have agency over their jobs and access to job crafting opportunities, and proactively addressing overly intense and lengthy job crafting that may signal underlying personal struggles.

**Limitations**
The sample focuses on employees whose work was unrelated to the loss event, who returned to stable workplaces, and who believed work helped them cope; this excludes more complex situations where work and the event are entangled or where work was experienced as primarily burdensome. The sample is largely Western (mostly US, with Dutch, French, and Russian participants), so cultural variation is not examined. The study does not deeply analyze how choice of job crafting tactic varies with event context.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine job crafting in non-grief contexts, including stressful but emotionally positive events such as marriage, and explore antecedents of job crafting beyond work-related triggers. Researchers could investigate cultural differences in how work-as-refuge is used, factors affecting the choice between distraction-oriented and processing-oriented job crafting tactics, and the role of remote/hybrid work arrangements. Identity-related factors (integrators vs. segmentors) and additional boundary conditions for negative outcomes also warrant investigation.
