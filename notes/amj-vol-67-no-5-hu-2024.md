# Faking It with the Boss’s Jokes? Leader Humor Quantity, Follower Surface Acting, and Power Distance

**Abstract**
Most scholarly work concludes that leader humor positively affects emotional outcomes for followers. However, two interrelated issues with past research make this view incomplete: (a) studies often conflate the act of expressing humor with the humor’s effectiveness, and (b) existing research overlooks follower emotion regulation. In addressing these issues, we generate and test new theory that challenges the theoretical consensus, highlighting why and when leader humor expression has emotional costs for followers. Specifically, we theorize that leader humor quantity, irrespective of its quality, puts pressure on followers to engage in “surface acting” to fake or exaggerate positive emotions. We further propose that these surface acting responses are more likely to occur when followers hold high power distance values, such that followers more willingly comply with expectations to display positive emotions in response to leader humor expression. This increase in surface acting then leads to more emotional exhaustion and less job satisfaction for followers. Results from three studies—incorporating a field experiment, a laboratory experiment, and a multi-wave field study—provide support for these hypotheses. We close with a discussion of how our findings provide a unique counterpoint regarding the effects of leader humor on follower emotional outcomes.

**Research Question**
Why and when does leader humor quantity (LH quantity) — irrespective of its quality — increase follower surface acting and, through this emotion-regulation pathway, impair follower emotional exhaustion and job satisfaction; and how does follower power distance moderate these effects?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: Leader humor quantity increases follower surface acting (positive main effect).
- H2: Leader humor quantity has a positive indirect effect on follower emotional exhaustion, mediated by follower surface acting.
- H3: Leader humor quantity has a negative indirect effect on follower job satisfaction, mediated by follower surface acting.
- H4: Follower power distance moderates the positive effect of leader humor quantity on follower surface acting, such that the effect is stronger for high-power-distance followers and weaker for low-power-distance followers (first-stage moderation).
- H5: Leader humor quantity's (a) positive indirect effect on follower emotional exhaustion and (b) negative indirect effect on follower job satisfaction, via surface acting, are moderated by follower power distance, such that the indirect effects are stronger (weaker) for followers with high (low) power distance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV: Leader humor quantity (LH quantity) — the frequency or extent to which a leader expresses humor (jokes, puns, stories, wit) intended to amuse the follower, conceptualized as an expressive behavior distinct from leader humor quality (LH quality).
- DV(s): Follower emotional exhaustion (feeling overextended and depleted) and follower job satisfaction (positive appraisal of one's job).
- Mediator: Follower surface acting — masking, faking, or suppressing felt emotions to display positive emotions (amusement, happiness, interest) toward the leader.
- Moderator: Follower power distance — the extent to which followers accept unequal power distribution between leaders and followers (first-stage moderator on LH quantity to surface acting).
- Controls: Leader humor quality, follower positive affect, deep acting, leader-member exchange (LMX), charismatic leadership, follower/leader demographics.

Drawing on emotional labor theory and the experience of power, the authors argue that leader humor signals a strong positive display rule (laughing, smiling, amusement) that followers — as lower-power actors dependent on leaders for resources and acceptance — feel obliged to meet. Because humor is unexpected and individualized, frequent expression creates frequent discrepancies between followers' naturally occurring affect and the expected display, making surface acting (rather than deep acting) the available response-focused regulation strategy. Surface acting then drains cognitive resources and produces inauthenticity, increasing exhaustion and reducing satisfaction. The effect is amplified for high-power-distance followers because (a) compliance with leader display standards is more instrumental to their goals and (b) leader humor more sharply violates their counter-normative expectation that leaders maintain social distance.

**Data & Measures**
The moderated-mediation model is tested across three studies.
- Study 1 (one-week field experiment, 88 leader-follower dyads, Southern China): Leader humor quantity (IV) was manipulated by randomly assigning leaders to an LH quantity intervention vs. a control condition (coded 1/0), with a leader-rated modified Cooper et al. (2018) three-item scale as a manipulation check (α = .90). Follower surface acting (mediator) used an adapted discrete-emotions emotional labor scale (Hu & Shi, 2015) capturing how often followers display amusement, happiness, and interest they do not genuinely feel (α = .91). Emotional exhaustion (DV) used a six-item Wharton (1993) scale (α = .94); job satisfaction (DV) a modified three-item scale (Tepper, 2000) (α = .92). Follower power distance (moderator) used three items from Dorfman and Howell (1988) (α = .62). Controls: follower positive affect (α = .87) and deep acting (α = .93). Identification strategy in the paper's terms: a field experiment with random assignment; ANOVA plus Mplus 8.5 path models (Hayes Models 4 and 7) with 10,000-resample bias-corrected bootstrapping for indirect effects.
- Study 2 (pre-registered laboratory experiment, 198 participants nested in 49 focus groups, northeastern United States): a 2 (LH quantity: low vs. high) × 2 (power distance: low vs. high) between-subjects design in which a trained actor-moderator delivered scripted humor and power-distance norms; both LH quantity and power distance were manipulated, with LH quality and power distance manipulation checks. Analyzed with cluster-robust standard errors in Mplus 8.5 to account for the nested (clustered) groups.
- Study 3 (pre-registered three-wave field study, 126 employees, a Southern China technology firm): LH quantity measured with the Cooper et al. (2018) three-item scale; power distance with the six-item Dorfman and Howell (1988) scale; surface acting with a modified version of the Study 1 scale; outcomes drawn from both employee surveys and company pulse-survey records; controls include LH quality, LMX, positive affect, charismatic leadership, and demographics. The paper describes this study as correlational (associational), not experimental.

**Key Findings**
Leader humor quantity had no significant main effects on follower well-being outcomes; its effects on emotional exhaustion and job satisfaction operated indirectly through surface acting.
- Study 1 (n = 88 dyads): H1 supported — LH quantity increased surface acting (ANOVA F(1, 86) = 5.50, p = .021; regression b = 0.27, p = .005). Surface acting positively predicted emotional exhaustion (b = 0.43, p < .001) and negatively predicted job satisfaction (b = -0.24, p = .013); H2 and H3 supported (indirect effects via surface acting). H4 supported (LH quantity × power distance on surface acting, b = 0.31, p = .016; positive at high power distance, non-significant at low). H5a and H5b supported.
- Study 2 (n = 198 in 49 groups): H1 supported (b = 0.30, p < .001). Surface acting positively predicted emotional exhaustion (b = 0.34, p < .001) and negatively predicted job satisfaction (b = -0.15, p = .029); H2 and H3 supported. H4 supported (interaction b = 0.36, p < .001; positive at high power distance, non-significant at low). H5a and H5b supported.
- Study 3 (n = 126): H1 supported (b = 0.32, p = .002). H2 supported (surface acting related to company-recorded emotional exhaustion, b = 0.16, p = .037), but H3 not supported (surface acting did not relate to job satisfaction). H4 supported (interaction b = 0.16, p = .015). H5a supported for both the company-recorded and employee-survey measures of emotional exhaustion; H5b not supported for job satisfaction.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the dominant positive view of leader humor on follower well-being by theoretically and empirically distinguishing leader humor quantity from leader humor quality, identifying surface acting as a novel emotion-regulation mechanism through which expressing humor frequently can backfire, and positioning follower power distance as a key moderator of when LH quantity is most damaging. Beyond leader humor research, the work contributes to emotional labor theory by showing that a positively intended leader behavior — not just negative interpersonal events — can elevate surface acting, and to the broader humor literature by adding "how much" and "to whom" as risks alongside the traditional "what kind."

**Practical Implication**
Leaders should scale back the frequency of humor expression and adopt a "less is more" approach — focusing on fewer, higher-reward humor attempts to capture the benefits of humor quality while avoiding the emotional-labor costs of humor quantity, especially because followers are often surface-acting positive feedback that masks the harm. Managers should also calibrate humor to follower power-distance values: high-power-distance followers are more likely to fake positive responses and bear well-being costs, so managers can use existing surveys (e.g., Kirkman et al., 2009 scale) to gauge follower values before expressing humor.

**Limitations**
Sample sizes were modest in Study 1 (n = 88) and Study 3 (n = 126), reducing power to detect direct effects of LH quantity on well-being and possibly affecting measure reliability; the Dorfman-Howell (1988) power distance measure had Cronbach's alpha below the 0.70 threshold in Studies 1 and 3, although within the range reported in prior work. Study 3 was correlational, could not rule out nesting effects given anonymized data, and relied on employees as the source for all model variables, raising common-method-bias concerns despite time separation and supplemental tests.

**Future Research**
Future studies should examine other boundary conditions (e.g., perceived appropriateness of leader humor, follower's own sense of humor, group humor norms, humor styles such as self-enhancing vs. self-defeating) and additional mechanisms beyond surface acting and positive affect — such as follower appreciation of humor attempts — to reconcile the joint effects of LH quantity and LH quality. Within-person/event-level designs that track multiple humor episodes throughout a day, larger and cross-cultural samples with stronger power-distance measures, and disentangling leader power from follower power distance would help specify the optimal ratio of high- versus low-quality humor and identify factors (e.g., leaders explicitly acknowledging display rules) that mitigate the harms of LH quantity for high-power-distance followers.
