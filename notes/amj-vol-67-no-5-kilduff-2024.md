# Hiding and Seeking Knowledge-Providing Ties from Rivals: A Strategic Perspective on Network Perceptions

**Abstract**
Rivalry is endemic in society and organizations, fueling competitive intentions and behaviors. According to social network theory, rivalry emerges among people who, like siblings, have many of the same connections to others. For this structurally equivalent rivalry to have its effects, the individual must see the other person as a rival. We ask whether, in the context of competition, people seek to identify the knowledge providers of their rivals while striving to hide their own knowledge providers from perceived rivals. We conducted two experiments that showed, for the first time, that structural equivalence does induce feelings of rivalry and does lead people to take action with respect to perceived rivals, namely to hide and seek knowledge providers. Our analysis of timeseparated social network and outcome data from all 73 employees in the headquarters of a chemical company found support for these patterns of hiding and seeking in relation to perceived rivals. We also found limited evidence that career outcomes may be influenced by individuals' success in hiding and seeking. Bringing together research on rivalry and network cognition, we provide a new approach to the strategic deployment of deception and detection in social networks.

**Research Question**
Do individuals in organizations hide their knowledge-providing ties from those they perceive as rivals, and do they actively seek to detect the knowledge-providing ties of those rivals — and does success in this hiding and seeking translate into career advantage? More foundationally, does the perception of structural equivalence engender rivalry and motivate action toward perceived rivals?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
The two experiments test underlying assumptions (that perceived structural equivalence engenders rivalry, and that people take action toward structural equivalents) rather than numbered hypotheses; the four formal hypotheses are tested in the field study.
- H1a (Seeking hypothesis): The more an individual considers a coworker as a rival, the fewer the errors of omission in the individual's perception of the coworker's knowledge-providing relationships.
- H1b (Hiding hypothesis): The more an individual is considered a rival by a coworker, the more errors of omission there are in the individual's perception of the coworker's knowledge-providing relationships.
- H2a: The more that individuals minimize errors of omission in their perceptions of their rivals' knowledge-providing relationships, the more likely it is that individuals will achieve favorable career outcomes.
- H2b: The more that individuals increase errors of omission in their rivals' perceptions of the individuals' knowledge-providing relationships, the more likely it is that individuals will achieve favorable career outcomes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived structural equivalence (experimentally manipulated; in field study, ego's perception of overlap between her and alter's knowledge-receiving relationships, indexed 0-100%); rivalry as perceived by ego (Hypothesis 1a) and rivalry as perceived by alter (Hypothesis 1b); ego's success in seeking from rivals and ego's success in hiding from rivals (Hypotheses 2a/2b).
- DV(s): A sense of rivalry (experiments); hiding and seeking intentions and behaviors (experiments); ego's errors of omission in perceiving alter's knowledge-providing relationships (field study, dyadic); career outcomes — number of projects led and percentage compensation increase at t+3 and t+4 years (field study, individual).
- Mediators: Sense of rivalry mediates the effect of perceived structural equivalence on hiding intentions and disclosing behavior (Experiments 1 and 2 bootstrap mediation).
- Moderators: Mutual versus one-way rivalry (interaction of rivalry-with-alter and rivalry-with-ego in Model 3 of the dyadic regression); friendship tie between ego and alter (exploratory moderator that intensifies hiding and seeking effects).

The proposed mechanism extends structurally equivalent rivalry theory: when ego perceives a coworker to share many of the same knowledge providers, this triggers rivalrous feelings of competitive threat. Rivalry then motivates two complementary forms of strategic network manipulation — minimizing one's own errors of omission with respect to the rival's knowledge ties (seeking) and maximizing the rival's errors of omission with respect to one's own ties (hiding). Hiding success is hypothesized to confer career benefits because protected knowledge sources support innovative work, while seeking success is hypothesized to provide informational advantage through awareness of rivals' resources. The experiments validate the assumption that perceived structural equivalence ignites rivalry and prompts action; the field study shows these processes operate in a real organization even after controlling for actual structural equivalence, common friendship ties, and direct knowledge-sharing ties.

**Data & Measures**
- Experiments (U.S. working adults via CloudResearch online panels; between-participants designs). Experiment 1 (N=248): perceived coworker's knowledge network manipulated (structural equivalence vs. non-equivalence) using a name-generation technique; sense of rivalry = two items averaged (r=.77); hiding and seeking intentions on 7-point items. Experiment 2 (N=259): 2 (coworker gender) x 2 (perceived structural equivalence) design; rivalry = three items (Cronbach's alpha=.78); hiding intention = two items, seeking intention = one item; hiding and seeking behaviors coded 1/0 from participants' free-text replies to the coworker.
- Field study (73 headquarters employees; two-wave survey one year apart). Structurally equivalent rivalry = an asymmetric rivalry index (0-100%) measuring the percentage overlap between ego's and alter's perceived knowledge-receiving ties, split into rivalry-with-alter-as-perceived-by-ego (tests H1a) and rivalry-with-ego-as-perceived-by-alter (tests H1b). Dyadic DV: ego's errors of omission (false negatives) — ties reported by alter but not detected by ego. Individual DVs: number of projects led and percentage compensation increase, taken from company archives three and four years after wave 2. Seeking-from-rivals and hiding-from-rivals indices weight each false-negative rate by the rivalry index. Controls include actual structural equivalence, direct knowledge tie, friendship tie, common friends, ego/alter network size, false positives, and same department/rank/tenure/gender.
- Estimation: dyadic hypotheses (H1a, H1b) via double-Dekker semi-partialling MRQAP; individual hypotheses (H2a, H2b) via negative binomial regression (projects led) and OLS (compensation increase). The field design is associational; no causal-identification strategy is claimed.

**Key Findings**
- Experiments (assumption tests, largely convergent across both studies): perceived structural equivalence significantly increased a sense of rivalry (Experiment 1: F(1,246)=8.37, p=.004; Experiment 2: F(1,256)=10.84, p<.001) and hiding intention (Experiment 1: p=.013; Experiment 2: p=.002). Seeking intention rose significantly in Experiment 1 (p=.039) but not in Experiment 2 (p=.951) — a divergence between the two experiments. In Experiment 2, structurally equivalent participants disclosed their knowledge providers less (p<.001) and sought their coworker's providers more (p=.050). Rivalry mediated the effects of structural equivalence on hiding/disclosing (both experiments) and on seeking intention in Experiment 1.
- Field study (formal hypothesis tests, MRQAP). H1a supported: rivalry as perceived by ego was associated with fewer errors of omission (b=-19.031, p=.001) — egos detected rivals' knowledge-providing ties. H1b supported: rivalry as perceived by alter was associated with more errors of omission in ego's perceptions (b=4.234, p=.001) — alters successfully hid their ties from those who saw them as rivals. Both held after controlling for actual structural equivalence and common friends.
- Career outcomes (H2a, H2b) were mixed at best. Seeking from rivals did not predict project leadership (n.s.) but predicted a higher t+4 compensation increase (b=3.640, p=.047), not t+3 (b=4.019, p=.339) — partial support for H2a. Hiding from rivals predicted more projects led at t+3 (b=21.351, p=.005) and t+4 (b=11.190, p=.004) but did not significantly predict compensation (t+4 only marginal, b=7.828, p=.099) — partial support for H2b.
- Convergence/divergence: experiments and field study align on the core hiding-and-seeking patterns tied to perceived rivalry; the main divergence is seeking (significant in Experiment 1 and in the field-study H1a test, but non-significant in Experiment 2), alongside weak and inconsistent career-outcome effects.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides the first empirical test of two foundational but previously untested assumptions of structural-equivalence research: that perceived structural equivalence engenders rivalry, and that individuals take strategic action toward those perceived as structural equivalents. It bridges the social-psychological literature on dyadic rivalry with the network literature on structural equivalence, and contributes a new perspective on cognitive social networks by showing that errors in network perception are not merely cognitive limitations but partly the outcome of strategic deception (hiding) and detection (seeking) in pursuit of competitive advantage.

**Practical Implication**
Encouraging individual-level competition for valued goals such as salary increases or promotions can have the unintended consequence of stifling the cooperation organizations require, because rivalrous employees devote effort to concealing their knowledge sources and probing those of others. Managers may need to make knowledge-sharing relationships more transparent and to design incentives that reward collaboration over the maintenance of exclusive knowledge ties, while balancing the motivational benefits of competition against the corrosive effects of rivalry.

**Limitations**
The field-study sample is relatively small (73 employees, 69 retained for individual-level career outcomes), limiting statistical power particularly for the conservative two-tailed career-outcome tests. The study captures hiding and seeking outcomes through observed network-perception data rather than directly observing tactics in real time, and the three- to four-year lag between independent variables and career outcomes introduces noise that may obscure effects. Mutual rivalry was rare in the empirical context, so the seeking-trumps-hiding interaction result should be interpreted with caution.

**Future Research**
Future work could establish the causal chain from perceived structural equivalence through rivalry to hiding and seeking using longitudinal observational designs, identify the specific tactics individuals use to conceal knowledge providers and to detect rivals' contacts, and examine whether individual gains from hiding and seeking damage broader organizational productivity. Researchers could also investigate boundary conditions in non-competitive settings (e.g., mentorship dyads, multiplex relationships) and extend the framework to firm-level effects of false-positive and false-negative network perceptions.
