# Constructing Envelopes: How Institutional Custodians Can Tame Disruptive Algorithms

**Abstract**
The infusion of algorithms into organizational fields—accelerated by advances in artificial intelligence—can have disruptive effects that trigger defensive responses. One important response involves establishing a boundary around an algorithm to delimit its interactions with its environment—in engineering terms, constructing an "envelope." Yet, we know little about the process through which such envelopes are constructed. We address this issue by exploring how institutional custodians construct envelopes around disruptive algorithms. We empirically examine custodians' responses to the high-frequency trading algorithms that disrupted the field of U.S. securities trading, focusing on the years 2009–2016. Our inductive analysis shows that custodians created an envelope with interconnected normative, governance, and practice "layers" that jointly constrained high-frequency trading. Each layer emerged as custodians "coupled" one element of the field (e.g., its values) to one aspect of the disruptive algorithms (e.g., their impacts). Our study contributes to research on the social dynamics of algorithms by generating novel theory of how envelopes around algorithms are constructed, and to research on institutional custodianship by highlighting the constructing of envelopes as a custodial response to a wide range of threats—including, but not restricted to, disruptive algorithms.

**Research Question**
How do custodians construct envelopes around algorithms that threaten to disrupt valued elements of organizational fields?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper. This is an inductive, grounded-theory study (Corbin & Strauss, 2008) in which all concepts emerged inductively and no a priori hypotheses were tested; the authors instead develop an empirically grounded process model of how institutional custodians construct envelopes around disruptive algorithms.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: institutional custodians vs. disruptive algorithms; values, governance, and practices of the field vs. impacts, inputs/outputs, and usage of algorithms; "envelope" as a boundary delimiting interactions
- Process / phases: Phase 1 (2009–2010, normative layer — guardians couple values to algorithmic impacts); Phase 2 (2010–2014, governance layer — reformers couple governance to algorithmic inputs/outputs); Phase 3 (2014–2016, practice layer — regulators couple legitimate practices to algorithmic usage)
- Triggering conditions: attention-increasing events (Aleynikov arrest 2009, Flash Crash 2010, publication of Flash Boys 2014) that heightened anxiety and fueled political contests
- Practices: rhetorical work (guardians), diagnostic and showcasing work (reformers via IEX, whistleblowing), enforcement actions and rule-making (regulators)
- Emergent outcomes: a three-layered envelope that transformed high-frequency trading from a disruptive threat into "an unproblematic part of the field"
- Boundary conditions: tightly bounded field; relatively passive algorithm-developing firms; well-connected custodians with clear roles

The process model shows custodians constructing envelopes through a "conjunctive" rather than linear sequence: each layer coupled a field element to an algorithmic aspect, with established layers motivating and resourcing subsequent custodians. Different custodian types drew on heterogeneous resources — guardians on prominence, reformers on technical expertise, regulators on formal authority — and their cumulative work delimited the algorithm's interactions with the field without any single actor needing to fully understand the algorithm's inner workings.

**Data & Measures**
Inductive, grounded-theory analysis (Corbin & Strauss, 2008) in which all concepts emerged inductively. The data corpus drew on four sources: (1) a "primary newspaper dataset" of 1,512 articles on high-frequency trading from the five highest-volume U.S. outlets (Chicago Tribune, Los Angeles Times, New York Times, Wall Street Journal, and Washington Post) for January 2009 to December 2016; (2) nine U.S. Congressional hearing transcripts (1,185 pages); (3) 39 semi-structured interviews with 29 actors (33 hours in total); and (4) a "secondary newspaper dataset" of 90,322 articles from 639 U.S. newspapers referring to the 140 individuals identified as publicly responding to high-frequency trading. Analysis proceeded through five sequential (though iterative) steps. Step 1 identified 133 individuals from the primary newspaper dataset and coded the 260 statements they made in terms of whether they highlighted positive or negative social and economic impacts of high-frequency trading; drawing additionally on the Congressional-hearing transcripts, the authors identified 140 individuals in total who publicly responded to high-frequency trading. Step 2 grouped individuals into five actor types — three custodian types (guardians, reformers, regulators) and two noncustodian types (high-frequency traders, challengers) — operationalizing each actor's position through dictionary-based content analysis (WordStat) of three resources: public prominence (the number of 2009–2016 newspaper articles mentioning the actor), specialization in high-frequency trading (the share of an actor's articles that mentioned it, using an 18-keyword dictionary), and prior participation in public discussions of financial markets. Step 3 identified key political contests and used temporal bracketing (Langley, 1999) to distinguish three phases, coding data in NVivo from open codes to first-order categories, second-order themes, and aggregate dimensions (Gioia, Corley, & Hamilton, 2013). Step 4 searched for commonalities across custodians' work, theorized as a "coupling" of one element of the field to one aspect of high-frequency trading. Step 5 analyzed how each coupling constrained high-frequency trading, theorized as a "layer" of the envelope.

**Key Findings**
The study develops an empirically grounded process model in which custodians in the field of U.S. securities trading constructed an envelope around high-frequency trading algorithms out of three interconnected "layers," each built in a distinct phase:
- Normative layer (Phase 1, 2009–2010): guardians (high-profile politicians, journalists, and pundits) coupled the value of fairness for retail investors to the impacts of high-frequency trading through rhetorical work, establishing a normative reference point for evaluating those impacts.
- Governance layer (Phase 2, 2010–2014): reformers (highly specialized actors) coupled the governance of the field to the inputs and outputs of high-frequency trading by diagnosing how it produced unfair impacts and showcasing alternatives such as the IEX trading venue.
- Practice layer (Phase 3, 2014–2016): regulators coupled legitimate practices to the usage of high-frequency trading through routinized surveillance, enforcement actions, and industry self-regulation.
Each phase was set off by an attention-increasing event — the July 2009 arrest of Sergey Aleynikov, the "flash crash" of May 2010, and the March 2014 publication of Flash Boys — that heightened anxiety and fueled a political contest between one custodian type and one noncustodian type, in which custodians proved more successful than noncustodians. Construction was "conjunctive" rather than linear: a layer established in one phase motivated and resourced custodians in subsequent phases. Jointly, the three layers transformed high-frequency trading from a disruptive threat to the field into an unproblematic part of it, allowing the algorithms to operate while mitigating their harmful impacts, without any single actor fully understanding the algorithms' inner workings.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on the social dynamics of algorithms by generating empirically grounded process theory of how envelopes around algorithms are constructed, showing actors can work around algorithmic opacity by leveraging field-specific expertise rather than mastering code. It contributes to institutional custodianship research by identifying envelope construction as a third custodial response — distinct from buffering institutions and intervening in threats — that allows custodians to steer institutional change when threats make change unavoidable.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that actors lacking deep technological expertise or access to proprietary algorithms can still meaningfully influence the algorithmic transformation of society by coupling familiar field elements (values, governance arrangements, practices) to specific aspects of algorithms (their impacts, inputs/outputs, usage). Custodians can thereby tame disruptive algorithms without halting their adoption.

**Limitations**
The high-frequency trading firms played a relatively passive role during the studied period, leaving open how proponents of algorithms work to counteract envelope construction. The field of U.S. securities trading is also relatively tightly bounded, limiting generalizability to algorithms that span multiple, less well-bounded fields (e.g., Google search algorithms).

**Future Research**
Future work should explore how proponents of algorithms actively resist or counter custodial efforts, and how envelopes can be constructed around algorithms that traverse multiple fields or that disrupt less well-bounded fields. Researchers can also examine how exogenous shocks and adaptive algorithm development trigger new cycles of envelope-maintenance work.
