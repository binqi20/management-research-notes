# Avoidance and Aggression in Stakeholder Engagement: The Impact of CEO Paranoia and Paranoia-Relevant Cues

**Abstract**
We develop and test theory regarding the effect of CEO paranoia, defined as stable tendencies toward suspicion, feelings of ill will or resentment, mistrust, and belief in external control or influence, on firm stakeholder engagement. We theorize that because CEOs higher in paranoia have tendencies toward hypervigilance and the biases of self-as-target and sinister attributions they typically avoid engagement with external stakeholders. Further, we argue that CEOs higher in paranoia are subject to paranoid activation when confronted with trait-relevant cues that confirm suspicions of being targeted by a malevolent external entity (here, regulatory rulings or rival attacks), thus eliciting a shift away from avoidance to a more aggressive engagement with those stakeholders. To do so, we develop a content-analytic measure of CEO paranoia following both theory and evidence in psychology and methodological best practices, finding evidence that broadly supports our premise. In total, our study draws attention to how the manifestation of CEO paranoia changes the way CEOs engage stakeholders over time, contributing to understanding in multiple ways.

**Research Question**
How does CEO paranoia affect firms' engagement with powerful external stakeholders (government and competitors), and how do paranoia-relevant cues (regulatory rulings and rival attacks) activate the trait to shift CEOs from avoidance toward more aggressive engagement?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: CEO paranoia is negatively related to government lobbying breadth (predicted negative direct effect).
- H2: CEO paranoia is negatively related to firm competitive actions (predicted negative direct effect).
- H3: There will be a positive interaction effect of paranoia and regulatory ruling severity on lobbying breadth, such that the negative CEO paranoia–lobbying breadth relationship is mitigated as regulatory ruling severity increases (predicted positive interaction).
- H4: There will be a positive interaction effect of paranoia and rival attacks on competitive actions, such that the negative CEO paranoia–competitive actions relationship is mitigated as rival attacks increase (predicted positive interaction).

**Mechanism Process**
- IV: CEO paranoia (content-analytic measure of stable tendencies toward suspicion, ill will, mistrust, and belief in external control, captured from earnings call transcripts via a six-component dictionary: anxiety, distrust, fear, obsession, suspicion, threat).
- DVs: Lobbying breadth (standardized count of unique government agencies and pieces of legislation targeted) and competitive actions (RavenPack count across eight categories: new product, capacity, pricing, marketing, acquisitions, alliances, market expansion, legal).
- Moderators: Regulatory ruling severity (penalty amount in U.S. dollars from goodjobsfirst.org) and rival attacks (number of competitive actions targeting the focal firm).
- Controls: Firm size, market performance (Tobin's Q), tax aggressiveness, governmental subsidies and contracts, lobbying expenditures, blockholder ownership, TMT power, CEO compensation and pay sensitivity, perceived political and nonpolitical risk, inverse Mills ratio, year and two-digit SIC dummies.

Drawing on upper echelons theory and trait-activation logic, the paper argues that CEO paranoia — anchored in hypervigilance, rumination, self-as-target bias, and sinister attribution bias — manifests primarily in avoidance safety behaviors that reduce engagement with external stakeholders perceived as threatening. When trait-relevant cues (severe regulatory rulings against the firm; rival attacks on the firm) signal that avoidance has failed to provide protection, the trait activates an alternative response: aggression, defined as direct engagement intended to deter further targeting. Empirically, paranoia is negatively associated with lobbying breadth (avoidance of government), and this relationship flips toward aggression as regulatory rulings become more severe; the predicted negative direct effect on competitive actions is not supported, but the activation interaction with rival attacks is positive and significant.

**Data & Measures**
- Data: CEO earnings-call transcripts from CapitalIQ, linked to Execucomp and to control/outcome data from the Center for Responsive Politics, Thomson-Reuters, Compustat, USAspending.gov, goodjobsfirst.org, and RavenPack; sample of S&P 1500 firms over 2010-2017 (925 CEOs, 774 firms, 3,823 firm-year observations).
- IV — CEO paranoia: a newly developed content-analytic dictionary of six components (anxiety, distrust, fear, obsession, suspicion, threat) applied to CEO speech in earnings calls, then summed to a yearly measure; validated via management-faculty ratings (retaining components with >75% agreement), a single-factor solution (eigenvalue 1.16, 67% of variance), within-CEO reliability (Cronbach's α = 0.83), and correlations with related content-analytic measures.
- DV — lobbying breadth (government engagement): standardized and summed count of the number of agencies and pieces of legislation a firm targeted with lobbying expenditures (Ridge et al., 2017).
- DV — competitive actions: RavenPack count of eight action types (new product, capacity, pricing, marketing, acquisitions, alliances, market expansion, legal).
- Moderator — regulatory rulings: penalty amount in millions of U.S. dollars for assessed rulings (goodjobsfirst.org). Moderator — rival attacks: count of RavenPack competitive actions in which the focal firm is the target.
- Estimation: Tobit regression for the left-censored lobbying-breadth outcome and a negative binomial model for the count-valued competitive-actions outcome, with two-digit SIC and year dummies and an inverse Mills ratio; dependent variables measured at t+1 and predictors at t. The design is observational; the paper addresses endogeneity through diagnostics and robustness checks — robustness of inference to replacement (RIR) and two-stage residual inclusion (2SRI, using sadness and negativity as instruments) — rather than claiming clean causal identification.

**Key Findings**
- H1 supported: CEO paranoia is negatively and significantly related to lobbying breadth (coefficient –105.82, p = .02); a one-standard-deviation increase in paranoia corresponds to roughly a 7% decrease in lobbying breadth (avoidance of government).
- H2 not supported: the CEO paranoia coefficient for competitive actions is positive (opposite the predicted negative direction) and not statistically significant (p = .30).
- H3 supported: a positive, significant interaction of CEO paranoia and regulatory ruling severity on lobbying breadth (0.13, p < .01); at higher ruling severity the negative paranoia–lobbying relationship weakens and flips toward positive (aggression).
- H4 receives some support: a positive, significant interaction of CEO paranoia and rival attacks on competitive actions (2.25, p = .01), but the practical effect is small and the interaction strengthens an already-positive (statistically non-significant) main effect rather than weakening a negative one.
- Overall, the effects of CEO paranoia differ by stakeholder: the avoidance-then-activation pattern holds for government engagement (H1, H3), whereas the predicted avoidance of competitors is not supported (H2).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends upper echelons theory by integrating trait activation, showing that CEO traits — typically treated as stable predictors of stable outcomes — manifest differently when triggered by trait-relevant cues, opening a more dynamic view of how CEOs engage stakeholders over time. It introduces CEO paranoia as a previously overlooked but consequential trait in a literature dominated by self-assured, attention-seeking executive characterizations, and theorizes how paranoia produces stakeholder-specific patterns of avoidance and aggression. Methodologically, it develops and validates the first content-analytic measure of paranoia in management research, derived from psychology and applicable beyond CEOs to other linguistic populations.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that CEO paranoia has tangible firm-level consequences — for example, a one-standard-deviation increase in CEO paranoia is associated with roughly a 7% decrease in lobbying breadth, and small lobbying changes can carry meaningful tax-rate and contracting consequences. Boards, advisors, and shareholders should recognize that paranoid CEOs may avoid information exchange with regulators yet swing aggressively when targeted, while also potentially fostering bright-side outcomes such as more prudent risk management, supply-chain diversification, and verification practices.

**Limitations**
The paper focuses only on two stakeholder types (government and competitors), so generalization to other stakeholders (boards, suppliers, investors, partners) is uncertain, and CEO paranoia is shown to operate differently across different stakeholders, suggesting context-specific contingencies the study cannot fully unpack. The negative direct effect of paranoia on competitive actions is not supported, indicating boundary conditions on the avoidance argument. The empirical setting is the relatively stable 2010-2017 economic period within S&P 1500 earnings-call discourse, raising concerns about extension to post-pandemic or other firm-level contexts; CEO selection and other endogeneity concerns are addressed but cannot be fully eliminated.

**Future Research**
The authors call for studies extending CEO paranoia to other stakeholder relationships (cooperative-strategy partners, acquisitions, investors, boards, suppliers, buyers), examining tournament-theory and structural-interdependence dynamics within TMTs, and exploring how paranoia interacts with agency-theoretic accounts of executive decision-making. They encourage further validation of the content-analytic paranoia measure across different settings and populations, exploration of "bright" versus "dark" sides of subclinical paranoia (e.g., risk-management benefits vs. missed partnerships), and broader application of trait-activation logic to other CEO traits whose outcomes may also be cue-dependent rather than uniformly stable.
