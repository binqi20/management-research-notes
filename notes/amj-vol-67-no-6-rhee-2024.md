# CEO Attentional Vigilance: Behavioral Implications for the Pursuit of Exploration

**Abstract**
This study proposes that CEO attentional vigilance, defined as maintaining a consistent level of attention over time, can contribute to a firm's pursuit of exploration. Using data from new product introductions in the software industry, along with a text-based measure of attentional vigilance derived from earnings conference calls, I find that CEO attentional vigilance to exploration has a positive effect on the firm's rate of exploration. Additionally, I observe that this positive impact is more pronounced when a firm's financial performance falls below aspiration levels. Furthermore, based on an analysis of Glassdoor review data, I demonstrate that the level of satisfaction among employees in explorative units partially mediates the effect of CEO attentional vigilance on the firm's pursuit of exploration. The findings hold significant implications for research in the attention-based view, performance feedback, and micro-foundations of strategy.

**Research Question**
Independent of the quantity of CEO attention to exploration, does the vigilance of CEO attention — the consistency with which a CEO sustains attention to exploration over time — promote a firm's rate of exploration, and through what conditions and micro-foundational mechanisms does this protective role operate?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
- H1: Independent of the amount of CEO attention to exploration, CEO attentional vigilance to exploration has a positive effect on the rate of firm exploration.
- H2: The positive effect of CEO attentional vigilance on exploration is stronger as a firm's financial performance falls below aspiration levels.
- H3: The positive effect of CEO attentional vigilance on firm exploration is mediated by the satisfaction level of employees in explorative units with the firm.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV: Vigilance of CEO Attention to Exploration — the (reverse-coded) standard deviation of quarterly exploration-keyword frequencies in CEO presentation sections of earnings calls within a firm-year.
- DV: Number of explorative (brand new) software product introductions per firm-year, measured at year t + 2.
- Mediator: Employee Satisfaction in Explorative Units — average Glassdoor satisfaction rating from R&D employees in year t + 1.
- Moderator: ROA - Aspiration < 0 (negative attainment discrepancy spline) capturing financial performance below a 0.3 historical / 0.7 social aspiration blend.
- Controls: Amount of CEO Attention to Exploration, R&D intensity, firm size, firm age, slack, service orientation, CEO tenure, CEO duality, CEO stock ownership, year dummies, firm fixed effects.

The attention-based view treats CEO attention as a scarce resource for monitoring and resource allocation, but firms drift toward exploitation, so paying attention to exploration does not guarantee sustaining it. Because explorative routines and tacit knowledge decay when interrupted by exploitative demands, CEOs must keep attention vigilant — distributed evenly over time — to protect explorative units from resource diversions, thereby maintaining the continuity of explorative activities. Vigilance is amplified under underperformance, when problemistic search would otherwise withdraw resources from exploration, and operates through a micro-foundational pathway in which sustained protection raises the satisfaction of R&D employees, who reciprocate with greater creativity and task engagement that translates into more brand-new product introductions.

**Data & Measures**
- Data sources: 4,296 quarterly earnings-call transcripts (CEO presentation sections) for 221 firms, drawn from Factiva, Morning Star, and Seeking Alpha; 8,104 product-launch news articles (e.g., CNET, Computerworld, Infoworld) for the exploration outcome; and 165,308 Glassdoor employee reviews for the satisfaction mediator.
- DV (firm exploration): annual count of brand-new (explorative) software product introductions with no prior versions, measured at year t + 2.
- IV (Vigilance of CEO Attention to Exploration): quarterly relative frequency of March (1991)-derived plus inductively generated exploration keywords in CEO earnings-call presentations, converted to the reverse-coded standard deviation across the four quarters (a high value indicates stable, vigilant attention); the annual mean of that attention serves as the control Amount of CEO Attention to Exploration.
- Moderator (below-aspiration performance): spline function of the discrepancy between ROA and an aspiration level weighted 0.3 historical and 0.7 social, using the ROA - Aspiration < 0 term.
- Mediator (Employee Satisfaction in Explorative Units): firm-year average of R&D employees' Glassdoor satisfaction ratings on a 5-point scale, measured at year t + 1.
- Controls: R&D intensity, firm age, firm size, slack, service orientation, CEO tenure, CEO duality, CEO stock ownership, and year dummies.
- Estimation: firm fixed-effects Poisson regressions with robust standard errors clustered by firm and a two-year lag structure; mediation tested with Baron-Kenny and a 50,000-sample bootstrap; keyword validity checked against expert manual ratings; endogeneity probed with propensity score matching (no reliable instrument was available) and falsification tests on exploitative and overall product introductions.

**Key Findings**
- H1 (supported): Vigilance of CEO Attention to Exploration has a significant positive effect on explorative product introductions (b = 0.169, p < .001); a 1 SD increase in vigilance yields a 6.81% rise in explorative introductions two years later, whereas the Amount of CEO attention to exploration is positive but insignificant, indicating that vigilance matters more than quantity.
- H2 (supported): The interaction of vigilance with the below-aspiration spline (ROA - Aspiration < 0) is negative and significant (b = -0.025, p < .05); the marginal effect of vigilance on exploration grows as performance falls further below aspirations, rising from a 5.78% to a 9.43% increase at 1 SD below the mean.
- H3 (supported, partial mediation): Vigilance raises the satisfaction of employees in explorative units (b = 1.213, p < .01), which in turn raises exploration (b = 0.084, p < .01); adding the mediator lowers the vigilance coefficient from 0.169 to 0.091, and a 50,000-sample bootstrap confirms partial rather than full mediation.
- Supplementary: vigilance interacts positively with attention quantity (b = 0.017, p < .05), R&D intensity (b = 0.020, p < .01), and service orientation (b = 0.215, p < .05); falsification tests find no significant effect of vigilance on exploitative product introductions and no mediation through overall employee satisfaction, supporting the exploration-specific protective mechanism.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the attention-based view beyond its dominant quantity-based framing by showing empirically that the temporal consistency of CEO attention — not just its amount — drives firm exploration, and uncovers a previously neglected protective function of attention that complements its monitoring and resource-allocation roles. By integrating performance feedback theory, it specifies an underperformance condition under which the protective role becomes more crucial, and by introducing employee satisfaction in explorative units as a partial mediator, it contributes a micro-foundational bridge linking CEO-level attentional dynamics to firm-level innovation outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
CEOs aiming to promote exploration should focus on sustaining a steady cadence of attention to explorative agendas across quarters rather than episodic high-intensity bursts, particularly when firms underperform and pressures to redirect engineers toward current-product support intensify. Because the effect is partially transmitted through the satisfaction of R&D employees, leaders should pair vigilant attention with consistent, visible protection of explorative units' resources and autonomy so that employees perceive reliable managerial support and reciprocate with creative effort.

**Limitations**
The study relies on textual proxies of CEO attention from earnings conference calls and on Glassdoor reviews for employee satisfaction rather than survey or interview measures, which more direct instruments could improve. Despite firm fixed effects, lagged designs, and propensity score matching, the author cannot deploy a strong instrumental variable or external shock to fully identify the causal effect of attentional vigilance on exploration. The findings are restricted to a single industry (prepackaged software, SIC 7372) over 2008-2012 and to a sample where CEO attention to exploration never falls below 12 percent, limiting generalizability to other contexts and to extreme low-attention regimes.

**Future Research**
Scholars should extend the attentional-vigilance construct to other firm agendas vulnerable to interruption to test the generality of its protective role, and examine when prolonged vigilance shifts into cognitive inertia that hinders adaptation to new opportunities. Differential patterns within low attentional vigilance (e.g., increasing-then-decreasing versus decreasing-then-increasing trajectories) deserve study, as does the interplay between attentional vigilance and CEO cognitive attributes such as paradoxical thinking and cognitive complexity that may drive the capacity to attend vigilantly to competing exploration-exploitation demands.
