# Custodianship across Generations: Preserving the Practice of Vinyl Record Manufacturing

**Abstract**
Drawing on a global, longitudinal case study of the vinyl record manufacturing practice following a global decline in sales, we examine the work undertaken to preserve it over time. Our process model of practice preservation highlights that a declining practice may persist across generations if it is made more accessible and resilient by reducing its dependence on specific types of meanings, materials, and competences that risk being lost. Our analysis suggests that these outcomes depend on the work of two types of custodians—legacy professionals and new enthusiasts—in terms of "diversifying" the practice to accommodate multiple configurations of meanings, materials, and competences, thus constituting new practice variants, as well as supporting the continued enactment of variants over time by engaging in processes we label "propping up," "recovering," "replenishing," and "releasing." In addition to developing a rich understanding of preservation, our findings contribute to a nuanced perspective on practice evolution by conceptualizing a practice as comprising multiple, potentially fluid variants. Moreover, by highlighting inclusive custodianship processes, which differ from gatekeeping and guarding so far emphasized, and by unpacking the role of materials to complement the traditional emphasis on symbolic elements, our findings enrich the nascent custodianship literature.

**Research Question**
How can custodians preserve a declining practice across generations? Specifically, in the wake of practice decline that depletes meanings, materials, and competences, how can custodians gain access to and become proficient in scarce materials and competences, and pass them on to future generations of practitioners?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper. This is an inductive, longitudinal qualitative case study with no a priori hypotheses; the authors pursue an exploratory research question and abductively build a process model of practice preservation from the case data rather than testing predicted relationships or stating numbered propositions.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: practice (meanings, materials, competences); practice decline; practice preservation (distinct from conservation/maintenance); practice variants; custodians; legacy professionals (entered before decline; depended on practice for income) vs. new enthusiasts (entered during/after decline; non-economic motivations).
- Five interrelated custodianship processes:
  - Phase 1 (initial decline, 1980-1994): "Propping up" — legacy professionals continue enacting and supporting existing variants (scaling down, hoarding remnants, repairing/servicing); "Diversifying" — both legacy professionals and enthusiasts modify the practice to compete with substitutes (e.g., DMM), to enact more easily (e.g., polycarbonate embossing), or to profit from residual demand (e.g., PETG diamond cutting), creating new practice variants.
  - Phase 2 (abating decline, 1995-present): "Recovering" — enthusiasts scavenge abandoned remnants, restore them via trial and error, and demonstrate competences to gain trust of legacy professionals; "Replenishing" — enthusiasts replicate scarce parts (using remnants or new tools such as 3D printing) and upgrade with better-performing alternatives; "Releasing" — enthusiasts share information online (e.g., the Secret Society of Lathe Trolls forum) and exchange materials/services, dispersing meanings, materials, and competences.
- Triggering / contextual conditions: substantial sales decline (≤1% of 1977 levels by 2007); cessation of specialized tool manufacturing; loss of apprenticeships and competences as legacy professionals retire or die.
- Emergent outcomes: increased practice resilience (reduced dependence on specific configurations of meanings, materials, competences) and increased accessibility for new entrants — together attracting further custodians and reinforcing multiplicity.
- Boundary conditions: level of practice institutionalization; embeddedness in adjacent popular practices; perceived risk of extinction; barriers to entry; potential for mastery and experimentation.

The process unfolds as differentiation rather than uniformity (ex uno plures rather than e pluribus unum). Propping up alone is insufficient because it does not renew depleted elements; diversifying alone is insufficient without subsequent regenerative work. The ongoing recovering–replenishing–releasing loop performed by enthusiasts converts brittle, dependence-laden variants into a multiplicity of more resilient and accessible variants, which in turn attracts new custodians who reinforce the loop across generations.

**Data & Measures**
- Data corpus: 107 semi-structured interviews conducted between 2016 and 2023 with 101 individuals across the global vinyl record manufacturing supply chain (mastering/disc-cutting engineers, pressing-plant managers, electroplating specialists, lacquer-disc and cutterhead/stylus makers, repair technicians, and antistatic-sleeve makers); 29 interviews were combined with site visits (in Austria, Brazil, Canada, Czech Republic, France, Germany, Japan, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and the United States), and 20 respondents active before 1985 were later classified as legacy professionals. All but eight interviews were recorded, transcribed, and imported into NVivo.
- Archival and observational data: newspaper and magazine articles (Nexis Uni and general searches), books, academic articles, trade magazines, blogs, and forum entries; audio and video recordings (YouTube, Vimeo, the Women in Vinyl podcast, the Chasing Sound Oral History Project); the Secret Society of Lathe Trolls online forum (created 2005; a complete archive of messages from roughly 5,000 enthusiast members); and participation in a practitioner conference.
- Constructs and coding: interviews used a biographical approach (Rosenthal, 2004) to elicit narratives; analysis was abductive (Mantere & Ketokivi, 2013), guided by Shove et al.'s (2012) social practice framework of meanings, materials, and competences. The authors compiled lists of the materials and competences that became scarce, constructed and then variant-mapped timelines (Figure 2), and structured the data into first-order codes, second-order codes (activities), and themes (processes) (Table 3), grouping activities into custodianship processes assigned to legacy professionals and new enthusiasts.
- Design: interpretive, inductive, and genealogical (backward tracing complemented by forward-looking analysis), triangulated across sources (Flick, 2004) and validated through member-checking iterations with practitioners (Lincoln & Guba, 1985); no measurement scales, statistical estimation, or causal-identification strategy is claimed.

**Key Findings**
As an inductive single-case study, the central finding is a process model (Figure 3) rather than hypothesis tests:
- The study identifies two types of custodians — legacy professionals (who entered before decline and depended on the practice for financial income) and new enthusiasts (who entered during or after decline for primarily non-economic reasons) — who together preserve a declining practice across generations.
- Preservation operates through five interrelated custodianship processes: propping up and diversifying during the initial-decline phase (1980-1994), and recovering, replenishing, and releasing during the abating-decline phase (1995-present).
- Propping up (legacy professionals continuing to enact and support existing variants) and diversifying (creating new practice variants) are necessary to stem decline but not sufficient to sustain enactment; the recovering–replenishing–releasing loop, performed mainly by new enthusiasts, regenerates and disperses depleted materials and competences.
- Directional outcomes: creating and supporting multiple practice variants increases the practice's resilience (reducing dependence on specific configurations of meanings, materials, and competences) and its accessibility for new entrants, which in turn attracts further custodians and reinforces the multiplicity of custodians and variants.
- Core theoretical insight surfaced by the case: differentiation (ex uno plures) rather than the defense of uniformity (e pluribus unum) supports a practice's persistence over time, and preservation requires renewing not only meanings — the prior custodianship focus — but also materials and competences.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper foregrounds practice preservation as a distinct phenomenon of theoretical interest, conceptually separating it from emergence, growth, diffusion, and dominance maintenance, and integrating the previously disconnected literatures on practice evolution and custodianship. It theorizes that preserving a declining practice requires renewing not only meanings (the predominant focus of prior custodianship research) but also materials and competences, and that practice diversity—the creation and uptake of multiple variants—is a key mechanism for resilience and intergenerational transmission. By identifying inclusive custodianship (opening up rather than guarding), unpacking the material dimension of custodianship, and theorizing custodial succession as enthusiast-led empowerment rather than active grooming, the paper enriches the nascent custodianship literature and offers a process model of how practices persist between decline and possible re-emergence.

**Practical Implication**
For communities and individuals seeking to preserve declining practices (crafts, traditions, manufacturing know-how, cultural heritage), the model suggests that survival depends less on policing purity and more on enabling differentiation and inclusion: legacy professionals add value by maintaining residual enactment and sharing remnants, while new enthusiasts can become future custodians of the practice. The findings are relevant to debates about the throwaway society and the preservation of endangered crafts, suggesting that bottom-up, enthusiast-driven preservation can succeed even without market revival or substantial institutional support.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on one practice—vinyl record manufacturing—whose decline did not threaten its societal acceptability and whose adjacency to popular practices (music, technological tinkering) plausibly favored enthusiast involvement; the model's applicability to once-popular but now controversial practices, or to practices lacking such adjacency, is left for future work. Most legacy professionals and new enthusiasts in the sample were male, White, and middle class, raising questions about how demographic diversity among custodians shapes preservation. The model captures preservation in the absence of strong top-down state or corporate subsidies and may apply less directly to practices preserved primarily through governmental or large-firm sponsorship.

**Future Research**
Future research could examine boundary conditions of the model, including: how high diversity may dilute a practice into a different practice altogether (the core vs. peripheral elements distinction); how practices that are no longer compatible with societal norms or regulations can be preserved; how barriers to recovering, replenishing, and releasing change as production and communication technologies (CAD, CNC, online forums) become more accessible; and how within- and across-generation diversity of custodians shapes long-run persistence and erosion of practices.
