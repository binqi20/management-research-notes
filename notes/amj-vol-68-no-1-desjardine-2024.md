# Information-Based Competition: The Case of Rival Owners in Rating Agencies

**Abstract**
This study extends competitive dynamics research by theorizing how and when information about a firm may be shaped by competitive forces operating through information intermediaries. Arguing that ownership bestows investors with influence over information intermediaries from which investors can profit, we suggest that firms receive less favorable ratings from rating agencies in which their rivals' investors have greater ownership. Building on research in this vein, we develop a dynamic theory of information-based competition that suggests the rating discounts a firm receives from rating agencies owned by its rivals' investors are shaped by the reputational threats and opportunities faced by the firm and its rivals, which motivate and enable investors to use information intermediaries to intervene in competitive dynamics. Analyses of environmental, social, and governance (ESG) ratings of 2,787 firms by a leading ESG rating agency support these ideas. Our study introduces the concept of information-based competition and illuminates the conditions under which investors engage in information-based competitive attacks.

**Research Question**
How and when do competitive forces operating through information intermediaries shape the information that stakeholders receive about a firm — specifically, do firms receive less favorable ESG ratings from rating agencies in which their rivals' institutional investors hold greater ownership stakes, and how is this rating bias amplified or attenuated by reputational threats (rival/target ESG media controversies) and reputational opportunities (rival/target ESG awards)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV: Rival-MSCI common institutional ownership (rival-MSCI CIO) — the average product of an investor's ownership in MSCI and in the target firm's industry rivals, summed across common institutional owners with >=1% stakes.
- DV: MSCI ESG rating of the target firm (7-point scale converted to 0-6 integer values; AAA=6 to CCC=0).
- Moderators (reputational threats): rival ESG media controversies (strengthens negative effect, H2a); target firm ESG media controversies (weakens negative effect, H2b).
- Moderators (reputational opportunities): rival ESG awards (strengthens negative effect, H3a); target firm ESG awards (weakens negative effect, H3b).
- Controls: target firm-MSCI CIO, rival/target firm-other agency CIO, target firm-rival CIO, market overlap, size similarity, target/rival firm financial and governance characteristics, ESG compensation/board/regulatory enforcement, industry-year fixed effects.

The theorized mechanism is that institutional investors who simultaneously own significant stakes in MSCI and in firms outside the rating industry have both motive (portfolio returns benefit when their portfolio firms appear better than rivals on ESG) and means (legal "advisor" status, opaque ESG rating processes, executive incentives to placate large investors) to tilt MSCI's ESG ratings against the portfolio firms' rivals. The bias intensifies when reputational stakes are high — rival ESG media controversies create motivation to repair rivals' reputations via ratings, and rival ESG awards heighten raters' receptivity to negative information about less-decorated peers — and weakens when target firms themselves are already drawing reputational scrutiny or have countervailing ESG awards that buffer their ratings.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces information-based competition — competition waged by manufacturing, assembling, and disseminating information that shapes stakeholders' perceptions of other organizations — as a new construct in competitive dynamics research, and adds a dynamic dimension by theorizing the reputational threats and opportunities under which outside actors leverage their influence over information intermediaries. It extends the information-intermediary literature by showing that bias originates not only from rated firms and direct economic exchange partners but also from the rivals' common investors, and contributes to the common-ownership literature by uncovering an indirect channel — boundary-spanning ownership in publicly traded rating agencies — through which common owners profit from competitive intervention.

**Practical Implication**
Firms should manage information flows with intermediaries by disclosing ESG activities transparently in standardized formats, enabling stakeholders and privately owned raters to assess them directly and limiting common-owner influence. Socially responsible investors should use ESG ratings from multiple agencies and account for the ownership/governance structures of those agencies when weighting them. Regulators (notably the SEC) should treat common owners of ESG rating agencies as a category of "unaffiliated third parties" whose conflicts of interest warrant disclosure, internal-control requirements, and ownership-transparency standards.

**Limitations**
The empirical strategy does not lag explanatory variables (because investor influence is theorized to operate during current holding periods), which the authors acknowledge weakens causal claims; endogeneity is mitigated through Heckman selection, ITCV, and quasi-natural experiments rather than fully resolved. The setting is U.S. firms rated by a single agency (MSCI), and although robustness checks against ASSET4 align with the theory, generalizability to non-publicly-traded raters and non-U.S. settings is untested. The two channels of investor influence — direct intervention versus executive pacification of investors — could not be empirically separated.

**Future Research**
Scholars should explore information-based competition along three dimensions: the actor engaging in it (institutional vs. individual investors, formal officeholders inside intermediaries), the type of intermediary (social media platforms like X, online review aggregators like Yelp), and the tie through which influence is exerted (ownership, formal position, advertising). Future work could disentangle whether common-owner influence operates through investor-initiated intervention or executive-initiated pacification, examine information-based competition in non-rating contexts, and probe how multiple competing information sources interact in shaping firm reputations and competitive dynamics.
