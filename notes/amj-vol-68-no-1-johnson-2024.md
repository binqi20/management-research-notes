# Does Identification Hurt or Help Under Identity Threat? The Exacerbating Role of Identity Centrality on Feeling Offended and the Buffering Role of Coworker Solidarity on Identity-Protection Behaviors

**Abstract**
Research on how identification impacts the experience of identity threat has uncovered mixed findings, which the current work helps resolve. We uncouple two conceptually distinct aspects of identification that research has conflated: identity centrality and solidarity. Identity centrality is focused inward on the extent to which an identity is important and salient to an individual, whereas solidarity is focused outward on the strength of the bond an individual perceives with another person or group. We propose that higher centrality exacerbates reactions to identity threat and ultimately predicts identity-protection responses (e.g., derogating or avoiding the threat source), whereas greater solidarity with coworkers mitigates negative responses stemming from identity threat. We consider two groups with whom individuals might experience solidarity in organizations: (1) others who share the threatened identity and (2) coworkers. We test and find support for our hypotheses in two studies using two-wave, time-lagged online experimental methods investigating political identity (Study 1) and working parent identity (Study 2). Notably, solidarity with coworkers provides a buffering effect while solidarity with others sharing the threatened identity does so to a lesser degree. Our work helps reconcile how and why identity centrality and certain forms of solidarity can differentially influence identity threat reactions.

**Research Question**
How and why do different facets of identification — specifically, identity centrality (an inward-focused trait reflecting how important an identity is to one's self-concept) versus solidarity (an outward-focused bond with another person or group) — differentially shape employees' emotional and behavioral responses to identity threats posed by coworkers, and does the referent of solidarity (others sharing the threatened identity vs. coworkers) matter for identity-protection responses?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV: Identity threat (manipulated; high vs. low via coworker vignettes targeting political identity in Study 1 and working parent identity in Study 2).
- DV(s): Three identity-protection responses — derogation, positive distinctiveness, and avoidance directed at the source of threat.
- Mediator: Feeling offended (composite of "offended," "insulted," "hurt").
- Moderator (first stage): Identity centrality of the threatened identity (10-item validated scale) — exacerbates the threat-to-feeling-offended link.
- Moderator (second stage): Coworker solidarity (Leach et al., 2008) — buffers the feeling-offended-to-protection-response links.
- Controls: solidarity with others sharing the threatened identity, political affiliation (Study 1), gender, child age, coworker identity centrality (Study 2).

Drawing on cognitive-motivational-relational (CMR) theory of emotions, the authors theorize that identity threat from a coworker triggers a primary appraisal in which individuals feel offended — particularly when the threatened identity is highly central to their self-concept and thus more sensitive to value-diminishing events. Feeling offended then prompts a secondary appraisal that mobilizes engagement coping (derogating the source, asserting positive distinctiveness) or disengagement coping (avoidance). Coworker solidarity blurs self-other boundaries, making harmful responses toward coworkers feel self-harming and thereby weakening dysfunctional behavioral reactions; solidarity with others sharing the threatened identity, by contrast, exhibits weaker and less consistent buffering, suggesting the relational referent of solidarity matters for which identity-protection behaviors are dampened.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper untangles identity centrality from solidarity — two facets of identification that prior identity-threat research has conflated — and shows they operate at different stages of the threat-response process: centrality exacerbates emotional appraisal (feeling offended), while solidarity moderates behavioral coping. By contrasting solidarity with the threatened-identity in-group versus solidarity with coworkers, the authors reveal that the relational referent of solidarity matters, with coworker solidarity providing the more robust buffer against dysfunctional identity-protection responses. The work also introduces a validated 10-item identity centrality measure that distinguishes centrality from solidarity, and extends Petriglieri's (2011) identity-threat framework empirically by linking CMR-theoretic emotional mediators to derogation, positive distinctiveness, and avoidance outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations and leaders pursuing diversity and inclusion should recognize that highly central identities make even benign coworker remarks potential triggers of dysfunctional protective behaviors, and that fostering coworker solidarity — through transformational leadership, employee-focused climates, and structured opportunities for cross-identity social connection — may neutralize derogation and avoidance reactions. Beyond affinity groups for shared marginalized identities, leaders should cultivate a superordinate shared identity that subsumes employees' diverse central identities. When designing policies that differentially affect identity groups (e.g., parental-leave changes), leaders should consult focus groups composed of those with high identity centrality to anticipate threat reactions.

**Limitations**
Reliance on self-report measures raises common-method-variance concerns, although Phase 1/Phase 2 temporal separation and experimental manipulation of the IV mitigate this; identity centrality and solidarity-with-identity were highly correlated in Study 1 (CFA/AVE tests support discriminant validity but third-party ratings of dependent variables would strengthen inference). The mediator (feeling offended) was measured contemporaneously with the dependent variables, weakening causal claims about emotion-to-behavior links. Behavioral measures captured intentions in hypothetical scenarios rather than actual workplace behaviors, raising external-validity concerns despite supplemental qualitative evidence (Supplement A) that real workplace identity threats elicit comparable responses. The model assumes identity centrality and solidarity are trait-like, but both may shift over time in response to identity-relevant events.

**Future Research**
Scholars should use experience-sampling methods to capture real-time emotional and behavioral responses to identity threats in naturalistic settings, and measure actual rather than self-reported behaviors via coworker/supervisor reports. Future work should explore the broader range of discrete emotions (e.g., anger when injustice is salient) elicited by different identity threats, treat threat severity as a continuum, and test whether the type of threatened identity (personal, role, or group) shapes which protection responses dominate and whether solidarity with the threatened-identity in-group interacts with coworker solidarity. Researchers should also investigate identity-restructuring responses (beyond identity-protection) and how organizational context — leadership style, collectivist culture, organizational justice — amplifies or dampens identity-threat consequences.
