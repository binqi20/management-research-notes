# The Paradox of Spatial and Relational Embeddedness: Tie Reinitiation after a Trust Violation

**Abstract**
How does embeddedness—spatial and relational—influence interfirm tie reinitiation when trust is violated? The taken-for-granted relationship between prior ties and repeat tie formation becomes complex when trust violations are introduced. Two divergent schools of thought emerge: an "embeddedness as a buffer" logic, wherein embedded partners may be more forgiving, versus an "et tu Brute" logic, wherein embedded partners feel betrayed. We tackle this theoretical tension by examining brokerage relationships. When spatially or relationally embedded, focal firm–broker ties further enhance trust but the ensuing higher expectations also make embedded trust more brittle due to the broker's divided loyalties, resulting in a steeper decline in trust after a violation. Our central hypotheses thus highlight a paradox of embeddedness: while spatial and relational trust foster repeat ties under normal circumstances, it takes proportionately longer for such embedded ties to be reinitiated when trust is violated. We use a unique hand-collected data set in the global dry cargo shipping industry with voyage-level data on 3,618 exchanges between a Copenhagen-based shipowner–operator (the focal firm) and 235 shipbrokers from 2011 to 2018 and a Weibull hazard rate estimator. We find support for the paradox of embeddedness when trust is violated in brokerage relationships.

**Research Question**
How does embeddedness — both spatial (collocation) and relational (a history of prior dyadic ties) — influence interfirm tie reinitiation when trust is violated, and does embeddedness in brokerage relationships act as a buffer that absorbs the shock of a trust violation, or does it instead aggravate the trust impairment such that embedded ties take proportionately longer to be reinitiated than non-embedded ones?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. Following a trust violation, a focal firm's likelihood of forming a repeat tie with a broker partner firm is lower (days to tie reinitiation increase).
H2. A focal firm's likelihood of forming a repeat tie with a shipbroker partner firm increases (days to tie reinitiation decrease) with the focal firm–broker partner's spatial embeddedness (collocation).
H3. A focal firm's likelihood of forming a repeat tie with a broker partner firm increases (days to tie reinitiation decrease) with the focal firm–broker partner's relational embeddedness.
H4. Following a trust violation, a focal firm's likelihood of forming a repeat tie lowers more (a greater percentage increase in days to tie reinitiation) for a spatially embedded (collocated) shipbroker partner than for a non-spatially embedded one.
H5. Following a trust violation, a focal firm's likelihood of forming a repeat tie lowers more (a greater percentage increase in days to tie reinitiation) for a relationally embedded shipbroker partner than for a less relationally embedded one.

**Mechanism Process**
- IVs: Trust violation (dummy = 1 if the shipbroker mediated a follow-on deal letting another ship operator capture the cargo intended for the focal firm); Focal firm-broker collocation (dummy = 1 if both firms are in Copenhagen); Relational embeddedness (cumulative count of prior deals between the focal firm and the broker).
- DVs: Hazard of focal firm-broker tie reinitiation, with paired components Reinitiation (dummy) and Days (waiting time to event in continuous time).
- Moderators (focal interactions): Focal firm-broker collocation x Trust violation (H4); Relational embeddedness x Trust violation (H5).
- Controls: Broker status (top-10 Lloyd's List ranking), Broker commission, DWT, Vessel type (six categories), Contract type (five categories), Focal firm-charterer collocation, Broker-charterer collocation, Voyage start year fixed effects.

The theorized mechanism is the paradox of embeddedness in brokered settings: while collocation and prior interactional history both enhance trust and routinely foster tie reinitiation, the same elevated expectations make embedded trust unusually brittle once a broker — whose role inherently involves divided loyalties between the focal firm and the charterer — engages in tertius-gaudens behavior. Spatial embeddedness binds managers in multiplex informal relationships generating affect-based interpersonal trust, so a violation feels like betrayal by a friend; relational embeddedness creates bilateral routines and norms whose breach signals expectancy violation and raises fears of future information leakage. In both cases the "et tu Brute" logic dominates the "embeddedness as a buffer" logic, producing a proportionately greater (relative) decline in tie-reinitiation hazard after violation among embedded brokers (a 226% increase in median days to reinitiation for collocated brokers vs. 65% for non-collocated; 127% increase for highly relationally embedded vs. 62% for low).

**Data & Measures**
Data come from a novel hand-collected voyage-level data set of 3,618 relational exchanges between the focal Copenhagen-based shipowner–operator and its 235 shipbroker partner firms in the global dry cargo shipping industry, 2011–2018 (the focal firm reinitiated ties with 169 of these brokers, yielding 3,383 tie reinitiations; non-reinitiated brokers are right-censored). Trust violations occur in 26.4% of cases. The data are supplemented by 14 semi-structured interviews (2016–2023) used for context and triangulation. Focal constructs were operationalized as follows:
- DV — focal firm–broker tie reinitiation (event-history hazard), with two components: Reinitiation (dummy = "1" if the focal firm reinitiated a tie with the broker during the observation period, "0" otherwise) and Days (continuous waiting time until reinitiation, or until censoring).
- IV — Trust violation: dummy = "1" for a voyage in which the shipbroker mediated a follow-on deal directing the intended cargo to another operator/charterer using the focal firm's ship (so the focal firm merely rents out its ship), "0" otherwise.
- IV — Focal firm–broker collocation (spatial embeddedness): dummy = "1" if both the focal firm and the broker are collocated in Copenhagen, "0" otherwise.
- IV — Relational embeddedness: cumulative index of the total number of deals since the focal firm and the shipbroker began working together.
- Interactions: Collocation × Trust violation (H4); Relational embeddedness × Trust violation (H5).
- Controls: Broker status (top-10 Lloyd's List ranking), Broker commission, DWT, Vessel type (six categories), Contract type, Focal firm–charterer collocation, Broker–charterer collocation, and voyage start-year fixed effects.

Estimation used a continuous-time Weibull regression model for multiple ordered events (event-history analysis) with cluster-robust standard errors at the focal firm–broker dyad level. The design is primarily associational; the authors address endogeneity and robustness through instrumental-variable estimation (Diaspora as instrument for collocation), Lee's (1983) two-stage selection model, broker and charterer fixed effects, an inverse-Gaussian frailty model, alternative hazard distributions (exponential, Gompertz, Cox), continuous geographic-proximity measures, floor-effect tests, and qualitative triangulation from the interviews.

**Key Findings**
All five hypotheses were supported in this single focal-firm setting (Weibull event-history models, Table 2):
- H1 supported: a trust violation lowers the hazard of tie reinitiation (Models 2–3, p < .01; Model 4, p < .05; Model 5, p < .10); the predicted median time to reinitiation rises from 29.32 days (no violation) to 49.01 days (violation).
- H2 supported: focal firm–broker collocation raises the reinitiation hazard (Models 2–5, p < .01); predicted median time falls from 35.58 days (non-collocated) to 10.97 days (collocated).
- H3 supported: relational embeddedness raises the reinitiation hazard (Models 2–5, p < .01); a one-standard-deviation increase cuts the marginal predicted median time from 26.04 to 11.47 days.
- H4 supported: after a violation, the collocated broker's median days to reinitiation rise 226.35% versus 64.70% for the non-collocated broker — a 161.65-percentage-point collocation penalty (Model 3: β = −0.65, p < .05; Model 5: β = −0.56, p < .10).
- H5 supported: after a violation, the highly relationally embedded broker's median days rise 126.78% versus 62.07% when relational embeddedness is low (Model 4: β = −0.00, p < .01; Model 5: β = −0.00, p < .05; the raw coefficient is ≈ −0.002, small because relational embeddedness is a large deal-count index).

Robustness checks (instrumental variables, Lee selection model, broker and charterer fixed effects, frailty, alternative distributions, continuous-proximity and floor-effect tests) leave the pattern intact. Post hoc (not hypothesized), spatial embeddedness is the more potent penalty than relational embeddedness: it has both a significantly larger main effect on tie reinitiation (χ²(1) = 17.09) and a greater post-violation penalizing effect (χ²(1) = 2.99, p < .10), and a further post hoc analysis finds an even more severe penalty for the collocated-and-relationally-embedded broker than for the non-collocated relationally embedded one.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to trust and network research by analyzing how embeddedness conditions the trust-violation–tie-persistence relationship in brokered settings, reconciling the "embeddedness as a buffer" and "et tu Brute" perspectives by showing that the broker's divided loyalty inverts the buffering logic and turns embeddedness into a sanctioning amplifier in relative terms. It links trust dynamics to network research on the fragility of brokerage relations and develops a paradoxical view of trust in tie repetition: spatial and relational trust foster repeat ties under normal conditions but lengthen the reinitiation clock disproportionately after a violation. The study also distinguishes the distinct mechanisms by which spatial embeddedness (informal, interpersonal, affect-based) and relational embeddedness (formal, aspatial, routine-based) translate into proportionate sanctioning, with post hoc evidence that spatial embeddedness exerts the more potent penalty.

**Practical Implication**
For shipbrokers and other market intermediaries who depend on repeat business and commissions, the findings imply that maintaining collocated or relationally embedded client relationships requires more than arbitrage value — it demands sharing private market information and tacit recognition of client needs, because trust violations in such close-knit ties trigger sharper relative declines in reinitiation than in arm's-length ones. For client firms, the results show that trust-violating brokers may still be retained for specific routes or cargoes since severing embedded ties is often infeasible, but slowing the speed and frequency of reinitiation acts as a clear sanctioning signal that registers dismay at the violation while preserving commercial flexibility.

**Limitations**
The voyage-level longitudinal database originates from a single focal firm in a single empirical setting (global shipbroking), which sacrifices breadth for depth and limits generalizability to other mediated markets, especially those where brokers and partners directly compete or are in horizontal relationships. The data lack information on all of the charterer's ties via this broker (only those involving the focal firm are observed) and on brokers' ties with other focal firms, so the frailty robustness check must be interpreted with caution. The study also cannot definitively separate trust-based from coordination/communication-cost or balance-theory explanations, though falsification tests with continuous proximity measures and charterer fixed effects support the trust-based mechanism.

**Future Research**
Future work could theorize about the effect of geography on brokerage stability when the broker and its partners stand in horizontal rather than vertical relationships — for example, brokerage structures within networks of strategic alliances — and disentangle trust violation from broker competitive behavior in such settings. The interplay between different types of embeddedness deserves deeper conceptual treatment, building on the paper's post hoc finding that collocated relationally embedded shipbrokers face a more severe reinitiation penalty than non-collocated relationally embedded ones, examining how spatial and relational embeddedness combine and substitute. Extending the framework to other mediated market contexts would test the generalizability of the paradox.
