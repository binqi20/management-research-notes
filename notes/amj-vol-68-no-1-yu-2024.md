# A Tale of Two Signals: Partner CSR versus CSI and Alliance Formation

**Abstract**
This study outlines two signaling mechanisms—trust and spillover—through which a potential partner's corporate social responsibility (CSR) and corporate social irresponsibility (CSI) affect alliance formation. Extending a key insight in signaling theory that positive and negative signals are conceptually distinct, we propose that which mechanism is dominant in explaining alliance formation varies between CSR and CSI. Specifically, we argue that the dominant signaling mechanism for CSR is the trust mechanism, through which CSR signals the moral character of a potential partner, which is used by the focal firm to infer the partner's trustworthiness. In contrast, CSI negatively affects alliance formation primarily through a spillover mechanism: CSI signals a potential partner's moral character to a firm's external stakeholders. Stakeholders' negative assessments based on this signal might then spill over to the focal firm if it forms an alliance with that partner. We further identify two contingency factors—namely, proximity and media coverage— that help verify the dominant signaling roles of partner CSR and CSI. Using a sample of alliances formed by high-tech firms between 1995 and 2016, we find support for our predictions.

**Research Question**
How do a potential partner's CSR and CSI affect the likelihood of alliance formation, and — given that positive and negative signals are conceptually distinct — which signaling mechanism (trust vs. spillover) dominates in each case?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Partners' CSR (sum of dyadic partners' standardized KLD strength scores across five dimensions); Partners' CSI (sum of standardized KLD concern scores).
- DV(s): Alliance formation in a given dyad-year (binary; contractual alliances and equity joint ventures from SDC).
- Moderators: Same location (binary, same CBSA); Partners' media coverage (summed unique major-outlet Factiva mentions per year, scaled by 1/100).
- Controls (firm-level summed across the dyad): firm size (log sales), ROA, Tobin's Q, R&D intensity, general alliance experience (5-year count), reputation (Fortune Most Admired). Dyad-level controls: partner-specific prior alliance experience, same industry (4-digit SIC), relative partner CSR, relative partner CSI, and year fixed effects.
- Theoretical mechanisms: (1) Trust mechanism — CSR/CSI signals a partner's moral character to the focal firm, shaping inferences about behavioral trustworthiness in the alliance (endogenous uncertainty, manageable via governance). (2) Spillover mechanism — CSR/CSI signals partner moral character to the focal firm's external stakeholders, who may extend their (positive or negative) evaluations to the focal firm via affiliation (exogenous uncertainty, beyond the firm's direct control).

The central argument is that signal valence determines the dominant mechanism: because firms have limited proactive influence over stakeholder spillovers (exogenous uncertainty) but considerable governance-based capacity to manage behavioral risk (endogenous uncertainty), and because negative signals are processed more intensively than positive ones, the trust mechanism dominates for CSR while the spillover mechanism dominates for CSI. This implies opposite moderation patterns: factors that reduce information asymmetry about partner trustworthiness (proximity, media coverage) should weaken the CSR-alliance link, while the same factors — by amplifying stakeholders' attention to partner misdeeds and the visibility of co-location — should strengthen the (negative) CSI-alliance link. The empirical pattern (negative CSR×proximity, CSR×media; negative CSI×proximity, CSI×media) is consistent with these dual mechanisms.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances signaling theory by providing a nuanced account of how positive and negative signals operate distinctly in strategic decisions: rather than negative signals being mere mirror images of positive ones, partner CSR and CSI work through different dominant mechanisms (trust for CSR, spillover for CSI). It contributes to the alliance literature by introducing partners' social activities as a novel and important antecedent of alliance formation and by linking specific signal valences to the two long-recognized partner-selection concerns — trustworthiness and spillover — thereby clarifying which type of concern each signal primarily addresses.

**Practical Implication**
Managers evaluating potential alliance partners should treat CSR as a primary cue about a partner's likely trustworthiness inside the alliance, and CSI as a primary indicator of reputational spillover risk to the focal firm's stakeholders. Reliance on CSR signals is most useful when alternative information about partner behavior is scarce (e.g., low proximity, low media coverage), whereas vigilance about CSI is especially warranted when partners are co-located or highly visible in the media, because spillover is then amplified.

**Limitations**
The empirical setting is restricted to U.S.-listed high-tech firms, which face heightened information asymmetry and somewhat lower CSI scores than firms in other industries; generalization to other contexts requires further work. The study treats CSR and CSI as separate distinct signals and does not theorize their interplay or asymmetric differences between partners' CSR/CSI levels. Because alliance formation requires mutual agreement and the dyadic partners are indistinguishable, the analysis is conducted at the dyad level and cannot distinguish initiator from receiver effects.

**Future Research**
Scholars should test the framework in other industry contexts, examine the interplay between partner CSR and CSI rather than treating them only as independent signals, and use field or survey data that distinguishes alliance initiators from receivers to study how partner role asymmetries shape signal use. Further work could also extend the framework beyond alliance formation to other alliance outcomes such as governance-mode choice, alliance stability, and innovative or financial performance.
