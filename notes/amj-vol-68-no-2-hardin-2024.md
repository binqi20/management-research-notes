# A Window into Coworkers' Worlds: The Relational Outcomes of Learning Vivid, Unintentional, and Nonwork-Related Information about Coworkers

**Abstract**
Research suggests that changes in where and how employees work may make it difficult to build meaningful coworker relationships. These same changes, however, may provide a window into their coworkers' nonwork lives—offering vivid and seemingly unintended insights into how their coworkers parent, what their hobbies are, and what their homes look like. In this way, the changing nature of work provides a potential paradox: it can simultaneously increase social distance between coworkers while potentially decreasing that distance by providing a vivid and unfiltered glimpse into their nonwork lives. Extending social penetration theory, we use a multimethod approach including experimental studies and surveys to examine how three dimensions of coworker learning—learning that is (1) more vivid, (2) perceived as unintentional, and (3) about a coworker's nonwork life—lead an employee to view that coworker as more authentic, trustworthy, and human. Moreover, seeing a coworker in these ways increases an employee's actual and intended investment in their personal and professional relationship with that coworker. Our findings contribute to the literatures on work relationships, social cognition, and the physical work environment.

**Research Question**
How do three dimensions of interpersonal learning about a coworker — vividness, perceived unintentionality, and nonwork relatedness — alter how employees perceive that coworker (as authentic, trustworthy, and human) and motivate them to invest in a multiplex (combined personal + professional) relationship with that coworker?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): vividness of learning (high vs. low; e.g., video vs. audio Zoom stimulus); perceived unintentionality (unintended interruption vs. deliberate disclosure); nonwork relatedness (coworker pet/family vs. work accomplishment)
- DV(s): multiplex relational investment (intent to pursue future work collaboration and friendship in Study 1; actual personal and professional investment since the incident in Study 2)
- Mediators: perceived authenticity, perceived trustworthiness, perceived humanness of the coworker
- Moderators / controls: information valence (negativity), participant gender, coworker gender (Study 2 controls)

Drawing on social penetration theory, the authors argue that how learning happens (vividness, perceived unintentionality) and what is learned (nonwork-related content) jointly shape three relational social judgments. Vivid learning intensifies emotional resonance and approximates real-life human presence; perceived unintentional disclosure functions as an unfiltered backstage glimpse that signals reduced impression management and prompts reciprocal vulnerability; nonwork-related content individuates the coworker beyond their work role. Together these perceptions of greater authenticity, trustworthiness, and humanness motivate employees to broaden and deepen relational investment, sometimes producing a "fast-forward" leap in intimacy that violates social penetration theory's incremental, intentional-disclosure tenet.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends and partially upends social penetration theory by demonstrating that nonwork-related, perceived-unintentional, vivid disclosures can drive non-incremental "leaps" in coworker intimacy, in contrast to the theory's tenet of slow, intentional, escalating disclosure. It contributes to social cognition by showing that within-channel variation in how (vividly, unintentionally) and what (nonwork) employees learn matters more for relational judgments than between-channel media-richness differences. It also opens a new theoretical front on the physical work-from-home environment as a relational affordance, complementing prior office-based research on workspace and relationship development.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should treat work-from-home as a potential relational asset, not only a productivity setting: video meetings from home can reveal authentic, humanizing nonwork glimpses that strengthen coworker investment. Employees should weigh the relational cost of curating blurred or false Zoom backgrounds, since hiding nonwork cues can preclude the vivid, perceived-unintentional learning that motivates coworkers to invest. The findings caution against orchestrated "authenticity" — the relational benefit hinges on the disclosure being seen as unintentional rather than as image management.

**Limitations**
The studies measure only short-term intent or recent investment, leaving open how these effects persist or evolve as relationships repeat such disclosures over time. The authors capture only the perspective of the employee learning about the coworker, not the coworker who is learned about, limiting insight into the dyadic and potentially asymmetric consequences of this learning. The phenomenological exploration also yielded relatively few negatively valenced incidents, suggesting unobserved boundary conditions around when negative unintentional disclosures help versus harm relationships.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine longer-term affective and behavioral consequences of these learning dimensions (e.g., avoidance, ambivalence, ongoing engagement); explore additional learning dimensions such as the source of information (direct observation vs. gossip), the timing/location of learning, and audience size; and investigate contextual antecedents like organizational culture, open office plans, and individual differences (openness, extraversion). Researchers should also adopt dyadic designs (live video recordings, paired interviews) to capture how the person being learned about experiences such disclosures.
