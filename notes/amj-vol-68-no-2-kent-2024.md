# Chasing Storms: Temporal Work to Foster Group Engagement under Uncertainty

**Abstract**
Experiences of group engagement have a significant impact on members' performance and satisfaction with work. In temporally uncertain settings, however, unpredictable transitions between idle and busy periods disrupt engagement. Through an ethnographic study of storm chasing teams in Tornado Alley, we investigate how groups foster engagement under uncertainty. We found that while efficient coordination helped teams get to storms, it could frustrate members' engagement with tasks across unpredictable transitions. We develop a process model explaining how groups use temporal work to foster immersive episodes of engagement across transitions that are hard to control or predict. We show that broadening focus and compressing duration foster experiences of temporal control that enable engaging transitions into busy periods of work, whereas narrowing focus and extending duration foster experiences of immersive duration that enable satisfying transitions into idle periods. We develop a temporal perspective to engagement at work, showing how groups set the temporal context within which they encounter work episodes and how this context enables or constrains engagement. We also advance a durational perspective for organizational temporality research, elucidating how experiences of duration represent a key issue to be managed in organizations.

**Research Question**
How do groups foster engagement across the idle and busy periods in temporally uncertain work?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: engagement inertia (inertia of expectations vs. inertia of immersion); experienced duration as subjective and modifiable; temporal uncertainty as boundary condition
- Process / phases: idle periods → idle-to-encounter transitions → encounter (busy) periods → encounter-to-idle transitions → return to idle periods
- Practices (forms of temporal work): broadening focus (constructing multiple possible futures; restraining immersion in specific aspects of the future); compressing experienced duration (temporal autonomy, detours/games/socializing, filler tasks); narrowing focus on the present encounter (running commentary, redundant tasks, dismissing forgone targets); extending duration (bringing near future into present; bringing past into present)
- Triggering conditions: unpredictable transitions between idle and busy periods that disrupt the durations needed for mutual immersion
- Emergent outcomes: an experience of temporal control (enabling willing engagement at encounter onset) and an experience of immersive duration (enabling willing disengagement at encounter end)
- Boundary conditions: extreme temporal uncertainty in self-governing teams whose members have stake in core tasks

Across idle and busy periods, groups manage members' shared expectations and experience of duration to foster immersive episodes of engagement. In idle periods, broadening focus and compressing duration produce a sense of temporal control that prevents inertia of expectations; in encounters, narrowing focus and extending duration produce immersive duration that prevents inertia of immersion. The two interdependent transitions are managed as a balancing act between opposing forms of temporal work tailored to upcoming events and members' anticipated reactions.

**Data & Measures**
Data corpus: roughly 1,400 hours of first-author ethnographic fieldwork with three U.S.-based storm chasing teams over five trips (College Team, 20 days, 2018-2019; Private Team, 35 days, 2018-2019; Commercial Team, 7 days, 2017), plus 22 semi-structured and 20 informational interviews with chasers, tour operators, meteorologists, scientists, and equipment retailers, and archival data (145 issues of Storm Track magazine, Stormtrack.org forums/Discord, participants' chasing logs, memoirs, and documentaries/films). Analysis proceeded through open coding and memo writing (Glaser & Strauss), abstracting into a Gioia-style first-/second-/third-order data structure. The focal construct, group engagement, was operationalized by coding interaction episodes using the fieldworker's attention to members' vocalizations, posture, and facial expressions, guided by interaction ritual studies of focus and immersion: high-engagement episodes involved members frantically debating targets, shouting, and leaning over seats to point at radar screens or storm clouds, whereas low-engagement episodes involved ignoring radar screens, not participating in decision-making, wandering off, or reading emails during intercepts. Four extended vignettes and member checks with team leaders (Bill, Richard, Chris) tested and refined the process model. The design is interpretive and processual; the paper makes no causal-identification claims.

**Key Findings**
Efficiently structuring and coordinating time helped teams reach storms but could exacerbate "engagement inertia" across the unpredictable transitions between idle and busy periods. Two transitions each carry a characteristic inertia and a corrective form of temporal work:
- Idle-to-encounter transition — inertia of expectations: persisting attention to a scenario expected in downtime produces unsuitably low engagement when the intercept arrives with unexpected timing or quality (College Team's "Real Chase Day": students stayed fixated on a promised tornado and disengaged even while intercepting two severe storms and large hail). Teams preempt this in idle periods by broadening focus (constructing multiple possible futures; restraining immersion in specific aspects of the future) and compressing experienced duration, fostering an "experience of temporal control" that enables willing engagement at encounter onset (Private Team's "Desert Odyssey").
- Encounter-to-idle transition — inertia of immersion: persistent immersion in core tasks produces unsuitably high engagement and resistance to disengaging as work demands stop (Private Team's "Repositioning Act": a member refused to pull off a storm, creating a safety risk and later burnout). Teams preempt this in encounters by narrowing focus on the present encounter and extending duration (bringing the near future and the past into the present), fostering an "experience of immersive duration" that enables willing, satisfied disengagement at encounter end (College Team's "Sun and Rain Games").

The paper integrates these into a process model in which managing engagement is a balancing act between opposing forms of temporal work (broadening vs. narrowing focus; compressing vs. extending duration), tuned to the predictability of events, members' anticipated reactions, and whether the immediate goal is to get members to engage or to disengage. Findings are illustrated through paired vignettes rather than tested as hypotheses.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a temporally founded view of group engagement, showing that engagement and disengagement are transition processes shaped by the temporal context in which groups encounter their work, not merely by task design or recovery intervals. It also develops a durational perspective for organizational temporality research, recasting duration as a subjective and modifiable experience that members extend or compress through temporal work, and highlighting risks of letting members invest in precise expectations under uncertainty.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders and members in temporally uncertain settings (e.g., emergency response, broadcast journalism, professional services) can foster performance and satisfaction by deliberately broadening expectations, compressing the experience of waiting, narrowing focus during encounters, and extending immersive duration through preparatory work, dramatized transitions, and debriefing rituals.

**Limitations**
The study examines small-group interactions in a setting of extreme temporal uncertainty, so the scale and visibility of temporal work observed may exceed what occurs in less uncertain or more routinized settings. The empirical context (storm chasing) blends serious leisure and work, and findings may require adaptation in settings with more institutionalized durational norms or interorganizational contexts.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how temporal work manifests in settings with more predictable or routinized transitions, how interdepartmental or interorganizational settings shape durational experiences, how "temporal macro-structures" in large-scale projects influence experienced duration, and how individuals develop "temporal competence" to feel in control and immerse themselves in work despite incomplete control over events.
