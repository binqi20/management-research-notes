# The Psychological Design of Firm Boundaries: Preempting Escalating Commitment through Buy vs. Make

**Abstract**
Strong ownership of the product development process, while facilitating the generation of new products, can exacerbate escalating commitment and impede timely termination. We examine how managers, with foresight, use buy vs. make decisions to create psychological and organizational distance from a product and preempt escalating commitment. Using detailed product-level data from the fashion industry and its unique features, we show that managers contract manufacture risky products even when they can be readily manufactured in-house, especially core products that are central to a firm's identity and hence more prone to escalating commitment. Our findings highlight cognitive bias as a source of internal transaction cost and bring its preemption to the forefront of buy vs. make decisions. Our study develops a cognitive foundation for transaction cost theory that does not depend on opportunism or asset specificity and highlights the psychological designing of firm boundaries as a strategy for improving the decision environment.

**Research Question**
How do boundedly rational managers use buy vs. make decisions ex ante to weaken psychological and organizational ownership over a product, thereby preempting escalating commitment and lowering the internal transaction cost of timely product termination?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. Products at a higher risk of commercial failure are more likely to be contract manufactured (buy) even when they can be readily produced in-house (make).
H2. The positive effect of failure risk on contract manufacturing (H1) is greater for "core" products, i.e., categories central to a brand's identity and competitive advantage that cultivate stronger ownership and are more prone to escalating commitment.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): product failure risk (proxied by fashionability category — basic, semi-trendy, trendy — and by realized percentage sold / "miss" indicator)
- DV(s): BUY (binary indicator equal to one if a product is contract-manufactured rather than produced via internal CMT); secondary DVs include per-unit cost ratio and log nominal production cost
- Mediators: psychological ownership and organizational ownership generated during new-product development (theorized, not measured directly)
- Moderators: "core" product category (high strategic importance / brand identity) — bottoms in men's brands and historically high-share categories — strengthens the failure-risk → buy relationship; quick-response (QR) production — which de-risks trendy designs — reverses it

Managers anticipate that internal production cultivates strong psychological attachment (control, intimate knowledge, invested time), which fuels endowment-, IKEA-, and sunk-cost-style biases that delay termination of failing products. Because escalating commitment resists ex post debiasing, managers proactively design firm boundaries to create psychological and organizational distance: outsourcing risky and core products via contract manufacturing "blackboxes" development, mobilizes fewer internal stakeholders, and makes failure smaller and more reversible. Firms accept 9–16% higher production costs on trendy outsourced products as the price of preempting escalating commitment.

**Data & Measures**
Data: a product-level archival panel from "Life Holding Co.," a diversified multinational financial holding company, covering 21 independent high-end fashion brands and 249,705 products, obtained during an internal due-diligence process; supplemented by roughly two years of fieldwork (interviews with designers, sourcing teams, and merchandisers) and a comparative case study of Zara. Production-cost analyses use a 49,646-product subset with cost data.
- DV: BUY, a binary variable set to one if a product was contract-manufactured rather than produced internally through CMT (cut, make, and trim). Secondary DVs: the cost ratio (per-unit production cost normalized by retail price) and log nominal production cost.
- IV (failure risk), two proxies: (1) product fashionability (basic / semi-trendy / trendy); (2) realized sales performance, operationalized as "Miss" (=1 if a product sold less than 75% of produced quantity) and percentage sold.
- Core (H2) proxies: CORE (=1 if a category's average percentage sold over the sample period exceeds 80%, ~10% of the portfolio) and Bottoms (pants/shorts) compared across men's- vs. women's-focused brands; quick-response (QR) production serves as a de-risking test of the mechanism.
- Estimation: OLS and logit regressions with year-by-season, product-category, brand, product-category-by-brand, and (for one brand) contract-manufacturer fixed effects; standard errors two-way clustered at the brand-by-product-category and year-by-season levels with a Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller (2008) small-cluster adjustment.
- Identification is associational: the author explicitly does not claim causal inference, instead leveraging the factoryless, concurrent-sourcing setting and granular fixed effects to rule out alternative explanations (asset obsolescence, missing capabilities, learning).

**Key Findings**
- H1 supported. Higher-failure-risk (trendy) products are more likely to be contract-manufactured even though they can be made in-house: BUY is 6.6% more likely for Trendy with product-category fixed effects (p < .01) and still 3.1% more likely under the more granular product-category-by-brand fixed effects (p < .01); the Semi-Trendy effect attenuates to negligible once product-category fixed effects are added. Using realized sales, BUY is ~2.1% more likely for "miss" products, with the Miss coefficient positive throughout but statistically significant only for trendy products, and a 10% rise in percentage sold lowers the likelihood of buy by ~0.6%.
- Mechanism (falsification) test: the Trendy coefficient is positive and significant for regular products (0.052, p < .01) but negative and significant for quick-response products (-0.084, p < .10), consistent with failure risk (rather than idiosyncratic features of trendy designs) driving the result.
- H2 supported. Failure risk has a larger positive effect on buy for core products: the Trendy coefficient is roughly three times larger for core than noncore categories (0.076 vs. 0.028; p < .10 for the difference). With the Miss proxy the core coefficient is about twice the noncore one (0.039 vs. 0.019), but this difference is not statistically significant at 10%. In the bottoms test, the Bottoms x Miss interaction is positive and significant for men's brands (where bottoms are core) but insignificant for women's brands.
- Cost of preemption (associational, Table 6): contract manufacturing is associated with higher production cost concentrated in trendy products, at +16.0% in log nominal cost for Trendy and +2.1% for Semi-Trendy, but -2.9% (lower) for Basic; the overall cost ratio is 0.002 higher (~1.0%). The author summarizes the premium as a 9% to 16% higher production cost that firms pay to preempt escalating commitment.
- Generalization: the Zara case is consistent with H1. Zara internally produces trendy designs that its quick-response model has already de-risked (i.e., it makes safe products), rather than contradicting the risk-to-buy logic.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a cognitive foundation for transaction cost theory that does not rely on opportunism or asset specificity, recasting buy not as a last-resort response to external market failure but as a debiasing intervention against internal hierarchical failure. By framing buy vs. make as part of the "psychological design of organizations," it bridges TCT, behavioral economics on escalating commitment, and choice architecture, and adds termination cost — alongside production and coordination costs — as a constitutive concern in the theory of the firm.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of innovation-intensive firms should resist the default that core or strategically important products must be produced in-house: when failure risk is high, contract manufacturing — even at meaningfully higher unit cost — can pay for itself by enabling timely exit and preserving capacity for future experimentation. Sourcing decisions should be evaluated jointly on generation benefits and termination costs, with explicit attention to managers' own psychological vulnerabilities.

**Limitations**
The study does not claim causal inference and cannot rule out unobserved product attributes correlated with failure risk. The factoryless, concurrent-sourcing setting of high-end fashion enhances internal validity but limits generalizability to asset-heavy or capability-intensive industries. Psychological ownership is theorized rather than directly measured, and identifying information on contract manufacturers is available for only a subset of brands.

**Future Research**
Future work should employ field and laboratory experiments to manipulate psychological attachment directly and to observe underlying psychological constructs that archival data only proxy. Researchers should examine how managers' own characteristics (regulatory focus, risk preference, overconfidence) interact with cognitive biases to shape buy vs. make choices, and extend the framework to other biases (envy, home bias, homophily, 1/N allocation) and to hybrid governance forms such as alliances and joint ventures across asset-heavy industries.
