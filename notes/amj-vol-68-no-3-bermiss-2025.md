# Reengaging with the Classics

**Abstract**
Classics in management offer a wealth of ideas and knowledge—arguably, wealth that is underutilized.

**Research Question**
How can scholars effectively draw on management classics to inform developments in the world of today, particularly amid transformational phenomena such as AI, digital platform consolidation, and geopolitical polarization?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Management classics — widely influential works that have shaped theory and practice — remain valuable but are often subject to "institutional forgetting," typified citation, and selective attention; substantive reengagement (rather than ceremonial citation) generates new theoretical and practical insights for transformational times.
- Three pathways for reengagement:
  - Revise: challenge taken-for-granted assumptions and propose substantive changes (e.g., Sine, Mitsuhashi & Kirsch 2006 revising Burns & Stalker 1961 by showing new ventures benefit from mechanistic, not organic, structures).
  - Repurpose: leverage insights from a classic to resolve a problem in an adjacent theory (e.g., Colquitt & Rodell 2011 repurposing Blau's 1964 social exchange theory to reconceive justice-trust relations).
  - Rediscover: identify previously overlooked theoretical claims in a classic and bring them back into focus (e.g., Trevor 2001 rediscovering untested ideas in March & Simon 1958 about individual-level determinants of voluntary turnover).
- Application domains: AI era (job design theory, bounded rationality), large digital tech (Chandler's Strategy and Structure), and political polarization (Burns's transformational leadership).
- Boundary condition / caution: reengagement requires "vigilant reading" that situates classic theory in its political, social, and economic context rather than blindly idealizing or transposing it.

The editorial argues that scholarly progress is cumulative and path-dependent, which causes classic works to be selectively read and "typified" in citation practices; deliberate revising, repurposing, and rediscovering counteract this drift and let foundational theories address new phenomena while clarifying scope conditions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial offers a typology — revise, repurpose, rediscover — for substantive engagement with management classics, distinguishing it from normative or ceremonial citation. It clarifies how each pathway produces a distinct kind of theoretical contribution (new boundary conditions, novel cross-domain application, recovery of latent claims) and links these pathways to contemporary phenomena (AI, large tech firms, geopolitical leadership) where classics regain explanatory power.

**Practical Implication**
The editors call on scholars, doctoral advisors, and journal reviewers to move beyond ceremonial citation of classics — introducing PhD students to foundational texts, encouraging deep engagement, and proactively suggesting opportunities to reengage with classics in reviews — so that contemporary research on AI, digital platforms, and geopolitical disruption can build on, rather than reinvent, prior insights.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the three approaches they identify are "merely indicative, and not exhaustive"; surely additional ways to reengage with the classics exist beyond the typology offered.

**Future Research**
The editorial invites scholars to reengage with classics across three illustrative domains: (a) the AI era, by extending bounded rationality, job design, and team-effectiveness classics to AI-augmented work; (b) large digital technology firms, by revisiting Chandler's strategy-structure thesis; and (c) leadership amid political polarization and geopolitical tension, by reexamining Burns's transformational leadership in light of values conflict and instability.
