# How Identity Structure Influences Identity Adoption: The Case of Hybrid Entrepreneurs

**Abstract**
Scholars have theorized that individuals have an intrapersonal identity network comprised of multiple identities and the relationships between them. However, it remains unclear how individuals cognitively structure these networks and how such structures influence the adoption of a new identity. This matters because such adoptions facilitate growth and adaptation, and the literature hints that the structure will affect the adoption process. To explore these issues, we conducted an inductive, qualitative study of hybrid entrepreneurs (specifically, academic entrepreneurs). Our findings reveal that individuals combine four dimensions (depth, overlap, density, and range) to create one of three cognitive structures (hierarchical, integrative, or web), and that high levels of one dimension may preclude high levels of certain others. Further, we develop a model of how these structures impact the adoption of a new identity by focusing attention on some dimensions while limiting attention to others. In doing so, they guide individuals into a unique adoption path involving a certain need that identity adoption satisfies by serving a particular function in the network. This function influences the logic used in determining whether the new identity becomes stabilized in the network. Thus, our theory contributes to the literatures on multiple identities and hybrid entrepreneurs.

**Research Question**
How does the cognitive structure of individuals' intrapersonal identity network influence the process of adopting a new identity? To answer this, the authors first ask: How do individuals cognitively structure their intrapersonal identity network in the first place?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: intrapersonal identity network; four dimensions (depth, overlap, density, range); three structure types (hierarchical, integrative, web); experienced needs; identity functions; evaluative logics
- Identity structures: hierarchical (high depth), integrative (high overlap), web (high density), with horizontal range informing each
- Adoption paths and needs: proving path (need for upward affirmation; new identity acts as conduit); holism path (need for coherence in a purpose; new identity acts as partner); distributive path (need for action-based resources; new identity serves utilitarian function)
- Evaluative logics for stabilizing/revising adoption: competing logic (proving), converging logic (holism), additive logic (distributive)
- Outcome: reinforcement or revision of the new identity's position in the network

The process model holds that an individual's cognitive structure focuses attention on some dimensions while limiting others, which makes a particular identity-based need salient. Individuals adopt a new identity to serve a function corresponding to that need (conduit, partner, or utilitarian resource provider) and then evaluate the adoption with a logic that matches the function. Whether the new identity is reinforced or revised in the network depends on whether it satisfies the underlying need without unduly threatening other identities, producing a recursive structuring process.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study identifies three distinct cognitive structures (hierarchical, integrative, web) built from a common set of four dimensions, challenging the long-held assumption that hierarchies of importance are pervasive and showing that high levels of certain dimensions tend to preclude others. It develops a process model linking each structure to a unique adoption path, experienced need, identity function, and evaluative logic, thereby explaining why provisional identities are reinforced or revised. It also reframes hybrid and entrepreneurial identity research by showing that the broader network of identities, not just the academic-entrepreneur dyad, shapes how an entrepreneurial identity emerges and stabilizes.

**Practical Implication**
Assessing a person's identity structure reveals what they treat as opportunities versus threats and which incentives or framings will resonate. Entrepreneurial training programs (for example, NSF I-Corps) are likely to be more effective when tailored to the trainee's structure: emphasizing market validation for hierarchical structures, alignment with a higher purpose for integrative structures, and resource flows for web structures. Managers can use the same insight to design role assignments and rewards that fit employees' structures.

**Limitations**
The sample is restricted to U.S. academic entrepreneurs who voluntarily entered entrepreneurship and were therefore likely to have well-articulated identity sets, which may limit transferability to less voluntary or non-academic contexts. Although the authors believe their tripartite typology and adoption model travel broadly, this generalizability has not been empirically tested beyond the present setting. The study also does not speak to antecedents of structures or to outcomes beyond identity adoption.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how each identity structure influences thinking, feeling, and acting beyond identity adoption, including links to performance, growth, and well-being; assess transferability of the tripartite typology to other work settings; explore conscious versus unconscious identity adoption; investigate what it means for an identity to "fit" a structure and how individuals tolerate misfit; and identify antecedents (developmental, individual differences, identity motives) that shape which structure emerges.
