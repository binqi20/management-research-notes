# Falling Off the Unicorn: The Maintenance of Unrealistic Startup Labor Narratives

**Abstract**
This study examines the persistence of unrealistic generalized startup labor narratives, which promise autonomy, rapid career growth, and significant financial rewards, despite high failure rates and unmet expectations. Drawing on interviews with founders, employees, and venture capitalists in high-growth tech startups, we explore why and how these narratives endure. Our findings highlight three primary mechanisms driving narrative maintenance: (1) structural misalignments between investor, founder, and employee incentives; (2) social pressures within the entrepreneurial ecosystem that reinforce optimism, and (3) structural constraints in the labor market that restrict employee mobility and career alternatives. Contrary to prior research focusing mainly on founders, we reveal that investors and employees also actively participate in sustaining these narratives despite their divergence from reality. This study contributes to the literature on entrepreneurial storytelling by demonstrating how the structural configuration of high-tech investing creates labor market constraints and social dynamics that foster false narrative maintenance, yielding hidden costs for startup employees while benefiting investors and founders.

**Research Question**
Why and how are startup labor narratives maintained even as employees' expectations remain unmet? The authors examine how generalized narratives promising autonomy, rapid career growth, and financial rewards persist in VC-backed high-growth startups despite repeated failure to deliver on those promises.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: generalized startup labor narratives (autonomy, rapid career growth, financial windfalls) vs. divergent lived experiences; entrepreneurial narrative revision vs. narrative maintenance; employee agency vs. labor-market entrapment.
- Process / phases: four startup stages structure the dynamic — (1) resource acquisition (opportunity narratives established and labor narratives leveraged), (2) launch (talent attraction and socialization), (3) growth and adaptation (recrafting external pivot narratives), and (4) exit (entrapment in startup labor market and narrative reproduction).
- Mechanisms of maintenance: (1) misaligned incentives between VCs (portfolio diversification + de-diversification of individual ventures), founders, and employees; (2) social pressures of normative optimism, motivated sampling, ageism, and stigma of failure; (3) structural labor-market features producing a parallel startup labor market with restricted mobility back to established firms.
- Emergent outcomes: employees shift from accepting to actively propagating narratives they privately doubt, becoming "entrapped in startup markets and narratives."

The process unfolds when VCs structure investments as portfolios that absorb individual failures while pushing portfolio companies to de-diversify and "swing for the fences." Founders and investors knowingly use ambitious labor narratives to attract talent, while employees, once inside, find themselves in a secondary labor market with limited exit options. Social pressure to remain optimistic, fear of being labeled a failure, ageism, and motivated sampling of positive stories suppress dissent and keep employees publicly endorsing narratives they have learned to distrust.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends entrepreneurial narrative theory by shifting attention from narrative revision (how founders adapt stories for external audiences) to narrative maintenance, showing investors are fully aware of overly optimistic promises and actively sustain them. It identifies employees as active narrative co-producers rather than passive recipients, and links the perpetuation-of-false-narratives literature (pluralistic ignorance, socially constructed valuation) to the specific structural configuration of VC-backed entrepreneurship.

**Practical Implication**
Employees should join broader information networks outside the startup community to form more realistic expectations about salaries, stock packages, and developmental paths. Founders should provide transparency on risks and invest in retention and learning. Investors and policymakers should challenge unfounded narratives, support entrepreneurship education that reflects realistic career trajectories, and improve safety nets for startup employees.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on VC-backed high-growth startups in the U.S. and Denmark across four high-tech industries, so dynamics may differ for bootstrapped or independent startups where investor pressures are weaker. Retrospective interviews may introduce bias, although the authors argue any bias would work against their findings. Comparative outcomes across firm types holding employee ability constant remain unobserved.

**Future Research**
Future work should pursue longitudinal analyses of career, health, and personal outcomes for comparable employees across established firms, VC-backed startups, and independent startups. Additional qualitative work is needed on young firms to understand startup work first-hand, and further research could map the distribution of risks, rewards, benefits, and harms across stakeholders as entrepreneurial ecosystems evolve.
