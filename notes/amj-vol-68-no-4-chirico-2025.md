# Calm in the Storm: Job Security and Post-Merger Performance in Family versus Nonfamily Firms

**Abstract**
Building on social identity theory, we theorize and find that in a merger, paired family firms are better able to retain employees and improve post-merger performance compared to other merger pairs. We contribute to social identity theory by theorizing better post-merger performance as mediated by job security for family firm combinations. We also contribute to the job security and merger and acquisition literature by examining how job security and post-merger performance vary based on the paired social identity of owners. In addition to identity similarity, the type of identity also matters in mergers. We argue that family owner social identity similarity fosters greater integration between merging parties while allowing family owner pairs to retain some autonomy through their employees, thereby maximizing post-merger performance. Our data on private Swedish firms, complemented by 11 qualitative interviews across five countries and three continents, confirm that family mergers outperform other merger combinations via job security. In a supplementary critical experiment examining industry dissimilarity, we compare the socioemotional wealth perspective—which emphasizes loss aversion and predicts family firms' unrelated diversification avoidance—to social identity theory. Consistent with social identity theory, our results show that both job security and post-merger performance improve with unrelated family firm mergers.

**Research Question**
How do owners' social identities (family vs. nonfamily) of the two firms involved in a merger influence employee job security and post-merger performance, and how does industry dissimilarity moderate this relationship?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Merger type (family-family vs. nonfamily-nonfamily; family-family vs. family-nonfamily), with family-family merger as the reference category grounded in shared family-owner social identity.
- DV(s): Post-merger performance (industry-adjusted ROA at four-digit SNI; alternative residualized change score).
- Mediators: Job security (1 minus the percentage reduction in employees in the post-merger years).
- Moderators: Industry dissimilarity (computed from overlap in SNI digits between the two merging firms) — supplementary critical experiment.
- Controls: Differences in firm age, CEO age, sales, employees, prior merger experience, slack, past ROA, environmental dynamism, geographic distance, log of time, plus an endogeneity score from 2SRI using percentage of family firms in the same municipality and industry as instruments.

Family owners share a salient, long-term social identity that fosters in-group recognition between merging family firms, reinforcing homophily-based agreement on strategy and a stewardship orientation toward employees. Two paired family owners therefore offer greater job security, preserving each family's tacit knowledge and identity through their respective employees, which sustains commitment, productivity, and ultimately post-merger performance. In contrast, mergers involving at least one nonfamily firm import diffuse social identities (founder, investor, partner, foreign-controlled) that prioritize short-term efficiency and cost cutting, generating layoffs that erode the combined resource base. Under industry dissimilarity, family-family pairs further benefit because each family can retain autonomy in its own segment while shared identity enables integration — turning unrelated diversification into a complementarity rather than the SEW-predicted liability.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends social identity theory to merger contexts by showing that beyond identity similarity, the *type* of owner identity matters: family-owner identity is uniquely strong and salient compared to other ownership types, generating a "calm in the storm" advantage that resolves the integration-autonomy tension via shared identity plus employee retention. It revises Neckebrouck et al.'s (2018) finding that family firms are worse organizational stewards by showing that in transformative events such as mergers, family firms are simultaneously better organizational stewards (higher job security) and better financial stewards (higher post-merger performance). The critical experiment against the socioemotional wealth perspective overturns the standard SEW prediction by showing family-firm mergers perform best under industry dissimilarity through cross-industry complementarities.

**Practical Implication**
Owners and managers should weigh social identity factors — not only financial fit — when selecting merger partners, as identity compatibility shapes both economic and human outcomes. Family-firm owners are advised to seek other family firms as partners, particularly in unrelated industries where complementarities can be exploited while each family retains autonomy. More generally, leaders should resist using post-merger employee downsizing as an "easy lever," because workforce reduction erodes the merged firm's resource base, demoralizes survivors, and undermines performance.

**Limitations**
The study emphasizes positive aspects of family-firm characteristics and does not fully model negatives such as bifurcation bias, parental altruism, and nepotism, which could attenuate the documented effects. It treats all family firms as possessing a uniformly strong familial social identity, while in reality family firms are heterogeneous in identity strength and form. The Swedish private-firm setting and the inability to exhaustively test all competing mechanisms (e.g., behavioral theories, resource dependence, hubris) constrain generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future studies could replicate the findings in other countries and industries, and examine fine-grained differences in how family-firm partners manage dysfunctional conflicts and integration with nonfamily employees. Researchers could investigate family-firm heterogeneity in the salience of family identity, and compare merger choice and post-merger performance between publicly traded and privately held family firms. Additional work could explore alternative theoretical mechanisms (resource-based view, imitation, hubris) and specific synergy motives behind workforce reduction versus other forms of asset restructuring.
