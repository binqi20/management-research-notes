# Fostering Entrepreneurship in Nonmetro Areas: Ecosystem Architects as Conduits of Knowledge Spillovers

**Abstract**
Prevailing theories hold that regional entrepreneurial activity is a function of each region's preexisting local resource endowments. But these theories do not explain entrepreneurial activity in regions that possess few of the requisite endowments, such as nonmetropolitan areas, which often lack many of the resources conducive to entrepreneurship. Drawing on institutional theory and knowledge spillover theory, we theorize that individuals engaged in institutional work play a key role in catalyzing entrepreneurial activity within such regions by serving as conduits of voluntary, horizontal knowledge spillovers. We propose that their ability to do so is enhanced when they access nonlocal tacit knowledge from others working in similar contexts. We further propose that, consistent with sociological theories of social capital and trust, local socioeconomic conditions can constrain the absorption of such knowledge and thereby local venturing in their home communities. Empirically, we study these propositions through a field intervention: an in-person gathering fostering tacit knowledge spillovers among individual entrepreneurial ecosystem architects from nonmetro counties in a Midwestern U.S. state. We conducted additional tests of alternative specifications, identification, and behavioral impacts. Taken together, these arguments and findings deepen our theoretical understanding of an important pathway in the development of regional entrepreneurial ecosystems.

**Research Question**
How do locally rooted "entrepreneurial ecosystem architects" (EEAs) in less-endowed nonmetropolitan regions catalyze entrepreneurial activity, and how is their efficacy as conduits of voluntary, horizontal knowledge spillovers moderated by community-level social capital and social trust?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): A binary indicator for whether one or more EEAs from a county participated in the cross-county in-person gathering (intervention participation, post-September 2019).
- DV(s): Monthly new business registrations in a county (log-transformed in main FE models; raw counts in count/GLM robustness models), interpreted as "founding events."
- Mediators: (Theorized but not directly modeled) cross-county exchange of tacit knowledge — including private contextual knowledge of successes and failures, programs, and resources — that EEAs subsequently transmit to nascent entrepreneurs in their home counties, enabling additional firm-formation activities including registration.
- Moderators: (1) Number of preexisting community improvement associations in the county (proxy for community social capital; H2, positive interaction); (2) county-level Gini coefficient of income inequality (inverse proxy for generalized social trust; H3, negative interaction). Additional tests use religious congregations and median household income as alternative interactions.

Drawing on institutional theory and the knowledge spillover theory of entrepreneurship, the authors argue that EEAs perform institutional maintenance work that catalyzes voluntary, horizontal knowledge spillovers within their communities. Because tacit knowledge transmission decays with geographic distance, EEAs in dispersed nonmetro counties typically lack access to nonredundant, cross-context knowledge. A temporary in-person gathering supplies that nonlocal tacit knowledge through cross-county "bridging" ties, which EEAs then channel to nascent entrepreneurs back home, "nudging" them past the registration threshold. This conduit effect is amplified where preexisting voluntary associations provide trusted, organized channels for dissemination, and dampened where high economic inequality erodes the generalized social trust required for entrepreneurs to act on advice from non-intimates. Empirically, participating counties registered roughly 10% more monthly foundings (about 1.8 additional registrations/month) than non-participants, with a one-SD increase in community improvement associations boosting the marginal effect by 23% and a two-percentage-point higher Gini wiping it out.

**Theoretical Contribution**
First, the paper integrates institutional theory and knowledge spillover theory by recasting EEAs as individual-level conduits of voluntary, horizontal knowledge spillovers — augmenting a knowledge-spillover tradition historically focused on technical knowledge from established firms and universities. Second, by drawing on adjacent sociological theories of social capital and social trust, it specifies community-level moderators (civic associations, economic inequality) that determine whether spillovers are absorbed, addressing the "human dimension" of place that knowledge-spillover scholars have flagged but underdeveloped. Third, it advances a model of "bridging" institutional work that helps deeply embedded local actors in sparse, scattered communities escape the double-edged sword of localism without breaking the local ties that enable trust.

**Practical Implication**
For regional entrepreneurship policy, the study illustrates that comparatively inexpensive "investments in spillover conduits" — informal temporary gatherings that help dispersed EEAs build cross-geographic bridging ties — can yield near-term increases in business registrations, particularly in resource-scarce nonmetro areas. However, policymakers should expect heterogeneous payoffs: communities with denser civic and improvement associations are better positioned to absorb the imported tacit knowledge, while communities with high economic inequality may absorb little of the benefit, suggesting that pairing such convenings with complementary investments in social capital and trust is necessary to translate knowledge access into entrepreneurial activity.

**Limitations**
The study is confined to a single U.S. state (Minnesota), so generalizability across different regional, national, and institutional contexts is unverified. It is a quasi-experiment with first-come, first-served (rather than randomized) participation, which the authors mitigate but cannot fully eliminate through robustness checks and an instrumental-variable strategy. The dependent variable captures only one type of firm-formation event (business registration) and the study lacks individual-level evidence from entrepreneurs themselves linking specific EEA actions to specific registrations. Finally, social trust is proxied indirectly via economic inequality; trust has additional drivers the design does not measure.

**Future Research**
Future work could systematically compare EEAs' work and influence across metro vs. nonmetro contexts and across different institutional regimes; trace at the individual level how EEAs' knowledge transmissions advance specific ventures from idea to registration; theorize and test additional drivers of community social trust beyond inequality; and examine richer temporal and spatial dynamics, including potential cross-county spillovers from neighboring counties' participation as time passes. Researchers might also clarify how EEAs shape the quality and composition of new ventures, and how EEAs themselves are reshaped by the ventures they support.
