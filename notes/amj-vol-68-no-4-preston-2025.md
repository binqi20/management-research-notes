# The Moral Case Revisited: Moral Framing as a Double-Edged Sword for Motivating Majority Group Leaders to Support DEI

**Abstract**
Employees’ use of moral frames to sell the importance of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives is gaining popularity over business-case frames. Yet, it remains unclear when and why moral framing is effective at motivating majority group leaders (e.g., White, male leaders) to support DEI initiatives. Drawing on the focus theory of normative conduct, I argue that employees’ use of moral frames can be a double-edged sword for motivating majority group leaders to support DEI initiatives. On the one hand, moral framing can heighten majority group leaders’ psychological standing, thereby increasing their support for DEI initiatives. On the other hand, moral framing can trigger psychological reactance for majority group leaders, thereby reducing their support for DEI initiatives. To reconcile these competing pathways, I argue that, when employees emphasize majority group leaders’ problem-solving autonomy concerning how to address DEI issues, moral framing has a stronger effect on psychological standing and a weaker effect on psychological reactance, thus enhancing majority group leaders’ overall support for DEI initiatives. I find support for these predictions across several studies that cover racial and gender equity issues, and discuss theoretical and practical implications.

**Research Question**
When and why is employees' moral framing of DEI issues effective at motivating majority group leaders (e.g., White or male leaders) to support DEI initiatives, and how can issue sellers reconcile moral framing's competing effects on these leaders' psychological standing and psychological reactance?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1: Moral framing for DEI issues (vs. control and economic framing) is positively associated with majority group leaders' psychological standing.
H2: Moral framing (vs. control and economic framing) has a positive indirect effect on majority group leaders' DEI issue support through psychological standing.
H3: Moral framing (vs. control and economic framing) is positively associated with majority group leaders' psychological reactance.
H4: Moral framing (vs. control and economic framing) has a negative indirect effect on majority group leaders' DEI issue support through psychological reactance.
H5: When issue sellers highlight majority group leaders' problem-solving autonomy for how to address a DEI issue, moral framing has a stronger positive indirect effect on issue support through psychological standing.
H6: When issue sellers highlight majority group leaders' problem-solving autonomy for how to address a DEI issue, moral framing has a weaker negative indirect effect on issue support through psychological reactance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee DEI issue-selling frame (moral vs. economic vs. control no-framing baseline) — manipulated experimentally in all three studies.
- DV(s): Majority group leaders' DEI issue support, operationalized via Hussain et al. (2023) issue-support (Studies 1 and 3) and voice-endorsement (Study 1) scales, a behavioral DEI-engagement index (Study 2: willingness to volunteer for a taskforce, mentorship, and attending a college recruiting event to attract racial-minority employees), and budget-allocation and time-allocation behavioral intentions (Study 3).
- Mediators: Majority group leader psychological standing (perceived legitimacy to engage with DEI; positive pathway) and psychological reactance (resistance to perceived behavioral-autonomy threat; negative pathway), modeled simultaneously.
- Moderators: (1) Leader social identity — race in racial-equity context (White vs. racial-minority leaders, Studies 1 and 2) and gender in gender-equity supplementary studies; (2) employees' highlighting of leaders' problem-solving autonomy (high vs. low, Study 3).

Drawing on the focus theory of normative conduct, the paper theorizes moral framing as an interpersonal normative cue that simultaneously conveys social approval (legitimizing leaders' engagement) and prescriptive expectation (an obligation to act). Approval focus elevates psychological standing — particularly for majority group leaders, who otherwise lack identity-based stakes — and increases DEI support. Obligation focus elevates psychological reactance, especially for majority group leaders who feel implicated by historical injustices, reducing support. Highlighting leaders' problem-solving autonomy cognitively reframes moral appeals from "I should" to "I could," amplifying the standing pathway and dampening the reactance pathway, and producing a positive overall effect of moral framing on DEI support.

**Data & Measures**
Three main between-subjects experiments plus six OSF supplementary studies. Study 1 (recall, n=581 full-time leaders on Prolific) randomly assigned a moral vs. economic recall frame; Study 2 (field experiment, n=782) embedded a three-condition (control/economic/moral) design in a firm's annual engagement survey; Study 3 (preregistered online experiment, n=821 White leaders on Prolific) crossed issue frame (economic vs. moral) with problem-solving autonomy (low vs. high). Mediators: psychological standing (three items adapted from Sherf et al., 2017) and psychological reactance (three items adapted from Hong & Page, 1989), on 5-point scales. Dependent variable, leader DEI issue support: Hussain et al. (2023) three-item issue-support and five-item voice-endorsement scales (Study 1); a behavioral DEI-engagement index of willingness to volunteer for a racial-equity task force, mentoring, and attending a college recruiting event to attract more racial-minority employees (Study 2); and budget-allocation ($20,000 across four items) and time-allocation (30 minutes across four issues) behavioral intentions plus the three-item support scale (Study 3). Moderators: leader race (White = 1, racial minority = 0; Studies 1-2) and highlighted problem-solving autonomy (Study 3). Moral-frame and autonomy manipulations were checked with Mayer et al. (2019) and a one-item discretion measure. Hypotheses tested with two-way ANOVA, simple-effects tests, and Hayes (2022) PROCESS Models 4 and 7 (5,000 bootstrap samples) with standing and reactance as simultaneous mediators; random assignment supports causal inference, though mediators and dependent variables were measured concurrently from the same source.

**Key Findings**
Study 1 (recall, n=581): supported H1-H4 for majority (White) leaders but not for racial-minority leaders. Moral (vs. economic) framing raised White leaders' psychological standing (F(1,577)=20.49, p<.001, H1) and psychological reactance (F(1,577)=52.34, p<.001, H3); the indirect effect on issue support was positive via standing (b=0.22, 95% CI [0.12, 0.32], H2) and negative via reactance (b=-0.13, 95% CI [-0.21, -0.06], H4), with both moderated-mediation indices significant.
Study 2 (field experiment, n=782): supported H1-H4 for White leaders — moral framing (vs. control and economic) raised both standing and reactance, and the positive (standing) and negative (reactance) indirect effects on issue support and behavioral DEI engagement were stronger for White than racial-minority leaders.
Study 3 (online experiment, n=821 White leaders): the standing pathway was null — H1 not supported (p=.134) and H2's standing-mediated indirect effect nonsignificant — which the author attributes to the vignette design; the reactance pathway held — the moral-vs-economic reactance contrast was significant (p=.002, H3) and H4's negative reactance-mediated indirect effect was significant (though the Study 3 discussion characterizes the standing main effects as yielding null results for both H1 and H3). H5 and H6 were supported across all three issue-support measures: highlighting problem-solving autonomy strengthened the standing pathway and weakened the reactance pathway, shifting moral framing's total effect on issue support from significantly negative (autonomy not highlighted) to significantly positive (autonomy highlighted).
Supplementary studies (Table 8, six studies): support was heterogeneous across DEI subdomains and robustness checks — a gender-equity recall study supported only the reactance pathway while a gender-equity vignette supported both pathways; a de-moralization vignette showed partial standing-pathway and full reactance-pathway support; a moral-vs-economic comparison found no significant interaction; and two alternative-mechanism studies found no significant effects of moral framing on the tested alternatives.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper complicates the prevailing view that moralizing DEI issues uniformly empowers leaders, recasting moral framing as a double-edged sword whose net effect on majority group leaders depends on whether their attention is directed to social approval or prescriptive obligation. It introduces a dual-pathway psychological model — psychological standing (granting moral license) and psychological reactance (imposing moral obligation) — to explain when moral appeals galvanize versus alienate majority group leaders, and it advances the issue-selling literature by introducing "highlighting problem-solving autonomy" as a novel persuasion tactic that converts moral framing's liability into an asset.

**Practical Implication**
For majority group leaders, the findings underscore the value of recognizing one's psychological reactions to moral appeals and actively reframing them around problem-solving autonomy and shared responsibility rather than coercion. For issue sellers (employees, students, activists), moral framing should be paired with explicit emphasis on the leader's discretion to design and select DEI solutions; over-prescriptive moral demands ("you should adopt initiative X") risk triggering reactance and reducing support, while presenting a menu of viable initiatives sustains both legitimacy and behavioral freedom. The author also illustrates the implication for university leaders facing student activism on contested issues (e.g., racial justice, geopolitical conflicts).

**Limitations**
Mediator and dependent-variable data were collected at the same time from the same source, raising common-method concerns; the author calls for designs that manipulate mediators or use multi-source data. The studies offer limited examination of issue-seller demographic and status characteristics (e.g., organizational status, tenure, position) beyond race and gender. Study 3's vignette-based design produced weaker effects on psychological standing than the real-employee-communication contexts of Studies 1 and 2, yielding null results on H1 and H2 in Study 3 (H2 receives only partial support across the full set of studies). The supplementary studies on gender equity show only partial replication of the standing pathway, suggesting boundary conditions across DEI subdomains.

**Future Research**
Future work could investigate how moral characteristics (e.g., moral awareness) shape majority group leaders' susceptibility to moral framing, and whether moral framing translates into effective — not just declared — DEI advocacy. Researchers could examine how distinct moral values (fairness vs. harm) channel leaders toward "non-discrimination" initiatives like bias training versus "resource" initiatives like targeted recruiting. Additional avenues include retaliatory responses toward moral issue sellers, the welfare consequences of leaders' choices among autonomy-granted initiatives, and contextual factors that shape whether moral and economic frames interact rather than substitute.
