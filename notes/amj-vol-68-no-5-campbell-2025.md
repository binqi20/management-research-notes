# The Gendered Complexity of Sponsorship: How Male and Female Sponsors’ Goals Shape Their Social Network Strategies

**Abstract**
Providing sponsorship, when senior colleagues (sponsors) mobilize resources in their social network on junior colleagues’ (prot eg es) behalf, is consequential to the effective functioning of organizations. Yet, the psychological processes underlying male and female sponsors’ goals, priorities, and strategies for activating resources in their networks for proteges are not well understood. We conduct four studies to examine male and female sponsors’ goals and the resulting impact on their approach to activating resources in their social network. Studies 1a and 1b provide converging, initial evidence that men approach sponsorship with less complexity than women, by setting fewer goals that focus more on benefiting themselves. Study 2 provides additional evidence: male sponsors are more likely than female sponsors to perceive sponsorship as an opportunity to benefit themselves while female sponsors perceive sponsorship as an opportunity to benefit their proteges. Study 3 experimentally links the differences in goal pursuit to sponsors’ social network strategies: when asked to balance prot eg e-centric and sponsorcentric goals, women cognitively activate networks higher in closure, but men activate sparse networks regardless of their goals. The implications of these network activation configurations for understanding the sponsorship process are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do male and female sponsors’ goals and goal complexity differ when providing sponsorship, and how do these gendered differences in goal pursuit shape sponsors’ cognitive social network activation strategies for mobilizing resources on behalf of proteges?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Sponsor gender (male vs. female); experimentally manipulated goal condition (protege-centric, sponsor-centric, dual-goal)
- DV(s): Goal complexity (number and content of sponsorship goals); cognitive network activation density/closure; network configuration (sparse vs. dense)
- Mediators: Goal complexity and goal conflict between self- and other-oriented goals
- Moderators: Goal condition (single vs. dual goal); gender homophily of activated networks

Drawing on psychological theories of goal pursuit and social capital, the authors argue that gendered workplace expectations, stereotypes, and masculine defaults lead women to experience greater goal complexity than men when considering sponsorship. Men generate fewer, more self-beneficial goals, while women balance protege- and sponsor-centric goals, creating goal conflict. Because goals shape how individuals cognitively activate their social networks, this goal complexity differential produces distinct network activation strategies: women balancing dual goals activate denser, higher-closure networks (conducive to cooperation and social support for relational goals), while men activate sparse, low-density networks (offering informational advantages and self-advancement opportunities) regardless of goal framing.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a sponsor-centric approach to sponsorship research, disentangling sponsorship from other forms of career support and revealing gender as a key differentiator in how leaders engage their social capital for others. It contributes novel theoretical and empirical insight into a micro-level psychological mechanism—gendered goal complexity—that shapes how male and female sponsors activate networks, and it suggests sponsorship may inadvertently perpetuate workplace inequality by differentially burdening female sponsors and differentially rewarding male sponsors.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should reconsider broad calls for leaders to provide more sponsorship, since such calls may result in male leaders, but not female leaders, benefiting personally while expending social capital. To bolster equitable sponsorship, firms could design goal-based interventions that make sponsorship normative, clearly articulate its purpose, and differentiate its goals from those of mentorship to provide clearer cognitive guidance for leaders of both genders.

**Limitations**
The research focuses exclusively on sponsors’ cognitive processes after a high-quality protege has been selected, leaving selection effects and actual network mobilization unexamined. It relies on experimental and survey methods with online panels, which sidestep field-setting confounds but raise external-validity concerns. Results may vary across organizational cultures, occupational contexts, and intersecting identities (e.g., race, disability) not examined here.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how sponsor and protege characteristics (race, ethnicity, disability status) intersect with gender to shape sponsorship goals and network strategies, study gender differences in actual network mobilization (not just cognitive activation), and investigate how organizational culture, occupational norms, and selection into sponsorship shape sponsors’ decision-making. Scholars should also empirically test how different cognitively activated network configurations relate to sponsor effectiveness over time.
