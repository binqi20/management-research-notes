# The Gendered Complexity of Sponsorship: How Male and Female Sponsors’ Goals Shape Their Social Network Strategies

**Abstract**
Providing sponsorship, when senior colleagues (sponsors) mobilize resources in their social network on junior colleagues’ (prot eg es) behalf, is consequential to the effective functioning of organizations. Yet, the psychological processes underlying male and female sponsors’ goals, priorities, and strategies for activating resources in their networks for proteges are not well understood. We conduct four studies to examine male and female sponsors’ goals and the resulting impact on their approach to activating resources in their social network. Studies 1a and 1b provide converging, initial evidence that men approach sponsorship with less complexity than women, by setting fewer goals that focus more on benefiting themselves. Study 2 provides additional evidence: male sponsors are more likely than female sponsors to perceive sponsorship as an opportunity to benefit themselves while female sponsors perceive sponsorship as an opportunity to benefit their proteges. Study 3 experimentally links the differences in goal pursuit to sponsors’ social network strategies: when asked to balance prot eg e-centric and sponsorcentric goals, women cognitively activate networks higher in closure, but men activate sparse networks regardless of their goals. The implications of these network activation configurations for understanding the sponsorship process are discussed.

**Research Question**
How do male and female sponsors’ goals differ in complexity, and how do those goal differences shape the social networks they cognitively activate when deciding whether and how to provide sponsorship (mobilizing social capital on a protege’s behalf)?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
The paper uses an abductive design and states no formally numbered hypotheses; it advances the following theorized predictions (summarized in Figure 1, its theoretical model):
- Male sponsors experience lower goal complexity than female sponsors — pursuing fewer goals, more focused on benefiting themselves (sponsor-centric), and therefore experiencing less goal conflict.
- Female sponsors, constrained by gendered expectations about communality, experience greater goal complexity — pursuing more (and more conflicting) goals that balance the needs of multiple constituencies (protege-centric and sponsor-centric).
- These goal differences shape cognitive network activation: when balancing protege-centric and sponsor-centric goals, female sponsors activate networks higher in closure (higher density), whereas male sponsors, focused on a sponsor-centric self-advancement goal, activate sparse, low-density networks.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Sponsor (participant) gender (male vs. female); in Study 3, an experimentally manipulated goal condition (control, single protege-centric goal, dual protege-centric + sponsor-centric goal).
- DV(s): Goal complexity — number of goals and self-benefit content of goals (Studies 1a-1b); perceived sponsorship opportunity for self / protege / firm (Study 2); cognitive network activation — network density/closure and gender homophily (Study 3).
- Mediators: Goal complexity / goal conflict, theorized to transmit gender differences into network activation (tested by experimentally manipulating goals in Study 3 rather than by statistical mediation).
- Moderators: Sponsorship experience (Study 2 — amplifies men’s self-benefit perception); goal condition (Study 3 — the gender difference in network density emerges only under dual/conflicting goals).

Drawing on goal systems theory and work on social capital and cognitive network activation, the authors argue that because women face gendered expectations to be communal and other-oriented, female sponsors set more numerous and more conflicting goals (balancing their own and their proteges’ interests), whereas men, held to agentic expectations, set fewer, more self-beneficial goals. Greater goal complexity leads women — when goals conflict — to cognitively activate denser, higher-closure networks (which offer cooperation, social support, and lower-risk mobilization), while men activate sparse, information-rich networks that serve their own advancement regardless of goals. Because gender is measured rather than manipulated, the gender-to-goals link is associational; Study 3 supplies the experimental link from goals to network activation.

**Data & Measures**
- Data: Four online-panel samples of U.S. full-time employees / managers reporting experience providing sponsorship (Studies 1a and 3 via CloudResearch; Studies 1b and 2 via Prolific), using vignette/scenario surveys and, in Study 3, a randomized experiment; all materials and data are posted on OSF.
- Number of Goals: participants list goals in an open-ended box and self-count them; a research assistant independently recounted goals blind to gender (IRR = .964).
- Goal content (Study 1b): a research assistant blind to condition content-coded each goal statement on warmth, other-orientation, dominance, competence, self-benefit, and mentorship mentions (0 = not at all, 1 = a little, 2 = a lot), then averaged per participant.
- Perceived opportunity (Study 2): ratings (1-5) and rankings of six items forming sponsor-, protege-, and firm-focused composites (alpha = .885 / .819 / .724); prior sponsorship experience on a 1-5 scale.
- Network activation (Study 3): a name generator (up to 10 real contacts); Network Density (closure) computed from pairwise tie strength among named contacts; Percent Women and Network Homophily.
- Identification: Studies 1a-2 are associational gender comparisons (t-tests, ANOVA, Mann-Whitney). Study 3 randomly assigns goal conditions, so the goals-to-network-activation link is experimentally identified; sponsor gender is a measured attribute, not manipulated.

**Key Findings**
Across four studies, men approached sponsorship with lower goal complexity than women, but the support pattern differs by study and several theorized effects were null or only marginal.
- Study 1a (n = 215): Women reported significantly more goals than men (M = 3.130 vs. 2.460; t(213) = -2.446, p = .015, d = .334). No gender difference in goal specificity (p = .888) or word count (p = .471); women spent more time on the task (p = .035).
- Study 1b (n = 619, preregistered): The gender effect on number of goals was only marginal — women reported more goals than men (M = 2.576 vs. 2.399; F(1,613) = 3.23, p = .073). A marginal protege-gender main effect emerged (p = .053), but the protege-gender x participant-gender interaction was non-significant (p = .337). Exploratory content coding found no gender differences in warmth, other-orientation, dominance, competence, or mentorship mentions (all p > .50); the only significant content difference was self-benefit, with men’s goals rated higher on self-benefit than women’s (M = 0.321 vs. 0.219; t(616) = 2.263, p = .024, d = .182) — i.e., women’s goals were rated as focusing less on benefiting themselves.
- Study 2 (n = 307, preregistered): Men were more likely than women to perceive sponsorship as an opportunity to benefit themselves (F(1,305) = 4.34, p = .038; M = 2.961 vs. 2.681), while women were more likely to perceive it as an opportunity to benefit proteges (F(1,305) = 8.52, p = .004; M = 4.645 vs. 4.426); there was no gender difference for firm benefit (p = .249). Rankings concurred (own advancement ranked higher by men, Z = -2.35, p = .019; protege advancement ranked marginally higher by women, Z = -1.87, p = .061). Men’s self-benefit perception rose with sponsorship experience (slope = .238, p = .008) but women’s did not (p = .387).
- Study 3 (n = 591, experiment): A significant participant-gender x goal-condition interaction on network density emerged (F(1,585) = 4.268, p = .014). Women activated denser (higher-closure) networks than men only in the dual-goal condition (M = 0.771 vs. 0.615, p = .013); densities did not differ by gender in the control (0.710 vs. 0.691) or single-goal (0.664 vs. 0.670) conditions. Within women, the dual-goal condition was denser than the single-goal condition (p = .028); men showed no differences across conditions (p = .101), activating sparse networks regardless of goals. Men also activated more gender-homophilous networks (M = 0.713 vs. 0.545, p < .001) and networks with a far smaller share of women (M = 0.014 vs. 0.545, p < .001). The authors flag the null gender difference in the control condition as unexpected.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a sponsor-centric (rather than protege-centric) account of sponsorship, disentangling it from mentorship by treating social capital as the unique resource sponsors expend, and identifies sponsors’ goals / goal complexity as a microlevel cognitive mechanism that links gender to how sponsors mobilize their networks. It establishes a novel connection between goal pursuit and cognitive network activation in sponsorship, showing gender differentiates how individuals interact with their social capital. It also refines the gender-and-work literature by showing gendered patterns are nuanced: men’s goals are more self-beneficial but do not differ on broader communality/agency dimensions (warmth, other-orientation, dominance, competence), challenging the notion that gendered expectations exert uniform pressure on behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Within its own stated managerial and organizational implications, the paper cautions against broad calls for all leaders to simply provide more sponsorship: doing so may lead male leaders (but not female leaders) to sponsor with their own needs in mind, potentially producing inequities in who benefits from providing sponsorship. To support equitable sponsorship, firms could design goal-based interventions and make sponsorship’s purpose explicit — articulating the goals of sponsorship and differentiating them from the goals of mentorship — to give leaders clearer cognitive guidance.

**Limitations**
Focusing exclusively on the sponsor’s perspective is both a strength and a limitation. The studies examine goal pursuit and network activation only after a high-quality protege has been selected, leaving possible sponsor-gender differences in protege selection unexamined. The reliance on online-panel survey and experimental designs risks lower external validity and generalizability, which the authors offset by sampling working supervisors with sponsorship experience and by using cognitive activation of real network contacts. The work also isolates cognitive processes under the sponsor’s control rather than actual network mobilization or downstream sponsor effectiveness.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how other sponsor and protege characteristics intersect to shape sponsors’ goals and strategies (e.g., race and ethnicity, disability status); on gender differences in protege selection and in actual network mobilization (not just cognitive activation); on how specific occupational and organizational norms and cultures shape sponsors’ decision-making; on how the configurations of sponsors’ cognitively activated networks affect their effectiveness as sponsors (an open empirical question); and on sponsors’ decision-making using alternative methods, including whether these gendered tendencies are intentional or occur through more unconscious processes.
