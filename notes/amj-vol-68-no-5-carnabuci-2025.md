# Catalyzing Categories: Category Contrast and the Creation of Groundbreaking Inventions

**Abstract**
We hypothesize that “low-contrast categories” (those lacking sharp differentiation from adjacent categories) catalyze the creation of groundbreaking inventions by influencing two key stages in the life of an invention: (1) idea-creation and (2) idea-positioning. During “idea-creation,” low-contrast categories increase the likelihood that descendant inventions will combine the focal invention with more (a) boundary-spanning, (b) novel, (c) original, and (d) atypical knowledge inputs. During “idea-positioning,” they allow greater leeway in articulating how descendant inventions depart from the focal invention’s lineage and chart new technological directions. We find robust support for our hypothesis using data from the United States Patent and Trademark Office’s classification system spanning nearly four decades. Further analyses demonstrate that the catalyzing effect of low-contrast categories has important material consequences: inventions classified in low-contrast categories spur descendant inventions that generate substantially higher economic value and exert more enduring technological impact than those in high-contrast categories. By introducing the concept of catalyzing categories, this study offers a novel theoretical perspective on the genesis of groundbreaking inventions and the role of categorical structures in the inventive process.

**Research Question**
Where do groundbreaking inventions come from? Specifically, how does the categorical structure of an institutionalized classification system — in particular, the category contrast of the patent class in which an invention is classified — shape whether that invention has a groundbreaking (path-creating) rather than consolidating (path-reinforcing) technological impact?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Central hypothesis: The lower the contrast of the category in which an invention is classified, the more groundbreaking its impact — holding invention characteristics and other factors constant, an invention is more likely to spur descendant inventions that break away from its technological predecessors as the contrast of its category decreases.
Idea-creation mechanism prediction: Inventions classified in low-contrast categories generate descendant inventions that exhibit greater (a) boundary spanning, (b) novelty, (c) originality, and (d) atypical combinations.
Idea-positioning mechanism prediction: When a focal invention is classified in a low-contrast category, patent examiners afford its descendants greater leeway to frame their claims and patent text as novel, distinctive, and broad — departing from, rather than consolidating, the focal invention’s lineage.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Category (patent-class) contrast of the class in which the focal patent is classified — a time-varying measure of how sharply the class is differentiated from adjacent classes.
- DV(s): Groundbreaking versus consolidating technological impact of the focal invention, measured by the consolidation–disruption (CD) index (higher = more groundbreaking).
- Mediators: Two theorized pathways probed empirically — idea-creation (descendants’ boundary spanning, novelty, originality, atypical combinations) and idea-positioning (leeway in patent claim scope and text to emphasize distinctiveness).
- Moderators: None formally hypothesized; exploratory tests examine whether the effect varies by whether a class’s contrast is increasing, decreasing, or stable over time.

Low-contrast categories comprise more heterogeneous knowledge and offer more ambiguous evaluation criteria. Through idea-creation, building on an invention classified in a low-contrast category leads descendant inventions to combine it with more novel, atypical, original, and boundary-spanning knowledge inputs, opening technological paths that diverge from the status quo. Through idea-positioning, the ambiguity of low-contrast categories gives applicants and their attorneys greater latitude during the patent examination negotiation to frame descendants as distinctive departures rather than consolidations, so the codified patent text emphasizes novelty, uniqueness, and breadth. Both pathways raise the likelihood that a focal invention spurs groundbreaking technological trajectories.

**Data & Measures**
Data: USPTO utility patents granted 1975–2013 (drawn from PatentsView), excluding patents reclassified during the observation window; final sample of 3,127,467 focal patents.
Dependent variable: Consolidation–disruption (CD) index (Funk & Owen-Smith, 2017), the proportion of overlap between a descendant’s and the focal patent’s backward citations, computed yearly using 1-, 3-, and 5-year windows and ranging from −1 (maximally consolidating) to +1 (maximally groundbreaking).
Independent variable: Category (patent-class) contrast (Carnabuci, Operti & Kovács, 2015), derived from the share of a class’s patents that are cross-classified into other primary classes, with exponential discounting (parameter 0.9).
Mechanism variables: Boundary spanning, Novelty, Originality, and Combination atypicality of descendants (idea-creation); changes in independent-claim word counts and claim number, and text similarity to closest patents (Arts et al., 2018), plus descendant classification patterns (idea-positioning).
Controls: Class size, technological fertility, average age of cited patents, count of descendants’ backward citations, and focal patent’s inventor productivity, with year and focal-patent fixed effects.
Identification: Patent and year fixed effects, matching estimators, and an instrumental-variable regression using retroactive USPTO reclassification orders (1999–2013) as exogenous variation in contrast (instrument passes Stock–Yogo, Kleibergen–Paap, and Anderson–Rubin tests). The design is observational; the IV provides the causal test. Implication measures use patent economic value from stock-price reactions (Kogan et al., 2017), citation lag, and next-generation CD.

**Key Findings**
The central hypothesis is robustly supported: category contrast is negatively related to the CD index across all three windows (Contrast ≈ −0.047 [CD1], −0.052 [CD3], −0.059 [CD5] with patent fixed effects; ≈ −0.031 / −0.031 / −0.032 with matched-strata fixed effects), so lower contrast means more groundbreaking impact; the effect strengthens over longer windows, and a one-standard-deviation increase in contrast reduces CD by about 1.1% of its standard deviation. The idea-creation mechanism is supported (Table 4): lower contrast yields descendants with greater boundary spanning (−0.061), novelty (−0.006), originality (−0.074), and atypical combinations (−0.013), all significant. The idea-positioning mechanism receives only partial support in the claims-change test (Table 5): higher contrast lengthens (narrows) descendant claims for the shortest independent claim (+11.389) and the average claim word count (+11.455), both significant, but the change in the number of independent claims is not significant (0.035, ns) — the authors explicitly label this "partial support." Remaining idea-positioning tests are supported: higher contrast increases text similarity to closest patents (Table 6: +0.025 full, +0.035 matched) and makes descendants more likely to stay in the focal patent’s class, remain in its prior-art class, and cite its prior art (Table 7, all six models). The instrumental-variable regression supports causal interpretation (Table 8): exogenous increases in contrast significantly reduce groundbreaking impact (second-stage Contrast on CD = −0.213). Results are robust across contrast quintiles (a monotonic effect that flattens at high contrast), alternative contrast measures, pre-/post-AIPA subsamples, extreme-value exclusions, logit specifications for the most groundbreaking patents, and class-year aggregation / GLS. On material consequences, low-contrast categories spur descendants with higher economic value (Table 10: Contrast = −$15.349M; a one-SD contrast increase corresponds to roughly $1.4M lower average descendant value) and greater value dispersion (a one-SD contrast increase lowers the probability of a very-high-value descendant by 24.6% and of a very-low-value one by 5.6%), longer-lasting impact (citation lag rises as contrast falls), and more groundbreaking next-generation descendants (Table 9: −0.007).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper offers a novel theoretical perspective on the genesis of groundbreaking inventions: an invention’s impact depends not only on its content but on how it is categorized, with low-contrast "catalyzing categories" fostering breakthroughs through idea-creation and idea-positioning. It also contributes to the sociology of categories literature by showing that low-contrast (fuzzy) categories — usually viewed as inefficient and disadvantageous for information search — can catalyze groundbreaking inventions, challenging the prevailing view that classification in high-contrast categories is always more beneficial.

**Practical Implication**
For the design of institutionalized classification schemes such as patent systems, the study surfaces a tension in the paper’s own terms: maximizing category contrast improves information-search efficiency but may reduce the collective capacity to generate groundbreaking inventions. Digitized classification schemes that maintain crisp categorical boundaries could, in doing so, diminish disruptive innovation.

**Limitations**
As with all observational research, isolating the theorized mechanisms is challenging: the authors lack direct data on inventors’ search behavior and must rely on proxies for idea-positioning, and the main outcome (the CD index) is an indirect, though widely validated, measure. They cannot compare the relative magnitude of the idea-creation and idea-positioning channels, endogeneity remains a concern because inventions cannot be randomly assigned to categories, and the empirical test is limited to patent data, which have known limitations and biases.

**Future Research**
The authors call for measuring the mechanisms more directly through interviews, experiments, or case studies; testing the argument in other domains (e.g., how academic classification into disciplines shapes groundbreaking science); examining whether inventions can simultaneously consolidate and break ground across different technology streams and how category contrast affects different groups of inventors (e.g., "outsiders"); exploring how technological cycles drive shifts in contrast; and pursuing the intellectual-property-strategy questions the study raises about whether low- versus high-contrast categories favor strategically broadening patent scope or the activity of patent assertion entities.
