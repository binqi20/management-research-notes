# Catalyzing Categories: Category Contrast and the Creation of Groundbreaking Inventions

**Abstract**
We hypothesize that “low-contrast categories” (those lacking sharp differentiation from adjacent categories) catalyze the creation of groundbreaking inventions by influencing two key stages in the life of an invention: (1) idea-creation and (2) idea-positioning. During “idea-creation,” low-contrast categories increase the likelihood that descendant inventions will combine the focal invention with more (a) boundary-spanning, (b) novel, (c) original, and (d) atypical knowledge inputs. During “idea-positioning,” they allow greater leeway in articulating how descendant inventions depart from the focal invention’s lineage and chart new technological directions. We find robust support for our hypothesis using data from the United States Patent and Trademark Office’s classification system spanning nearly four decades. Further analyses demonstrate that the catalyzing effect of low-contrast categories has important material consequences: inventions classified in low-contrast categories spur descendant inventions that generate substantially higher economic value and exert more enduring technological impact than those in high-contrast categories. By introducing the concept of catalyzing categories, this study offers a novel theoretical perspective on the genesis of groundbreaking inventions and the role of categorical structures in the inventive process.

**Research Question**
Where do groundbreaking inventions come from, and specifically, how does the category contrast of the institutionalized knowledge classification in which a focal invention is placed shape its likelihood of spurring groundbreaking rather than consolidating descendant inventions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Category contrast of the USPTO patent class in which a focal patent is classified (low vs. high contrast)
- DV(s): Groundbreaking versus consolidating impact of the focal invention, operationalized via the consolidation-disruption (CD) index of Funk and Owen-Smith (2017); descendant patents' economic value (market-based measure from Kogan et al., 2017); long-term technological impact
- Mediators: Idea-creation inputs (novelty, originality, atypicality, boundary-spanning combinations of descendant inventions); idea-positioning outputs (patent examiners' narrowing of claims, textual emphasis on uniqueness/distinctiveness in titles, abstracts, and bodies, reclassification of descendants into different classes, reduced citation of focal patent's prior art)
- Moderators: Not the primary focus; supplementary analyses explore patent-level covariates and robustness across measures

Institutionalized classification schemes shape cognition and creative recombination. Low-contrast categories are internally heterogeneous and weakly differentiated from neighbors, which during the idea-creation stage exposes would-be followers to a more diverse knowledge base and increases the probability that descendant inventions will draw on novel, atypical, original, and boundary-spanning inputs. During the idea-positioning stage, ambiguous evaluative standards in low-contrast categories give patent applicants greater interpretive leeway to frame descendants as distinct from the focal invention's lineage; examiners in such categories are less able to enforce narrow claims or strict prior-art linkages. Together, these two mechanisms make inventions in low-contrast categories more likely to catalyze technological paths that diverge from existing trajectories, yielding descendants with higher economic value and more enduring impact.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces the concept of "catalyzing categories" and advances a novel theoretical framework rooted in the sociology of categories to explain the genesis of groundbreaking inventions. It challenges the prevailing view that high-contrast categories are unambiguously beneficial by showing that categorical fuzziness can actually foster breakthrough innovation through idea-creation and idea-positioning mechanisms. The study thereby extends research on classification systems, knowledge recombination, and the disruptiveness of inventions by treating classification not merely as a mechanism for efficient information search but as a cognitive and institutional force that shapes the inventive process itself.

**Practical Implication**
For policymakers and patent-office administrators, the findings caution that efforts to digitize and sharpen patent classification schemes—while enhancing information retrieval—may inadvertently suppress the emergence of groundbreaking inventions by raising category contrast. For inventors and firms concerned with IP strategy, the results suggest that inventions positioned within low-contrast categories may afford greater strategic leeway to broaden claims, signal novelty, and generate higher-value descendant technologies.

**Limitations**
As with all observational research, isolating the theorized idea-creation and idea-positioning mechanisms is difficult; direct data on search behavior are unavailable and the idea-positioning channel relies on indirect proxies, while the main outcome (the CD index) is itself an indirect though widely validated measure. The design cannot directly compare the magnitudes of the idea-creation and idea-positioning channels, and endogeneity concerns remain because inventions cannot be randomly assigned to categories. The empirical test is confined to USPTO patents, which have known biases, and the paper does not investigate whether inventions may simultaneously consolidate and break ground across distinct technological streams.

**Future Research**
Future work could use interviews, experiments, or case studies to measure the idea-creation and idea-positioning mechanisms more directly, and should explore whether similar dynamics operate in other classification domains such as academic disciplines or scientific fields. Researchers could examine how technological cycles drive shifts in category contrast over time, whether low-contrast categories offer a more favorable environment for strategic patent scope broadening and for patent assertion entities, and how category contrast differentially attracts "outsider" inventors from other domains. The authors also encourage work on how category contrast affects different groups of inventors and on inventions that simultaneously consolidate one technological stream while breaking ground in another.
