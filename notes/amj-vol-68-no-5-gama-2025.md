# Cross-Sector Relational Realignments after an Institutional Crisis: Checking the Instrumentalization of Corporate-Nonprofit Partnerships

**Abstract**
We examine how government corruption scandals influence corporate-nonprofit partnerships by altering the institutional context in ways that reshape the value of nonmarket relationships. We analyze these dynamics following Operation Carwash (2014) in Brazil, using comprehensive data on all publicly listed firms and registered nonprofits from 2010 to 2017. We find that partnerships became more common after the scandal’s disclosure, as firms and nonprofits sought to collaborate on socioeconomic development. However, firms seen as directly or indirectly implicated in the scandal struggled to form partnerships due to risks to nonprofits’ legitimacy. Moreover, nonprofits perceived as closely aligned with firms (i.e., trade associations) or the state (i.e., education and research nonprofits or those with political capital) were especially cautious about entering partnerships to avoid appearing complicit in advancing corporate political influence. Our findings thus “check” the instrumentalization of corporate-nonprofit partnerships in two ways: by examining how it unfolds after an institutional crisis and by revealing how nonprofits resist being used as vehicles for corporate reputational repair or political gain. We show that such crises can trigger a broader realignment of corporate partnerships with nonmarket actors, highlighting the need to study shifts across nonmarket sectors, and not just within them.

**Research Question**
How do revelations of government corruption scandals influence corporate-nonprofit partnerships by altering the institutional context and shifting the benefits and risks for both parties?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Disclosure of government corruption scandal (Operation Carwash, post-2014 dummy); firm taint (directly implicated, state ownership, political board ties); nonprofit susceptibility to co-optation (trade association, education & research nonprofit, nonprofit political capital)
- DV(s): Likelihood of a corporate-nonprofit partnership (firm-nonprofit dyad-year binary)
- Mediators: Not explicitly modeled (theoretical mechanisms: shifts in perceived benefits of socioeconomic development collaboration and perceived risks to nonprofit legitimacy)
- Moderators: Firm taint (H2a, H2b); nonprofit type—trade association (H3a), education & research (H3b); nonprofit political capital (H4)

A highly mediatized government corruption scandal reshapes the institutional context by eroding trust in government and shifting legitimacy toward nonprofits, amplifying the joint benefits of corporate-nonprofit collaboration on socioeconomic development (H1). At the same time, firms tainted directly or indirectly by the scandal become risky partners because association with them threatens nonprofits' legitimacy (H2). Nonprofits that could serve as vehicles for corporate political influence—trade associations, education and research organizations, and those with political capital—become especially cautious because partnering heightens their exposure to accusations of co-optation and astroturfing (H3, H4). Thus, scandals trigger a realignment of cross-sector ties driven by the coevolution of benefits and risks perceived by both parties.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances a dynamic perspective on nonmarket strategy by showing that institutional changes—specifically government corruption scandals—trigger a realignment of corporate partnerships with nonmarket actors that spans political and nonprofit arenas rather than operating within each in isolation. It illuminates how nonprofits actively resist instrumentalization for corporate reputational repair and political influence, integrating corporate social responsibility with corporate political activity research. By theorizing and measuring firm- and nonprofit-level heterogeneity in post-scandal partnering, the paper reveals limits to using nonprofits as vehicles for stakeholder support and political goodwill even for firms not directly implicated in the scandal.

**Practical Implication**
The findings can help firms and nonprofits adapt more quickly when corruption scandals or similar institutional crises occur, or become more proactive and intentional in crafting partnership strategies in advance. Managers gain a clearer view of how the benefits and risks of nonmarket ties shift from different perspectives after such events. Policymakers and civil society gain insight into the sometimes opaque implications of firm-nonprofit relationships for government and public trust.

**Limitations**
The theoretical rationale is based on the combined net benefits and risks to firms and nonprofits, but the authors cannot directly observe which partnerships were declined or the reasons behind such decisions, nor can they measure the specific benefit and risk combinations that led to each collaboration. Operation Carwash is a rare large-scale scandal, limiting generalizability to smaller or structurally different corruption events. The arguments assume nonprofits are credited with disinterested pursuit of social welfare, so results could change if nonprofits themselves face allegations of misconduct.

**Future Research**
Future work could extend the theory using configurational approaches and examine generalizability to other settings and types of institutional change that introduce political uncertainty, such as the Sunflower Movement in Taiwan. Researchers could assess the longer-term sustainability of the observed realignment, including whether effects persist if public concern about corporate political influence recedes. Cross-country analyses could explore how rising anti-government and anti-corporate sentiments shape firm-nonprofit dynamics, and investigators could study how nonprofit financial misconduct or government opposition alters these patterns.
