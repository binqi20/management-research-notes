**Theoretical Contribution**
As an editorial introducing the Special Research Forum, the piece frames today as a "transformational era" in which new technologies (AI, algorithms, digital trace data, platforms) and urgent societal challenges (climate change, inequity, public health, migration) jointly destabilize foundational assumptions about what firms are, for whom they exist, and how scholars should study them. The editors organize the SRF's 10 studies into three thematic clusters—(1) technology and the changing nature of knowledge and work, (2) identity, inclusion, and human flourishing in the workplace, and (3) governance, stakeholders, and the reconfiguration of institutional relationships—and argue that bridging micro and macro lenses and adopting "researching forward" designs (real-time studies, field experiments, prospective case studies, racing designs) is needed to keep theory relevant to rapidly unfolding phenomena.

**Practical Implication**
The editors urge scholars to move beyond retrospective theorizing and to articulate practical and policy implications of their work more clearly to broad audiences, and they call on organizations and leaders to build inclusive, adaptive, intersectoral forms of organizing capable of engaging diverse stakeholders and addressing grand challenges that no single entity can solve alone.

**Future Research**
The editors highlight opportunities to study the nonlinear, sometimes reversing trajectories of institutional change and public expectations; to investigate links between work arrangements (e.g., virtual work), mental illness, and individual/organizational strategies for managing engagement; and to advance "researching forward" approaches—real-time studies of unfolding issues, collaborative inquiry, field experiments, prospective case studies, and racing designs—that remain empirically open to the future and engage more directly with practice.
