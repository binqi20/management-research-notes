# Interlacing Situated and Algorithmic Modes of Knowledge Work: A Workplace Jurisdiction Perspective

**Abstract**
Algorithmic technologies are challenging to integrate into knowledge work; they produce knowledge claims profoundly differently from the situated mode in which experts work. Because knowledge work is often accomplished through the flow or sequential progression of work across multiple occupations, knowledge claims must be legitimized and accepted across those occupations and not by just a single focal user group. Yet, we know little about the deeper changes that organizations must make regarding who has the legitimate expertise to produce knowledge claims and what counts as valuable knowledge when the algorithmic mode of knowledge work enters the workplace. To explore this, we draw on workplace jurisdictions as the foundation for dividing and coordinating work among occupations to study how the algorithmic mode of knowledge work becomes integrated with the situated flow of work, creating a new workplace jurisdiction. Our three-year ethnographic study of the radiology workflow in a medical center shows that interlacing practices weave together the two distinct modes of knowledge work by reorganizing existing relations among tasks, workplace artifacts, and technologies and enacting those reorganized relations. Contrary to the literature’s focus on the interface between algorithmic knowledge claims and a single focal user group, our study proposes a trans-group focus that refines scholarly understanding of how organizations can integrate algorithmic technologies with situated practices of professionals and their knowledge claims, as well as how new workplace jurisdictions emerge around technologies.

**Research Question**
How does the algorithmic mode of knowledge work become integrated with the situated mode of knowledge work across the flow of work to create new workplace jurisdictions?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / modes: situated mode vs. algorithmic mode of knowledge work; expert knowledge claims vs. algorithmic knowledge claims; workplace jurisdiction as the legitimate division and coordination of work among occupations.
- Reorganizing interlacing practices: (1) redefining tasks and interactions (co-developing detailed image-processing protocols with radiologists); (2) reorchestrating workplace artifacts through technology (reprogramming the PACS workflow engine to auto-route images and results).
- Enacting interlacing practices: (3) systematically using algorithmic technologies (Vitrea, ISP) to construct accurate, consistent image-processing results; (4) embedding algorithmic knowledge claims into expert knowledge claims (prefilling radiology reports with standardized templates).
- Consequences for jurisdiction creation: unique combinatory expertise developed; distinct value added to the flow of work; a new task area created.
- Triggering conditions: temporally constrained, interdependent knowledge work whose situated mode left algorithmic capabilities dormant and whose idiosyncratic expert claims spawned demands for standardization and replicability.

A low-status group of radiographers (the image processing lab, IPL), established merely to relieve radiologists of routine tasks, used four interlacing practices to weave the situated and algorithmic modes of knowledge work together. It first reorganized task boundaries and the technological infrastructure of the workflow, then enacted systematic algorithmic-technology use and embedded algorithmic knowledge claims into radiologists’ reports. By establishing and reinforcing a new structure of coordination and securing downstream occupations’ legitimization of its algorithmic knowledge claims, IPL created a new workplace jurisdiction whose boundaries simultaneously demarcated its distinctive expertise and connected occupations across the flow of work.

**Data & Measures**
Three-year ethnography at "Southern" (pseudonym), the radiology department of one of six Dutch university medical centers. Corpus: roughly 192.5 hours of non-participant observation (167.5 hours seated beside IPL members across 37 days, plus 25 hours across the broader workflow), 34 semi-structured interviews (9 IPL members, 8 radiologists, 1 referring physician, 14 managers, 2 IT specialists), and 544 documents (9 public and 535 internal, chiefly evolving image-processing protocols and Vitrea/ISP manuals), triangulated with on-site member checks. Analysis was grounded and inductive in four steps (immersing in the case; defining the analytical focus around the situated/algorithmic gap; conceptualizing interlacing practices; operationalizing jurisdiction-creation outcomes), building an in-depth process narrative (Langley, 1999). Focal constructs were coded from the data: algorithmic vs. expert knowledge claims, algorithmic technologies (Vitrea, ISP) vs. workflow technology (PACS), and the four interlacing practices.

**Key Findings**
As an inductive study, the paper reports an emergent process model rather than hypothesis tests. Despite its low status and a mandate merely to offload routine, time-intensive tasks, the IPL created a new workplace jurisdiction over algorithmic image processing and won legitimacy from radiologists, referring physicians, and managers. Jurisdiction creation manifested in three interrelated acts: unique combinatory expertise developed (anatomical/pathological knowledge combined with skilled use of Vitrea and ISP), distinct value added to the flow of work (standardized and enriched reports, alleviated radiologist workload, improved inter-occupational coordination), and a new task area created (IPL formalized as a separate section with its own section head and 5% extra compensation). The four interlacing practices were co-constitutive and mutually reinforcing, generating a positive feedback loop that expanded interlacing onto new tasks (task protocols grew from 1 to 13 over the observation window). Four triggering conditions enabled the process: temporally constrained work performed interdependently across occupations, whose situated mode left algorithmic capabilities dormant and whose idiosyncratic expert knowledge claims spawned growing demands for standardization and replicability.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study makes two contributions, both emphasizing a trans-group focus. First, it introduces interlacing practices as a means of integrating the algorithmic mode of knowledge work with the situated mode across the flow of work, through a new structure of coordination and downstream occupations’ legitimization of algorithmic knowledge claims, moving beyond the literature’s focus on the local interface between an algorithm and a single focal user group. Second, it shows how interlacing creates a new workplace jurisdiction by proactively and simultaneously altering an occupation’s (human) relational and non-relational aspects, leveraging novel technology both to develop unique expertise within a group and to improve inter-occupational coordination, thereby revealing the duality of jurisdictional boundaries that demarcate and connect.

**Practical Implication**
For managers of professional knowledge work adopting algorithmic technologies, the study suggests approaching adoption holistically by comprehensively reorganizing the workflow before or in conjunction with deploying the technologies, rather than relying on local quick fixes. Managers can support cross-occupational knowledge sharing (for example, by assigning roles or creating a cross-functional team to reassess the workflow) and collaborate with IT specialists so that integration minimally disrupts the flow of work. Creatively reallocating the tasks that algorithmic technologies tackle, preferably to a nonexpert group upstream while experts retain authority to validate and modify algorithmic knowledge claims, can break the vicious cycle of limited use and limited value and even upskill rather than weaken an occupation.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that their case represents one of various possible sociotechnical constellations, so the specificities of a single-case setting may have shaped the theorization. For example, the referring physicians who played a crucial role in IPL gaining legitimacy were located in the same organization as IPL members and radiologists.

**Future Research**
The paper explicitly calls for research on whether IPL’s workplace jurisdiction will be formalized in the public or legal realm, including developments in legal protections and professional associations; on how jurisdiction creation through interlacing unfolds when occupations sharing a flow of work are located across different organizations; and on the conditions under which interlacing is or is not likely to create a jurisdiction, such as settings where professional accountability is not crucial or non-professional settings where the algorithmic mode may substitute for, rather than complement, the situated mode.
