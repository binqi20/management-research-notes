# Interlacing Situated and Algorithmic Modes of Knowledge Work: A Workplace Jurisdiction Perspective

**Abstract**
Algorithmic technologies are challenging to integrate into knowledge work; they produce knowledge claims profoundly differently from the situated mode in which experts work. Because knowledge work is often accomplished through the flow or sequential progression of work across multiple occupations, knowledge claims must be legitimized and accepted across those occupations and not by just a single focal user group. Yet, we know little about the deeper changes that organizations must make regarding who has the legitimate expertise to produce knowledge claims and what counts as valuable knowledge when the algorithmic mode of knowledge work enters the workplace. To explore this, we draw on workplace jurisdictions as the foundation for dividing and coordinating work among occupations to study how the algorithmic mode of knowledge work becomes integrated with the situated flow of work, creating a new workplace jurisdiction. Our three-year ethnographic study of the radiology workflow in a medical center shows that interlacing practices weave together the two distinct modes of knowledge work by reorganizing existing relations among tasks, workplace artifacts, and technologies and enacting those reorganized relations. Contrary to the literature’s focus on the interface between algorithmic knowledge claims and a single focal user group, our study proposes a trans-group focus that refines scholarly understanding of how organizations can integrate algorithmic technologies with situated practices of professionals and their knowledge claims, as well as how new workplace jurisdictions emerge around technologies.

**Research Question**
"How does the algorithmic mode of knowledge work become integrated with the situated mode of knowledge work across the flow of work to create new workplace jurisdictions?"

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Introduction of algorithmic technologies (Vitrea, ISP) into a situated professional workflow; assignment of their use to a nascent group of nonexpert technicians (IPL members).
- DV(s): Creation of a new workplace jurisdiction legitimized across multiple occupations; successful integration of algorithmic knowledge claims into the flow of professional knowledge work.
- Mediators: "Interlacing practices" - reorganizing practices (redefining tasks and interactions; reorchestrating workplace artifacts through technology) and enacting practices (systematically using algorithmic technologies; embedding algorithmic knowledge claims into expert knowledge claims).
- Moderators: Nonexpert group's openness to exploring algorithmic capabilities; sufficient knowledge overlap with expert occupations; temporal pressures that lead experts to make pragmatic concessions; algorithmic technologies' potential to substantially enrich rather than substitute for expert knowledge claims.

Algorithmic and situated modes of knowledge work are incommensurable - one produces context-transcending, systematic knowledge claims via predefined procedures on digital data, the other produces context-embedded claims via embodied skills and intuition. When a nascent group of nonexperts is assigned the use of algorithmic technologies within a multi-occupational workflow, interlacing practices prevent the situated mode from overriding the algorithmic mode by reorganizing tasks, artifacts, and technologies (including reprogramming the PACS workflow engine) and by enacting those reorganized relations through systematic technology use. This simultaneously alters the occupation's non-relational aspects (unique expertise with algorithmic technologies) and relational aspects (coordination with upstream radiographers and downstream radiologists/referring physicians), yielding a jurisdiction whose boundaries both demarcate task area and connect groups, thereby legitimating algorithmic knowledge claims across the full flow of work.

**Theoretical Contribution**
First, the paper introduces interlacing practices as a trans-group mechanism for integrating algorithmic technologies into the flow of knowledge work across multiple occupations, departing from prior literature's emphasis on local interfacing between algorithmic tools and a single focal user group (e.g., questioning, validating, interrogating, translating by algorithmic brokers). Second, it advances workplace jurisdiction theory by showing that new jurisdictions around novel technologies can emerge through the simultaneous and proactive alteration of both an occupation's relational and non-relational aspects, producing boundaries that both demarcate and connect, and by foregrounding the reconfiguration of the broader technological infrastructure (workflow technology) as a central jurisdiction-creating move.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of professional knowledge work should adopt algorithmic technologies holistically by comprehensively reorganizing the workflow before or alongside deployment, rather than applying local quick fixes. They should support cross-occupational knowledge sharing, creatively reallocate tasks to an upstream nonexpert group with sufficient knowledge overlap (e.g., technicians rather than administrators), and collaborate with IT specialists to reprogram workflow infrastructure so that algorithmic knowledge claims flow seamlessly into expert work - thereby upskilling rather than weakening professional occupations.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a single ethnographic case within one radiology department in one Dutch academic medical center, so the specific sociotechnical and organizational setting may shape theorization. Referring physicians were housed in the same organization as IPL members and radiologists, leaving unexplored how interlacing unfolds when occupations in a shared workflow span organizational boundaries. The authors also did not assess whether IPL's jurisdiction will be formalized in legal or public realms, or test boundary conditions in settings where professional accountability is less central.

**Future Research**
Future research could trace whether workplace jurisdictions created through interlacing (like IPL) are formalized in legal and public realms and evolve into full occupations. Scholars might study interlacing across organizational boundaries, in non-healthcare settings, in non-professional knowledge work contexts (where the algorithmic mode may substitute for rather than complement the situated mode), and where algorithmic technologies substitute for rather than enrich expert knowledge claims. Studies could tease out conditions under which interlacing is most and least likely to create jurisdictions, including settings lacking strong professional accountability or where slow, careful work is normative (e.g., R&D).
