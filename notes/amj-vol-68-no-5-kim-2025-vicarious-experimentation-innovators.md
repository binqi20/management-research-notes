# Vicarious Experimentation: Do Innovators Learn by Being Imitated?

**Abstract**
Strategy scholars conventionally view imitation as a one-way knowledge transfer from an innovating firm to an imitating firm. We propose that being imitated also serves as a source of learning for the innovator. An innovator learns by being imitated—observing the imitator’s choices and outcomes and comparing them to its own. A key challenge in such learning, and in vicarious learning more broadly, is causal ambiguity in identifying the factors that drive observed performance outcomes. We theorize that an innovator’s learning by being imitated is particularly effective in overcoming causal ambiguity when it takes the form of vicarious experimentation, where an imitator’s near-clone of the innovator’s product functions as a quasi-experimental treatment from which the innovator can learn. This concept extends the logic of strategic experimentation by highlighting how firms can learn from experiments they do not control. We test our theory in the video game industry, demonstrating that vicarious experimentation enhances the quality of the innovator’s next-generation product and offering support for the theorized boundary conditions under which this effect holds.

**Research Question**
How and what can innovators learn from being imitated? Specifically, when and under what conditions do near-clonal imitations enable an innovator to improve the quality of its next-generation product through "vicarious experimentation"?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Extent of vicarious experimentation (aggregated angular-distance deviation in peripheral feature keywords between near-clone imitations and the original game)
- DV(s): Quality of the innovator's sequel game (critics' ratings from MobyGames and Metacritic)
- Mediators: Not explicitly modeled (causal inference about feature-outcome links is the theoretical mediator)
- Moderators: Prominence of imitators (market share, entropy, award status, imitation-game sales); disruption of tacit product-specific knowledge (change in the innovator's game development unit)

Because near-clones preserve the innovator's core features while varying peripheral features, each imitator's product functions as a quasi-experimental treatment against the innovator's original (the control), enabling the innovator to draw causal inferences about which feature variations improve performance. A larger and more varied pool of near-clones supplies more parallel "experiments" than any single firm could run internally, reducing causal ambiguity and allowing the innovator to selectively incorporate successful modifications into its sequel. This learning is stronger when imitators are prominent (richer, more reliable observational data) and weaker when the innovator's development unit changes (erosion of tacit, product-specific absorptive capacity needed to interpret observed variations).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces "vicarious experimentation" as a novel mechanism situated at the intersection of vicarious learning and strategic experimentation, reframing being imitated from a purely competitive hazard into a source of targeted, causally actionable learning for innovators. By distinguishing near-clonal imitation from both perfect clones and recombinatory knowledge spillovers, it specifies when imitation overcomes rather than reproduces causal ambiguity, and it identifies informational (imitator prominence) and absorptive (tacit-knowledge retention) boundary conditions that govern the effect.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should reframe imitation: rather than dismissing copycats or focusing exclusively on forestalling imitation, innovator firms should systematically study near-clones of their products as quasi-experiments that reveal which peripheral feature variations improve performance. Firms should also protect the continuity of the original development unit, since organizational changes (team turnover, studio reassignment, M&A-driven reorganization) erode the tacit product-specific knowledge needed to interpret and integrate insights from imitators' variations.

**Limitations**
The analysis cannot fully disentangle learning effects from competitive dynamics, and the results do not establish strict causality—the authors rely on extensive controls, Oster (2019) unobservables bounds, and alternative specifications rather than instruments or natural experiments. The study focuses narrowly on vicarious experimentation and does not jointly model other channels through which imitation shapes innovator outcomes (e.g., customer perceptions, competitive responses, recombinatory spillovers). It also does not resolve whether innovators or imitators gain more from the process, nor does it empirically test disruptive-innovation boundary conditions.

**Future Research**
Future work should disentangle the interaction between competition and imitation, develop a comprehensive framework integrating vicarious experimentation with shifts in customer perceptions, rival responses, and recombinatory knowledge spillovers, and examine when being imitated is better for the innovator versus the imitator under different industry and product-category conditions. Scholars should also pursue stronger causal identification in imitation research and explore how vicarious experimentation interacts with disruptive innovation, Red Queen competition, and convergence/divergence in product features.
