# The Limits of Pivoting: A Culturally Informed Model of Resource Commitments during Repeated Organizational Transformation

**Abstract**
In contrast to models of organizational transformation that focus on one-time episodes of change, this study reveals limits to the number of transformations an organization can make without losing the support of key stakeholder groups. To better understand the process of repeated transformation, we study an extreme case of a medical technology startup with a history of pivoting, focusing on the degree to which employees and investors continued to support the venture. Although new ventures are often advised to be highly adaptive, we find that repeated pivoting introduces relational liabilities for even highly flexible organizations. Drawing on our analysis, we develop a culturally informed process model of resource commitments that reveals how differences in pivoting narratives and stakeholder orientations affect the flow of support from employees and investors, even as the organization repeatedly pivots. Our model contributes to existing theories of organizational transformation, offering an enriched understanding of how organizations can narratively sustain resource commitments to enable continuous adaptation.

**Research Question**
How do different and changing narratives affect employees' and investors' resource commitments as a venture repeatedly pivots? The study asks why some stakeholders sustain and others withdraw their human and financial resource commitments across successive organizational transformations.

**Mechanism Process**
- Pivoting narratives (two types the CEO circulated simultaneously): a *revolutionary* narrative (a "clean break" from the past, a *proximate* future with no further change needed, *individual* agency) versus a *scientific revision* narrative (a process that *connects with* the past, a *distal* future of continuous experimentation, *collective* agency).
- Narrative elements coded across both narratives: "the past" (break from / connect with), "the future" (proximate / distal), and "source of agency" (individual / collective).
- Stakeholder orientations: *market pricing* (an extractive basis of exchange — valuing interactions for personal or professional benefit) versus *communal sharing* (a generative basis of exchange — valuing contribution to shared purpose), identified across both employees and investors.
- Cultural resonance: the degree of fit between a pivoting narrative and a stakeholder's orientation, which shifts over time and across pivots.
- Resource commitments (emergent outcomes): human (extra-role effort, tenure) and financial (capital-raise participation, buy/hold/sell decisions), aggregating to the venture's entrepreneurial "runway."

The process model holds that resource commitments flow not from a narrative alone but from its cultural resonance with a stakeholder's orientation. Revolutionary narratives promise proximate, specific deliverables that resonate with market-pricing stakeholders and produce a short-lived surge of resources; because repeated pivots break those promises, resonance collapses and these stakeholders withdraw. Scientific-revision narratives frame pivots as connected, collective experiments toward a distal purpose, resonating with communal-sharing stakeholders whose expectations are actualized rather than violated by continued change, so their commitments persist across repeated pivots and sustain the venture's runway.

**Data & Measures**
Real-time, longitudinal ethnographic case study of "Respiro" (a pseudonym), a publicly listed Australian micro-cap medical-technology venture that pivoted from patent licensing to sports products (SportsPro) to sleep applications (SleepPro) to medical cannabis. The data corpus comprised 150 ethnographic observations, 75 semi-structured interviews (including the CEO nine times and 11 employees), and 245 archival documents (company announcements, investor presentations, board materials, and 624 comments across investor discussion-forum threads), collected February 2013-December 2015 by an insider-outsider team (the first author served on the board). Analysis proceeded inductively in four stages: (1) constructing a timeline of pivots and human/financial resource flows; (2) identifying the two pivoting narratives and their three elements; (3) identifying stakeholders' orientations (market pricing vs. communal sharing); and (4) linking the cultural resonance of narratives to resource commitments over time (Table 4 color-coded each stakeholder's reactions across the three pivots). The design is interpretive and process-oriented rather than causal-identification-based.

**Key Findings**
Respiro's CEO advanced two pivoting narratives that circulated simultaneously; over time the scientific-revision narrative became dominant. The revolutionary narrative initially resonated with market-pricing-oriented stakeholders (e.g., the CFO Julia, marketing staff, and day traders), raising short-term human and financial resource commitments; but across repeated pivots its broken promises destroyed that resonance, and these stakeholders curtailed extra-role effort, divested, refused capital raises, and eventually left the venture. The scientific-revision narrative resonated with communal-sharing-oriented stakeholders (e.g., Liz, Nadine, and mission-driven investors), who sustained or increased their human and financial commitments across all three pivots despite lackluster financial performance, because repeated pivots confirmed rather than violated the narrative's promise of continuous, collective experimentation toward a broad purpose. The central finding is that cultural resonance between narrative and stakeholder orientation — not the narrative type alone — governed whether commitments were temporary or sustained, so scientific-revision narratives paired with communal-sharing orientations enabled repeated pivoting by sustaining the entrepreneurial runway.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study develops a culturally informed process model of resource commitments during repeated organizational transformation, extending transformation research beyond single change episodes to show how the residual effects of prior pivots constrain later ones and how pivoting narratives can narratively sustain (or erode) stakeholder support across successive change. It theoretically elaborates the concept of the entrepreneurial "runway," reconceptualizing it as resources contributed by both employees and investors (human as well as financial capital) that are dynamically constructed and reconstructed through pivoting narratives rather than a fixed function of cash and burn rate. It also extends stakeholder theory by showing how within-group differences in stakeholders' orientations (market pricing vs. communal sharing) shape the cultural resonance of narratives and thereby their sustained access to resources.

**Practical Implication**
For entrepreneurial leaders of ventures that must pivot repeatedly, the study surfaces a cultural "toolkit": framing change as "scientific revision" — repositioning strategic goals as hypotheses, strategic action as tests, strategic failure as learning, and strategic change as pivoting — helps leaders maintain stakeholder engagement and sustain resource commitments across repeated transformations without rendering themselves culpable, whereas revolutionary "clean break" narratives tend to secure only short-lived commitments that collapse once repeated change exposes broken promises.

**Limitations**
Insights derive from a single micro-cap venture, raising questions of generalizability; the authors position the findings as most applicable to organizations closest to Respiro and as inviting re-theorization when extended to organizations farther from this template, such as larger corporations. Only two of the four stakeholder orientations from prior relational theory — market pricing and communal sharing — were observed in the case, leaving authority ranking and equality matching unexamined.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research into how different cultural contexts shape the effectiveness of pivoting narratives and into the limits and resilience of scientific-revision narratives over extended periods, as well as extensions across a broader range of industries and organizational sizes (including large corporations) to test whether the narrative and orientation effects are universal. They also invite study of other pivoting narratives built on alternative metaphors of change (e.g., disruptive innovation, evolutionary change, high-impact social mission), settings in which all four stakeholder orientations are present, and other forms of capital (technological, intellectual, social, and institutional) beyond the human and financial resources examined here.
