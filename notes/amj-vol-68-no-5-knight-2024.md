# The Limits of Pivoting: A Culturally Informed Model of Resource Commitments during Repeated Organizational Transformation

**Abstract**
In contrast to models of organizational transformation that focus on one-time episodes of change, this study reveals limits to the number of transformations an organization can make without losing the support of key stakeholder groups. To better understand the process of repeated transformation, we study an extreme case of a medical technology startup with a history of pivoting, focusing on the degree to which employees and investors continued to support the venture. Although new ventures are often advised to be highly adaptive, we find that repeated pivoting introduces relational liabilities for even highly flexible organizations. Drawing on our analysis, we develop a culturally informed process model of resource commitments that reveals how differences in pivoting narratives and stakeholder orientations affect the flow of support from employees and investors, even as the organization repeatedly pivots. Our model contributes to existing theories of organizational transformation, offering an enriched understanding of how organizations can narratively sustain resource commitments to enable continuous adaptation.

**Research Question**
How do different and changing narratives affect employees' and investors' resource commitments as a venture repeatedly pivots?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Pivoting narratives (revolutionary narrative of change vs. scientific revision narrative of change), varying along three elements — relation to the past ("break from" vs. "connected with"), horizon of future promises ("proximate" vs. "distal"), and source of agency ("individual" vs. "collective").
- DV(s): Sustained human and financial resource commitments from employees and investors across repeated pivots (resource flows into/out of the venture).
- Mediators: Cultural resonance between a pivoting narrative and stakeholders' motivations/expectations for resource exchange.
- Moderators: Stakeholders' orientations toward joint value creation — market pricing (professionals, day traders) vs. communal sharing (entrepreneurs, mission-driven investors).

As a venture repeatedly pivots, leaders construct pivoting narratives that frame the past, future, and source of agency for change. Revolutionary narratives initially mobilize resources by promising a sharp break and proximate payoffs, but expose leaders to culpability and break down as repeated pivots fail to deliver on specific short-term promises, prompting market-pricing-oriented stakeholders to withdraw. Scientific revision narratives frame pivots as learning experiments anchored to an abstract, long-term vision and a collective source of agency; these resonate with communal-sharing-oriented stakeholders, whose motivations for joint value creation are reinforced (rather than undermined) by repeated change, thereby sustaining resource commitments across pivots.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a culturally informed process model of resource commitments during repeated organizational transformation, showing that the narrative practices known to enable single episodes of change can fail under repeated pivoting, and that "scientific revision" narratives paired with communal sharing orientations enable continuous adaptation. It theoretically elaborates the concept of the entrepreneurial "runway" to include human (not only financial) capital and to position it as a culturally situated product of narrative-stakeholder interaction. It also extends stakeholder theory by showing how within-group variation in market pricing vs. communal sharing orientations shapes the resonance of entrepreneurial narratives and sustained resource flows.

**Practical Implication**
Entrepreneurial leaders managing repeated pivots should frame strategic change as scientific revision — strategies as hypotheses, actions as tests, failures as learning — rather than as revolutionary breaks, because revolutionary narratives render leaders culpable and erode support when pivots recur. Leaders should also recognize that different stakeholders (even within the same formal group) hold market pricing or communal sharing orientations, and should tailor narratives accordingly to preserve both financial and human resource commitments during continuous transformation.

**Limitations**
The study is an inductive single-case study of one publicly listed micro-cap medical-technology venture, which limits statistical generalizability and invites re-theorization in settings farther from this template. Only two of the four relational-model stakeholder orientations (market pricing and communal sharing) were observed as pervasive at Respiro, leaving authority ranking and equality matching unexamined. The effectiveness of scientific revision narratives may have boundary conditions in settings where stakeholders demand greater precision than the narrative's abstraction allows.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how different cultural contexts and alternative narrative devices (e.g., disruptive innovation, evolutionary change, high-impact social mission) shape the effectiveness of pivoting narratives, and test whether the findings generalize to larger organizations and other industries. Researchers should explore the full set of stakeholder orientations (including authority ranking and equality matching), investigate the long-term limits and resilience of scientific revision narratives, and extend the culturally informed runway concept to additional forms of capital (technological, intellectual, social, institutional).
