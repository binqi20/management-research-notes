# Would Archimedes Shout “Eureka” with Algorithms? The Hidden Hand of Algorithmic Design in Idea Generation, the Creation of Ideation Bubbles, and How Experts Can Burst Them

**Abstract**
Does “eureka” still ring true in the algorithmic age? This paper investigates algorithmic design and its impact on idea generation. The use of algorithms transforms knowledge work processes, redefining experts’ roles across industries. While experts are typically viewed as the cornerstone of knowledge work, an emerging debate is underway about whether expertise is still necessary in the idea generation process for creativity and innovation. Integrating creativity, expertise in knowledge work, and algorithmic design literatures, we theorize on the interplay between expertise and algorithmic design in idea generation. We suggest that the prevailing algorithmic design, focusing on information exploitation, is misaligned with the recombinant innovation processes needed for idea generation, especially for experts. We design an exploration-based modification to the prevalent exploitation-based algorithm—Google Search. We hypothesize that when using exploration-based algorithms, experts can overcome confirmation bias and generate more creative ideas through recombinant innovation. Moving beyond the individual level, experts are central to bursting “ideation bubbles”—clusters of similar ideas—thus enhancing idea diversity. We test our theory in a laboratory study and a field experiment through a global ideation challenge in sustainability launched on Freelancer. Findings offer insights into designing and using algorithms to augment human expertise for innovation.

**Research Question**
Does domain expertise still matter for idea generation in the algorithmic age, and how does algorithmic design logic—exploitation-based versus exploration-based—shape whether experts generate more creative ideas (individually) and greater diversity of ideas (at the aggregate level)? As the paper frames it in its discussion: "What role does expertise play in idea generation with algorithms?"

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Hypothesis 0 (baseline). In the idea generation process, using algorithms based on exploration design logic leads individuals to generate ideas that are more creative, compared to algorithms based on exploitation design logic.
Hypothesis 1a. Algorithm design logic and domain expertise interact to affect idea creativity, such that individuals with high levels of domain expertise will generate ideas that are more creative when using exploration-based algorithms (compared to those with low levels of domain expertise).
Hypothesis 1b. The recombinant innovation process mediates the interactive effect of algorithm design logic and domain expertise on the generation of creative ideas.
Hypothesis 2. Algorithm design logic and domain expertise interact to affect the diversity of ideas, such that those with high levels of domain expertise will contribute to greater diversity of ideas when using exploration-based algorithms (compared to those with low levels of domain expertise).

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Algorithmic design logic — exploration-based (XYZ, a modification that promotes the "tail end" of search results) vs. exploitation-based (Google Search)
- DV(s): Idea creativity (individual level); diversity of ideas / cognitive diversity (aggregate level, operationalized as the number of semantic clusters, i.e., ideation bubbles)
- Mediator(s): Recombinant innovation (reconfiguring the topics in the algorithm-provided information set into new combinations)
- Moderator(s): Domain expertise (years of prior experience in the sustainability domain)

Exploitation-based algorithms surface popular, familiar, relevance-optimized information that triggers "algorithmic confirmation bias," locking experts into prevailing knowledge and suppressing creative recombination. Exploration-based algorithms instead surface diverse, uncommon information; domain experts, drawing on their deep knowledge reservoir, can synthesize these distant elements through recombinant innovation to generate more creative ideas. Beyond the individual level, experts using exploration-based algorithms can burst dominant "ideation bubbles" (bounded clusters of similar ideas) and form new, less conventional ones, thereby expanding the diversity of ideas and heterogeneity of thought at the aggregate level.

**Data & Measures**
Pilot Study 1 (laboratory, N = 104 undergraduate students): random between-subjects assignment to Google Search (exploitation) or XYZ (exploration) before generating an idea to reduce overconsumption; idea creativity rated by three blind domain-expert evaluators on six 7-point items ("original," "novel," "unique," "practical," "feasible," "applicable"); controls for age and gender. Study 2 (preregistered field experiment on Freelancer, N = 245 creators across 40+ countries, household food-waste challenge): same exploitation-vs-exploration manipulation; idea creativity scored by the same expert panel; domain expertise measured as self-reported years of prior experience in the sustainability domain (continuous; 37% novices with zero years); recombinant innovation quantified via a ratio approach (number of topics combined weighted by pairwise co-occurrence frequency, following Fleming & Sorenson, 2004); diversity of ideas measured with NLP sentence embeddings plus DBSCAN density-based semantic clustering (counting ideation bubbles); controls for age, gender, general digital-tool use, task motivation, and geographic region. Both studies use randomized experimental assignment, supporting causal identification of the design-logic effect.

**Key Findings**
- Baseline H0 supported in BOTH studies: exploration (XYZ) produced more creative ideas than exploitation (Google Search). Pilot Study 1: M = 4.77 (SD = 0.76) vs. M = 3.35 (SD = 1.27), t(102) = 2.13, p = .04; OLS b = .43, p = .04. Study 2: M = 4.33 (SD = 0.79) vs. M = 4.03 (SD = 0.88), t(243) = 2.75, p = .01; OLS b = .31, p = .01. (Abstract summary: XYZ increased idea creativity by ~14% in the lab and ~11% in the field.)
- H1a supported (tested in Study 2 only, which measured expertise): positive algorithm-design × domain-expertise interaction on idea creativity (two-way ANOVA F(1,241) = 5.63, p = .02; OLS interaction b = .05, p = .01). Simple slopes — under exploration, expertise related positively to creativity (b = .04, p = .01); under exploitation, the relationship was non-significant (b = −.01, p = .57).
- H1b supported (Study 2 only): recombinant innovation mediated the interactive effect. Exploration produced significantly greater recombinant innovation (b = .05, p < .001); the design × expertise interaction on recombinant innovation was significant only at the 10% level (b = .01, p = .09); recombinant innovation in turn predicted idea creativity (b = 1.38, p = .03); positive indirect effect = 0.01, 90% CI [0.01, 0.02].
- H2 supported (Study 2 only): domain experts enhanced aggregate idea diversity, and the effect was stronger under exploration. Semantic clustering — with exploration (XYZ), experts' ideas formed five clusters vs. two for novices; with exploitation (Google Search), experts formed two clusters vs. one for novices — i.e., experts burst dominant ideation bubbles and formed new, less conventional ones.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the future-of-work debate on the relevance of expertise by unveiling the central role of domain expertise in reaping algorithms' benefits for idea generation: exploration-based design mitigates algorithmic confirmation bias and enables experts to conduct productive recombinant innovation, which the paper operationalizes in an algorithmic context. It extends the creativity/grand-challenges discourse by showing experts' benefits manifest beyond the individual level, fostering diversity of ideas and heterogeneity of thought, and introduces the concept of "ideation bubbles" (the output analogue of filter bubbles). Finally, it advances the conversation on how algorithms shape knowledge work by shifting attention from efficiency to idea generation and revealing the "hidden hand" of algorithmic design logic.

**Practical Implication**
For creators, managers, and organizations, the paper suggests that during early ideation stages, exploration-based approaches that reveal diverse information may be more valuable for creative idea generation than standard exploitation-based tools. Practitioners can customize interfaces or adjust parameters in algorithmic tools (e.g., temperature settings in generative AI) to promote exploration when seeking creative solutions, strategically pair domain experts with exploration-based algorithms to maximize recombinant innovation, and monitor the distribution of ideas across solution spaces to help burst dominant thought patterns.

**Limitations**
The empirical focus is a single search algorithm (Google Search) and its modification, which does not encapsulate the full spectrum of algorithmic tools. The design documents only the short-term effect of algorithms on idea generation rather than dynamics over repeated interaction. The study examines domain expertise specifically and focuses on the early idea-generation phase and on individuals.

**Future Research**
The authors call for examining a broader range of algorithmic tools (e.g., interactive and collaborative algorithms such as LLMs, including prompting for narrower/broader search or different personas); investigating long-term and repeated algorithm exposure and whether effects magnify or induce different biases across expertise types; exploring how different forms of expertise shape the ability to leverage algorithms; studying different algorithmic designs and interfaces at different stages of the innovation process (generation vs. evaluation/implementation) and their short- versus long-term effects; and extending from individuals to teams and collaborative creativity.
