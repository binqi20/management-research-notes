# Would Archimedes Shout “Eureka” with Algorithms? The Hidden Hand of Algorithmic Design in Idea Generation, the Creation of Ideation Bubbles, and How Experts Can Burst Them

**Abstract**
Does “eureka” still ring true in the algorithmic age? This paper investigates algorithmic design and its impact on idea generation. The use of algorithms transforms knowledge work processes, redefining experts’ roles across industries. While experts are typically viewed as the cornerstone of knowledge work, an emerging debate is underway about whether expertise is still necessary in the idea generation process for creativity and innovation. Integrating creativity, expertise in knowledge work, and algorithmic design literatures, we theorize on the interplay between expertise and algorithmic design in idea generation. We suggest that the prevailing algorithmic design, focusing on information exploitation, is misaligned with the recombinant innovation processes needed for idea generation, especially for experts. We design an exploration-based modification to the prevalent exploitation-based algorithm—Google Search. We hypothesize that when using exploration-based algorithms, experts can overcome confirmation bias and generate more creative ideas through recombinant innovation. Moving beyond the individual level, experts are central to bursting “ideation bubbles”—clusters of similar ideas—thus enhancing idea diversity. We test our theory in a laboratory study and a field experiment through a global ideation challenge in sustainability launched on Freelancer. Findings offer insights into designing and using algorithms to augment human expertise for innovation.

**Research Question**
How do algorithmic design logic (exploration vs. exploitation) and domain expertise interact to shape idea generation—specifically, the creativity of ideas at the individual level and the diversity of ideas (including the formation and bursting of "ideation bubbles") at the aggregate level?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Algorithmic design logic (exploration-based XYZ vs. exploitation-based Google Search); domain expertise (years of experience in the sustainability domain)
- DV(s): Idea creativity (expert-rated novelty, originality, uniqueness, practicality, feasibility, applicability); diversity of ideas at the aggregate level (semantic clusters / ideation bubbles)
- Mediators: Recombinant innovation (extent to which ideas integrate semantically distant knowledge topics drawn from the algorithmic information set, weighted by pairwise topic co-occurrence)
- Moderators: Domain expertise moderates the effect of algorithmic design logic on idea creativity and on diversity of ideas

Exploitation-based search algorithms return popular, relevant, and similar information, which—when used by domain experts—reinforces familiar knowledge and triggers algorithmic confirmation bias, locking experts into a bounded ideation space and limiting recombinant innovation. In contrast, exploration-based algorithms present a wide, semantically diverse information set that bypasses experts' familiar cues and exposes them to unexpected concepts. Experts' deep domain knowledge then allows them to identify valuable associations across distant topics and synthesize them into creative recombinations that novices cannot easily execute. At the aggregate level, experts using exploration-based tools burst dominant "ideation bubbles"—bounded clusters of similar ideas—and seed new, less conventional bubbles, thereby expanding cognitive diversity and idea variety for the collective problem space.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper theorizes algorithmic design logic as a "hidden hand" that shapes idea generation and reveals a critical interplay between algorithmic design and domain expertise. Against claims that algorithms make expertise obsolete in creative work, the authors show that experts are precisely the actors best positioned to reap creative benefits from exploration-based algorithms because of their capacity for recombinant innovation, while exploitation-based algorithms trigger algorithmic confirmation bias and entrench experts in familiar frames. The study also introduces the concept of "ideation bubbles"—output-side counterparts to filter bubbles—and demonstrates how expert-by-algorithmic-design interactions shape cognitive diversity beyond the individual level, and it operationalizes recombinant innovation in an algorithmic context.

**Practical Implication**
When using digital tools in early-stage ideation, creators, managers, and organizations should favor exploration-based designs (e.g., adjusted parameters or temperature settings in GenAI) rather than standard exploitation-based tools to surface diverse information. Organizations should strategically pair domain experts with exploration-based algorithms—rather than treating algorithms as replacements for expertise—to maximize recombinant innovation and to burst dominant thought patterns, especially when tackling complex societal challenges that require heterogeneity of thought.

**Limitations**
The empirical test focuses on a single class of algorithm (a search engine modeled on Google Search) rather than the full spectrum of algorithmic tools such as LLMs and interactive collaborative algorithms. The studies document only the short-term effects of algorithm use on idea generation and do not track dynamics as users interact with algorithms repeatedly over time. The work centers on domain expertise and on the idea generation stage of the creative process, so it does not speak directly to other forms of expertise (e.g., process, algorithmic, functional) or to later stages such as idea evaluation, validation, championing, and implementation.

**Future Research**
Future work can broaden the range of algorithmic tools studied—particularly LLMs and collaborative AI—and examine how prompting strategies, personas, and interface features shape knowledge work in specific domains. Longitudinal designs should test whether repeated algorithm exposure amplifies or attenuates effects on creativity, whether different types of expertise generate different biases, and how algorithm familiarity and search habits condition the impact of exploration- versus exploitation-based designs. Researchers could also extend the framework to teams and collaborative creativity, to later stages of the innovation process where exploitation-based algorithms may be preferable, and to long-term consequences of ideation bubbles for homogeneity of thought at the organizational and societal levels.
