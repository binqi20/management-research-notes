# Subordinating Humanism: How Colliding Beliefs about a Living Wage Shape Personal Fulfillment and “Professional-Class” Identities in Working-Class Jobs

**Abstract**
In a society dominated by market-based ideology and management practices that prioritize financial considerations, some organizations are shifting toward humanistic ideology and practices that emphasize human welfare. To examine this transformation in pay-setting, we studied a U.S. company that introduced a living wage for its low-wage workers. Interviews with 64 participants across two sites revealed both intended and unintended effects. Motivated by humanistic aims, the living wage was designed to reduce financial insecurity; indeed, workers felt more financially secure and fulfilled in their personal lives. However, its humanistic intent conflicted with the dominant market-based ideology linking wages to performance, raising concerns about whether these workers deserved higher pay. To resolve this tension, managers and workers altered expectations for workers to reflect two aspects of professional-class roles: autonomy and overwork. As workers internalized these expectations, they adopted elements of professional-class identities while remaining in working-class jobs. Simultaneously, managers reaffirmed their own role identities as shapers of performance despite their weakened control over wages. These findings inform a multilevel model conceptualizing how a transformative humanistic practice can be subordinated to market-based ideology through identity work. We contribute to research on humanistic management and the interconnections between wage, class, work roles, and identity.

**Research Question**
How does the introduction of a humanistic practice — here, a living wage — into a context dominated by market-based ideology affect managers' and workers' identities?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Market-based ideology (wages tied to performance and external market value) versus humanistic ideology (pay as a means to human welfare and a good life); workers' and managers' beliefs about deservingness; working-class versus professional-class work-role identities, the latter defined by autonomy and overwork.
- Process / phases (three-phase model): (1) Status quo — market-based ideology in which financially insecure working-class workers, and their managers, treat that insecurity as normal and "deserved" for such jobs; (2) Destabilizing — a living wage is introduced, delivering financial security and fulfillment, but its decoupling of pay from performance collides with the entrenched market-based ideology, sparking workers' undeservingness concerns and threatening managers' ability to enact their role identities; (3) Restabilizing — managers demand, and workers embrace, professional-class traits, resolving the dissonance while subordinating the humanistic practice.
- Practices: Managers convey escalated expectations through performance reviews and revised hiring criteria, invoking labels such as "be your own CEO" (autonomy) and rejecting the "clockpuncher" (overwork); workers take on autonomy and overwork out of gratitude/reciprocity, relief from feelings of undeservingness, and felt pressure.
- Emergent outcomes: Workers adopt elements of a professional-class identity while the actual tasks of their working-class jobs stay the same; managers reaffirm their identities as shapers of performance despite losing control over wages; the humanistic practice is subordinated to market-based ideology through identity work.

The paper theorizes a reciprocal, multilevel process of humanistic transformation and subordination. A humanizing pay practice produces genuine resource gains and personal fulfillment, but because it is embedded in a market-based context, both parties read the raise through market logic and question whether it was earned. To restore a sense of deservingness, managers use their remaining control over hiring and performance expectations to demand professional-class conduct, and workers internalize autonomy and overwork to "deserve" the wage. Through this identity work, market-based ideology reasserts its dominance and the living wage's humanistic intent is subordinated — even though workers' underlying jobs are unchanged.

**Data & Measures**
Data corpus: 77 semi-structured interviews with 64 participants (51 workers and 13 managers; 13 participants interviewed twice) across two sites of a single U.S. financial-technology firm ("WageCo"), gathered in three waves in 2017–2018, plus two site visits with observations and organizational documents treated as supplemental data. Analysis: inductive coding (Charmaz, 2014) — analytical memos, open coding of all interview data into broad themes, then focused coding of emergent constructs (fulfillment, conflict with market-based ideology, undeservingness, autonomy, overwork; coding scheme in Online Appendix C), with managers and workers analyzed as separate subgroups and construct prevalence tallied (e.g., 27 of 51 workers described autonomy, 30 of 51 described overwork). The design is interpretive and associational rather than causal.

**Key Findings**
- Intended humanistic effect realized: the living wage moved workers from financial insecurity to greater financial security and personal fulfillment outside work.
- Unintended clash: decoupling pay from performance contradicted WageCo's entrenched market-based, pay-for-performance ideology, leading both workers and managers to view the raise as undeserved.
- Resolution via identity work: managers demanded, and workers embraced, two professional-class traits — autonomy ("be your own CEO") and overwork (distancing from the "clockpuncher") — while the jobs' actual tasks and definitions stayed the same; managers simultaneously reaffirmed their own role identities as shapers of performance despite their weakened control over wages.
- Net result: the humanistic living-wage practice was ultimately subordinated to market-based ideology.
- Heterogeneity across workers (not uniform): the transition was amplified for the lowest-level workers, who received the largest increases and expressed undeservingness far more often (58.3% of lowest-level vs. 25.9% of higher-level workers); construct prevalence was 27 of 51 workers describing autonomy, 30 of 51 overwork, and 16 describing both. A minority of workers with prior professional/managerial experience or credentials, or who were hired after the announcement, did not feel undeserving (they saw the pay as commensurate with their qualifications), yet they still upheld market-based ideology and demanded that other workers "prove" they deserved the wage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances humanistic-management scholarship by documenting both the genuine benefits of a living wage (real improvements in workers' lives outside work) and how such a humanistic practice can be subordinated to a dominant market-based ideology, and by surfacing the psychological and identity roots of market-based-versus-humanistic tension — showing that worker and manager identities, not only structures, can drive resistance to humanistic management. It also contributes to theory on class and work identity: changes in pay-setting can spark transitions toward new class-based work-role identities even when job tasks remain the same, and taken-for-granted market beliefs about self-worth shape both workers' identity work and managers' identity control. On this basis the authors argue that scholars of work identity must take wages more seriously in accounts of what it means "to be" a given type of worker.

**Practical Implication**
For leaders, managers, and workers interested in living wages and humanism, the paper argues that living wages have clear benefits and should be implemented wherever feasible, but their humanistic intent can be undone by the prevalence of market-based ideology. Leaders should explicitly affirm and protect the humanistic rationale of a new practice, train managers to enact it in concrete practices such as performance reviews and hiring, and pay particular attention to reassuring workers in the lowest-level jobs, who are most prone to viewing themselves as undeserving. Because market-based ideology is deeply entrenched, the authors caution that humanistic initiatives cannot be introduced piecemeal but should be part of a broad, ambitious organizational transformation of governance, power, and formal and informal practices, and that leaders should be externally facing and advocate for more organizations to embrace humanistic management.

**Limitations**
The authors studied a single organization and relied primarily on interview data, which is subject to self-enhancing reports and retrospective sensemaking. The interview data cannot establish the CEO's true motivations for the living wage (he may have simultaneously held a market-based belief that better-paid workers would be more productive), and as a single case the study cannot say whether the opposite move — from humanistic to market-based pay — would be similarly or oppositely destabilizing. An explicit boundary condition is national context: the study was situated in the United States, where market-based pay practices are taken for granted, so in countries with more social-welfare infrastructure the demand that living-wage recipients take on professional-class identities may be less likely.

**Future Research**
The authors call for quantitative work on how introducing a living wage alters labor supply, work hours, and turnover, and for tracking whether people adjust psychologically over time and whether the professional-class identity enactment tapers off. They encourage further unpacking of the meaning of autonomy in working-class jobs, including the positive processes that may emanate from embracing autonomy even when the underlying job does not change. They also invite research on when workers' and managers' views of undeservingness diverge (identity asymmetry), on whether workers' broader social-class identity is altered, and on a systems-psychodynamic perspective for understanding the underlying anxieties and social defenses that can produce such subordination.
