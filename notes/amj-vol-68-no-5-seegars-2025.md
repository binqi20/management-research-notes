# Subordinating Humanism: How Colliding Beliefs about a Living Wage Shape Personal Fulfillment and “Professional-Class” Identities in Working-Class Jobs

**Abstract**
In a society dominated by market-based ideology and management practices that prioritize financial considerations, some organizations are shifting toward humanistic ideology and practices that emphasize human welfare. To examine this transformation in pay-setting, we studied a U.S. company that introduced a living wage for its low-wage workers. Interviews with 64 participants across two sites revealed both intended and unintended effects. Motivated by humanistic aims, the living wage was designed to reduce financial insecurity; indeed, workers felt more financially secure and fulfilled in their personal lives. However, its humanistic intent conflicted with the dominant market-based ideology linking wages to performance, raising concerns about whether these workers deserved higher pay. To resolve this tension, managers and workers altered expectations for workers to reflect two aspects of professional-class roles: autonomy and overwork. As workers internalized these expectations, they adopted elements of professional-class identities while remaining in working-class jobs. Simultaneously, managers reaffirmed their own role identities as shapers of performance despite their weakened control over wages. These findings inform a multilevel model conceptualizing how a transformative humanistic practice can be subordinated to market-based ideology through identity work. We contribute to research on humanistic management and the interconnections between wage, class, work roles, and identity.

**Research Question**
How does the introduction of a humanistic practice (a living wage) in a market-based context affect managers' and workers' identities?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Introduction of a humanistic pay-setting practice (a living wage) into a market-based organizational context
- DV(s): Workers' personal fulfillment and financial security; workers' adoption of elements of a professional-class work role identity (autonomy, overwork); managers' reaffirmation of their role identity as shapers of performance
- Mediators: Perceptions of (un)deservingness arising from market-based pay-for-performance logic; identity work by workers and managers; managerial demands to reset role expectations
- Moderators: Dominance of market-based ideology in the broader societal, organizational, and relational context; availability of a humanistic counter-narrative and managerial toolkit
- Boundary condition: The U.S. setting, where market-based pay practices are taken for granted and professional-class identities are valorized; the authors note that the dynamics they observe may differ in countries with stronger social welfare infrastructure where market-based ideology is less dominant.

The living wage initially delivered its intended humanistic benefits (financial security and personal fulfillment), but the deeply entrenched market-based ideology linking pay to performance generated tension by framing the higher wage as undeserved. To restore a sense of deservingness, managers raised role expectations toward professional-class traits—autonomy and overwork—and workers internalized these demands, undertaking a partial class-based identity transition within the same working-class jobs. In parallel, managers reaffirmed their own role identities as performance shapers despite losing control over wages. The result is a multilevel process whereby identity work subordinates the humanistic practice to the market-based ideology it was meant to transform.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances research on humanistic management by showing how a transformative humanistic practice (a living wage) can be subordinated to market-based ideology through identity work at individual and interpersonal levels. It offers novel insights into class and work identity by demonstrating that changes in pay-setting can trigger class-based work role identity transitions even when tasks remain unchanged, and it argues that scholars of work identity must take wages seriously as a constitutive element of what it means "to be" a type of worker.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders introducing humanistic practices such as living wages must explicitly communicate and protect their humanistic intent, train managers in humanistic management practices (hiring, performance reviews), and pair wage change with systemic transformation of governance, power distributions, and formal/informal practices. Piecemeal humanistic interventions embedded in a market-based society risk being subordinated, so leaders should also be externally facing advocates for broader humanistic management.

**Limitations**
The study is a single-organization qualitative case based primarily on interview data, which carries risks of self-enhancement and retrospective sensemaking, and it cannot directly ascertain the CEO's true motivations. It also cannot adjudicate whether the opposite shift (humanistic to market-based pay) would produce symmetric effects, and its U.S. context with taken-for-granted market-based pay practices may not generalize to settings with stronger social welfare infrastructure.

**Future Research**
Future work should use quantitative data to examine how living wages alter labor supply, hours, turnover, and workforce composition over time, and should track whether professional-class identity enactment tapers. Scholars should further unpack the meaning of autonomy in working-class jobs, study cases where worker and manager views of deservingness diverge, and adopt systems-psychodynamic perspectives to explore the underlying anxieties and defenses that sustain market-based identities during humanistic transitions.
