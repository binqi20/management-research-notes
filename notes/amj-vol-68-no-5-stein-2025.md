# Distributed Knowledge and the Creation of Public Value: Community-Based Organizing and Wildfire Management in Northern California

**Abstract**
We build on extant theories of collective action and public–private collaborations to unpack the distinctive role self-organizing communities may play alongside other organizations in the creation of public value. We suggest that, when communities selforganize to address a common goal and, in particular, when they collaborate with higher-level public organizations, they discover more and better opportunities to reduce market frictions. Using an original longitudinal dataset of community-level fire prevention plans in Northern California from 1998 to 2018, we find that communities taking local actions and coordinating them with state and federal organizations manage wildfires better and experience fewer property losses than communities that do not organize. The number of stakeholders participating in these collaborations and the extent of the collaborations across hierarchical levels reinforce this effect. We conjecture that one possible mechanism explaining why self-organizing and cross-level collaborations tend to yield positive outcomes may be because they allow stakeholders to benefit from otherwise idle local knowledge. Unpacking the role played by self-organizing communities and their cross-level collaborations is important because it may enable contextualized solutions and prevent the potentially ineffective application of homogenous practices to heterogeneous problems, even in the absence of formal contracts.

**Research Question**
How and why do self-organizing communities, and their cross-level collaborations with higher-level public organizations, contribute to the creation of public value beyond what is achievable by public organizations alone? Specifically, how do stakeholder participation and the intensity of cross-level public–private collaborations shape public value outcomes such as wildfire-related property losses?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Community self-organizing (fire prevention community and fire prevention plan dummies); number of stakeholders participating in a FPP; presence and intensity of cross-level collaboration with higher-level public organizations; cross-level diversity (Blau index)
- DV(s): Property losses from wildfires (proxy for public value), including homes, commercial and public buildings destroyed in a community each year
- Mediators: Mobilization of otherwise-idle local knowledge; knowledge flows between local stakeholders and higher-level public organizations (conjectured, not directly tested)
- Moderators: Type and intensity of cross-level collaboration (extensive, concentrated, limited, as identified via PCA); number of stakeholders (nonlinear)

Building on Ostrom's governing principles for collective action and Hayek's notion of distributed local knowledge, the authors argue that self-organizing communities of vested and mutually vulnerable stakeholders can internalize coordination costs because private and public value overlap. Communities accumulate embedded local knowledge through trial-and-error learning, and cross-level collaborations with higher-level public organizations enable that local knowledge to be combined with "global" knowledge (e.g., scientific best practices and economies of scale), producing more contextualized and effective solutions to public problems. Broad stakeholder participation and extensive, active cross-level orchestration reinforce the benefits, though with diminishing returns once coordination costs outpace added knowledge.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances Ostrom-style collective action theory by explaining how and why self-organizing communities create public value alongside higher-level public organizations, rather than merely as substitutes for public governance. It reframes self-organizing communities as a distinct organizational form that functions as a repository of distributed local knowledge, and posits noncontractual cross-level collaborations as a governance mechanism that reduces knowledge (not just resource) constraints. In doing so, it enriches new stakeholder theory and the public–private collaborations literature by showing that the effectiveness of community-level public–private arrangements is contingent on the type and intensity of cross-level engagement.

**Practical Implication**
For policy, the results support distributed, polycentric governance: local self-organizing with active orchestration of higher-level public agencies tends to outperform top-down application of homogenous best practices, with implications for wildfire management and for public education, water, sewage, roads, waste, healthcare, and public safety. For practice, managers and public officials can leverage noncontractual, relational governance mechanisms to mobilize community-based organizing, even when formal contracts are incomplete or infeasible due to high coordination costs.

**Limitations**
The quasi-experimental and 2SLS-DiD design does not fully rule out endogeneity from unobservable reasons some communities face higher fire risk, though the likely direction of bias runs against the findings. Property losses are a limited proxy for public value since they combine public and private structures and do not capture all wildfire impacts. The authors cannot directly measure knowledge or knowledge flows and therefore cannot rule out alternative mechanisms such as improved coordination, incentive alignment, or richer resource environments.

**Future Research**
Future work should test the boundary conditions of the theory in other contexts (e.g., education, water, sanitation, healthcare) and where community engagement or information asymmetries are less pronounced. Scholars could unpack the relative importance and interplay of stakeholder engagement, cross-level collaborations, and subsequent decision-making and resource use, and examine the specific content and mechanisms of knowledge dissemination within self-organizing communities. Finally, researchers could dig into communities' histories and leadership structures to explain the origins and evolution of self-organizing.
