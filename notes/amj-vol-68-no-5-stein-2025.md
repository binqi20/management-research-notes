# Distributed Knowledge and the Creation of Public Value: Community-Based Organizing and Wildfire Management in Northern California

**Abstract**
We build on extant theories of collective action and public–private collaborations to unpack the distinctive role self-organizing communities may play alongside other organizations in the creation of public value. We suggest that, when communities self-organize to address a common goal and, in particular, when they collaborate with higher-level public organizations, they discover more and better opportunities to reduce market frictions. Using an original longitudinal dataset of community-level fire prevention plans in Northern California from 1998 to 2018, we find that communities taking local actions and coordinating them with state and federal organizations manage wildfires better and experience fewer property losses than communities that do not organize. The number of stakeholders participating in these collaborations and the extent of the collaborations across hierarchical levels reinforce this effect. We conjecture that one possible mechanism explaining why self-organizing and cross-level collaborations tend to yield positive outcomes may be because they allow stakeholders to benefit from otherwise idle local knowledge. Unpacking the role played by self-organizing communities and their cross-level collaborations is important because it may enable contextualized solutions and prevent the potentially ineffective application of homogenous practices to heterogeneous problems, even in the absence of formal contracts.

**Research Question**
How and why do self-organizing communities create public value — often achieving outcomes superior to those of higher-level public organizations alone — and how do the breadth of stakeholder participation and cross-level collaborations with higher-level public organizations shape this value creation?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. More public value is created in communities that self-organize than in communities that rely solely on existing public organizations.
H2. More public value is created in communities that, in addition to self-organizing, rely on the participation of more stakeholders than in communities relying on fewer stakeholders — though this relationship is nonlinear.
H3. More public value is created in communities that, in addition to self-organizing, collaborate with higher-level public organizations than in communities that do not.
H3a. More public value is created in communities that, in addition to self-organizing, collaborate more actively with higher-level public organizations than in communities that do not collaborate as actively.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): community self-organizing (adoption of a fire prevention plan, FPP; fire prevention community / fire prevention plan dummies); breadth of stakeholder participation (count of participating organizations plus unaffiliated local individuals); cross-level (community–higher-level public) collaboration and its intensity (extensive, concentrated, limited components from a polychoric PCA); cross-level diversity (Blau's index across local / state / federal levels)
- DV(s): public value, operationalized as property loss from wildfires (structures destroyed per community-year; fewer losses = more public value)
- Mediator (theorized, not directly tested): mobilization of otherwise-idle distributed local knowledge (Hayek, 1945)
- Instrument: climate shocks (largest month-over-month drop in SPEI over the preceding 15 fire seasons), used to instrument a community's propensity to self-organize
- Reinforcing conditions: the number of participating stakeholders and the extent of cross-level collaboration reinforce the self-organizing effect on public value

The paper argues that self-organizing communities function as repositories of distributed local knowledge — the decentralized, place-specific understanding held by community stakeholders (Hayek, 1945) — that would otherwise remain idle. When communities self-organize and, in particular, collaborate across hierarchical levels with higher-level public organizations, they mobilize this local knowledge and combine it with the "global," place-neutral best-practice knowledge of higher-level agencies, enabling contextualized solutions, reducing market frictions, and creating public value in the form of fewer property losses, even without formal contracts. Because the number of stakeholders and the intensity of cross-level collaboration shape how much knowledge is pooled, they reinforce the self-organizing effect. The authors are explicit that they cannot test the knowledge mechanism directly; it is inferred from FPP content and the pattern of results, so alternative mechanisms (better coordination, stronger incentive alignment, or a richer resource environment) cannot be ruled out.

**Data & Measures**
Data: an original longitudinal (panel) dataset of community-based fire prevention plans (FPPs) in the seven northernmost counties of California, 1998–2018. The authors identify 321 communities (human settlements); 103 had adopted an FPP by 2018 and 218 serve as controls, yielding 6,741 community–year (community–fire dyad) observations. FPP participant lists were hand-coded for stakeholder counts and collaboration structure; QGIS/GIS geospatial (nearest-neighbor) matching linked communities to CAL FIRE fire perimeters; property-loss data come from the National Wildfire Coordinating Group, CAL FIRE, and the National Fire Incident Reporting System.
DV: property loss (proxy for public value) = number of public and private structures destroyed by wildfires per community-year, entered with an inverse hyperbolic sine transformation.
IVs: fire prevention community and fire prevention plan (dummies); stakeholders (count of represented organizations plus unaffiliated individuals; robustness uses ind. stakeholders and org. stakeholders separately); cross-level collaboration (binary; the FPP references a collaboration with a higher-level public organization); cross-level diversity (Blau's 1977 index); and extensive / concentrated / limited cross-level collaboration (latent components from a polychoric PCA of collaboration characteristics).
Identification: two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) difference-in-differences (DiD) and 2SLS instrumental-variable DiD with community fixed effects; the instrument is climate shocks (the largest month-over-month decrease in SPEI over a community's preceding 15 fire seasons). Controls: fire risk, community-at-risk (CAR) designation, SPEI, per-capita income, education, ln(acres burned), ln(distance to nearest fire), and fire-management jurisdictions. Robustness: Goodman-Bacon decomposition of the staggered DiD and alternative climate-shock windows (8, 10, 15 years).

**Key Findings**
Hypothesis 1 is supported: communities that self-organize (create an FPP) experience fewer property losses than communities that do not. In the TWFE DiD, FPP communities see about 5% less property loss per year after plan creation (fire prevention plan b = -0.05, p < .05; no pre-creation difference, b = -0.01, p > .10), and the finding holds in the 2SLS IV DiD (fire prevention plan b = -1.06 / -1.39 / -0.97, p < .05 / .01 / .10 across the 8-, 10-, and 15-year climate-shock windows). The IV also shows FPP-adopting communities are higher-risk on average (fire prevention community b > 0, p < .05–.10), yet the plan itself reduces losses.

Hypothesis 2 is only partially supported. The loss-reducing effect of broader stakeholder participation holds — more stakeholders is associated with less property loss (stakeholders b = -0.01, p < .10 in the TWFE DiD; b = -0.22, p < .05 in the 2SLS IV) — but the hypothesized nonlinear/diminishing-returns pattern is NOT found in the main analysis (stakeholders² not significant). The predicted nonlinearity emerges only in robustness tests using individual- and organization-level stakeholder counts (ind. stakeholders² b = 0.00, p < .10; org. stakeholders² b = 0.01, p < .05).

Hypothesis 3 is supported in the 2SLS IV analysis but not in the unadjusted TWFE DiD: cross-level collaboration is non-significant in the TWFE DiD (b = -0.01, p > .10), whereas in the IV model communities with a cross-level collaboration experience about 51% less property loss (b = -0.72, p < .05).

Hypothesis 3a is supported, with the benefit concentrated in the most active collaborations: extensive cross-level collaborations are associated with the largest loss reductions / best outcomes (b = -0.07, p < .05 in the TWFE DiD; b = -3.15, p < .10 and b = -2.11, p < .05 in the IV); concentrated collaborations show smaller reductions (b = -0.43, p < .05; b = -0.22, p < .10); and limited collaborations are mixed (one positive coefficient, b = 1.04, p < .05, alongside b = -0.18, p < .05, in the IV, though b = -0.05, p < .10 in the TWFE DiD). Robustness (Goodman-Bacon decomposition) confirms FPP communities experience 4%–7% less property loss, and cross-level diversity is associated with less property loss (b = -1.01 / -2.64, p < .05) with a nonlinear component (b = 2.85, p < .05).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends Ostrom's theory of collective action and commons governance by explaining how and why self-organizing communities achieve outcomes superior to those of higher-level public organizations alone, and by positioning the self-organizing community as a distinct organizational form that creates public value rather than merely a context for, or beneficiary of, it. It advances research on public value and public–private collaboration by showing that noncontractual, cross-level collaborations between communities and higher-level public organizations enhance public value, and that their effectiveness is contingent on the type of collaboration (extensive, concentrated, or limited). It also reframes such collaborations as reducing not only resource constraints but also knowledge constraints, theorizing self-organizing communities as repositories of distributed local knowledge (Hayek) whose privileged access to place-specific knowledge is a source of strategic advantage.

**Practical Implication**
For policy, the results support distributed governance: self-organizing communities can be effective producers of public value both independently and alongside higher-level public organizations, and top-down policies that impose homogeneous best practices may underperform because of context mismatch — so local initiatives developed together with state and federal authorities tend to yield better outcomes. The authors suggest these lessons may generalize to other public-value domains they name (public education, water distribution, sewage systems, roads and waste management, and healthcare and public safety). For practice, managers can leverage community-based organizing and other noncontractual, relational governance mechanisms when formal contracts are incomplete or infeasible because coordination costs are high.

**Limitations**
First, the quasi-experimental 2SLS DiD design mitigates but does not fully remove endogeneity — some communities may be more fire-prone for unobservable reasons (though the authors argue this bias likely works against them, since FPP communities are typically those at greatest initial fire risk). Second, public value is difficult to define and measure; the property-loss proxy captures both public and private structures but is not a comprehensive measure and may diverge from alternative operationalizations. Third, the knowledge mechanism cannot be tested directly — FPP content is only an indirect measure of knowledge — so alternative explanations (better coordination, stronger incentive alignment, or a richer resource environment) cannot be ruled out.

**Future Research**
First, future work could test the theory's boundaries in other contexts — settings where community engagement matters less or information asymmetries are less pronounced — and check whether the extensive/concentrated/limited collaboration typology generalizes beyond wildfire management. Second, researchers could test the relative importance and interplay of stakeholder engagement, cross-level collaboration, and subsequent decision-making and resource use, and investigate the specific content and forms of local knowledge leveraged, how it is disseminated, whether the nature of particular stakeholders outweighs broad participation, and whether improved coordination or stronger incentive alignment are the main drivers. Third, future studies could dig into communities' histories and leadership structures to unpack the origins of self-organizing.
