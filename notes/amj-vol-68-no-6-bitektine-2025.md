# Temporary Regulations and Institutional Change: Insights from the Brazilian World Cup Experience

**Abstract**
While institutional theory has extensively examined how durable regulations contribute to institutional change, little is known about how temporary regulations—pervasive in modern societies—can transform institutions. This study examines how and why temporary regulations can lead to lasting institutional change by analyzing Brazil's temporary legalization of beer sales and consumption in stadiums during the 2014 FIFA World Cup. Using extensive qualitative data, we develop a process model explaining how the introduction of a temporary regulation creates an opportunity for society to experience a counter-normative practice and learn from it. During this period, multiple institutional actors—while pursuing objectives far removed from considerations of institutional change—perform various types of institutional work to contain the practice's expected negative outcomes. This institutional work unintendedly generates new societal understandings about the practice's risks and consequences, which interested actors then leverage to achieve lasting institutional change. We advance institutional theory by identifying an unexplored path of institutional change through temporary regulations, demonstrating how temporary regulations can enable practice variations and institutional learning, and revealing how institutional maintenance work can drive change. Our insights suggest that temporary regulations can be valuable policy tools for reducing uncertainty and enabling learning about alternative institutional arrangements.

**Research Question**
How and why can temporary regulatory changes—regulations adopted for a limited time and typically misaligned with prevailing societal understandings—lead to lasting institutional change? The study asks what path of institutional change temporary regulation initiates, what institutional work such change requires, and what creates variance in temporary-regulation outcomes, using Brazil's temporary legalization of beer sales and consumption in stadiums during the 2014 FIFA World Cup.

**Mechanism Process**
- Institutional system (practice lens): three recursively aligned elements—understandings, regulations, and practices—sustained by ongoing institutional (maintenance) work.
- Triggering condition: an exceptional circumstance (the 2014 FIFA World Cup and FIFA's demands) prompts a temporary regulation (the Lei Geral da Copa / World Cup Law) that legalizes a counter-normative practice (beer sales and consumption in stadiums) misaligned with prevailing understandings and the durable ban.
- Tension 1 (introducing the temporary regulation): resolved by "discursively countering critiques" (sovereignty critiques; beer-and-violence critiques) and "using power" (FIFA's use of power; governmental use of executive power); allows the counter-normative practice to be performed.
- Tension 2 (managing the temporarily allowed counter-normative practice): resolved by "preventing negative outcomes"—redesigning practice performances (new stadium-security model, stewards, command-and-control standardization) and redefining practice participants from "fan" to "client"—and by "constructing the outcome as 'success'"; generates institutional learning and new understandings.
- Tension 3 (new understandings versus old understandings and regulations): resolved by "undermining practice contentiousness," "presenting the practice as a solution" (economic and security problems), "linking the issue to macro-social discourses" (consumer rights/freedoms; local and international isomorphism), and "exploiting legal ambiguity" (judicial hierarchy; countering legalistic arguments); enables revision of durable regulations and lasting legalization.

A temporary regulation opens a legally protected, publicly visible, time-bounded space in which a counter-normative practice can be performed and widely observed. Actors performing institutional maintenance work to contain the practice's expected negative outcomes—though not seeking institutional change—unintendedly generate new societal understandings (that beer in stadiums does not necessarily cause violence; learning about "how things can be done differently"). Once the temporary regulation lapses and the durable ban returns, interested actors leverage these new understandings to revise durable regulations, producing lasting institutional change with changed practices, understandings, and regulations. The account thus locates institutional maintenance work as an unintended driver of change and offers one resolution to the paradox of embedded agency.

**Data & Measures**
Data corpus: 52 semi-structured interviews (December 2018-May 2021) with two sets of interviewees—31 professionals (beer producers, stadium security personnel, venue managers, public administrators, journalists, academics, activists) and 21 football fans sampled across five host states; 611 newspaper articles from Folha de S. Paulo, O Estado de S. Paulo, and O Globo covering 1995-2023 (124 tied to emergent themes); 66 legal and governmental documents (bills, laws, parliamentary hearings) plus Senate hearing videos; beer-industry data (Sicobe, Euromonitor, Passport, Hoovers) and FIFA regulations, bid, and inspection reports; and participant observation at eight FIFA-organized matches. Analysis: abductive, iterating between theory and data; a reconstructed historical timeline of beer regulation; open coding into first-order concepts (e.g., "Training and deploying stewards," "Dissociating beer and violence"), axial coding into second-order themes, and abstraction into aggregate theoretical dimensions organized by the three tensions. Interpretive process design; no causal-identification strategy is claimed.

**Key Findings**
The temporary World Cup Law legalized beer sales and consumption in stadiums for the event only; when it terminated, the durable ban returned, but the new understandings the episode generated persisted. During the event, actors' institutional maintenance work (a preventive FIFA security model, stewards, standardized command-and-control, redefining fans as "clients," and the discursive construction of the World Cup as a "success") kept violent episodes relatively infrequent despite the taken-for-granted beer-violence link; the observed outcome—"the drink was released and there was no increase in violence"—unsettled that link. These new understandings ("beer in stadiums does not necessarily lead to violence") were then leveraged after the event by legislators, football clubs, stadium operators, and the beer industry—through undermining contentiousness, presenting the practice as an economic and security solution, linking to macro-social discourses, and exploiting legal ambiguity—to push for permanent legalization. The result was lasting institutional change: by the study's end thirteen states had authorized beer sales and consumption in stadiums, with the Supreme Federal Court upholding several such state laws (ADI decisions). The paper also shows the outcome is not deterministic—the 2010 South Africa "swift justice" courts illustrate a temporary regulation that lapsed because a negative narrative blocked favorable new understandings.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances institutional theory by identifying a previously unexplored path of institutional change that runs through temporary regulation—distinct both from durable-regulation paths and from temporary organizing—showing that temporary regulations can destabilize a stable institutional order and create a bounded, publicly visible space for practice variation and institutional learning. It extends research on institutional work by demonstrating that institutional maintenance work, performed by actors with no intention of changing institutions, can unintendedly drive lasting change (a solution to the paradox of embedded agency) even in highly visible and tightly controlled settings. It also foregrounds institutional learning about "how things can be done differently"—rather than learning for conformity—as a precursor to institutional change, and identifies facilitating factors such as high public visibility, broad participation, media attention, and the construction of outcomes as "success."

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that temporary regulations can be a valuable tool for policymakers and regulators, facilitating learning, reducing uncertainty, and complementing experimental and "experimentalist" approaches to regulatory change by piloting alternative practices as time-bounded experiments before committing to durable rules—illustrated with environmental/emission standards and artificial-intelligence governance. They note a parallel use in private governance (temporary self-regulation, e.g., the Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety). They also caution that temporary regulations can be misused to establish states of exception and erode rights (e.g., the U.S. Patriot Act), so their use should be checked by the regulatory review of democratic institutions.

**Limitations**
The authors note that qualitative theory-building from a single context faces limitations and that care is required when discussing transferability. The durable institutional change stemming from a temporary regulation is not deterministic: they identify at least five factors that must be met—overcoming maintenance-work resistance (e.g., through use of power), broad societal observation of the counter-normative practice, outcomes broadly perceived as favorable, generation of novel understandings that challenge the ones supporting existing durable regulations, and legal or political opportunities for renegotiating durable regulations. Not all temporary regulations produce lasting change, as the South Africa "swift justice" counter-example shows.

**Future Research**
The paper explicitly calls for (1) further exploration of the time and temporality of regulation—for example, the effect of temporariness on actors' decisions to support or tolerate counter-normative institutional arrangements; (2) empirical validation of the boundary conditions and generalizability of the findings—including how "success" is socially constructed when relevant actors do not share interests, cannot coordinate, or lack power, and the effect of varying durations and public visibility of the temporary regulation; and (3) deeper study of the role of institutional learning in institutional change, which has so far been largely limited to learning for institutional maintenance.
