# Temporary Regulations and Institutional Change: Insights from the Brazilian World Cup Experience

**Abstract**
While institutional theory has extensively examined how durable regulations contribute to institutional change, little is known about how temporary regulations—pervasive in modern societies—can transform institutions. This study examines how and why temporary regulations can lead to lasting institutional change by analyzing Brazil’s temporary legalization of beer sales and consumption in stadiums during the 2014 FIFA World Cup. Using extensive qualitative data, we develop a process model explaining how the introduction of a temporary regulation creates an opportunity for society to experience a counter-normative practice and learn from it. During this period, multiple institutional actors—while pursuing objectives far removed from considerations of institutional change—perform various types of institutional work to contain the practice’s expected negative outcomes. This institutional work unintendedly generates new societal understandings about the practice’s risks and consequences, which interested actors then leverage to achieve lasting institutional change. We advance institutional theory by identifying an unexplored path of institutional change through temporary regulations, demonstrating how temporary regulations can enable practice variations and institutional learning, and revealing how institutional maintenance work can drive change. Our insights suggest that temporary regulations can be valuable policy tools for reducing uncertainty and enabling learning about alternative institutional arrangements.

**Research Question**
How and why can temporary regulatory changes—regulations adopted for a limited time and not expected to continue beyond an established end date—lead to lasting institutional change, even when their initial introduction conflicts with prevailing societal understandings and actors involved have no intent to transform institutions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Introduction of a temporary regulation that conflicts with prevailing societal understandings (driven by exceptional circumstances, e.g., FIFA's demands for the 2014 World Cup)
- DV(s): Lasting institutional change (durable regulatory reform legalizing beer sales and consumption in Brazilian football stadiums)
- Mediators: Institutional maintenance work (discursively countering critiques, redesigning practice performances, redefining practice participants, constructing outcomes as "success"); institutional learning about "how things can be done differently"; emergence of new societal understandings
- Moderators: Broad public visibility of the counter-normative practice; perceived success of outcomes; availability of legal/political opportunities for renegotiation; actors' capacity to socially construct a narrative of success

A temporary regulation creates a bounded time window in which a counter-normative practice can be performed and observed at scale. Because actors expect the practice to revert when the regulation lapses, they tolerate it and engage in institutional maintenance work (training, standardizing, gentrifying participants, discursively framing) to contain anticipated negative outcomes. Successfully containing these outcomes unintendedly generates new societal understandings about the practice's risks and viability—a form of institutional learning distinct from socialization to conformity. Interested actors then leverage these new understandings to challenge the old regulation and push for permanent change, resolving three sequential tensions between regulations, understandings, and practices.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper identifies a previously unexplored path of institutional change through temporary regulations, developing a process model in which maintenance work—typically a force for stability—unintendedly drives change. It extends institutional work scholarship by showing how practice deviations can occur even in highly visible, tightly controlled settings when temporary regulations create bounded time periods shielded from maintenance pressures. It also reframes institutional learning as learning about "how things can be done differently," distinct from socialization-based learning, positioning it as a critical precursor to institutional change.

**Practical Implication**
Temporary regulations can be a valuable policy tool for reducing uncertainty, enabling institutional learning, and complementing experimentalist governance approaches in domains facing polycrisis conditions such as climate change, AI, and pandemics. Policymakers can pilot counter-normative arrangements (e.g., emission standards, work-from-home policies) under time-limited rules, observe outcomes, and use accumulated learning to design durable solutions. However, temporary regulations can also be misused to establish states of exception and erode democratic institutions, so safeguards are essential.

**Limitations**
The study builds theory from a single context (beer regulations in Brazilian stadiums around the 2014 World Cup), so transferability requires care. The authors identify five boundary conditions (resistance must be overcome, broad observability, favorable perceived outcomes, generation of novel understandings, and legal/political opportunity for renegotiation) whose generalizability requires empirical validation. The qualitative historical design limits causal inference and depends on retrospective accounts and media sources of varying credibility.

**Future Research**
Future work should explore how temporariness shapes actors' willingness to tolerate counter-normative arrangements, empirically validate the boundary conditions and examine varying durations and visibility levels of temporary regulations, and investigate how "success" is socially constructed when actors lack shared interests, coordination, or power. Researchers should also further develop the underexplored role of institutional learning—beyond maintenance-oriented socialization—in driving institutional change.
