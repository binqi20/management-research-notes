# Bearing, Belittling, or Befriending Doubt: Understanding Doubt’s Generative versus Corrosive Effect on Entrepreneurs’ Early-Stage Idea Validation

**Abstract**
Doubt in the early-stage entrepreneurial process is pervasive. However, we argue that our understanding of the types of doubt individuals experience during entrepreneurial action— and how their responses to these doubts affect their ability to determine the value of their ideas and remain committed to this goal—remains incomplete. When entrepreneurs set out to validate their ideas, doubt is typically framed in one of two ways: as corrosive or as generative. In both perspectives, the focus is on idea-related doubts, overlooking the possibility that once entrepreneurial action commences, self-related doubts about one's abilities or motivation may resurface. Furthermore, while the generative approach provides valuable insights into how entrepreneurs can engage with idea doubts, it tells us little about why some individuals can do so while others may not. To address these questions, we followed early-stage entrepreneurs weekly during an incubation program. We found that they experienced not only idea-related but also self-related doubts. Their responses to these doubts— namely embracing, downplaying, or normalizing them—shaped their ability to make progress toward their goal and influenced their commitment to it. Ultimately, this commitment was underpinned by the emergence of three distinct orientations toward expending further goal-directed effort: mastering, curtailing, or yearning.

**Research Question**
How do different types of doubt emerge as entrepreneurs work to validate an early-stage idea, and how and why do their responses to these doubts play a corrosive or a generative role in their ability to identify the value of the idea and sustain their commitment to that goal?

**Mechanism Process**
- Trigger: perceived lack of desired goal progress (a shared starting point across participants).
- Causal attribution (Weiner): the progress shortfall is read as internal–controllable, internal–uncontrollable, or external–uncontrollable.
- Types of doubt made salient: idea-related doubts; self-related doubts — self-feasibility (ability) and self-desirability (personal attractiveness/motivation).
- Doubt regulation responses (practices): embracing, normalizing, downplaying.
- Emergent orientations to further goal-directed effort: mastering (engagement), yearning (disengagement), curtailing (disengagement).

When entrepreneurs perceive a shortfall in goal progress, the causal attribution they make renders particular doubts salient and shapes how they regulate them. An internal–controllable attribution (insufficient effort) yields aspirational self-doubts alongside idea doubts that entrepreneurs embrace, freeing cognitive capacity to question and refine their product-market beliefs, surface interim proof of concept, and maintain self-desirability beliefs (a mastering orientation). An internal–uncontrollable attribution (lack of ability) yields ruminative self-feasibility doubts that entrepreneurs normalize; these hijack attention from idea doubts, limit proof of concept, and erode self-desirability into gradual disengagement (a yearning orientation). An external–uncontrollable attribution preserves self-confidence but leads entrepreneurs to downplay idea doubts, so they under-engage, gain little clarity, and disengage by conditioning effort on external factors (a curtailing orientation). The cycle repeats, with embracing making doubt generative and normalizing or downplaying making it corrosive for identifying the idea's value and sustaining commitment.

**Data & Measures**
Data comprised 308 weekly follow-up interviews (~134 hours of audio) plus intake interviews with 35 entrepreneurs (29 in the primary analytic sample) followed across six consecutive incubation cohorts, triangulated with archival materials (training slides, business model canvases, newsletters, press) and 116 pages of participant-observation memos. Analysis was inductive and interpretive with no causal-identification strategy: open coding in NVivo, constant comparison, and axial coding; doubt and confidence were coded on a continuum, with doubts further distinguished by direction (self vs. idea); temporal bracketing isolated episodes of lower-than-desired progress as comparative units; visual mapping and an insider–outsider coding procedure (Gioia et al., 2013) supported the emergent process model.

**Key Findings**
The study surfaces a doubt-regulation process model with three recurrent paths, distinguished by how entrepreneurs attributed a perceived lack of goal progress. (1) Mastering (generative): internal–controllable attributions produced aspirational self-doubts and embraced idea doubts, letting entrepreneurs surface interim proof of concept, maintain self-desirability beliefs, and sustain commitment. (2) Yearning (corrosive): internal–uncontrollable attributions produced ruminative self-feasibility doubts that were normalized; attention stayed trapped on the self, proof of concept remained limited, self-desirability doubts emerged, and entrepreneurs disengaged by passively hoping and indulging in positive fantasies. (3) Curtailing (corrosive): external–uncontrollable attributions preserved self-confidence but led entrepreneurs to downplay idea doubts, gain little clarity, and disengage by conditioning effort on external factors and procrastinating. Across paths, self-feasibility and self-desirability doubts resurfaced after action had commenced, and only the embracing response made doubt generative for identifying the idea's value and sustaining goal commitment.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions to research on doubt in entrepreneurial action. First, it shows empirically that self-feasibility doubts (about one's ability) resurface after entrepreneurial action has commenced, alongside idea-related doubts, rather than being resolved beforehand as opportunity-evaluation accounts assume. Second, by drawing on self-regulation and goal-striving theory, it demonstrates limits to entrepreneurial motivation, showing how resurfacing self-desirability doubts erode sustained commitment. Third, it integrates the previously divergent corrosive and generative perspectives on doubt into a doubt-regulation process model that specifies the conditions (causal attributions) under which and the mechanisms (embracing, normalizing, downplaying) through which doubt becomes generative or corrosive.

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest that supporting entrepreneurs in making effort-based (internal, controllable) attributions and in embracing—rather than ruminating over or dismissing—their doubts could help them keep engaging in idea validation and avoid premature disengagement. They point to interventions that build entrepreneurial self-efficacy and foster growth mindsets as potentially useful in early-stage support and education settings.

**Limitations**
The authors note that their sample is composed mostly of novice entrepreneurs with little prior entrepreneurial experience, so the triggers for progress reflections may partly be artifacts of the milestones set by the incubator. They can only speculate about the origins of participants' attributions (e.g., perceived self-efficacy), which were not directly measured, and they cannot yet demonstrate whether entrepreneurs who embraced their idea doubts avoid classic decision-making biases over the longer term.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for longitudinal research on whether entrepreneurs who embrace idea doubts still fall prey to classic decision-making biases over time, and for engagement with the literature on effective reasoning strategies under uncertainty. It suggests examining psychological-ownership reappraisal and whether "market-approved" representations of an idea retain enough personal desirability to warrant continued effort, as well as how intra- and inter-individual progress comparisons, their timing, and accompanying emotions shape what makes a progress shortfall a trigger. It also points to the role of founders' prior background and experience, the origins of attributions in perceived self-efficacy, and intervention-based designs in the pre-entry learning period to prevent premature abandonment of ideas.
