# Bearing, Belittling, or Befriending Doubt: Understanding Doubt’s Generative versus Corrosive Effect on Entrepreneurs’ Early-Stage Idea Validation

**Abstract**
Doubt in the early-stage entrepreneurial process is pervasive. However, we argue that our understanding of the types of doubt individuals experience during entrepreneurial action— and how their responses to these doubts affect their ability to determine the value of their ideas and remain committed to this goal—remains incomplete. When entrepreneurs set out to validate their ideas, doubt is typically framed in one of two ways: as corrosive or as generative. In both perspectives, the focus is on idea-related doubts, overlooking the possibility that once entrepreneurial action commences, self-related doubts about one’s abilities or motivation may resurface. Furthermore, while the generative approach provides valuable insights into how entrepreneurs can engage with idea doubts, it tells us little about why some individuals can do so while others may not. To address these questions, we followed early-stage entrepreneurs weekly during an incubation program. We found that they experienced not only idea-related but also self-related doubts. Their responses to these doubts— namely embracing, downplaying, or normalizing them—shaped their ability to make progress toward their goal and influenced their commitment to it. Ultimately, this commitment was underpinned by the emergence of three distinct orientations toward expending further goal-directed effort: mastering, curtailing, or yearning.

**Research Question**
(1) How do different types of doubt emerge in the course of validating one’s entrepreneurial idea and (2) how and why does one’s response to these doubts play a corrosive or a generative role in one’s ability to identify the value of the idea and in sustaining commitment to this goal?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Entrepreneurs' perceived lack of (or positive) goal progress toward surfacing a proof of concept during idea validation in an incubation program
- Attribution patterns: Internal-unstable-controllable (insufficient effort); internal-stable-uncontrollable (lack of ability); external-stable (task difficulty/others' control)
- Focal doubt types: Idea-related doubts; self-feasibility doubts (about one's abilities); self-desirability doubts (about one's motivation)
- Doubt regulation practices: Embracing (engaging with doubt and working toward improvement); downplaying (dismissing doubt as unimportant); normalizing (treating doubt as "part of the game" and emotionally tolerating it)
- Emergent orientations: Mastering (sustained engagement and skill-stretching); curtailing (rationing of effort and procrastination); yearning (gradual disengagement accompanied by lingering attachment to the goal)

As entrepreneurs iterate through weekly idea validation work, perceptions of goal progress become salient and trigger causal attributions that make different doubts focal. Effort-based attributions direct attention to idea-related doubts, which entrepreneurs embrace through active inquiry, producing refined understanding of the idea's value and a mastering orientation. Ability-based attributions surface self-feasibility doubts that become ruminative and hijack attention from idea work; entrepreneurs normalize these as part of the entrepreneurial experience, eroding progress and yielding a yearning orientation. External attributions lead entrepreneurs to downplay idea doubts and dismiss disconfirming feedback, sustaining self-desirability beliefs but producing a curtailing orientation. Recurring attributions across weekly cycles solidify these feedback loops between doubt regulation and perceived progress.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper empirically demonstrates that self-feasibility and self-desirability doubts resurface well after entrepreneurial action has commenced, challenging the assumption in opportunity evaluation and goal-striving research that such doubts are resolved prior to action. Drawing on self-regulation, goal-striving, and attribution theory, it develops a doubt regulation process model that integrates the generative (Locke et al., 2008) and corrosive (McMullen & Shepherd, 2006) perspectives on doubt by specifying the conditions and mechanisms (embracing, downplaying, normalizing) under which doubt plays a generative versus corrosive role. It also exposes the limits to entrepreneurial motivation during idea validation, showing how perceived progress (or its absence) shapes sustained commitment and three distinct orientations—mastering, curtailing, and yearning—toward further effort.

**Practical Implication**
The findings inform the design of interventions in early-stage incubation programs aimed at supporting entrepreneurs' doubt regulation. Interventions that build entrepreneurial self-efficacy by supporting effort-based (internal-controllable) attributions for outcomes, foster growth mindsets, and tailor experimentation programs to entrepreneurs' decision-making characteristics may prevent premature disengagement. Program designers should be aware that both over-confidence (downplaying) and self-defeating rumination (normalizing) can undermine idea validation, and should create conditions that help entrepreneurs embrace rather than avoid or internalize their doubts.

**Limitations**
The study relies on a sample of mostly novice early-stage entrepreneurs drawn from a single European incubation program, which may limit transferability to more experienced founders or different institutional contexts. The triggers for goal progress reflections may be artifacts of milestones set by the incubator, and the authors could not fully observe within-person variation in attributions across settings. The longitudinal weekly-interview design, while rich, entails trade-offs between frequent data collection and depth of reflection in each session.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether entrepreneurs who embrace idea doubts still fall prey to classic decision-making biases over longer horizons, and how effective reasoning strategies under uncertainty can support doubt regulation over time. Scholars should investigate the role of psychological ownership reappraisal, inter- and intra-individual comparisons, and emotions accompanying surprise and discovery in triggering and shaping doubt responses. Intervention-based designs in the pre-entry period could explore how doubt regulation approaches can be influenced to prevent premature idea abandonment, and how entrepreneurial self-efficacy and growth mindsets moderate attribution patterns and subsequent doubt regulation.
