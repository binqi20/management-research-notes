# Constraints, Competencies, or Choices? How Lay Theories of the Gender Gap Impact Diversity Support

**Abstract**
Resistance to diversity initiatives can undermine their effectiveness. I advance insight into diversity support by identifying lay theories about why the gender gap persists. I identify three gender gap lay theories, which differ in the implied locus and control women have over the gap. These attributions shape women’s perceived responsibility, which, in turn, undermines diversity initiative support. Attributing the gap to organizational barriers is positively associated with diversity support because it implies the causes are external and uncontrollable by women. Attributing the gap to dispositional factors (e.g., traits) is negatively associated with diversity support because it implies the causes are internal to women (but not necessarily controllable). Finally, attributing the gap to women’s personal choices is negatively associated with diversity support because it implies the causes are both internal to and controllable by women; thus, it is most undermining overall. I test these ideas in five multimethod studies, which generally support my theory. I also find gender gaps are attributed to personal choice more than racial gaps are. Overall, this project suggests that, beyond demographics and ideology, sensemaking about the causes of gender inequality and, in particular, the belief that it is due to personal choice, significantly undermines diversity support.

**Research Question**
Beyond demographics and ideology, do employees form lay theories about why the gender gap persists, and how do those lay theories shape support for (versus resistance to) diversity initiatives? Specifically, through what attributional process (locus and controllability of the gap, and women's resulting perceived responsibility) does each lay theory affect diversity initiative support?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. Endorsement of the organizational barriers theory is negatively related to attributions that the gender gap stems from factors (a) internal to and (b) controllable by women themselves.
H2. Endorsement of the dispositional factors theory is positively related to attributions that the gender gap stems from factors internal to women.
H3. Endorsement of the personal choice theory is positively related to attributions that the gender gap stems from factors (a) internal to and (b) controllable by women themselves.
H4. Attributions that the gender gap stems from factors (a) internal to and (b) controllable by women are positively related to beliefs that women are responsible for the gender gap.
H5. Belief in women's responsibility for the gender gap in career attainment is negatively associated with support for diversity initiatives.
H6. The organizational barriers theory has two positive indirect effects on diversity support due to decreased (a) internality and (b) controllability attributions, both of which predict lower responsibility assigned to women.
H7. The dispositional factors theory has one negative indirect effect on diversity support due to increased internality attributions, which predict greater responsibility to women.
H8. The personal choice theory has two negative indirect effects on diversity support due to increased (a) internality and (b) controllability attributions, both of which predict greater responsibility to women.
H9. The personal choice theory is more detrimental to diversity initiative support relative to the dispositional factors theory.
H10. The personal choice theory is more likely to be endorsed for gender gaps than for racial gaps in career attainment.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Endorsement of three gender gap lay theories — organizational barriers, dispositional factors, and personal choices (manipulated via a scientific human-capital report in Study 4).
- DV(s): Diversity initiative support (attitudinal support; Study 4 adds diversity prioritization/ranking as a behavioral indicator).
- Mediators: Internality (locus) attributions and controllability attributions, then women's perceived responsibility for the gap (serial mediation: lay theory → internality/controllability → responsibility → support).
- Moderators: None in the focal model; target group (gender vs. racial gap) is a between-conditions comparison used to test H10, not a moderator of the core path.

Drawing on classic attribution theory (Weiner, 1985), the paper argues that lay theories differ in the locus (internal vs. external) and controllability they imply for the gender gap, and that these two dimensions jointly determine how responsible women are held for the gap, which in turn drives diversity support. Attributing the gap to organizational barriers implies external, uncontrollable causes, lowering women's responsibility and raising support. Attributing it to dispositional factors implies internal (but not necessarily controllable) causes, raising responsibility and lowering support via one path. Attributing it to personal choices implies causes both internal to and controllable by women, producing the highest responsibility and lowest support via two paths — making the personal choice theory the most undermining.

**Data & Measures**
Five studies triangulate qualitative and quantitative data. Study 1a inductively-deductively coded open-ended explanations for the gender gap from a Prolific working-adult sample (Sample A) and archival newspaper reader comments (Sample B; NYT, WSJ, Washington Post), with two blind raters and Cohen's kappa reliability. Study 1b developed a lay-theory scale from an initial 50 items via EFA and second-order CFA, yielding a 30-item measure of three theories (organizational barriers, dispositional factors, personal choices; with a competence subdimension). Study 2 measured the locus and controllability implied by each first-order category (three items each, alpha approximately .97) and cluster-analyzed them. Study 3 (two-wave field survey, controlling for age, gender, race, political orientation, internal motivation to control prejudice, and diversity experience) measured the 30-item lay theories, locus/controllability attributions (four items each, alpha .93/.95), women's responsibility (three items, alpha .94; adapted from Tang et al., 2020), and diversity initiative support (three items, alpha .94; adapted from Bell et al., 2000). Study 4 manipulated lay theory (four conditions: organizational barriers, dispositional factors, personal choices, control) via an annual-report message and measured attributions, responsibility, diversity initiative support (alpha .96), and diversity prioritization (ranking). Causal identification rests on the randomized Study 4 experiment; Study 3 is explicitly correlational.

**Key Findings**
In the Study 3 field survey (N = 450) the author reports full support for H1–H10. Organizational barriers was negatively associated with internal (b = −0.48, p < .001) and controllable (b = −0.55, p < .001) attributions (H1). Dispositional factors was positively associated with internal (b = 0.20, p = .002) but not controllable (b = 0.14, p = .058) attributions (H2). Personal choices was positively associated with internal (b = 0.25, p < .001) and controllable (b = 0.21, p = .002) attributions (H3). Internality (b = 0.33) and controllability (b = 0.19) positively predicted responsibility (H4), and responsibility was negatively associated with diversity support (b = −0.16, p = .033; H5). The predicted indirect effects held (H6–H8), the negative controllability path was significant for personal choices only (H9), and personal choice was endorsed more for gender than racial gaps (b = 1.18, p < .001; H10). Two unexpected direct effects emerged: organizational barriers had a positive direct effect on support (b = 0.36, p < .001) and personal choices a negative direct effect (b = −0.13, p = .021). The Study 4 experiment (N = 680) supported H2–H8 (H6 only partially, via decreased controllability rather than locus) but did NOT support H1 (the organizational-barriers message did not lower attributions relative to control) or H9 (the negative indirect effects of personal choices did not differ in size from dispositional factors). Dispositional-factors and personal-choice messages causally raised internality and controllability attributions, increasing women's perceived responsibility and lowering both diversity support and prioritization.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces "gender gap lay theories," shifting explanations of diversity support from perceiver characteristics (self-interest, ideology) to attributional sensemaking about why groups achieve less, and offering a more dynamic, malleable account of support. It identifies attribution of the gap to personal choice — rather than competence or discrimination — as a novel driver of the maintenance of gender inequality that reinforces the gender hierarchy while appearing gender-neutral. It shows that gender gaps are attributed to personal choice more than racial gaps, implying diversity resistance varies by target group, and it extends organizational attribution theory by showing internal factors differ in perceived controllability (traits vs. choices), which is consequential for how much blame women receive.

**Practical Implication**
For organizational leaders, the findings suggest that assessing and targeting employees' beliefs about why the gender gap persists is key to increasing diversity initiative support and efficacy. Because lay theories form partly from workplace observation, leaders should be attuned to the rhetoric they use: for example, rather than emphasizing that women leave at higher rates (which can increase internal attributions), they can acknowledge how their own incentive structures and ideal-worker norms make advancement harder for women (increasing external attributions). Organizations are also advised to incorporate awareness of how gender gap lay theories shape diversity attitudes into diversity training, addressing the gap between lay and academic understandings of how controllable women's career choices actually are.

**Limitations**
The Study 3 design is correlational, limiting causal claims (addressed by the Study 4 experiment), and the samples skewed female and White, which the author frames as a conservative test. Diversity-support measures were global, so the type of initiative might moderate the relationships, and the boundaries between lay-theory categories (and between individual-level choice and group-level norms, or choices vs. demands) are hard to define precisely. In Study 4 there was little evidence that manipulating the organizational-barriers theory shifted attributions, and the dispositional-factors and choice conditions became unexpectedly positively associated with support after attributions were controlled.

**Future Research**
The author calls for research on whether lay theories differ depending on which aspect of the gender gap is highlighted (e.g., STEM vs. top leadership) and on how work-family decisions are construed (choices vs. demands). Further work should clarify the role of locus and controllability attributions, examine whether ambivalence toward diversity reflects interactions between lay theories and self-interest or ideology, and test whether lay theories explain why some initiatives (e.g., leadership training vs. diversity slates) are supported more than others. Additional stated directions include how gender gap lay theories form within organizations (culture, leader rhetoric, ideal-worker norms) and studying gender gap attributions from the organization's perspective.
