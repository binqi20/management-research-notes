# Understanding I-Deals through the Social Ledger Lens: The Role of Trust and Hindrance Networks

**Abstract**
Individually negotiated customized work arrangements or idiosyncratic deals (i-deals) are an increasingly common practice in contemporary organizations. We propose that these deals are created and implemented in the social context of the workgroup and that one’s position in the web of social relationships serves as a strategic advantage or barrier to accessing i-deals. Drawing on the social ledger model, we propose that being central in the trust network is positively associated with i-deals, whereas being central in the hindrance network is negatively associated with i-deals, with indirect effects on employee performance. Further, we predict that i-deals are affected more strongly by centrality in the hindrance network than by centrality in the trust network. Finally, we theorize that trust network density strengthens the negative relationship between hindrance network centrality and i-deals and that hindrance network density strengthens the positive relationship between trust network centrality and i-deals. Results of multilevel modeling and relative importance analyses in two field studies employing data from 305 employees and 57 supervisors in the United States and 502 employees and 101 supervisors in Iran provided support for our model. Implications and future directions for social network and i-deals research are discussed.

**Research Question**
How does an employee’s position in the workgroup’s social networks — centrality in the trust network (a social asset) versus the hindrance network (a social liability) — and the density of those networks shape the employee’s ability to secure i-deals, and how do i-deals transmit these network effects to task performance?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. Centrality in the workgroup’s trust network is positively related to i-deals.
H2. Centrality in the workgroup’s hindrance network is negatively related to i-deals.
H3. Relative to trust network centrality, hindrance network centrality is more strongly related to i-deals (negative asymmetry).
H4. Centrality in the trust network indirectly and positively affects task performance via i-deals.
H5. Centrality in the hindrance network indirectly and negatively affects task performance via i-deals.
H6. Hindrance network density moderates the relationship between trust network centrality and i-deals such that when the hindrance network is dense, this positive relationship is stronger.
H7. Hindrance network density moderates the indirect relationship between trust network centrality and task performance via i-deals such that when the hindrance network is dense, this positive indirect relationship is stronger.
H8. Trust network density moderates the relationship between hindrance network centrality and i-deals such that when the trust network is dense, this negative relationship is stronger.
H9. Trust network density moderates the indirect negative relationship between hindrance network centrality and task performance via i-deals such that when the trust network is dense, this negative relationship is stronger.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Trust network centrality (in-degree); hindrance network centrality (in-degree) — both individual-level.
- DV(s): I-deals (proximal outcome); task performance (distal outcome).
- Mediators: I-deals (mediates network centrality → task performance).
- Moderators: Hindrance network density; trust network density (both group-level, yielding cross-level interactions).

Under the social ledger model, being central in the trust network confers reputation, influence, and privileged access to private information, which raise both the employee’s confidence to seek an i-deal and the leader’s willingness to grant one at low perceived social cost; being central in the hindrance network marks the employee as difficult, lowering coworker acceptance and leader support. Because hindrance ties are rarer and more diagnostic than trust ties, they weigh more heavily (negative asymmetry). Integrating social comparison theory, a contrasting network context sharpens the salience of one’s centrality, so a dense hindrance network amplifies the benefit of trust centrality (and, symmetrically, a dense trust network was predicted to amplify the harm of hindrance centrality). Finally, per social exchange theory, employees who obtain i-deals reciprocate with greater task performance, carrying the network effects downstream.

**Data & Measures**
Two field studies. Study 1: 305 employees nested in 57 supervisor-led workgroups across 70 midsize restaurants in a Southwestern U.S. city; multisource and time-lagged (employees reported network ties and i-deals at Time 1; supervisors rated task performance one month later at Time 2). Study 2: 502 employees and 101 supervisors in a large retail business in Iran, using a multisource, three-wave design.
- I-deals: Study 1, 6-item scale from Rousseau, Hornung, and Kim (2009) (α = .82); Study 2, 9 items adapted from Rosen et al. (2013) (α = .70).
- Trust network centrality: in-degree from a single sociometric item (Lau & Liden, 2008) — coworkers checkmarked those with whom they "talk about confidential work-related matters"; scored as the count of times a focal employee was named.
- Hindrance network centrality: in-degree adapted from Sparrowe et al. (2001) — Study 1, coworkers named those they "avoid because he or she is difficult for me to work with"; Study 2, "[Name] hinders my work"; scored as count of times named.
- Network density: number of direct ties divided by the maximum possible ties in the group (Burt, 1992).
- Task performance: supervisor-rated 4-item scale (Tsui, 1984; Tsui & Gutek, 1984) (α = .87).
- Controls: LMX (Graen & Uhl-Bien, 1995), employee age, sex, dyadic tenure, and group size; Study 2 additionally controlled status, neuroticism, emotional intelligence, proactive personality, and group justice climate.
Analysis used multilevel path modeling (Mplus 8.4), relative weight analysis for relative importance (H3), and Monte Carlo resampling for (moderated) indirect effects. The design is associational and time-lagged; the authors caution it is not experimental or longitudinal and cannot rule out reverse causality.

**Key Findings**
- H1 supported: trust network centrality was positively related to i-deals (Study 1 γ = 0.14, p < .05; Study 2 γ = 0.12, p < .05).
- H2 supported: hindrance network centrality was negatively related to i-deals (Study 1 γ = −0.31, p < .01; Study 2 γ = −0.41, p < .01).
- H3 supported (negative asymmetry): hindrance centrality explained far more variance in i-deals than trust centrality (Study 1: 58.80% vs. 8.41%, ΔR² = 50.39; Study 2: 55.89% vs. 25.62%, ΔR² = 30.27).
- H4 supported: positive indirect effect of trust centrality on task performance via i-deals (Study 1 b = .02, 95% CI [.001, .054]; Study 2 b = .03, 95% CI [.001, .062]).
- H5 supported: negative indirect effect of hindrance centrality on task performance via i-deals (Study 1 b = −.05, 95% CI [−.113, −.015]; Study 2 b = −.11, 95% CI [−.154, −.073]).
- H6 supported: hindrance network density strengthened the positive trust-centrality → i-deals relationship (Study 1 γ = 0.24, p < .01; Study 2 γ = 0.28, p < .01); the relationship was positive under high hindrance density and nonsignificant under low density.
- H7 supported: the positive indirect trust-centrality → task-performance effect (via i-deals) held when hindrance density was high and was nonsignificant otherwise.
- H8 not supported: trust network density did not moderate the hindrance-centrality → i-deals relationship (Study 1 γ = 0.04, ns; Study 2 γ = 0.02, ns).
- H9 was not tested because H8 failed.
- Unexpected: in Study 1, hindrance network density × hindrance centrality was significant (γ = −0.30, p < .01) — hindrance centrality's negative relation with i-deals was stronger when the hindrance network was dense.
- Supplementary (Study 2): the model held for i-deals that were "asked for and granted," but not for i-deals "asked for" alone or "granted without being asked," reinforcing i-deals as employee-initiated and successfully negotiated.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions in its own terms. First, it integrates i-deals research into the social network perspective, relocating the driver of i-deals from individual traits and dyadic factors (e.g., proactivity, LMX) to the broader workgroup network structure. Second, it theorizes and empirically substantiates the long-assumed but previously untested negative asymmetry of the social ledger — hindrance (negative) ties outweigh trust (positive) ties in explaining i-deals. Third, it introduces network density as a moderator by integrating the social ledger model with social comparison theory, showing that a contrasting tie context (e.g., dense hindrance ties) amplifies the value of one's centrality in the opposing (trust) network.

**Practical Implication**
For managers, HR, and line managers, i-deals are a tool to motivate performance when the interests of employees, coworkers, workgroups, leaders, and the organization are aligned. Social structure can enable or bar access: employees central in trust networks — and especially those not central in hindrance networks — are advantaged, and trusted employees are further advantaged when hindrance ties are prevalent in the group. Because employees with the greatest need for customized arrangements (e.g., someone caring for a sick parent) may be structurally disadvantaged, managers can grant flexible schedules to help move such employees out of the hindrance network, but must do so carefully and with clear communication to the whole team. At the organizational level, HR and line managers should weigh workgroup member composition and train employees to build positive ties (trust, friendship, advice) while minimizing negative ties.

**Limitations**
The time-lagged design is not experimental or longitudinal, so reverse causality cannot be ruled out (e.g., i-deal recipients may later attract social ties). The theorized social-comparison mechanics were not directly measured. In Study 1, the hindrance measure may have confounded avoidance with hindrance. Some potentially relevant controls (e.g., ties outside the workgroup, leaders' personality, team-level factors) were omitted, and centrality — an aggregation of many coworkers' reactions — may be relatively insensitive to a focal employee's recent i-deal.

**Future Research**
The authors call for empirically verifying the underlying social-comparison mechanisms — for example, tracing the co-evolution of network development and i-deals negotiations over time and using realistic experimental designs to clarify causality. They suggest examining additional employee-level (ties outside the workgroup, past performance), leader-level (leaders' personality, psychological process in granting i-deals, leader's-leader exchange [LLX]), and workgroup/organization-level factors (team potency, turnover rate). They also encourage testing other outcomes such as employee innovation and creativity, counterproductive work behavior, and turnover, and investigating how social networks develop and i-deals are dispensed in virtual environments and in multicultural workgroups.
