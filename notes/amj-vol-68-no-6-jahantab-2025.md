# Understanding I-Deals through the Social Ledger Lens: The Role of Trust and Hindrance Networks

**Abstract**
Individually negotiated customized work arrangements or idiosyncratic deals (i-deals) are an increasingly common practice in contemporary organizations. We propose that these deals are created and implemented in the social context of the workgroup and that one’s position in the web of social relationships serves as a strategic advantage or barrier to accessing i-deals. Drawing on the social ledger model, we propose that being central in the trust network is positively associated with i-deals, whereas being central in the hindrance network is negatively associated with i-deals, with indirect effects on employee performance. Further, we predict that i-deals are affected more strongly by centrality in the hindrance network than by centrality in the trust network. Finally, we theorize that trust network density strengthens the negative relationship between hindrance network centrality and i-deals and that hindrance network density strengthens the positive relationship between trust network centrality and i-deals. Results of multilevel modeling and relative importance analyses in two field studies employing data from 305 employees and 57 supervisors in the United States and 502 employees and 101 supervisors in Iran provided support for our model. Implications and future directions for social network and i-deals research are discussed.

**Research Question**
How does an employee's position in the workgroup's trust and hindrance networks — and the density of those networks — shape the employee's ability to obtain i-deals, and, through i-deals, their task performance? More specifically, is the negative (hindrance) side of the social ledger more strongly related to i-deals than the positive (trust) side, and how do contrasting network densities moderate these effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Employee in-degree centrality in the workgroup trust network; employee in-degree centrality in the workgroup hindrance network
- DV(s): I-deals (employee-negotiated customization of skill development, performance goals, career development, schedule, and work hours); supervisor-rated task performance (distal outcome)
- Mediators: I-deals mediate the effect of trust and hindrance network centrality on task performance (first-stage moderated mediation)
- Moderators: Hindrance network density (strengthens the positive trust-centrality -> i-deals link); trust network density (predicted to strengthen the negative hindrance-centrality -> i-deals link, unsupported in the main models)

Drawing on Labianca and Brass's social ledger model, the authors argue that being central in the trust network increases an employee's confidence, reputation, and access to private information, lowers the leader's perceived social cost of granting differential treatment, and therefore raises the likelihood of obtaining i-deals. Centrality in the hindrance network does the opposite: it signals social liability, dampens coworker and leader willingness to support differentiated treatment, and suppresses i-deals attainment. Because negative ties are rarer and more diagnostic than positive ties, hindrance centrality exerts a stronger effect than trust centrality (negative asymmetry). Integrating social comparison theory, the authors further argue that a central employee's ties stand out more when they contrast with the prevailing network context — trust centrality is amplified when hindrance ties are dense, and hindrance centrality is amplified when trust ties are dense. Via social exchange, i-deals recipients reciprocate with higher task performance, producing indirect effects of both network positions on performance through i-deals.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study integrates i-deals research with the social network perspective, reframing i-deals as an individual-within-workgroup meso-level phenomenon rather than a purely individual or dyadic one, and identifying the social structure of trust and hindrance ties as a previously overlooked antecedent. It provides rare empirical evidence for the social ledger model's negative-asymmetry proposition by showing that hindrance centrality explains substantially more variance in i-deals than trust centrality. It also advances the social comparison perspective in network research by theorizing and testing how the density of opposing ties (the network context) moderates the effects of individual centrality on valued organizational outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and HR professionals should recognize that access to i-deals is shaped by employees' informal network positions, not only their individual traits or LMX quality. Because hindrance ties carry outsized consequences, organizations should monitor workgroup climate, train employees to build trust-based ties, and actively minimize avoidance/hindrance ties when composing teams. Managers can also use carefully designed i-deals — coupled with transparent communication to the wider team — to help employees in difficult personal circumstances (e.g., caregiving) move out of hindrance-network positions, while being mindful of contextual pressures against such allocations.

**Limitations**
The time-lagged two-wave design cannot rule out reverse causality between network position and i-deals, and is not a substitute for longitudinal or experimental evidence. The authors could not directly test the social comparison mechanism theorized to underlie the density moderation. The Study 1 hindrance measure may have confounded avoidance with hindrance, and several potentially relevant employee-, leader-, and group-level controls (e.g., personality, LLX, team potency, turnover rate, ties outside the workgroup) were not included.

**Future Research**
Future work should use experimental or genuinely longitudinal designs to establish the causal direction between network centrality and i-deals, and should directly measure the social comparison processes theorized to drive density moderation. Scholars are encouraged to examine additional individual-, leader-, and group-level moderators, other outcomes (employee innovation, creativity, CWB, turnover), and to investigate how social networks develop and how i-deals are dispensed in virtual and multicultural workgroups where interaction norms differ.
