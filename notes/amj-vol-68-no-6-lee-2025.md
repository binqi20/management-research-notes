# Emotional Signaling: How Helpers’ Emotional Expressions Affect Attributions of Motives, Relationship Quality, and Reciprocation

**Abstract**
Research has shown that interpersonal helping offers many social benefits for the helper, yet these social benefits depend on the recipients’ responses. Whether helping is perceived favorably by recipients depends on the underlying motives they attribute to the helper, which raises the questions of when and how recipients may attribute different motives to helping. To explain when and how those receiving help make these attributions, we adopt a social-functional approach to emotions to examine the impact of the social information conveyed through discrete emotional expressions. We draw on the framework of emotions as social information to theorize that the helper’s emotional expressions act as signals of social engagement or disengagement that differentially impact the recipient’s attribution of motives and reactions to help. Across three studies, we find that helpers’ expressions of socially engaging emotions (gratitude and sympathy) lead to greater attributions of prosocial motives, whereas expressions of socially disengaging emotions (pride and contempt) lead to greater attributions of instrumental motives. We find that the effect of these expressed emotions on relationship quality and reciprocation toward the helper is mediated by the recipient’s attribution of prosocial motives but not by the attribution of instrumental motives.

**Research Question**
When and how do recipients of help attribute different motives to a helper, and how do the helper’s discrete emotional expressions during helping (pride, contempt, gratitude, sympathy) shape those motive attributions and, in turn, the recipient’s relationship quality with and reciprocation toward the helper?

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. Recipients are more likely to attribute helping to prosocial motives when helpers express socially engaging emotions (gratitude or sympathy) rather than socially disengaging emotions (pride or contempt).
H2. Recipients are more likely to attribute helping to instrumental motives when helpers express socially disengaging emotions (pride or contempt) rather than socially engaging emotions (gratitude or sympathy).
H3. The attribution of prosocial motives is positively associated with the recipient’s relationship quality with the helper.
H4. The attribution of instrumental motives is negatively associated with the recipient’s relationship quality with the helper.
H5. The attribution of prosocial motives is positively associated with the recipient’s reciprocation toward the helper.
H6. The attribution of instrumental motives is negatively associated with the recipient’s reciprocation toward the helper.
H7. The effect of expressions of discrete emotions on (a) relationship quality and (b) reciprocation is mediated by the attribution of prosocial motives.
H8. The effect of expressions of discrete emotions on (a) relationship quality and (b) reciprocation is mediated by the attribution of instrumental motives.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Helper’s discrete emotional expression during task-related helping — socially engaging (gratitude, sympathy) vs. socially disengaging (pride, contempt); analyzed as a 0/1 dummy (1 = socially engaging).
- DV(s): Recipient’s relationship quality with the helper; recipient’s reciprocation (task- and person-focused interpersonal citizenship behavior in Study 1; reward/punishment, reward-allocation, and recommendation decisions in Study 2; reward- and time-allocation in-basket decisions in Study 3).
- Mediators: Attribution of prosocial motives; attribution of instrumental motives.
- Moderators: None hypothesized. Explicitly incidental emotions are framed as a boundary condition; positive/negative affect (Study 2) and perceived character-morality/compassion (Study 3) are modeled as alternative mediators, not moderators.

Drawing on the emotions-as-social-information (EASI) framework and the social engagement–disengagement dimension of discrete emotions, the paper argues that a helper’s expressed emotions are a salient, immediately accessible cue that signals how the helper is oriented toward the recipient. Socially engaging emotions (gratitude, sympathy) signal interest in and concern for the recipient, prompting attributions of prosocial motives; socially disengaging emotions (pride, contempt) signal status distance and self-orientation, prompting attributions of instrumental motives. Through the lens of social exchange theory and the norm of reciprocity, these attributed motives — especially the attribution of prosocial motives — then determine whether the recipient values the relationship and feels obligated to reciprocate.

**Data & Measures**
Identification rests on experimental manipulation of the helper’s expressed emotion with random assignment to four conditions (Study 2 uses student-actor videos; Study 3 uses text-based scenario descriptions plus a no-emotion control; Study 1 uses recalled critical incidents). Mediation is tested via path analysis (Mplus 8) with bias-corrected bootstrapping (10,000 resamples). Mediators: attribution of prosocial motives via a four-item measure (Grant, 2008) and attribution of instrumental motives via a six-item measure (Allen & Rush, 1998), reused across studies. DVs: relationship quality via coworker exchange quality (Sherony & Green, 2002) in Studies 1 and 3 and a two-item peer-evaluation measure in Study 2; reciprocation operationalized behaviorally as interpersonal citizenship behavior (Settoon & Mossholder, 2002) in Study 1 and as incentivized reward-, resource-, time-allocation, and recommendation decisions in Studies 2-3. Study 1 controls for reputational information (morality, compassion), Kelley covariation cues (consistency, distinctiveness, consensus), locus of causality, relative status, and helping norms; Studies 2-3 model affect and perceived character as competing mediators.

**Key Findings**
Coefficients are reported in Study 1 / Study 2 / Study 3 order; all are standardized path estimates.
- H1 SUPPORTED: expressing socially engaging (vs. disengaging) emotions increased attributions of prosocial motives (b = 0.16, p = .039 / b = 1.21, p < .001 / b = 1.00, p < .001).
- H2 SUPPORTED: socially engaging emotions decreased attributions of instrumental motives (b = -0.54 / -0.65 / -0.84; all p <= .001) — that is, socially disengaging emotions raised instrumental attributions.
- H3 SUPPORTED: attribution of prosocial motives was positively associated with relationship quality (b = 0.18, p = .004 / 0.39 / 0.42; both p < .001).
- H4 NOT SUPPORTED: attribution of instrumental motives was not significantly related to relationship quality in any study (b = -0.02, p = .698 / b = -0.10, p = .256 / b = 0.04, p = .405).
- H5 SUPPORTED: attribution of prosocial motives was positively associated with reciprocation (b = 0.17, p = .022 / 0.40 / 0.49; both p < .001).
- H6 NOT SUPPORTED: attribution of instrumental motives was not significantly related to reciprocation (b = -0.04, p = .361 / b = -0.09, p = .361 / b = -0.10, p = .253).
- H7a and H7b SUPPORTED: the effect of socially engaging emotions on both relationship quality and reciprocation was significantly mediated by the attribution of prosocial motives (bootstrap CIs excluded zero in all three studies).
- H8a and H8b NOT SUPPORTED: the attribution of instrumental motives did not significantly mediate either outcome.
Alternative mediators were weaker than the attribution of prosocial motives and not uniformly significant: in Study 2 the indirect effect through positive affect was significant but the one through negative affect was not, and in Study 3 perceived morality and compassion were significant for relationship quality but weaker or nonsignificant for reciprocation. Net result: only the attribution of prosocial motives (not instrumental motives) consistently drove downstream relationship quality and reciprocation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
First, the paper advances social exchange theory by challenging the presumption of reciprocity: reciprocation of ostensibly positive behavior involves an interpretive element, and it is the helper’s emotional signals of social (dis)engagement — not merely the material benefits exchanged — that give the transaction relational meaning and regulate the norm of reciprocity. Second, it reintegrates an affective perspective into the interpersonal-helping literature by shifting attention from the recipient’s experienced emotions to the helper’s expressed emotions, explaining why helping does not always elicit favorable responses. Third, it extends attribution theory by identifying discrete emotional expressions embedded within the exchange as a salient, accessible source of social information from which recipients infer motives when traditional attributional cues (reputation, behavioral norms) are unavailable.

**Practical Implication**
For leaders — often seen by subordinates as symbolic representatives of the organization and holding greater access to resources for helping — it is important to express emotions that signal prosocial motives when helping in order to strengthen reciprocal bonds, and to cultivate an affective culture that normalizes socially engaging (rather than disengaging) emotions during helping. For employees giving help, good deeds may not be reciprocated when accompanied by emotional signals that recipients do not attribute to prosocial motives; deep-acting emotion regulation and perspective-taking may help elicit authentic socially engaging emotions. For recipients of help, greater awareness of how they process these emotional signals — and, where maintaining the relationship holds practical value, regulating their own attributional processes — can guide their responses.

**Limitations**
There is a risk of common method bias, particularly in Study 1, where focal variables were measured from the same source at the same time (mitigated by inserting controls between mediators and dependent variables and by using different scale formats and in-basket decisions in Studies 2-3, but not fully eliminated). Given the ephemeral nature of emotions, the studies were conducted primarily in controlled settings; while the pilot and Study 1 offer real-work evidence, they cannot establish the real-world frequency of emotional expressions during helping. The studies also focused primarily on anticipatory (rather than reactive) help and on task-related helping.

**Future Research**
The authors call for extending the framework to naturalistic field settings and to reactive help and non-task-related helping domains (e.g., emotion helping), where different emotional expressions may elicit different motive attributions. They suggest examining the broader relational context and temporal dynamics in which episodes of helping are embedded, the role of helpers’ emotion regulation and whether recipients reappraise emotional signals, how virtual/electronic communication alters perceptions of expressed emotions, and how combinations of simultaneously expressed (mixed) emotions and additional discrete emotions (e.g., pity, guilt, disgust, anger) convey social engagement or disengagement.
