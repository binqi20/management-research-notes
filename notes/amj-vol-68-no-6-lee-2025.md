# Emotional Signaling: How Helpers’ Emotional Expressions Affect Attributions of Motives, Relationship Quality, and Reciprocation

**Abstract**
Research has shown that interpersonal helping offers many social benefits for the helper, yet these social benefits depend on the recipients’ responses. Whether helping is perceived favorably by recipients depends on the underlying motives they attribute to the helper, which raises the questions of when and how recipients may attribute different motives to helping. To explain when and how those receiving help make these attributions, we adopt a social-functional approach to emotions to examine the impact of the social information conveyed through discrete emotional expressions. We draw on the framework of emotions as social information to theorize that the helper’s emotional expressions act as signals of social engagement or disengagement that differentially impact the recipient’s attribution of motives and reactions to help. Across three studies, we find that helpers’ expressions of socially engaging emotions (gratitude and sympathy) lead to greater attributions of prosocial motives, whereas expressions of socially disengaging emotions (pride and contempt) lead to greater attributions of instrumental motives. We find that the effect of these expressed emotions on relationship quality and reciprocation toward the helper is mediated by the recipient’s attribution of prosocial motives but not by the attribution of instrumental motives.

**Research Question**
When and how do recipients of interpersonal help attribute different motives to the helper, and how do the helper’s discrete emotional expressions—varying in their socially engaging versus disengaging functions—shape attributions of prosocial versus instrumental motives, relationship quality, and reciprocal responses to help?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Helper’s expression of discrete emotions during helping, classified as socially engaging (gratitude, sympathy) versus socially disengaging (pride, contempt)
- DV(s): Recipient’s relationship quality with the helper; reciprocation behaviors (raffle ticket reward/punishment, gift-card allocation, recommendation for future studies, bonus allocation, time allocation to help the coworker)
- Mediators: Attribution of prosocial motives (supported); attribution of instrumental motives (not supported as a mediator of relationship quality/reciprocation)
- Moderators: Perceived incidental versus integral emotion (boundary condition); task-related versus non-task-related helping (boundary condition); helper’s character (morality, compassion) tested as alternative mediators

Drawing on the emotions as social information (EASI) framework and Markus and Kitayama’s social engagement–disengagement dimension, the authors theorize that helpers’ discrete emotional expressions provide recipients with salient, accessible social information about the helper’s orientation toward relational harmony or separation. Gratitude and sympathy signal engagement and lead recipients to infer prosocial (other-oriented) motives, whereas pride and contempt signal disengagement and lead recipients to infer instrumental (self-interested) motives. These inferred motives then shape the recipient’s perceived relationship quality and willingness to reciprocate, with only the attribution of prosocial motives consistently mediating downstream relational and reciprocal outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances social exchange theory by challenging the presumption of reciprocity and showing that reciprocation depends on an interpretive process grounded in social information embedded in the helper’s emotional expressions, rather than solely on the material benefits exchanged. It reintegrates an affective perspective into the workplace helping literature, reconciling prior inconsistent findings on emotionality and valence by introducing the social engagement–disengagement dimension of discrete emotions. It also contributes to attribution theory by identifying discrete emotional expressions as a salient and accessible source of information recipients use to infer prosocial versus instrumental motives when traditional reputational or normative cues are unavailable.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders and employees should recognize that the emotions they express while helping carry significant social information that shapes how recipients attribute motives and reciprocate. Helpers can benefit from deep-acting regulation and perspective-taking to authentically express socially engaging emotions (gratitude, sympathy) that strengthen reciprocal bonds, while leaders can cultivate affective cultures that normalize the expression of engaging emotions during helping. Recipients of help may also benefit from introspectively reappraising emotional signals when maintaining a work relationship is practically valuable.

**Limitations**
First, Study 1’s critical incident design carries a risk of common method bias because focal variables were measured from the same source at the same time, though separation, varied scale formats, and behavioral in-basket decisions in Studies 2 and 3 partially mitigated this concern. Second, the research was conducted primarily in controlled settings and cannot establish the real-world frequency of these emotional expressions during helping. Third, the studies focused on anticipatory, task-related helping, so findings may not generalize to reactive help or to non-task domains such as emotion helping or helping with personal problems.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine broader relational and temporal contexts in which help is exchanged, the role of emotional regulation and its personal costs for helpers, how virtual and text-based communication affects perceptions of helpers’ emotions, and how combinations of multiple emotions are interpreted by recipients. Scholars should also extend the framework to additional discrete emotions (e.g., pity, guilt, disgust, anger) to further develop the social engagement–disengagement dimension, and explore how recipients may reappraise emotional signals to sustain work relationships.
