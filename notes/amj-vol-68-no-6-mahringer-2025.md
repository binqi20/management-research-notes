# Autonomous, Yet Interdependent: Designing Interfaces across Routine Clusters

**Abstract**
This paper examines the process of designing interfaces between routine clusters in a world in flux in which interdependencies are emergent. We reveal that designing interfaces is an endogenous and iterative process of creating and harmonizing interdependencies. In our ethnographic study of the reorganization of agile software development, actors implemented a second routine cluster, which they envisioned to work autonomously. Because the teams shared resources, however, they created interdependencies across the two routine clusters that challenged their autonomy. Our findings contribute to research on routine dynamics, interdependence, and organizations design. They do so, first, by showing how the dynamics of designing interfaces are driven by the emergent nature of interdependencies, which routine participants may endogenously harmonize through routine performances. Second, our findings detail the practices through which “interface design work” is accomplished. Although some of these practices imply transitory variations in routine performances, others change the patterning of interfaces more sustainably. Third, resources play an important role in designing interfaces because making them readily available may require their reconfiguration. Moreover, the pooled interdependencies that flow from shared resources can escalate into more complex types of interdependence.

**Research Question**
The paper asks: (1) How do interdependencies emerge during the reorganization of routine clusters? (2) How does the process of designing interfaces to manage those interdependencies unfold?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering conditions: Reorganization of work into two semi-autonomous routine clusters through replication of routines across teams and the sharing of scarce resources (actors and artifacts such as software code).
- Key constructs / tensions: Autonomy aspiration vs. emergent interdependence; pooled, sequential, and reciprocal interdependencies (Thompson 1967); divisibility vs. indivisibility of shared resources; transitory vs. patterning interventions.
- Practices ("interface design work"): analyzing interdependencies, reconfiguring resources, mitigating interferences, and reconfiguring schedules — differentiated along two dimensions: durability (transitory vs. patterning) and design elements (artifacts vs. timing).
- Process / phases: (1) implementing the second cluster and discovering emergent pooled interdependencies; (2) iterative harmonization that escalates pooled interdependencies into sequential and, under time pressure, reciprocal ones; (3) stabilization of interfaces that allows the clusters to operate relatively autonomously.
- Emergent outcomes: Stabilized interfaces between the clusters and a mutually constitutive relationship between resourcing, patterning, and performing.

Because contemporary organizations operate in flux, interdependencies between routine clusters are not exogenously given but emerge as actors replicate routines and share resources across clusters. These emergent pooled interdependencies trigger "interface design work": actors analyze interdependencies and mitigate interferences through transitory adjustments within routine performances, while reconfiguring resources and reconfiguring schedules produce more durable patterning of interfaces. Harmonizing one interdependence often creates new and more complex ones — pooled interdependencies can escalate into sequential ones when reconfiguration work must precede regular work, and sequential interdependencies compress into reciprocal ones under time pressure. Through iterative cycles of creating and harmonizing interdependencies, routine participants themselves act as endogenous designers who eventually stabilize interfaces, disentangling the clusters.

**Data & Measures**
Data corpus: a 12-month ethnographic single-case study at "Technology Innovation Corporation" (TIC, a pseudonym), a medium-sized high-tech manufacturing company, drawing on four data sources collected between November 2016 and October 2017 — participant observation (two to three days per week; ~1,000 pages of fieldnotes and 122 hours of audio), 35 formal interviews (30 during fieldwork plus 5 follow-ups two years later, covering at least one member of each of teams Alpha and Beta), ~6,300 pages of documents and ~2,000 emails, and ~163,000 digital-trace actions logged in the Jira issue-tracking system. Analysis proceeded inductively in five steps: abstracting each team's routine cluster and identifying its routines by coding the data with in vivo codes tied to the actors' own language (e.g., Sprint, Refinement, Review, Retrospective routines); constructing an event timeline (Langley, 1999) of the reorganization; classifying the emerging cross-cluster interdependencies with Thompson's (1967) pooled/sequential/reciprocal typology; inductively coding four interface-design-work practices and cross-classifying them by durability (transitory vs. patterning) and design element (artifacts vs. timing); and modeling the dynamics linking reorganization, interdependencies, and interface design across three analytically bracketed periods. Interpretive, process-oriented design; no measurement scales, statistical estimation, or causal-identification strategy is claimed.

**Key Findings**
The study surfaces a process model in which designing interfaces between routine clusters is an endogenous and iterative process of creating and harmonizing emergent interdependencies, carried out by routine participants themselves rather than by detached, exogenous designers. Reorganizing work into two envisioned-autonomous clusters — by replicating Team Alpha's Scrum routines in the new Team Beta and sharing scarce resources (the shared actor "Austin-Blake" and Alpha's code) — generated pooled interdependencies across the clusters. Actors harmonized these through "interface design work," which the authors resolve into four practices: analyzing interdependencies and mitigating interferences (transitory work) and reconfiguring resources and reconfiguring schedules (patterning work), further split by whether they act on artifacts or on timing. A central finding is directional: harmonizing one interdependence tended to create new, more complex ones, so that pooled interdependencies proliferated into sequential ones (when reconfiguring a shared resource had to precede regular Sprint work) and then into reciprocal ones once several harmonizing efforts were temporally compressed under a synchronized joint release date — reciprocal interdependence emerging when sequential interdependence is temporally compressed. Disentangling the clusters therefore paradoxically produced heightened entanglement before, over three periods, the teams stabilized the interfaces so the clusters could operate relatively autonomously; resourcing, patterning, and performing are theorized as mutually constitutive.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances routine dynamics, interdependence, and organizational design research by reframing interface design as an endogenous, iterative process of creating and harmonizing emergent interdependencies, performed by routine participants rather than by detached exogenous architects. It uncovers "interface design work" as four distinct practices differentiated along two dimensions — durability (transitory vs. patterning) and design elements (artifacts vs. timing) — and theorizes a mutually constitutive relationship between resourcing, patterning, and performing. Third, it unpacks the role of resources in interface design, showing that sharing scarce resources generates pooled interdependencies that can escalate into sequential and reciprocal forms, such that disentangling routine clusters may paradoxically produce heightened forms of entanglement before stable autonomy is achieved.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations reorganizing work into more autonomous units, the study suggests that designing the interfaces between those units is less an up-front architectural task than an ongoing, endogenous one: because interdependencies surface as routines are performed and resources are shared, the routine participants themselves are positioned to harmonize them. The paper details four practices through which they do so — analyzing interdependencies, reconfiguring resources, mitigating interferences, and reconfiguring schedules — combining transitory fixes within ongoing routines with more durable patterning of shared artifacts and timing. It also shows that sharing scarce, indivisible resources can escalate pooled interdependencies into sequential and reciprocal ones, so on the paper's account treating interface design as continuous, participant-led work rather than fixed up-front coordination is what sustains the autonomy such reorganization is meant to create.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that their theoretical insights rest on a single 12-month ethnographic case study of two agile (Scrum) software development teams in one high-tech manufacturing firm, so the findings are bounded by this particular context. The setting is one in which both teams valued autonomy and operated with high mutual respect, shared scarce (rather than freely copiable) resources, used Scrum routines that combine task decomposition with regular collective-reflection meetings, and involved only two clusters that replicated essentially the same routines. The authors flag each of these features as scope conditions rather than general properties, noting in particular that the absence of power asymmetries and the presence of Scrum-enabled reflection may have shaped how interface design work unfolded in ways that would not necessarily transfer to other settings.

**Future Research**
The authors identify four boundary conditions inviting further work: (a) settings with more than two clusters sharing resources, or with orchestrated resource allocation across multiple clusters, and settings where resource allocation shifts over time; (b) reorganizations in which autonomy is less central, or which involve clusters with entirely different routines or temporal rhythms, potentially collapsing into a single cluster; (c) contexts where routines are not based on Scrum or task decomposition, and where routines afford less collective reflection, to clarify how task decomposition and reflection shape interface design; and (d) cases of lower mutual respect or power asymmetries between clusters, where breakdowns or alternative resolutions might emerge.
