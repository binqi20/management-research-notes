# Vulnerability Practices among Professionals in Multidisciplinary Settings

**Abstract**
Professional workers are expected to display confidence and mastery in their work roles, and may be hesitant to reveal their doubts and insecurities, especially in multidisciplinary settings characterized by status differences. However, scholars have recognized the benefits of sharing vulnerabilities to create opportunities for social support. We contribute to the literature by revealing how this might be achieved through a ritualized sequence of “vulnerability practices” in a dedicated setting. Based on a qualitative study of a weekly meeting where a multidisciplinary group of professionals shares experiences, we develop a process model that explains how vulnerability practices evolve through phases of individual disclosure and communal reflection bridged by theorizing from experiences. We reveal how a delicate tension between social pressure (to display vulnerabilities) and social support (to contain them) underpins these practices, and how this tension is influenced by power relations associated with multidisciplinarity. We show how participants’ appraisals of past episodes shape their willingness to engage in subsequent episodes, reinforcing or undermining the capacity of the setting to serve as a “holding environment” offering interpretation and containment. We thus reveal holding environments at work to be precarious accomplishments, created and maintained through ritualized vulnerability practices, and imbued by power relations.

**Research Question**
How and with what perceived effects do professionals in multidisciplinary settings engage in practices that enable the sharing of doubts and insecurities experienced in the performance of their work roles?

**Mechanism Process**
- Setting (holding environment): a weekly, ritualized multidisciplinary meeting theorized as a precarious "holding environment" that can provide containment (managing difficult emotions) and interpretation (making sense of them).
- Phases: an individual disclosure phase (dominated by social pressure to display vulnerability) and a communal reflection phase (culminating in social support), with "theorizing from the case" bridging the two.
- Ritualized sequence of practices: offering/accepting the mic, storytelling to colleagues, questioning the storyteller, revealing vulnerability, theorizing from the case (boundary setting, categorizing, perspective shifting), validating, advising, and thanking colleagues.
- Core tension: a delicate "Catch-22" between social pressure (to display vulnerabilities) and social support (to contain them).
- Power and authority dynamics: the role of the facilitator, discretion to participate or not, theorizing capability, and position in institutionalized professional hierarchies.
- Feedback loop: participants' appraisals of past episodes (benefits vs. risks) shape their willingness to engage in subsequent episodes.
- Path anomalies: "under-vulnerability" (E19) and "under-validation" (E9), where the typical sequence breaks down.

The paper develops an inductive process model in which a ritualized sequence of vulnerability practices moves professionals from individual disclosure to communal reflection, with theorizing from the case as the pivotal bridge that enables the interpretive function of a holding environment and seeds social support. Whether the setting functions as a "good enough" holding environment is a precarious, ongoing accomplishment: when social pressure to disclose is not matched by social support, and when power and authority dynamics tied to multidisciplinarity intrude, the environment may "hold" some participants more than others.

**Data & Measures**
Qualitative data drawn from an academic primary care (family medicine) clinic in Quebec, Canada. The first author observed and recorded 20 weekly online meetings (about three hours each) over 10 months, conducted 19 semi-structured interviews (about 60 minutes on average), and ran 7 participatory group discussions, yielding 93 hours of audio-video recording and 960 single-spaced pages of transcripts and observation notes (quotes translated from French). Using an insider-outsider research team and an inductive approach (Charmaz, 2014), analysis proceeded in six phases: extracting 27 episodes of vulnerability practices across the meetings; identifying roles (facilitator, storyteller, colleagues); open coding of the moves/practices and their chronology; coding perceived effects (benefits and risks); analyzing power dynamics related to seniority and discipline; and developing a theoretical model. Focal constructs were operationalized as coded utterances/moves and participant appraisals illustrated in Tables 1-9.

**Key Findings**
As an inductive qualitative study, the paper reports an emergent process model rather than hypothesis tests. Most episodes (25 of 27) followed a typical ritualized sequence unfolding through an individual disclosure phase and a communal reflection phase, bridged by "theorizing from the case" (boundary setting, categorizing, perspective shifting). A delicate tension between social pressure (to display vulnerabilities) and social support (to contain them) underpins the practices; when balanced, the setting operates as a holding environment that yields perceived benefits — comfort/belonging, normalization and reassurance, and role-based learning. Two path anomalies reveal its precarity: in "under-vulnerability" (E19) the storyteller repeatedly resisted disclosing, and in "under-validation" (E9) colleagues skipped theorizing and validating, leaving the storyteller feeling devalued and reluctant to return. Power and authority dynamics (facilitator role, discretion to participate, theorizing capability, and position in institutionalized hierarchies) shaped who was "held": lower-status participants (residents, social workers, nurses, patient partners) more often reported social discomfort and intimidation, while multidisciplinarity both widened the interpretive resources available (notably patient partners' perspective shifting) and could intensify social pressure. Participants' appraisals of past episodes fed back into their willingness to engage in subsequent ones.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces the concept of "vulnerability practices" — a ritualized sequence of moves through which group members display, or lead others to display, and address the role-based doubts and insecurities experienced in their work — and derives an inductive process model moving from individual disclosure to communal reflection, with theorizing as the pivotal bridge that constitutes a holding environment. It reconceptualizes holding environments at work not as fixed settings people enter but as precarious, ongoing accomplishments nourished or undermined episode by episode, nuancing prior research that emphasizes the positive effects of vulnerability display by surfacing its risks. It also theorizes how power dynamics tied to multidisciplinarity seep into vulnerability practices, showing how some ritual elements flatten power relations while others do not, and how multidisciplinary epistemic diversity expands the interpretive resources available for role-based learning.

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest that recognizing sustained engagement in vulnerability practices as "real work" is a practical as well as a theoretical contribution: this recognition may help practitioners (including academics) value such dedicated settings as potential holding environments (or "lifeboats") for coping with the pervasive doubts and insecurities experienced in daily professional work.

**Limitations**
The single-case design was explicitly focused on vulnerability practices in one context where doubts and insecurities about professional roles were the central concern. The activity studied was conducted entirely online during the observation period. The multidisciplinary composition, while offering a rich array of interpretive resources, may also make exchanges appear intimidating or invalidating for some participants.

**Future Research**
The authors call for multi-case studies comparing various contexts, processes, and dynamics of vulnerability practices, and for analysis of how professionals engage in vulnerability practices online versus in person. They also suggest examining other groups spanning diverse disciplinary and seniority statuses to understand how to level the playing field and enhance the benefits of vulnerability practices — for example, rotating the facilitator role among participants of different disciplinary statuses, or organizing groups whose members do not otherwise work together.
