# Vulnerability Practices among Professionals in Multidisciplinary Settings

**Abstract**
Professional workers are expected to display confidence and mastery in their work roles, and may be hesitant to reveal their doubts and insecurities, especially in multidisciplinary settings characterized by status differences. However, scholars have recognized the benefits of sharing vulnerabilities to create opportunities for social support. We contribute to the literature by revealing how this might be achieved through a ritualized sequence of “vulnerability practices” in a dedicated setting. Based on a qualitative study of a weekly meeting where a multidisciplinary group of professionals shares experiences, we develop a process model that explains how vulnerability practices evolve through phases of individual disclosure and communal reflection bridged by theorizing from experiences. We reveal how a delicate tension between social pressure (to display vulnerabilities) and social support (to contain them) underpins these practices, and how this tension is influenced by power relations associated with multidisciplinarity. We show how participants’ appraisals of past episodes shape their willingness to engage in subsequent episodes, reinforcing or undermining the capacity of the setting to serve as a “holding environment” offering interpretation and containment. We thus reveal holding environments at work to be precarious accomplishments, created and maintained through ritualized vulnerability practices, and imbued by power relations.

**Research Question**
How and with what perceived effects do professionals in multidisciplinary settings engage in practices that enable the sharing of doubts and insecurities experienced in the performance of their work roles?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Engagement in a ritualized sequence of "vulnerability practices" (facilitator invitation, storytelling, individual disclosure moves, theorizing from the case, communal reflection); multidisciplinary composition and power/authority dynamics (seniority, discipline, theorizing capability, discretion to participate).
- DV(s): Constitution (or undermining) of a "holding environment" providing containment and interpretation; participants' willingness to reveal vulnerability in subsequent episodes; role-based learning and reconsideration of work-role performance.
- Mediators: Theorizing practices (boundary setting, categorizing, perspective shifting) that bridge individual disclosure to communal reflection and seed social support.
- Moderators: Tension between social pressure to display vulnerabilities and social support to contain them; power and authority dynamics tied to multidisciplinarity (facilitator role, degree of discretion to participate, theorizing capability, institutionalized hierarchy).

Professionals operate under institutional norms that equate competence with invulnerability, leaving role-based doubts and insecurities unvoiced. In a dedicated, bounded setting, a ritualized sequence of moves — an invitation to "take the mic," incremental questioning that moves the storyteller from facts to emotions, and multifaceted theorizing that abstracts from the case — legitimates disclosure and activates validation, empathy, and advice from colleagues. Theorizing is pivotal: it enacts the interpretive function of a holding environment, contains difficult emotions, and turns a single disclosure into collective learning. Positive appraisals of past episodes feed back into willingness to disclose in future episodes, while under-validation or power imbalances can derail the ritual and undermine the holding environment.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces "vulnerability practices" as a novel concept naming the ritualized sequence of moves through which group members display, lead others to display, and address work-role doubts and insecurities, and derives an inductive process model linking individual disclosure to communal reflection via theorizing. It reconceptualizes holding environments not as fixed social containers but as precarious, processual accomplishments sustained (or not) episode-by-episode through a tension between social pressure and social support. It also theorizes how power and authority dynamics — especially those tied to multidisciplinarity — seep into vulnerability practices and shape who can be "held" and how.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that recognizing sustained engagement in vulnerability practices as "real work" can help practitioners (including academics) legitimize dedicated settings that function as holding environments (or "lifeboats") for coping with pervasive doubts and insecurities at work. For organizations designing such settings in multidisciplinary contexts, attention to ritual predictability, incremental facilitation from facts to emotions, multifaceted theorizing resources, and the mitigation of power asymmetries is essential to avoid under-validation and unintended harm.

**Limitations**
The study relies on a single case — a weekly multidisciplinary meeting in one Quebec primary-care clinic — explicitly designed around vulnerability sharing, and was conducted entirely online during the COVID-19 lockdown, which limits the generalizability of the process model to other settings, formats, and professional configurations.

**Future Research**
Future work could adopt multi-case designs to compare vulnerability practices across contexts and processes, examine online versus in-person dynamics, and explore other groups mixing disciplinary and seniority statuses to understand how to "level the playing field." The authors also suggest investigating how rotating the facilitator role across disciplinary statuses, or convening groups whose members do not otherwise work together, might reshape power dynamics and participants' appraisals of the benefits and risks of vulnerability practices.
