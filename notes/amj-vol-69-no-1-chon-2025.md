# Not So Fast? Rapid Response to Voice Leads to Perceived Inauthenticity

**Abstract**
Employees voice their concerns in the hope that leaders will change their behavior. But how do employees evaluate these changes? In the present research, we highlight the rate of behavior change as a key factor in determining leader evaluations: Leaders are seen as less authentic when they change rapidly in response to employee voice because people believe that true change takes time. We investigate this claim with three studies that adopt a mixed-method approach (n 5 3,056). In Study 1, PhD students describe rapid improvement in their advisor’s behavior as less authentic than gradual improvement— even when these changes are desired. In Study 2, we employ a stimulus sampling design to test the robustness of this effect. Followers see rapid improvement as less authentic than gradual improvement across a wide array of leader behaviors. In Study 3, we highlight change difficulty as a moderator and subsequent voice as a downstream consequence: When change is hard, rapid improvement elicits a greater authenticity penalty, which undermines followers’ willingness to voice their concerns in the future.

**Research Question**
How does the rate at which a leader changes their behavior in response to leader-centric voice shape followers' evaluations of the leader's authenticity, and how do those evaluations in turn affect followers' willingness to voice concerns in the future?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Rate of leader behavior change in response to leader-centric voice (rapid vs. gradual)
- DV(s): Perceived authenticity of the leader; willingness to express subsequent voice
- Mediators: Perceived authenticity (primary); perceived responsiveness (exploratory)
- Moderators: Perceived difficulty of the requested behavior change (easy vs. difficult)

Drawing on theories of person perception and attribution in leader-follower relationships, the authors argue that followers hold lay beliefs that true, authentic behavior change takes time and effort because it requires a corresponding shift in the actor's core sense of self. When leaders improve rapidly in response to voice, this violates those lay expectations and triggers an attributional search that leads followers to see the change as spurious rather than reflecting an underlying transformation. The resulting authenticity penalty is amplified when the requested change is difficult (because rapid difficult change contradicts lay beliefs most sharply) and attenuated when the change is easy. Because followers evaluate leaders on authenticity as well as responsiveness, rapid change produces offsetting effects that, on net, reduce followers' willingness to voice future concerns—generating a recursive dynamic in voice processes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to voice research by identifying the rate of leader response as a previously overlooked factor shaping leader evaluations, challenging the prevailing assumption that faster change is unambiguously better. It introduces perceived authenticity as a second, overlooked basis on which followers judge leader responses to voice (alongside utility and responsiveness) and shows how the interplay between authenticity and responsiveness, moderated by change difficulty, shapes cyclical voice dynamics. It also answers Cha and colleagues' (2019) call to address the temporal dimensions of perceived authenticity by theorizing that authenticity judgments depend on followers' comparison of leaders' past and present selves.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders who aim to demonstrate responsiveness by changing rapidly in response to employee voice should be aware that swift transformations can backfire by eliciting perceptions of inauthenticity, especially when the requested change is difficult, which can in turn discourage subsequent voice. The authors do not simply recommend slowing down, because rapid change also signals responsiveness; instead they suggest leaders weigh these trade-offs and can mitigate the authenticity penalty by proactively communicating about why a change is more or less challenging than followers assume.

**Limitations**
The studies are largely hypothetical vignette-based designs and focus narrowly on leader-centric voice (discretionary feedback about the leader's own behavior) rather than other forms of voice such as concerns about routine business operations. The research does not capture how followers perceive leader changes at the precise moment they occur in ongoing relationships, and it relies primarily on Western-oriented theory and samples, leaving cultural variation in conceptions of the "true self" and authenticity unaddressed.

**Future Research**
Future work could conduct longitudinal or experience-sampling investigations of ongoing leader-follower relationships to examine how change-rate evaluations evolve over multiple timepoints and whether sub-variants of change rates (e.g., constant vs. slow-start-then-flurry) moderate the effect. Researchers could also explore possible asymmetries between judgments of authenticity and inauthenticity, test whether different types of leader behaviors (e.g., interpersonal vs. task-oriented) strengthen or attenuate the authenticity penalty, and replicate the findings using non-Western theoretical perspectives and populations with different conceptions of self.
