# Not So Fast? Rapid Response to Voice Leads to Perceived Inauthenticity

**Abstract**
Employees voice their concerns in the hope that leaders will change their behavior. But how do employees evaluate these changes? In the present research, we highlight the rate of behavior change as a key factor in determining leader evaluations: Leaders are seen as less authentic when they change rapidly in response to employee voice because people believe that true change takes time. We investigate this claim with three studies that adopt a mixed-method approach (n 5 3,056). In Study 1, PhD students describe rapid improvement in their advisor’s behavior as less authentic than gradual improvement— even when these changes are desired. In Study 2, we employ a stimulus sampling design to test the robustness of this effect. Followers see rapid improvement as less authentic than gradual improvement across a wide array of leader behaviors. In Study 3, we highlight change difficulty as a moderator and subsequent voice as a downstream consequence: When change is hard, rapid improvement elicits a greater authenticity penalty, which undermines followers’ willingness to voice their concerns in the future.

**Research Question**
When leaders change their behavior in response to leader-centric voice, how does the rate of that change (rapid vs. gradual) influence followers' perceptions of the leader's authenticity and, in turn, followers' willingness to express subsequent voice? The paper further asks whether the difficulty of the requested change moderates this authenticity penalty.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. Leaders who change their behavior rapidly in response to leader-centric voice are perceived as less authentic than leaders who change their behavior gradually.
H2. The negative effect of a rapid change on followers' willingness to express subsequent voice is mediated by perceived authenticity.
H3. The negative effect of a rapid change on perceived authenticity is mitigated when the behavior change is easy (versus difficult).
H4. The negative effect of a rapid change on followers' willingness to express subsequent voice is mitigated when the behavior change is easy (versus difficult).
H5. The negative effect of a rapid (versus gradual) change on followers' willingness to express subsequent voice when the change is difficult (versus easy) is mediated by lowered perceived authenticity.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Rate of behavior change in response to leader-centric voice (rapid vs. gradual), experimentally manipulated
- DV(s): Perceived leader authenticity; followers' willingness to express subsequent voice
- Mediators: Perceived authenticity (primary, preregistered); perceived responsiveness (exploratory parallel mediator, Study 3)
- Moderators: Change difficulty (easy vs. difficult, Study 3)

Followers hold lay beliefs that true, authentic change reflects a corresponding transformation of a leader's core sense of self and therefore requires substantial time and effort. When a leader changes rapidly in response to voice, this violates the lay expectation; drawing on attribution and person-perception logic, followers are prompted to seek an alternative explanation and infer that the swift change is a façade to appease them rather than a genuine transformation, so they judge the leader as less authentic. Because authenticity attributions shape observers' own behavior, this authenticity penalty reduces followers' willingness to voice future concerns. The penalty is sharper when the requested change is difficult (which more starkly contradicts the lay belief) and attenuates for easy changes, where a rapid response can instead raise perceived responsiveness.

**Data & Measures**
Rate of change (IV) was manipulated through vignettes across contexts: a doctoral advisor's behavior (Study 1), executive action plans written in response to 360-degree feedback and screened via stimulus sampling (Study 2), and a manager, "Mark" (Study 3). Change difficulty (moderator) was manipulated as easy vs. difficult in Study 3. Perceived authenticity (DV) was coded qualitatively in Study 1 by two hypothesis-blind coders on a tripartite scale (-1 = inauthenticity references, 0 = neutral/both/irrelevant, +1 = authenticity references), and measured in Studies 2-3 with a four-item scale adapted from Bailey and Levy (2022) on a 7-point scale (α = .95-.96). Willingness to express subsequent voice (DV, Study 3) used four items adapted from Liang et al. (2012), 7-point (α = .90); perceived responsiveness (exploratory mediator) used three items (α = .93). Analyses used ordered and multinomial logistic regression (Study 1), one-way and two-way ANOVA (Studies 2-3), and bootstrapped simple, mediated, and moderated-mediation models (5,000 resamples) estimated via structural equation models in Stata. Because rate and difficulty were randomized, their effects support causal interpretation; the authenticity-to-voice mediation rests on a measured mediator and is associational at that link.

**Key Findings**
All five hypotheses were supported, and the effect direction is consistent throughout: a faster rate of change lowers perceived authenticity. H1 was supported across studies — in Study 1, a faster (immediate) rate yielded more inauthenticity references (n = 15 vs. 2) and fewer authenticity references (n = 7 vs. 16), with a significant ordered logistic model, x2(1) = 12.88, p < .001 (log odds of authenticity references decreased by 1.37, p = .001); in Study 2, rapid changes were judged less authentic (M = 4.42) than gradual changes (M = 5.09), F(1, 1674) = 105.16, p < .001, robust across all eight stimuli. H3 was supported (Study 3): a significant rate × difficulty interaction on authenticity, F(1, 706) = 8.27, p = .004; when the change was difficult, rapid change was less authentic (M = 3.47) than gradual (M = 4.70), whereas when easy the gap attenuated (rapid M = 4.39 vs. gradual M = 5.06). H4 was supported: a significant interaction on willingness to voice, F(1, 706) = 4.52, p = .034; rapid change reduced subsequent voice under difficult change (M = 4.05 vs. 4.36) but not under easy change. H2 was supported by a significant negative indirect effect of rate on subsequent voice via perceived authenticity (b = -.31, 95% CI [-.42, -.23]). H5 was supported by significant moderated mediation (index b = -.19, p = .008): the negative indirect effect via authenticity was stronger for difficult (b = -.41) than easy (b = -.22) changes. Exploratorily, a rapid easy change also raised perceived responsiveness (a positive indirect path), which can offset the authenticity penalty on subsequent voice when change is easy; for difficult changes both authenticity and responsiveness paths were negative.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to voice research by identifying the rate of a leader's response — not merely whether the leader responds — as a determinant of leader evaluations, challenging the prevailing assumption that faster change is unambiguously better. It surfaces perceived authenticity as an overlooked consequence of how leaders respond to voice and theorizes a recursive dynamic in which authenticity attributions feed back into followers' willingness to voice. It also answers Cha et al.'s (2019) call to address the temporal dimension of authenticity, arguing that authenticity judgments hinge on followers' lay belief that genuine change takes time and effort.

**Practical Implication**
The authors deliberately refrain from simply urging leaders to slow down, because authenticity is only one dimension of leader evaluations: when a change is easy, rapid responders may be seen as less authentic but more responsive, letting employees feel seen and heard. They advise leaders to weigh this trade-off between perceived authenticity and perceived responsiveness when deciding how quickly to change in response to leader-centric voice. Drawing on King et al. (2019), they also suggest leaders may soften the authenticity penalty by proactively communicating how difficult a requested change actually is, shaping followers' expectations before enacting a rapid change.

**Limitations**
The studies are, for the most part, hypothetical vignette experiments; the authors note this and partly address it with a recall design (Study S3) but still do not observe change in real time or within an ongoing leader-follower relationship. In Studies 2 and 3 authenticity is captured with bipolar measures, leaving a possible asymmetry between authenticity and inauthenticity judgments (highlighted in Study 1) only partially examined. The work also does not account for cultural differences and relies primarily on Western-oriented theory and Western samples.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on whether differences in behavior type (e.g., interpersonal vs. task changes) correspond to differences in difficulty that strengthen or attenuate the authenticity penalty, and on weighted coding of authenticity-related keywords. They suggest longitudinal and experience-sampling designs to study the penalty across multiple timepoints and sub-variants of change rates (e.g., a constant rate vs. a slow start followed by a flurry), further examination of the asymmetry between authenticity and inauthenticity judgments, and replication using non-Western perspectives on authenticity and the self.
