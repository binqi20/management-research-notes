# Under Pressure to Be Perfect: How Dehumanizing and Rehumanizing Social Cues Lead to Maladaptive and Adaptive Perfectionism in Professional Ballet

**Abstract**
Research on workplace perfectionism has focused on harmful consequences of workers’ perfectionism. However, perfectionism is not a uniform experience. Maladaptive and adaptive perfectionism have different outcomes for worker performance and well-being. While perfectionism appears especially prevalent in high performance pressure environments, minimal research has examined how social cues shape workers’ experience of perfectionism. Through an inductive study of professional ballet dancers facing extreme performance pressure, we discovered that social cues influence whether people experience maladaptive or adaptive perfectionism. We found that organizational dehumanizing cues elicited perfectionistic concerns and increased self-destructive behaviors, leading to a vicious cycle of maladaptive perfectionism and an increased likelihood of breakdowns at work. However, rehumanizing cues precipitated a shift toward adaptive perfectionism characterized by perfectionistic strivings and self-compassionate behaviors. We also show how workers can shift back and forth between maladaptive and adaptive perfectionism cycles throughout their careers. We reveal new insights at the intersection of high performance pressure, dehumanization, and perfectionism, offering dehumanization and rehumanization as key mechanisms linking the environment and perfectionism. We also discuss how our findings, though in an extreme context, apply to other high-pressure workplace environments, and open new opportunities to explore the processes of maladaptive and adaptive perfectionism in other domains.

**Research Question**
How do social cues within high-pressure work environments influence the experience of different forms of perfectionism?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Organizational dehumanizing social cues (unwillingness to accept workers' mistakes; constraining workers' power, control, and voice; neglecting workers' emotional and physical pain; encouraging conformity and replaceability) and rehumanizing social cues (supporting workers in accepting mistakes; encouraging voicing of concerns; validating emotional and physical pain; affirming personal value and worth), set against a constant backdrop of high performance pressure.
- DV(s): Experience of maladaptive perfectionism (perfectionistic concerns plus self-destructive behaviors) vs. adaptive perfectionism (perfectionistic strivings plus self-compassionate behaviors); downstream breakdowns and exit from or persistence in the profession.
- Mediators: Internalization of dehumanizing vs. rehumanizing cues (denial vs. reaffirmation of fallibility, agency, subjectivity, and individuality); breakdowns that increase receptivity to rehumanizing cues; a "learned response" path that sustains adaptive perfectionism.
- Moderators: Workers' receptivity to social cues, which shifts after breakdowns; idiosyncratic features of the context (embodied performance, female-dominated sample) that amplify dehumanization salience.

Drawing on social information processing theory (Salancik & Pfeffer, 1978), the authors theorize that, under high performance pressure, organizational cues that deny workers' fallibility, agency, subjectivity, and individuality are internalized as organizational dehumanization, activating perfectionistic concerns and self-destructive behaviors in a self-reinforcing maladaptive cycle that culminates in breakdowns. Breakdowns open workers to rehumanizing cues from peers and supportive others, which reaffirm their humanity and facilitate internal shifts toward perfectionistic strivings paired with self-compassionate behaviors, constituting an adaptive perfectionism cycle. Because dehumanizing cues persist in the environment, dancers can relapse, but accumulated experience helps them stay adaptive more of the time. Dehumanization and rehumanization thus function as contextual mechanisms that translate the same performance pressure into qualitatively different perfectionism experiences.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study identifies organizational dehumanization and rehumanization as underlying contextual mechanisms that separate maladaptive from adaptive perfectionism, shifting the perfectionism literature from a predominantly trait-based view toward a situated, social-information-processing account of how workplace cues activate different forms of perfectionism. It contributes to the dehumanization literature by proposing denial of fallibility as a novel form of dehumanization alongside denial of agency, subjectivity, and individuality, and by showing how workers can internalize dehumanizing cues into self-destructive behavior rather than only rejecting them. It also advances theory on performance pressure by positioning dehumanization (vs. rehumanization) as a key moderator that channels pressure into maladaptive versus adaptive responses and by articulating perfectionism as a dynamic, cyclical process fueled by everyday social cues.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders and practitioners in high-pressure workplaces should recognize that perfectionism is shaped by environmental cues, not merely individual disposition, and take responsibility for affirming workers' fallibility, agency, voice, and individuality. Training managers and peers to detect signs of maladaptive perfectionism (e.g., harsh self-criticism, self-harming behaviors) and to deliver rehumanizing cues such as supportive remarks, constructive feedback, and validation of emotional and physical limits can help workers shift toward adaptive perfectionism. The findings are especially relevant for entry-level professionals in competitive industries such as the arts, academia, healthcare, law, elite sport, and tech, where "produce or be replaced" norms cue maladaptive patterns.

**Limitations**
The study may not be readily transferable to all competitive organizational settings: the sample is 76-78% female, which may inflate the salience of dehumanization given gendered dynamics of replaceability and objectification, and professional ballet uniquely emphasizes the physical body, so perfectionism is more embodied than in most workplaces. The inductive qualitative design is not intended to isolate causal effects of dehumanization or rehumanization on perfectionism, and qualitative interviews are subject to biased recall and self-protective framing, making it hard to know whether dancers truly shifted to adaptive perfectionism or reframed experiences more positively.

**Future Research**
Future deductive and experimental studies should test the moderating effects of dehumanization and rehumanization on perfectionism while controlling for trait perfectionism, conscientiousness, and gender, and should explore gendered dynamics in how dehumanization and perfectionism co-develop. Scholars should extend the model to less physically demanding work, investigate other maladaptive responses (e.g., unethical behavior) triggered by internalized dehumanization, and examine self-compassion as an environmentally induced response rather than a purely individual trait. Additional work is encouraged on organizational-level outcomes such as retention and performance tied to adaptive vs. maladaptive perfectionism, and on the nature of performance pressure (including time-vs-precision tensions) across competitive industries.
