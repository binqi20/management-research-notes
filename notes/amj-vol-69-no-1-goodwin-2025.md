# Under Pressure to Be Perfect: How Dehumanizing and Rehumanizing Social Cues Lead to Maladaptive and Adaptive Perfectionism in Professional Ballet

**Abstract**
Research on workplace perfectionism has focused on harmful consequences of workers' perfectionism. However, perfectionism is not a uniform experience. Maladaptive and adaptive perfectionism have different outcomes for worker performance and well-being. While perfectionism appears especially prevalent in high performance pressure environments, minimal research has examined how social cues shape workers' experience of perfectionism. Through an inductive study of professional ballet dancers facing extreme performance pressure, we discovered that social cues influence whether people experience maladaptive or adaptive perfectionism. We found that organizational dehumanizing cues elicited perfectionistic concerns and increased self-destructive behaviors, leading to a vicious cycle of maladaptive perfectionism and an increased likelihood of breakdowns at work. However, rehumanizing cues precipitated a shift toward adaptive perfectionism characterized by perfectionistic strivings and self-compassionate behaviors. We also show how workers can shift back and forth between maladaptive and adaptive perfectionism cycles throughout their careers. We reveal new insights at the intersection of high performance pressure, dehumanization, and perfectionism, offering dehumanization and rehumanization as key mechanisms linking the environment and perfectionism. We also discuss how our findings, though in an extreme context, apply to other high-pressure workplace environments, and open new opportunities to explore the processes of maladaptive and adaptive perfectionism in other domains.

**Research Question**
How do social cues within high-pressure work environments influence workers' experience of different (maladaptive vs. adaptive) forms of perfectionism? The study asks what contextual factors stimulate one type of perfectionism rather than the other.

**Mechanism Process**
- Performance pressure: a constant, high-stakes condition of the ballet profession that primes perfectionism.
- Dehumanizing cues (organization-signaled): denial of fallibility, denial of agency, denial of subjectivity, denial of individuality.
- Maladaptive perfectionism cycle: perfectionistic concerns and self-destructive behaviors mutually reinforcing, tending toward breakdown or exit.
- Breakdown: a physical/mental turning point that raises receptivity to rehumanizing cues.
- Rehumanizing cues (individual/small-group-signaled): reaffirming fallibility, agency, subjectivity, individuality.
- Adaptive perfectionism cycle: perfectionistic strivings and self-compassionate behaviors mutually reinforcing.
- Relapse / learned response: iterative shifting between the two cycles across a career.

Drawing on social information processing theory, the paper theorizes that dancers internalize social cues about their worth and humanity. When the organization signals that human error, autonomy, feeling, and individuality are unacceptable, dancers internalize dehumanization and slip into a self-reinforcing maladaptive cycle. When individuals or peers signal that mistakes, voice, pain, and uniqueness are legitimate, dancers rehumanize and enter an adaptive cycle. Because performance pressure and dehumanizing cues persist, the two cycles are dynamic and reversible rather than terminal states.

**Data & Measures**
Data corpus: 61 semi-structured interviews (average 52 minutes) with professional/semi-professional ballet dancers from 24 companies; 10 follow-up interviews to trace shifts through the emergent model; 5 interviews with industry leaders used only as a credibility check; ~21 hours of nonparticipant observation of classes, rehearsals, and facilities; and autoethnographic insight from an insider-outsider team. Analysis followed grounded-theory principles under a theory-elaboration and problematization approach: line-by-line open coding in NVivo (staying close to informants' own terms), axial coding into second-order themes (e.g., perfectionistic concerns, self-destructive behaviors), and theoretical coding into aggregate dimensions and a process model (Figures 1-2). The design is interpretive; the authors state it is not intended to isolate causal effects.

**Key Findings**
Organizational dehumanizing cues, internalized under performance pressure, elicited perfectionistic concerns and increased self-destructive behaviors (e.g., pushing through injury, eating disorders, substance abuse), forming a vicious maladaptive perfectionism cycle that often culminated in breakdown or exit. Rehumanizing cues that reaffirmed dancers' fallibility, agency, subjectivity, and individuality precipitated a shift toward adaptive perfectionism, marked by perfectionistic strivings and self-compassionate behaviors (boundaries, self-care, self-forgiveness). Dancers moved back and forth between the two cycles over their careers, with relapse always possible; relapses became less frequent, and breakdown less necessary, as dancers learned to resist internalizing dehumanizing cues. The study also surfaces a previously unnamed form of dehumanization — denial of fallibility — alongside the established denials of agency, subjectivity, and individuality.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper identifies organizational dehumanization and rehumanization as contextual mechanisms that separate maladaptive from adaptive perfectionism, responding to calls to examine the antecedents and mechanisms of workplace perfectionism. It offers a cyclical, iterative view in which each type of perfectionism is a mutually reinforcing loop of cognitions and behaviors, and — via social information processing theory — challenges the assumption that perfectionism is primarily a stable trait by showing how everyday social cues shape workers' shifting between types. It contributes to the dehumanization literature by proposing the denial of fallibility as a new form of dehumanization, showing that people can internalize dehumanizing cues in self-destructive ways, and positioning a dehumanizing environment as a moderator of whether performance pressure yields adaptive or maladaptive responses.

**Practical Implication**
For leaders, managers, HR professionals, and mentors in high-pressure organizations, the authors argue that person-centered perfectionism interventions are insufficient because they neglect the dehumanizing context; organizations should proactively affirm workers' humanity and avoid sending dehumanizing signals, especially to entry-level professionals seeking to prove themselves. Recommended managerial practices tied to the four forms of dehumanization include: objective, attainable metrics that leave room for making and reporting mistakes; leaders sharing (rather than hiding) weakness; structuring roles to support subordinate agency (e.g., anonymous reporting, active listening); prioritizing well-being through regular breaks and support resources; and aligning work with individual expression and recognition. The authors frame attenuating dehumanization as a moral obligation while maintaining high performance expectations.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that the sample has idiosyncratic features that raise transferability questions: 78% of interviewees were women, which may heighten the salience of replaceability and status denial, and ballet's extreme reliance on the physical body makes its perfectionism more body-focused than in most workplaces. The inductive design is not intended to isolate the causal effects of de- and rehumanization on perfectionism. As with qualitative data generally, there are concerns of biased recall and the possibility that dancers framed their experiences more positively to protect their self-concept.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for research on gendered dynamics of dehumanization and perfectionism; on embodiment and how perfectionism is experienced in the body, including in less physically demanding work; and for deductive/experimental tests (controlling individual differences such as trait perfectionism, conscientiousness, and gender) of the moderating effects of de- and rehumanization. It also invites study of other maladaptive reactions triggered by internalizing dehumanization (e.g., unethical behavior, and the dehumanization-to-unethical-behavior direction), self-compassion as a response to environmentally induced perfectionism and which strategies are most effective, the effects of adaptive vs. maladaptive perfectionism on retention and performance and the organizational costs and benefits of rehumanizing, the nature and degree of performance pressure across competitive industries, and how leaders and institutions dehumanize workers and whether rehumanization fixes or merely perpetuates the harm.
