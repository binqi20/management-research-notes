# The Dualities of Multiple Team Membership: Implications for Team Performance and Adaptability

**Abstract**
Researchers recognize that multiple team membership (MTM) represents a double-edged sword for teams, but the precise mechanisms that explain the countervailing effects and contingencies that modulate these relationships remain underexplored. We argue that team-level MTM allows team members to access heterogeneous external resources through more diverse external-network ties, benefiting team performance. In contrast, we advance ideas that team-level MTM can negatively impact team adaptability by diminishing the heterogeneity of team members' external roles. To reconcile these divergent arguments, we introduce an informational perspective to suggest that teams can strengthen the benefits and mitigate the downsides of MTM when they have high levels of internal informational diversity. Using data from financial forecast reports published by 473 analyst teams in J. P. Morgan from 2011 to 2019, we find support for our integrated theoretical framework. MTM indeed appears to be beneficial for team performance through increased external network heterogeneity, but detrimental for team adaptability through reduced external role heterogeneity. Teams' internal informational diversity was furthermore found to accentuate these indirect benefits of MTM for team performance, while simultaneously attenuating its indirect detriments for team adaptability.

**Research Question**
Through what countervailing mediating mechanisms, and under what team-internal contingency, does team-level multiple team membership (MTM) simultaneously benefit team performance and undermine team adaptability? The paper asks why and when team-level MTM positively or negatively affects distinct dimensions of team effectiveness.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. External network heterogeneity mediates the positive relationship between MTM and team performance.
H2. External role heterogeneity mediates the negative relationship between MTM and team adaptability.
H3. Internal informational diversity moderates the indirect relationship between MTM and team performance (mediated by external network heterogeneity); this positive indirect relationship is stronger when internal informational diversity is high.
H4. Internal informational diversity moderates the indirect relationship between MTM and team adaptability (mediated by external role heterogeneity); this negative indirect relationship is weaker when internal informational diversity is high.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV: Team-level MTM (average proportion of members' time and effort devoted to other teams, scaled 0-1).
- DVs: Team performance (forecast accuracy) and team adaptability (timely report updates).
- Mediators: External network heterogeneity (positive pathway to performance); external role heterogeneity (pathway to adaptability).
- Moderator: Internal informational diversity (variety of members' knowledge bases and perspectives).

Drawing on the social network perspective, higher team-level MTM gives members access to more diverse, nonredundant external resources (higher external network heterogeneity), which improves team performance. Drawing on the attention-based perspective, higher MTM pushes members to adopt focused, more homogeneous external roles to manage switching costs (lower external role heterogeneity), which impairs the team's capacity to deviate from routines and adapt its output. The team information processing framework identifies internal informational diversity as the lever that lets teams combine and elaborate member inputs, thereby amplifying the beneficial network pathway to performance and buffering (compensating for) the detrimental role pathway to adaptability.

**Data & Measures**
Archival authorship data from J. P. Morgan's U.S. firm research report summary files (ProQuest, 2011-2019), linked to I/B/E/S detailed one-year-ahead EPS forecasts. MTM: ratio of reports members authored in external teams to total reports (inside plus outside), aggregated to the team. External network heterogeneity: 1 minus the average Hoberg-Phillips (HP) product cosine similarity across the firms covered by external members. External role heterogeneity: Blau's (1977) index of members' authorship-order (role) distribution outside the team, aggregated following Bunderson and Sutcliffe (2002). Internal informational diversity: mean HP similarity of firms studied by the team's own members. Team performance: forecast accuracy (inverse of absolute forecast error). Team adaptability: number of report updates issued within one month after the focal forecast. Estimation is associational (unconflated 1-1-1 multilevel models); the study makes no causal-identification claim.

**Key Findings**
All four hypotheses were supported. H1: MTM is positively related to external network heterogeneity (B = 27.52, p < .01) and external network heterogeneity is positively related to team performance (B = 1.98, p < .01); the indirect effect of MTM on team performance is positive, 0.53, 95% CI [0.30, 0.75] (the direct MTM-performance effect was non-significant, B = 1.22, p = .06). H2: MTM is negatively related to external role heterogeneity (B = -33.61, p < .01), which is in turn positively related to team adaptability (B = 22.56, p < .01); the indirect effect of MTM on team adaptability is negative, -10.19, 95% CI [-11.56, -8.81]. H3: the external-network-heterogeneity x internal-informational-diversity interaction on performance is positive (B = 11.11, p = .02); the positive indirect relationship is significant only when internal informational diversity is high (+1 SD = 0.81, 95% CI [0.46, 1.15]) and non-significant when low (-1 SD = 0.14, 95% CI [-0.22, 0.50]). H4: the external-role-heterogeneity x internal-informational-diversity interaction on adaptability is negative (B = -106.69, p < .01); the negative indirect relationship is attenuated (weaker) when internal informational diversity is high (+1 SD = -2.21, 95% CI [-4.06, -0.37]) and accentuated when low (-1 SD = -9.46, 95% CI [-11.40, -7.52]).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study integrates the social network and attention-based perspectives through the team information processing framework to explain the countervailing team-level effects of MTM, identifying external network heterogeneity and external role heterogeneity as previously unexplored mediating mechanisms and distinguishing team performance from team adaptability as effectiveness dimensions affected in opposite directions. It positions internal informational diversity as a shared moderator that governs whether teams can leverage these external-diversity mechanisms. In doing so it extends O'Leary et al.'s (2011) seminal MTM model and contributes an open-systems reading of the categorization-elaboration model, the team boundary-spanning literature, and the diversity-bandwidth trade-off by jointly considering teams' internal and external forms of diversity.

**Practical Implication**
For team leaders, the identified mediators offer intermediate outcomes to monitor and target: leaders can gauge whether members' MTM yields a broad set of external contacts (external network heterogeneity) and whether members vary their external role assignments (external role heterogeneity), and can use levers such as role-rotation schemes and network-aware team composition. For team members, the findings help anticipate the benefits and costs of additional parallel team engagements, including weighing the internal diversity and similarity of teams they contemplate joining. For higher-level administrators, the results inform when to deploy MTM structures: high-performance, knowledge-intensive projects can benefit from MTM, whereas projects requiring rapid adaptation may need alternative or hybrid structures, and continued investment in MTM is more justified when internal informational diversity offsets its adaptability costs.

**Limitations**
The model was tested in a single organization of comparable financial analyst teams, and the competitive industry limited cross-firm interaction, so generalizability to other industries and regions requires replication. The archival design relies on proxies (authorship order for members' roles, and covered firms for external network and role heterogeneity). The conceptualization of team adaptability is relatively broad. Contrary to expectation, team adaptability and team performance were significantly negatively related, so the two effectiveness dimensions may not be fully independent.

**Future Research**
The authors call for replication in other industries (e.g., manufacturing) and regions (e.g., Africa, Europe, Asia) and for alternative operationalizations of the network and role mechanisms. They suggest examining why team performance and adaptability are negatively related (e.g., whether decision-making biases such as confirmation bias impede adaptation) and integrating structural adaptation theory to study which types of team adaptation MTM hinders or enables. Further directions include clarifying the role of autonomy in MTM engagements and identifying alternative mechanisms (e.g., supervisory or organization-level design) behind reduced external role heterogeneity, extending the model to other effectiveness dimensions such as team viability and turnover, and differentiating task-related from demographic diversity as well as short- versus long-term implications.
