# The Dualities of Multiple Team Membership: Implications for Team Performance and Adaptability

**Abstract**
Researchers recognize that multiple team membership (MTM) represents a doubleedged sword for teams, but the precise mechanisms that explain the countervailing effects and contingencies that modulate these relationships remain underexplored. We argue that team-level MTM allows team members to access heterogeneous external resources through more diverse external-network ties, benefiting team performance. In contrast, we advance ideas that team-level MTM can negatively impact team adaptability by diminishing the heterogeneity of team members’ external roles. To reconcile these divergent arguments, we introduce an informational perspective to suggest that teams can strengthen the benefits and mitigate the downsides of MTM when they have high levels of internal informational diversity. Using data from financial forecast reports published by 473 analyst teams in J. P. Morgan from 2011 to 2019, we find support for our integrated theoretical framework. MTM indeed appears to be beneficial for team performance through increased external network heterogeneity, but detrimental for team adaptability through reduced external role heterogeneity. Teams’ internal informational diversity was furthermore found to accentuate these indirect benefits of MTM for team performance, while simultaneously attenuating its indirect detriments for team adaptability.

**Research Question**
Why and when does team-level multiple team membership (MTM) positively or negatively impact team effectiveness? Specifically, through which mediating mechanisms does team-level MTM simultaneously benefit team performance and undermine team adaptability, and how does teams' internal informational diversity modulate these indirect relationships?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Team-level multiple team membership (MTM; average proportion of team members' time and effort devoted to other teams)
- DV(s): Team performance (forecast accuracy); team adaptability (number of forecast updates within one month of focal report)
- Mediators: External network heterogeneity (diversity of external network ties accessed through parallel team affiliations); external role heterogeneity (variety of roles team members undertake across their external teams)
- Moderators: Internal informational diversity (variety of knowledge bases and perspectives held by focal team members)

Drawing on the social network perspective, higher team-level MTM expands team members' connections to nonredundant external contacts with varied expertise, increasing external network heterogeneity and providing resources that enable efficient, high-quality task execution (i.e., team performance). Drawing on the attention-based view, higher MTM simultaneously induces switching costs that lead members to adopt focused coping strategies and converge on similar external roles, reducing external role heterogeneity and thereby limiting the broad cognitive constructs needed for team adaptability. Integrating these via the team information processing framework, internal informational diversity enables members to combine and integrate externally accessed knowledge (accentuating the performance benefits) and to compensate for missing heterogeneous role experiences through interpersonal elaboration (attenuating the adaptability costs).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reconciles divergent findings in MTM research by identifying two countervailing mediating mechanisms (external network heterogeneity and external role heterogeneity) that jointly explain MTM's curvilinear team-level effects, and by distinguishing team performance from team adaptability as two effectiveness dimensions differentially affected by these mechanisms. It integrates the social network and attention-based perspectives through the team information processing framework, positioning internal informational diversity as a common boundary condition. In doing so, it extends seminal MTM theory (O'Leary et al., 2011), the categorization-elaboration model, team boundary-spanning research, and the diversity-bandwidth trade-off literature by adopting an open-systems view of internal and external team diversity.

**Practical Implication**
Team leaders can monitor external network and role heterogeneity as intermediate outcomes of MTM and intervene (e.g., through role rotation schemes and deliberate composition of external ties) to preserve performance benefits while protecting adaptability. Team members can anticipate the consequences of joining additional parallel teams based on the composition of those teams, and higher-level administrators can decide when to deploy MTM structures: favoring MTM for high-performance, knowledge-intensive projects while considering alternative or hybrid structures when rapid adaptation is essential, especially by building internally informationally diverse teams.

**Limitations**
The model was tested in a single organization (J. P. Morgan) with comparable financial analyst teams, limiting generalizability to other industries and geographies. The study relies on archival proxies (authorship orders for roles; Hoberg-Phillips similarity for network/role/information diversity), which may not fully capture the underlying constructs. Team adaptability was conceptualized broadly as the rate of forecast updates, without distinguishing types of adaptation, and team performance and adaptability showed an unexpected negative relationship warranting further investigation.

**Future Research**
Future work should conduct replication studies in other industries (e.g., manufacturing) and regions (e.g., Africa, Europe, Asia), operationalize external network and role mechanisms in alternative ways, and integrate structural adaptation theory to examine which types of adaptations are hindered or enabled by MTM. Scholars should extend the model to team viability and turnover, investigate whether autonomy over role choice shapes MTM effects in contexts where roles are organizationally assigned, and differentiate task-related from demographic diversity in moderating MTM's team-level consequences.
