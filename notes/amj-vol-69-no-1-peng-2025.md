# When Dirty Work Inspires: The Joint Effect of Work Dirtiness and Perceived Stigma on Pro-Organizational Behavior

**Abstract**
Research reveals that many employees derive esteem and pride from their work despite indignities attributed to their occupations. Whereas studies have identified what these “dirty workers” think and do to maintain a positive work identity, it remains unclear why they do so whereas others tend to disengage from work. We draw from self-categorization theory to develop a model that conceptualizes how the salience of one’s social identity is determined jointly by engaging in dirty tasks (i.e., work dirtiness) and perceived occupational stigma. When perceiving that others in society stigmatize one’s occupation, high work dirtiness creates a match between one’s behavior and societal expectations linked to one’s job. Based on this mechanism of normative fit, work-related social identity is salient, encouraging pro-organizational behavior. Conversely, we argue that when stigma is perceived as low, individuals experiencing high dirtiness at work become more concerned about their personal identity, reducing engagement with their organization. Results from four studies using survey and experimental designs largely support our hypotheses. This research reconciles seemingly contradictory results concerning employee responses toward dirty work and provides a theoretical explanation for why people with dirty jobs engage positively with their work and organizations.

**Research Question**
Why do many individuals performing dirty work engage positively with their jobs and organizations whereas others become disengaged? The paper asks how the experience of performing dirty tasks (work dirtiness) and the perception of occupational stigma jointly determine which identity process, and thus which behavioral response, is triggered.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
Hypothesis 1. Perceived occupational stigma moderates the relationship between work dirtiness and OCB; the relationship is positive when perceived stigma is high and negative when perceived stigma is low.
Hypothesis 2. Organizational identification mediates the interaction effect of perceived occupational stigma with work dirtiness on OCB; the indirect effect of work dirtiness on OCB through organizational identification is positive when stigma perception is high and negative when stigma perception is low.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Work dirtiness (physical, social, and moral dirtiness modeled as a higher-order construct; manipulated high vs. low in Study 3)
- DV(s): Pro-organizational behavior, operationalized as organizational citizenship behavior — toward coworkers (OCB-I), the organization (OCB-O), and customers/patients (OCB-C)
- Mediators: Organizational identification
- Moderators: Perceived occupational stigma

Drawing on self-categorization theory, the authors argue that a work-related social identity becomes salient only when a social category is both accessible and matches social reality. High perceived occupational stigma makes the occupational/organizational category accessible; high work dirtiness then supplies normative fit (behavior matches the negative societal stereotype), activating the social identity and channeling attention toward supporting the organization. When perceived stigma is low, work dirtiness lacks normative fit, so personal identity becomes salient, dirty tasks are appraised as a threat to self-worth, and employees psychologically disengage. Organizational identification is the proximal expression of a salient social identity that carries the interaction into behavior.

**Data & Measures**
Work dirtiness was measured with the 12-item scale (three sub-dimensions) of Schaubroeck et al. (2018); Study 3 instead manipulated moral and social dirtiness through a personal-trainer scenario (with a six-item manipulation check), and Study 4 used a nine-item daily version. Perceived occupational stigma used Schaubroeck et al.'s (2018) five-item scale. The mediator, organizational identification, used Mael and Ashforth's (1992) six-item scale (four items in Study 4's daily measure). Pro-organizational behavior used established OCB scales: OCB-I (Podsakoff et al., 1990; Lee & Allen, 2002), OCB-C (Amarnani et al., 2022, reworded to "patients"), and OCB-O (Lee & Allen, 2002). Identification comes from Study 3's experimental manipulation of work dirtiness (causal), whereas Studies 1, 2, and 4 are associational surveys that mitigate common-method bias via cluster-robust errors, temporal separation of waves, and daily experience sampling rather than through causal identification. Interactions were probed with the Johnson-Neyman technique.

**Key Findings**
The work dirtiness × perceived stigma interaction was positive and significant in every study (Study 1: b = .16 on OCB-I, b = .30 on OCB-C; Study 2: b = .10 on OCB-I, b = .24 on OCB-O; Study 3: b = .62 on OCB-O, OCB-I nonsignificant; Study 4 between-person: b = .25 on OCB-I, b = .19 on OCB-O). Consistent with H1, work dirtiness had a positive effect on OCB when perceived stigma was sufficiently high and a negative effect when stigma was low, but support was asymmetric: the positive high-stigma slope was clearest in Studies 1-2, while the negative low-stigma slope was clearest in Studies 3-4 (whose samples skewed toward low stigma). H1 was therefore supported for OCB-C/OCB-O across studies (in Study 4 chiefly for the negative low-stigma half, with less support for the high-stigma part of the interaction) but only partially for OCB-I (the predicted negative low-stigma effect on OCB-I was often nonsignificant). H2 (moderated mediation through organizational identification) was partially supported in Studies 2-4: identification carried the interaction, the negative indirect effect under low stigma was significant in Studies 3-4 but not Study 2, whereas the positive indirect effect under high stigma reached significance only in Study 2. Overall the four studies "largely support" the hypotheses. A supplementary analysis showed the interaction also predicted organizational identity salience, and excluding very-low-stigma participants in Study 4 strengthened the predicted effects.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper offers a self-categorization-theory explanation of why incumbents of dirty jobs foster a positive social identity and act pro-organizationally, moving beyond prior work that described what workers think and do after their social identity is presumed already salient to specify when that salience arises — namely through normative fit created by the joint presence of work dirtiness and perceived stigma. It establishes that perceived occupational stigma (a meta-perception about society's view of one's job) is conceptually and empirically distinct from work dirtiness (performing degrading tasks) and that the two operate in concert, reconciling seemingly contradictory prior findings. It also extends the literature's focus from occupational identity to organizational identity and identification as the proximal, tangible social category among the general work population.

**Practical Implication**
For managers, the study implies that symbolic management aimed at reframing tainted work will be more effective when managers also make employees aware of the societal stigma attached to their jobs, because such awareness supplies the normative fit that activates a supportive social identity. Managers are advised to attend especially to employees who do not hold strong occupational stigma beliefs, since these workers are more likely to construe work dirtiness as a threat to personal identity and to withdraw; maintaining regular communication that frames dirty tasks as contributing to the organization and society, and as a marker of professionalism, can help sustain their engagement.

**Limitations**
Three of the four studies relied on self-reports, raising common-method-bias concerns; the authors mitigated this through temporal separation (Study 2), manipulation of work dirtiness (Study 3), and repeated daily measures (Study 4), and note that interaction effects are less susceptible to such bias. The studies were run only in the United States and China, so cultural differences may shape how workers attend to the two aspects of dirty work. Occupational stigma was conceptualized and measured at the individual level, leaving group-shared stigma unmodeled, and the theory and tests focused on between-person processes.

**Future Research**
The authors call for tests using external (non-self-report) ratings of pro-organizational behavior and for examining whether a group's shared perception of stigma has additional impact on the identity processes. They suggest studying incongruent combinations of the two constructs (e.g., tainted occupations performing ordinary tasks, or high work dirtiness in low-stigma occupations), exploring how other forms of societal stigma (such as discrimination or social exclusion) communicate identity information, considering comparative fit (alongside normative fit) as a route to stigmatized-identity salience, and investigating crucial within-person moments that temporarily heighten work dirtiness and activate social identity.
