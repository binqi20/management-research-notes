# When Dirty Work Inspires: The Joint Effect of Work Dirtiness and Perceived Stigma on Pro-Organizational Behavior

**Abstract**
Research reveals that many employees derive esteem and pride from their work despite indignities attributed to their occupations. Whereas studies have identified what these “dirty workers” think and do to maintain a positive work identity, it remains unclear why they do so whereas others tend to disengage from work. We draw from selfcategorization theory to develop a model that conceptualizes how the salience of one’s social identity is determined jointly by engaging in dirty tasks (i.e., work dirtiness) and perceived occupational stigma. When perceiving that others in society stigmatize one’s occupation, high work dirtiness creates a match between one’s behavior and societal expectations linked to one’s job. Based on this mechanism of normative fit, work-related social identity is salient, encouraging pro-organizational behavior. Conversely, we argue that when stigma is perceived as low, individuals experiencing high dirtiness at work become more concerned about their personal identity, reducing engagement with their organization. Results from four studies using survey and experimental designs largely support our hypotheses. This research reconciles seemingly contradictory results concerning employee responses toward dirty work and provides a theoretical explanation for why people with dirty jobs engage positively with their work and organizations.

**Research Question**
Why do many individuals performing dirty work engage positively with their jobs and organizations whereas others become disengaged? Specifically, how do the experience of performing dirty tasks (work dirtiness) and the perception of being stigmatized by the public (perceived occupational stigma) jointly shape pro-organizational behavior through processes of social-identity salience.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Work dirtiness (performing physically, socially, or morally degrading tasks); perceived occupational stigma
- DV(s): Pro-organizational behavior operationalized as organizational citizenship behaviors toward individuals (OCB-I), toward the organization (OCB-O), and toward customers/patients (OCB-C)
- Mediators: Organizational identification (reflecting salience of work-related social identity)
- Moderators: Perceived occupational stigma moderates the effect of work dirtiness on OCB and on organizational identification (moderated-mediation model)

Drawing on self-categorization theory, the authors argue that perceived occupational stigma renders a stigmatized work-related social category cognitively accessible, and that engaging in dirty tasks supplies the "normative fit" that activates this social identity by matching behavior to societal stereotypes about the job. When both work dirtiness and perceived stigma are high, the work-related social identity becomes salient, heightening organizational identification and motivating employees to support coworkers, customers, and the organization through OCBs. When perceived stigma is low, high work dirtiness no longer creates normative fit; instead, dirty tasks are appraised as a threat to personal identity, prompting employees to psychologically distance themselves from the organization and reducing pro-organizational behavior.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends dirty-work research by providing a theoretical account of why individuals engaged in dirty work develop a salient work-related social identity rather than merely describing the tactics dirty workers use once that identity is presumably already salient. It conceptually and empirically disentangles "work dirtiness" (the direct experience of degrading tasks) from "perceived occupational stigma" (beliefs about societal views of the occupation), showing that the two are distinct constructs that operate jointly via normative fit to determine identity salience and pro-organizational behavior. In doing so, the authors reconcile contradictory prior findings showing both positive and negative responses to dirty work and broaden attention from occupational identity to organizational identity as a locus of social categorization.

**Practical Implication**
Managers of jobs involving dirty tasks should recognize that employees who do not perceive strong occupational stigma are more likely to interpret work dirtiness as a personal-identity threat and disengage, so these employees — not only those who already hold strong stigma beliefs — warrant proactive communication framing dirty tasks as meaningful and professionally skilled work. Symbolic management efforts (stories, rituals, language, status symbols) are more effective when they explicitly acknowledge the unfavorable societal stereotypes about the job rather than ignoring them, because highlighting those stereotypes reinforces normative fit and strengthens the work-related social identity that drives pro-organizational behavior.

**Limitations**
Three of the four studies relied on self-reports, which may introduce common method bias, though the authors mitigated this through temporal separation, experimental manipulation, and daily repeated measures, and note that interaction effects cannot be explained by common method bias. Data come only from the United States and China, limiting generalizability to other cultural contexts, and occupational stigma was conceptualized and measured at the individual level rather than as a shared group perception. The theory and tests focused on between-person processes; the authors examined but did not find support for a within-person daily process linking fluctuations in work dirtiness to identity salience and OCB.

**Future Research**
Future work should test the theory using externally rated pro-organizational behavior, examine whether group-level shared perceptions of stigma add explanatory power beyond individual perceptions, and explore "incongruent" cases such as workers in tainted occupations performing ordinary tasks or workers in low-stigma occupations performing highly dirty tasks. Researchers could also investigate pivotal or crucial moments (e.g., a police officer using force) that temporarily heighten work dirtiness and activate social identity, and consider how comparative fit (alongside normative fit) and other forms of societal stigma (e.g., discrimination, social exclusion) guide identity processes and workplace behavior.
