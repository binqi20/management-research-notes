# On the Move: The Impact of Internal Mobility and Internal Comobility on Unit- and Organization-Level Outcomes

**Abstract**
Internal mobility is commonly used to reallocate talent within organizations. Despite its prevalence and potential implications for organizational performance, most prior work has focused on the individual-level outcomes associated with internal mobility. That is, there has been relatively limited theoretical and empirical attention to the mechanisms that connect internal mobility to unit- and organization-level outcomes, making it difficult to predict when or why internal mobility is beneficial to organizations. To address this oversight, we draw from human and social capital literatures to build theory to predict when and why internal mobility will have unit-level implications. We test this by examining a period of growth in a Fortune 500 company and evaluate gains and losses associated with moving employees within firms, including distinguishing between two types of internal movers—those who come from different units in the organization (solo movers) and those who come from the same unit in the organization (comobile movers). In doing so, we contribute to both internal mobility and strategic human capital resource literatures and provide actionable insights regarding when to source internal movers, and where from.

**Research Question**
Whether, when, and why internal mobility is beneficial or detrimental to unit- and organization-level outcomes: specifically, what the net gains and losses of internal transfers are for the units that receive movers and the units that supply them, and from where within the organization internal movers should be sourced.

**Hypotheses / Propositions**
H1. Transfers in are positively related to the performance of the unit they join.
H2. The positive effect of comobile transfers in on the performance of the unit they join is weaker than the positive effect of multisource transfers in.
H3. Transfers out are negatively related to the performance of the unit they leave.
H4. The negative effect of comobile transfers out on the performance of the unit they leave is stronger than the negative effect of multi-destination transfers out.
H5a. The positive effect of multisource transfers in on the performance of the unit they join is stronger in magnitude than the negative effect of multi-destination transfers out on the performance of the unit they leave.
H5b. The positive effect of comobile transfers in on the performance of the unit they join is weaker in magnitude than the negative effect of comobile transfers out on the performance of the unit they leave.
H6. The unit performance effects of transfers in are larger in magnitude in new units than in existing units.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Transfers in and transfers out; comobile transfers in (join a unit with at least one other person from their prior unit) vs. multisource transfers in (join with no one from their prior unit); comobile transfers out (leave together to the same unit) vs. multi-destination transfers out (leave for distinct units).
- DV(s): Unit (store) performance, operationalized as monthly retail sales.
- Mediators: Change in the quantity and quality of unit human capital resources (HCR) — the theorized channel, not directly measured.
- Moderators: Unit newness (new vs. established stores; H6).

Drawing on human capital resources (HCR) theory, the authors argue that internal mobility reallocates firm-specific human and social capital across units, so that adding internally sourced workers enhances a receiving unit's HCR and performance while transfers out dilute the supplying unit's HCR and depress its performance. Multisource transfers in add greater diversity of unit-specific knowledge, whereas comobile transfers in rely more on intact social ties whose redundant knowledge and potential fault lines limit their contribution. Because each transfer in is also a transfer out, the net organizational effect turns on whether gains to receiving units exceed losses to supplying units; these effects should be amplified in new units that have no established HCR, social structure, or path-dependent performance.

**Data & Measures**
Data are monthly personnel and sales records for 1,202 retail locations of a single Fortune 500 U.S. retail firm during a 2016–2018 growth period (884 established stores yielding 26,910 store-month observations; 259 new stores). DV: sales = monthly retail sales per store (for H6, Month 6 sales in new stores). IVs: transfers in and out measured as rolling six-month sums of monthly transfers; comobile transfers in/out as the rolling six-month sums of movers who entered/left with at least one other individual from the same prior/current store; multisource transfers in and multi-destination transfers out as movers who entered/left without any other individual from the same store; for new stores, "initial" versions were measured over the store's first three months. Covariates: new hires (rolling six-month sum of external hires), turnover (rolling six-month sum of employees leaving the organization), store size (monthly employee count), and, for new stores, initial budget sales. Estimation: a Hausman test favored fixed effects, so Hypotheses 1–5b used store and month fixed-effects models on established stores, while Hypothesis 6 used OLS regression without fixed effects on new stores (one observation per store). The design is observational/associational, identifying within-store variation rather than an experiment.

**Key Findings**
- H1 supported: transfers in are positively related to retail sales (b = 6,120.93; SE = 481.08; p < .001), about +$6,121 per transfer in over six months.
- H2 supported: multisource transfers in have a positive, significant effect (b = 8,895.77; SE = 588.84; p < .001) while comobile transfers in are non-significant (b = 274.67; SE = 863.48; p = .75); non-overlapping confidence intervals confirm comobile < multisource.
- H3 supported: transfers out are negatively related to retail sales (b = −5,334.26; SE = 626.22; p < .001), about −$5,334 per transfer out.
- H4 NOT supported: contrary to prediction, comobile transfers out are more weakly negative (b = −4,533.17; p < .05) than multi-destination transfers out (b = −5,268.99; p < .001).
- H5a supported: the positive effect of multisource transfers in (b = 8,895.77) exceeds in magnitude the negative effect of multi-destination transfers out (b = −5,268.99); the difference is significant (diff = 3,626.78; p < .001).
- H5b supported: comobile transfers in (b = 274.67, n.s.) are smaller in magnitude than comobile transfers out (b = −4,533.17, p < .05); the difference is significant (diff = 4,258.50; p < .05).
- H6 partially supported: transfers in are directionally more valuable in new stores (b = 9,272.07) than established stores (b = 6,120.93), but the overall difference is not significant (diff = 3,151.14; p = .08); H6 holds specifically for comobile transfers in, which are significantly more valuable in new stores (diff = 7,965.16; p < .001).
- Net organizational takeaway: reallocating established employees benefits the firm overall only when movers are drawn from a variety of (multiple) source units rather than moved as intact groups.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study recasts internal mobility as a mechanism for human capital resource (HCR) reallocation across the organization, expanding its theorized scope from individually relevant to organizationally impactful. It distinguishes unit-level attributes of internal mobility (comobile vs. multisource transfers in; comobile vs. multi-destination transfers out) and shows they operate through two distinct means: the diversity of unit-specific knowledge added or removed, versus the transfer of intact social structures. It is the first to theorize and quantify the net effects of internal mobility by simultaneously modeling gains to receiving units and losses to supplying units, thereby capturing organization-level impact, and it extends the macro literature on the reallocation of nonfinancial (human capital) resources.

**Practical Implication**
For managers making staffing and talent-reallocation decisions, the optimal outcome depends on where internal movers are sourced: firms should reallocate employees with firm-specific HCR from a variety of units, sourcing roughly one transfer each from as many units as can reasonably supply them, because transferring more than one worker from any given unit brings substantially less benefit and may be outweighed by disruption to the supplying unit. Because each transfer in is also a transfer out, leaders should weigh the gains to receiving units against the vacancies and HCR losses created in supplying units, taking an organization-level rather than single-unit perspective. The authors caution that this prescription rests on a single study and should be applied cautiously until other studies produce corroborating data.

**Limitations**
The setting is a single U.S. retail organization, so the authors cannot know how far the findings generalize to other industries or contexts. They do not directly test the theorized underlying mechanisms (the unit's social relationships and diversity of knowledge, i.e., changes in unit HCR), relying instead on transfer-type proxies. The measures capture the average number of workers with firm-specific HCR rather than the kind or quality of each mover's human capital. The number of comobile transfers out in the sample is small, limiting inference for that category.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on the boundary conditions of their theoretical conclusions and on the duration of transfer effects (how long, and with what intensity, effects on HCR persist). They encourage work assessing the kind and quality of employees' firm-specific human capital and more complex configurations of HCR (e.g., means, minima or maxima, variability, and fault lines), and untangling the distinct roles, processes, and value of different mover types (e.g., managers vs. individual contributors). They also encourage directly measuring the quality of transfer relationships and the diversity of knowledge structures to test the proposed mechanisms, and accounting for the causes of transfers into and out of units (e.g., employees being more or less attracted to units based on store performance).
