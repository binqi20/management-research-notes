# On the Move: The Impact of Internal Mobility and Internal Comobility on Unit- and Organization-Level Outcomes

**Abstract**
Internal mobility is commonly used to reallocate talent within organizations. Despite its prevalence and potential implications for organizational performance, most prior work has focused on the individual-level outcomes associated with internal mobility. That is, there has been relatively limited theoretical and empirical attention to the mechanisms that connect internal mobility to unitand organization-level outcomes, making it difficult to predict when or why internal mobility is beneficial to organizations. To address this oversight, we draw from human and social capital literatures to build theory to predict when and why internal mobility will have unit-level implications. We test this by examining a period of growth in a Fortune 500 company and evaluate gains and losses associated with moving employees within firms, including distinguishing between two types of internal movers—those who come from different units in the organization (solo movers) and those who come from the same unit in the organization (comobile movers). In doing so, we contribute to both internal mobility and strategic human capital resource literatures and provide actionable insights regarding when to source internal movers, and where from.

**Research Question**
Whether, when, and why does internal mobility (transfers into and out of a unit) affect unit- and organization-level performance, and how do the attributes of transfers—specifically comobile versus multisource transfers in, and comobile versus multi-destination transfers out—shape those effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Transfers in (rolling six-month count); transfers out; comobile transfers in; multisource transfers in; comobile transfers out; multi-destination transfers out; unit status (new vs. established store) as a contextual moderator.
- DV(s): Monthly retail sales of the store (unit performance); organization-level net effect computed by comparing gains in receiving units to losses in supplying units.
- Mediators: Theorized but not directly tested — changes in unit human capital resources (HCR) via transferred firm-specific knowledge and established social capital.
- Moderators: Whether the receiving unit is new (no pre-existing HCR) versus established (H6).

The authors integrate human capital resources (HCR) theory with the internal mobility literature to argue that internal transfers reconfigure a unit's HCR in two distinct ways: by importing/exporting firm-specific human capital (knowledge, skills, and abilities honed to the organization) and by moving intact social structures when employees travel together. Transfers in should raise receiving-unit performance because firm-specific human capital aligns decisions with organizational routines and raises productivity; multisource transfers in add breadth of unit-specific knowledge while retaining enough firm-wide overlap to communicate, yielding larger gains than comobile transfers in (whose value rests on pre-existing social ties that are redundant with firm-specific familiarity). Transfers out should depress supplying-unit performance by removing firm-specific HCR, and comobile transfers out should amplify that loss by dissolving intact social structures. These amplified effects should be stronger in new units, where path-dependent history and established routines do not yet buffer HCR change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reframes internal mobility from an individual-level phenomenon into a collective-level mechanism for HCR reallocation across units, introducing new constructs (comobile vs. multisource transfers in; comobile vs. multi-destination transfers out) and showing that their effects on unit and organizational outcomes diverge. By theorizing and testing the net organizational effect—gains in receiving units net of losses in supplying units—it answers Dalton's (1997) long-standing "domino effect" question and shows that reallocating firm-specific HCR is value-creating for the firm overall only when transfers in are broadly sourced. It also contributes to the macro resource-reallocation literature by treating human capital as a strategically allocated, nonfinancial resource distinct from financial capital.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should actively reallocate internal talent rather than minimize disruption, but they should source transfers from as many different units as possible rather than moving intact groups. In practical terms, sourcing one transfer each from several supplying units yields substantially larger net gains than moving a comobile cluster from a single unit; transferring more than one worker from any given supplying unit brings diminishing benefit and may be outweighed by the cost to that unit. The benefits are largest when populating new units or stores, where firm-specific knowledge from multisource movers has the strongest effect on early performance.

**Limitations**
The study uses data from a single U.S. Fortune 500 retail firm, so generalizability to other industries, countries, and organizational forms is untested. HCR is theorized as the underlying mechanism but is not measured directly — the authors do not observe the quality of transferred human capital, the content of relationships, or diversity of knowledge structures within units. All transferred employees are treated as equivalent even though they occupied different roles (e.g., managers vs. frontline workers), and the analyses cannot separate managerial from non-managerial transfer effects.

**Future Research**
Future research should test boundary conditions across industries and settings, examine the duration and decay of transfer effects on HCR over longer horizons, and directly measure the quality of transferred human capital, the content of pre-existing relationships, and within-unit knowledge diversity to test the proposed mechanism more cleanly. Scholars should also model the causes of transfer decisions (e.g., employee self-selection into higher-performing units), explore more complex HCR configurations such as fault lines and subgroup dynamics among transfers, and untangle role-specific effects (e.g., manager versus frontline transfers) on unit outcomes.
