# Anticipatory Impression Management for Potential Adverse Events: Positive Framing in the Wake of Short Seller Attacks on a Competitor

**Abstract**
Anticipatory impression management (AIM) provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the tactics organizations use to mitigate the potential adverse consequences of known upcoming events. We extend this theorizing to encompass potential upcoming events with known adverse consequences. We posit that a potential event is more likely to induce AIM to the extent that the firm is aware the event might occur, motivated to mitigate its potential consequences, and capable of engaging in tactics that could reduce the event’s likelihood. We test our theorizing in the scenario wherein a competitor has been attacked by a short seller, which raises the specter of a short seller attack on the focal firm. We contend that, following an attack on a competitor, chief executive officers use positive framing in earnings conference calls as an AIM tactic to deter short sellers. Examination of 1,989 firms with a direct competitor attacked by a short seller confirms our ideas. Our study thus extends the boundaries of theory on AIM and broadens research on short selling to encompass principles of attack prevention.

**Research Question**
Under what conditions do firms engage in anticipatory impression management (AIM) — specifically, CEO positive framing in earnings conference calls — following a short seller attack on a competitor, and how do awareness, motivation, and capability shape this response?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Short seller attack on a direct (TNIC-3) competitor (binary, four quarters before vs. four quarters after)
- DV(s): CEO positive framing in earnings conference calls (Loughran-McDonald positive minus negative words, scaled by speech length)
- Mediators: None formally modeled
- Moderators: Product-market similarity between focal and attacked firm (awareness); stock-price decline at the attacked firm (motivation, captured via cumulative abnormal returns); focal firm's prior stock-price performance (capability)

When a short seller attacks a competitor, focal-firm CEOs anticipate that they could be the next target and use positive language in earnings calls to persuade short sellers to look elsewhere. Drawing on Chen's (1996) awareness-motivation-capability logic, the authors argue the AIM response is amplified when the focal firm is more aware the threat applies to them (high product-market overlap with the attacked firm), more motivated to act (the attacked competitor suffered a severe stock-price decline), and more capable of credibly projecting positivity (strong recent performance makes the framing believable). Positive framing functions as a deterrent signal that raises short sellers' expected cost of attacking the focal firm.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study elaborates AIM theory by extending it from known upcoming events with uncertain consequences to potential upcoming events with known adverse consequences, shifting uncertainty from the response to the event itself. By importing Chen's awareness-motivation-capability framework, it specifies the boundary conditions under which anticipation of a potential adverse event triggers AIM and redirects AIM from an investor audience toward an adversarial audience (short sellers). Secondarily, it contributes to the short-selling literature by demonstrating a previously overlooked spillover effect of attacks on non-targeted competitors and showing that CEO language can operate as an ex ante attack-prevention tool.

**Practical Implication**
CEOs should treat a short seller attack on a competitor as a signal to proactively shape firm narrative in earnings calls, since positive framing is associated with reduced short interest and can deter attacks before they happen. Boards can encourage CEOs to use AIM selectively, drawing executives' attention to product-market similarity, the severity of the competitor's stock-price decline, and the firm's own credibility to perform the tactic. However, firms with weak recent performance should be cautious: overly positive language without supporting fundamentals is not credible and can backfire by raising investor expectations.

**Limitations**
The authors could not fully account for why particular firms are susceptible to short seller attacks, and their archival data cannot directly observe the CEO decision-making process that leads to AIM. The goodness-of-fit improvement of the full model over the baseline was only marginal, possibly reflecting nonlinearities or an excess of nonsignificant controls. The study also focuses on one class of potential adverse event (short seller attacks), leaving open how AIM operates against other anticipated threats.

**Future Research**
Scholars could test these ideas experimentally (e.g., policy-capturing studies with executives) to unpack decision-making; explore other forms of awareness such as shared buyers/suppliers or socially interconnected firms; integrate AIM with protection motivation theory, threat rigidity theory, signaling theory, and the attention-based view to build a multiplex theory of proactive responses; and examine other potential adverse events (hostile takeovers, market entry, labor disputes, regulatory changes) where CEO language may serve as a first line of defense.
