# More-than-capitalist economies: Insights from community supported agriculture, tiny houses and hitchhiking in Denmark

**Abstract**
Furthermore, more-than-capitalist economic activities in this landscape are explored. By zooming in on three cases, community supported agriculture, tiny houses and hitchhiking, I draw attention to diverse economies as a lens to reframe consumption-production relationships in a sufficiency-oriented direction. I contribute to this field by proposing three key dimensions in which more-than-capitalist actors share characteristics across food, housing and mobility: 1) spatial; 2) temporal; and 3) relational. Highlighting these, the paper calls for further research and attention to spatial proximity, long-term engagement and community-building in developing consumption-production relationships for a sustainable future.

**Research Question**
How can reading for economic difference across the domains of food, housing and mobility reveal the way more-than-capitalist economic activities can show a path towards more sustainable, sufficiency-oriented consumption-production relationships? Concretely: how do grassroots organizations offer alternative approaches to sustainable consumption that exist alongside, and challenge, dominant capitalist models?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: capitalocentrism vs. diverse / more-than-capitalist economies; efficiency-oriented (profit-driven) vs. sufficiency-oriented (collective) economic models; mainstream vs. grassroots actors
- Method-as-lens: "reading for economic difference" — making subordinated, less-visible economic activities visible and granting them independent agency (the iceberg metaphor)
- Cases (reading for difference across three domains): community supported agriculture (food), a tiny house project (housing), hitchhiking (mobility)
- Cross-cutting dimensions deduced from the cases: 1) spatial (local proximity, direct relationship to land/community over global supply-chain distance); 2) temporal (long-term, flexible engagement with time over immediate, predictable market exchange); 3) relational (face-to-face interaction, interdependency, trust and community over individualism and profit maximization)
- Boundary conditions: the Danish welfare-state context means these cases pursue ecological/relational aims rather than urgent responses to unmet needs (unlike U.S. solidarity economies); none of the cases is fully independent of capitalist structures (each still relies on money and resources)

The paper argues that economic systems are performative — how the economy is conceptualized and enacted shapes which sustainability strategies become possible. Efficiency-oriented sustainability reproduces profit-driven, market-based relationships, whereas sufficiency-oriented practices align with collective, more-than-capitalist forms of organizing. Using Gibson-Graham's diverse economies lens, the three grassroots cases are shown to share spatial, temporal and relational characteristics that decommodify consumption-production relationships and foster ethical and ecological bonds, illustrating a sufficiency-oriented pathway out of the dominant capitalist paradigm.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to diverse economies scholarship by bringing the framework into sustainable consumption research and, rather than studying single sectors or organizations in isolation, reading for economic difference across multiple high-emission domains simultaneously. Its central contribution is a crosscutting framework of three dimensions — spatial, temporal and relational — that travel across food, housing and mobility and serve as anchor points for identifying and framing more-than-capitalist economies as a lens for sustainable, sufficiency-oriented consumption-production relationships.

**Practical Implication**
For more-than-capitalist economies to proliferate from the margins, the paper argues for alternative, collective governance that integrates municipalities, funding bodies and regulatory agencies across public and private sectors. Public funding and subsidies, accessible public land and facilities, and public procurement policies that prioritize cooperatives, CSAs and other sufficiency-oriented enterprises can foster these models, alongside knowledge-sharing networks, hybrid public-grassroots partnerships, and education that brings these economic concepts into public discourse.

**Limitations**
The study is situated in a Danish welfare-state context with relatively low poverty, so the cases pursue slowing societal metabolism and reducing ecological harm rather than responding to injustices of unmet food, housing or mobility needs, limiting transferability to contexts where diverse economies arise as resistance to social, economic and racial exclusion. The paper also frames itself as one initial, exploratory step at the intersection of sufficiency and more-than-capitalist economies, and the long-term social and ecological implications of the cases are not assessed.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for further empirical investigation into the long-term social and ecological implications of more-than-capitalist initiatives, and for assessing the effectiveness of policy interventions that support them so as to refine future regulatory and funding strategies. It also invites continued attention to spatial proximity, long-term engagement and community-building in developing consumption-production relationships for a sustainable future.
