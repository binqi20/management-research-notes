# The Real Effects of Protecting Biodiversity

**Abstract**
Protected areas are a central instrument in biodiversity conservation. However, their broader implica­ tions for corporate behavior and financial performance remain underexplored. This study examines how proximity to newly designated protected areas shapes establishment-level environmental, opera­ tional, and financial outcomes from 1990 to 2021. We find that nearby establishments significantly reduce toxic emissions, driven not by cleaner technologies but by production cutbacks and workforce contraction. Exploratory evidence suggests that heightened regulatory oversight may be one channel that constrains operations and is associated with lower profitability and market value. These results un­ derscore the environmental benefits of conservation but also reveal the material costs imposed on affected firms. As global conservation initiatives expand, our findings show that biodiversity-related regulatory exposure constitutes a material financial risk that should be incorporated into policy design, corporate strategy, and capital allocation decisions.

**Research Question**
How do designations of nearby protected areas influence the operational, environmental, and financial outcomes of proximate firms, and through what adaptive strategies and channels (e.g., regulatory oversight) do firms respond to the stricter governance requirements these designations impose?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Corporate Biodiversity Exposure (CBE) — count of newly designated protected areas within a 30-km radius of an establishment (lagged one year); alternative CBE metrics weighted by IUCN category, area, employment, and sales.
- DV(s): On-site toxic releases (primary), establishment sales, labor productivity, workforce size, and parent-firm ROA, productivity, TFP, and Tobin's Q.
- Mediators: Reduced economic activity (output/scale cutbacks) rather than abatement investment; heightened regulatory oversight operating first through inspections, later through enforcement actions and penalties.
- Moderators: Pre-existing polluter status (large polluters more affected), parent-firm financial constraints, and government-managed protected areas / parent-firm government regulatory exposure (amplify pollution reductions).

Proximity to newly designated protected areas raises compliance costs, permitting complexity, and reputational and regulatory scrutiny for nearby establishments. Firms respond primarily by scaling down production (lower sales, productivity, and employment) rather than investing in cleaner abatement technologies, yielding a roughly 6 percent reduction in on-site toxic emissions for an interquartile increase in CBE. A falsification test on off-site releases and a PADDD (downgrading/downsizing/degazettement) test support a localized, enduring regulatory channel. These establishment-level disruptions aggregate to lower parent-firm profitability, productivity, and market valuation, indicating that biodiversity-related regulatory exposure is financially material.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to the emerging field of biodiversity finance by introducing CBE, a transparent, scalable, spatially explicit measure of establishment-level biodiversity-regulatory exposure, and by documenting a regulatory channel through which conservation mandates shape corporate environmental behavior and firm value. It distinguishes spatially targeted, enduring biodiversity shocks from transitory, diffuse climate-related risks, and aligns with emerging spatial finance frameworks that incorporate geospatial environmental data into financial analysis.

**Practical Implication**
For policymakers, the "30 by 30" conservation agenda delivers environmental benefits but imposes material costs on pollution-intensive firms, implying a need for transitional support and regulatory clarity. For firms and investors, proximity to protected areas is a salient, financially material risk that should be incorporated into location decisions, strategic planning, valuation, risk management, and capital allocation.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that protected-area designations may not be random (site selection correlates with local economic conditions), so despite extensive fixed effects some selection bias may remain and several findings (e.g., enforcement effects) are presented as suggestive rather than definitive causal estimates. CBE captures only localized on-site exposure and does not reflect supply-chain dependencies or ecological risks in unprotected areas, and high-frequency return tests are impractical given heterogeneous, multi-facility designation timing.

**Future Research**
The paper implies opportunities to extend the CBE framework to other countries and to supply-chain or value-chain biodiversity dependencies beyond localized on-site exposure, to further disentangle the regulatory enforcement versus reputational channels, and to examine firm adaptation strategies such as cross-border outsourcing in response to biodiversity-related regulatory pressure.
