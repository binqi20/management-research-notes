# Is a picture worth a thousand words? Image usage in ESG reports

**Abstract**
We collect a novel dataset on images in Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) reports using deep learning and artificial intelligence (AI), to test hypotheses arising from experimental literature that visual impression management enhances audience perception. We further combine AI and manual methods to contrast images that lack information content ("generic images" evoking broad ESG themes) against images with information content ("specific images" depicting firmspecific ESG activities). We hypothesize and find that firms (1) operating in socially problematic industries (e.g., "sinful", polluting, or controversial industries), (2) issuing less extensive textual ESG disclosures, and (3) experiencing poor ESG performance tend to use more images, especially generic images, consistent with strategic motivations. We then examine the impact of image usage on stakeholders, observing unduly enhanced perception in retail traders but seldom in institutional investors. We observe that ESG award committees reward specific images and penalize generic images. Our results show that ESG report visual impression management affects stakeholders in the real world, though its impact can be overcome by attention and expertise.

**Research Question**
Do firms use aesthetic images in ESG reports as a tool of visual impression management, and does such image usage actually shape stakeholders' perceptions of corporate ESG performance outside laboratory settings?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Report-level image usage (IMAGES/PAGE, IMAGES/TEXT), decomposed into specific vs. generic images; firm/industry impression-management proxies (problematic-industry membership, GRI adoption, textual content extensiveness, ESG performance scores)
- DV(s): Determinants stage — image usage; consequences stage — future ESG misconduct (Violation Tracker), one-year-ahead cumulative abnormal returns (CAR), shareholder proposals, and ESG-award wins
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Stakeholder sophistication (retail vs. institutional shareholders; ESG award committees); institutional ownership; ESG controversies score (interacted with image usage)

Grounded in legitimacy theory and the impression-management literature, the paper argues that because ESG claims are hard to substantiate and aesthetic images are processed subconsciously and are hard to cite as evidence in disputes, visual impression management is a low-cost way for firms—especially poor performers in stigmatized industries with thin textual disclosure—to inflate perceived legitimacy. Generic images carry more impression-management content than substantiating specific images. The consequences tests show the embellishment "works" on unsophisticated retail traders (overvaluation and later return reversals) but is seen through by sophisticated experts: ESG award committees reward specific and penalize generic images, and institutional activists extract information from specific images, revealing previously underestimated detection costs.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes large-scale archival evidence to the ESG-disclosure and impression-management literatures by distinguishing generic images (a presentation/reporting choice) from specific images (reflecting underlying ESG activities), thereby illuminating the decoupling of reporting from activities. It documents the first evidence the authors are aware of that visual impression management can elicit negative reactions from expert audiences, cautioning against over-generalizing prior experimental and small-scale findings to wider market participants.

**Practical Implication**
For regulators and standard-setters, the findings show that aesthetic images—largely left to preparer discretion in ESG frameworks—carry information and can mislead less sophisticated stakeholders, so heavy or generic image usage may warrant scrutiny as a greenwashing signal predictive of future misconduct. Preparers and sophisticated stakeholders should recognize that generic image usage can backfire with expert audiences such as award committees.

**Limitations**
Shareholder-response measures are noisier than in annual-report settings because only the month and year of ESG report publication are known, precluding short-window event studies. Translating experimental insights to archival settings is imperfect, the consequence settings do not perfectly overlap, large-sample tests capture only average effects, and ESG ratings are acknowledged as imperfect proxies for ESG performance. The high empirical correlation between specific and generic images limits inferences about their independent roles.

**Future Research**
The authors invite work using archival, field, and experimental methods to probe the mechanisms and conditions under which visual impression management elicits negative expert reactions, including survey evidence on report producers' choices, better disentangling of bundled ESG information, and study of how generative-AI image creation may cheapen or obscure the information content of "specific" images.
