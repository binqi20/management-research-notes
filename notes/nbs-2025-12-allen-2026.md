# Do Investors care about the Rainforest? Evidence from Voluntary Carbon Offsets around the World

**Abstract**
We explore how investors react to firms’ biodiversity-focused activities in the Voluntary Carbon Market.

**Research Question**
How do investors react to publicly listed firms' announcements of voluntary forestry-related carbon-credit retirements, and does the biodiversity impact of those credits (as signaled by CCB certification) and the January 2023 Guardian "worthless offsets" article shape the market reaction?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Retirement of forestry-related voluntary carbon credits; CCB (Climate, Community & Biodiversity) certification dummy distinguishing high- vs low-biodiversity-impact offsets
- DV(s): Cumulative average abnormal stock returns (CAARs/CARs) around the retirement announcement date
- Moderators: The January 18, 2023 Guardian article shock (pre- vs post-Guardian periods); industry sector (mining, manufacturing, financials); geographic region (esp. South and Central America)
- Controls: Firm size, market-to-book ratio

Carbon-credit retirements are publicly observable signals of corporate climate action, but their credibility is contested. The paper argues investors reprice firms based on the perceived environmental integrity of the offsets they retire: after a quasi-exogenous shock to public trust (the Guardian's claim that >90% of rainforest offsets are "worthless"), retirements of standard non-CCB forestry credits earn significantly negative abnormal returns, consistent with greenwashing concerns. CCB certification acts as a credible biodiversity signal that mitigates (but does not eliminate) this penalty, leaving CCB-credit retirements with neutral, statistically insignificant reactions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study is among the first to examine investor reactions to voluntary carbon-credit retirements and their biodiversity impact, extending the green-finance event-study literature (e.g., green bonds, eco-friendly behavior) into the Voluntary Carbon Market. It shows that markets distinguish offsets by perceived environmental credibility, treating biodiversity co-benefit certification (CCB) as a signal that softens reputational/greenwashing penalties, thereby linking biodiversity finance to firm value.

**Practical Implication**
Results underscore the need for enhanced disclosure standards, independent auditing, and stricter certification criteria in voluntary carbon markets to restore investor confidence and prevent misleading net-zero claims. Firms that integrate credible, high-quality offsets with certified biodiversity impact are likely to benefit from stronger investor trust and reduced reputational risk.

**Limitations**
Most CAAR differences between CCB and non-CCB credits are economically but not statistically significant at conventional levels; the authors attribute this to limited statistical power in a highly heterogeneous sample. Voluntary-market offset prices are OTC, opaque, and not standardized, so a reliable time series of offset prices could not be constructed. CCB certification is used as a proxy for biodiversity impact because stand-alone biodiversity credits are too scarce to measure directly.

**Future Research**
Constructing a comprehensive, reliable time series of voluntary offset prices is flagged as a challenge beyond the scope of this article. The puzzle of why firms voluntarily engage in costly climate-mitigation and biodiversity activities that are at best neutral (and potentially harmful) to shareholder value invites further investigation, as does tracking the developing market for stand-alone biodiversity credits.
