# Practice Paper—AI-Driven Behavioral Nudges for Organizations: An Integrative System for Sustainable Resource Management

**Abstract**
The results demonstrate significant financial savings and reduced environmental impact, benefiting organizations, regulators, service providers, and society. We contribute to research on resource management, systems thinking, and nudging in an organizational context by aligning technological tools with human processes.

**Research Question**
How can artificial-intelligence-driven demand forecasting be integrated with behaviorally informed communications (planning prompts) to help large organizations optimize energy consumption under critical peak pricing (CPP) schemes — reducing costs and emissions — and do such behavioral effects persist across repeated curtailment events?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Receipt of an AI-driven curtailment call (vs. none); receipt of a behaviorally informed planning-prompt email (experimental vs. control); forecasting method (NNTS vs. tree-based/benchmark models)
- DV(s): Firm-level hourly contribution to provincial peak demand / hourly energy consumption during curtailment windows; financial savings; estimated CO2 emissions reduction
- Mediators: Curtailment compliance / follow-through (planning prompts translate when-and-how into concrete action)
- Moderators: Generator ownership (controlled rather than analyzed as a key moderator given few generator-equipped firms); repeated-exposure (curtailment-call number) tested via interaction

The paper combines two streams. First, an NNTS model forecasts provincial peak demand up to 24 hours ahead, improving peak-identification accuracy while minimizing false alarms, so firms know *when* to curtail. Second, planning-prompt emails clarify *how* to act, embedding behavioral science (Rogers et al., 2015) to convert abstract goals into concrete steps. Grounded in dynamic capabilities, sociotechnical systems theory, and systems-thinking/resource-orchestration, the authors argue that effective demand response is a coordination problem requiring alignment of digital tools, stakeholder incentives, and organizational behavior — not merely an optimization problem. The intervention reduced peak demand by roughly 2,949 kWh per organization, yielding average net annual savings of ~$232,843, with nudge effects stable across nine repeated curtailment events.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends behavioral and resource-management theory to the underresearched organizational (rather than household) context, showing that planning-prompt nudges can shift energy use in large, highly motivated firms and persist across repeated decisions. It reframes energy curtailment as a coordination problem and demonstrates how AI forecasting and behavioral nudging operate in tandem, advancing the practical application of dynamic capabilities and sociotechnical systems theory in sustainability contexts.

**Practical Implication**
System operators, who hold real-time demand data, are best positioned to forecast peaks; local distribution companies should communicate both when and how to act, using simple, timely messages with concrete planning prompts, while energy consultants translate signals into operational guidance. Successful implementation requires integration across regulators, system operators, distribution companies, consultants, and commercial users.

**Limitations**
Participating firms were large industrial users who voluntarily subscribed to the advisory service, limiting generalizability; the authors lack data on intra-organizational communication chains. The bundled field intervention (revised subject line, visual salience, planning-prompt content) prevents isolating the precise causal mechanism, so field findings are suggestive rather than definitive. The small number of generator-equipped firms limits subgroup precision, and durability under different market conditions or personnel changes remains untested.

**Future Research**
Multiarm field experiments could disentangle the independent and interactive contributions of the bundled behavioral design elements. Researchers should vary advance notification lead times, examine longer-term load-shifting behaviors, and test whether the integrative approach scales to other uneven-demand domains such as water or traffic to assess generalizability across industries, organizations, and customers.
