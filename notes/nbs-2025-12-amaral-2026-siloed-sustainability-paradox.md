# Siloed Sustainability: How Paradox Management Unravels in Integrative Practice Implementation

**Abstract**
Organizations often struggle to implement sustainability practices, even when such efforts are strategically prioritized. While paradox studies have highlighted successful cases in which tensions between economic, environmental, and social goals are constructively balanced, less attention has been paid to how the characteristics of the practices themselves complicate paradox management. Through an ethnographic study of LatinCar, the Brazilian subsidiary of a multinational carmaker, we introduce the concept of "integrative practices," patterns of activity marked by indivisibility, complexity, and non-codifiability, to explain why certain sustainability practices require extensive cross-functional coordination and generate persistent contradictions. In siloed organizations, these integrative practices trigger an integration–differentiation paradox that becomes increasingly difficult to manage. Our process analysis reveals how efforts to work through this paradox inadvertently surface new tensions—jurisdictional, outcome, and attributional—that accumulate and derail implementation. We contribute to paradox theory by illuminating the dark side of paradox management, showing how practice characteristics can undermine actors' ability to balance competing demands. We also advance sustainability and practice implementation literatures by theorizing the distinctive intraorganizational challenges posed by integrative practices.

**Research Question**
How do efforts to work through paradoxes unravel in the implementation of sustainability practices? The authors ask why organizations fail to embed sustainability "in meaningful and lasting ways" even under seemingly favorable conditions of leadership commitment and resources.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: An underlying "integration–differentiation paradox" (the simultaneous, contradictory demands for strategic integration and specialized differentiation) plus three ensuing secondary tensions — jurisdictional, outcome, and attributional.
- Practice characteristics: "Integrative practices" defined by three properties — indivisibility (cannot be split across teams without undermining coherence), complexity (hard to grasp cognitively and operationally), and non-codifiability (context-dependent, reliant on tacit knowledge). Sustainability sits at the high end of all three.
- Process / phases: Stage 1 — "centering practices" (creating a sustainability department) provoke jurisdictional tensions; Stage 2 — "mandate enlargement" (a process integration office to break silos) surfaces complexity and outcome tensions / goal displacement; Stage 3 — "focus on visibility" (short-term symbolic wins) triggers attribution tensions over credit.
- Emergent outcome: A self-reinforcing, recursive cycle in which each well-intentioned attempt to work through the paradox generates new tensions that compound, narrow actors' courses of action, and derail implementation.

Through temporal bracketing of a failed (negative) case, the authors show that paradox management unravels not because actors fail to recognize the paradox but because the indivisible, complex, and non-codifiable nature of sustainability practices structurally constrains their responses. Each move to balance integration and differentiation surfaces deeper structural and political fault lines, so the original reflective effort is progressively displaced by reactive "containment of tension," producing a downward spiral of constrained action and organizational fatigue.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study makes three contributions: (1) to paradox theory, by illuminating the "dark side" of paradox management — showing how and why deliberate, well-intentioned attempts to work through paradoxes can inadvertently escalate tensions and hinder implementation; (2) to sustainability research, by uncovering intraorganizational constraints on embeddedness beyond the traditional focus on social/environmental/economic goal tensions; and (3) to practice theory, by introducing "integrative practices" as a novel lens (indivisibility, complexity, non-codifiability) for understanding overlooked complexities of practice implementation.

**Practical Implication**
Conventional integration fixes — central coordination, mandate expansion, and role flexibility — backfire when highly integrative practices meet entrenched silos. Organizations should instead reconfigure how authority, accountability, and recognition are distributed: define clear roles for indivisible practices before implementation, appoint "sustainability integrators," align reward structures with processes (not just outcomes), and create "safe spaces" that let actors pause, reassess evolving tensions, and learn from failure.

**Limitations**
The theorizing is bounded: organizations lacking commitment may respond by decoupling rather than attempting paradox management; the jurisdictional tensions observed may be less salient in organizations structured for cross-functional teamwork with shared goals; and the process model is most relevant to early stages of implementing new, immature sustainability practices, before they become institutionalized, codified, and divisible.

**Future Research**
A key avenue is to examine how organizations transition from fragile, early-stage enactment to mature, institutionalized integrative practices — investigating to what extent inherent practice features delay institutionalization and which factors mitigate them over time. Scholars could ask whether integrative practices can become more divisible, codifiable, or less complex through learning, emerging standards, or peer exchange, and study the material configurations, social practices, and institutional arrangements that entrench paradoxes.
