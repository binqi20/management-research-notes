# Invisible Inequality in Entrepreneurial Ecosystems: The Micro-Foundations of Navigating Marginalization

**Abstract**
While entrepreneurial ecosystems research has increasingly discussed inclusivity and systemic barriers facing women or racial minorities, it has largely overlooked how invisible inequalities restrict marginalized entrepreneurs. In this study, we focus on Dalits, a historically marginalized community in India facing invisible inequality (on caste lines), to examine how such inequalities manifest in the entrepreneurial ecosystem, turning the ecosystem into a non-cooperative space, and how marginalized entrepreneurs navigate such non-cooperative spaces. Drawing on qualitative interviews with entrepreneurs and other ecosystem actors within the Dalit community, we identify three ways in which invisible inequalities manifest in the entrepreneurial ecosystem: moral disqualification in economic opportunities, relational gatekeeping in entrepreneurial networks, and undermining social credibility. **Research Question**
How do invisible inequalities (such as caste) manifest in the entrepreneurial ecosystem, turning it into a non-cooperative space, and how do marginalized entrepreneurs navigate such non-cooperative spaces?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Caste as an invisible inequality (inferable from last name, diet, occupation rather than visible markers) that structures access to networks, capital, and legitimacy in the Indian entrepreneurial ecosystem.
- Manifestations of invisible inequality (turning the ecosystem into a non-cooperative space): (a) moral disqualification in economic opportunities (caste as credit liability) — assumptions of untrustworthiness and distrust of Dalit credibility embedded in bureaucratic and lending processes; (b) relational gatekeeping in entrepreneurial networks (caste as an invisible credential) — exclusion from social networks, limited representation in business associations, and barriers to mainstream networks; (c) undermining of social credibility (caste as reputational liability) — market devaluation of Dalit-made products and stereotypes undermining business legitimacy.
- Navigational micro-practices (marginalized agency): advocacy for targeted efforts (engaging government and private sector), creation of alternate spaces (founding own chambers like DICCI, cultivating virtual communities, leveraging religious networks), and clientelism (developing relationships with political leaders, upper-caste partners, and bureaucrats).
- Emergent outcome / reframing: Dalit entrepreneurs substitute political capital and cultural capital for the financial and social capital denied to them, reconfiguring the ecosystem from within.

The process model shows the entrepreneurial ecosystem functioning not as a neutral support system but as a hostile, caste-coded "non-cooperative space" (Chowdhury, 2021b) that reproduces invisible inequalities through informal codes of exclusion, denial, and misrecognition. Marginalized entrepreneurs are not passive recipients of discrimination; they exercise agency through micro-practices, though these often entail coerced compromises (clientelism, symbolic appeasement, or assimilation into dominant-caste business cultures) born of the structural compulsion to survive.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends entrepreneurial-ecosystem theory beyond its inclusivity assumption by theorizing exclusionary mechanisms that render ecosystems non-cooperative spaces for marginalized groups, and answers calls to develop the micro-foundations of ecosystems by identifying the granular, individual-level practices through which invisible inequalities become embedded. It advances research on entrepreneurship by marginalized groups by showing how caste-based stratification demands unique adaptive strategies and reframes clientelism from a power-sustenance tool into an empowering survival strategy. It also contributes to research on caste in organizational settings by foregrounding caste as an active structuring force and portraying Dalit entrepreneurs as resilient agents rather than passive recipients of discrimination.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should abandon the assumption that entrepreneurial ecosystems are universally accessible and design interventions targeting not only access to credit and capital but also the informal, relational, and symbolic exclusions that communities like Dalits face. By naming mechanisms such as moral disqualification and relational gatekeeping, the study offers marginalized entrepreneurs tools to recognize and build counter-strategies, and points to integrating marginalized-led ecosystems (e.g., DICCI) with mainstream ones to bridge accessibility gaps.

**Limitations**
The authors could include only two female Dalit entrepreneurs, leaving the gender-caste intersection underexplored. The findings are context-specific, as caste is predominantly a feature of South Asian society, which constrains transferability to other settings and marginalized groups.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine the intersection of gender and caste, study other marginalized groups within the Indian ecosystem (e.g., scheduled tribes) and non-business-oriented dominant castes such as Brahmins, and investigate how marginalized-led ecosystems integrate with mainstream ones. Scholars should also explore how marginalized entrepreneurs use clientelism differently from non-marginalized groups in contexts with entrenched social hierarchies, and reconceptualize political and cultural capital as adaptive resources.
