# Reaffirming and Renewing Our Declaration of Interdependence

**Abstract**

**Research Question**

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: As a polycrisis "shaped by ecological, economic, political, social, and technological trends" reshapes the institutional environment, the journal *Business & Society* has an even more critical agenda-setting role to play at the business-society interface.
- Six interrelated features of the societal environment: (1) contested and conflictual institutional environments; (2) climate change, biodiversity, and ecosystem collapse; (3) Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) contestation; (4) autocratization and democratic backsliding (including techno-feudalism); (5) social inequalities and inequities; (6) immigration and the movement of people / "tribalistic" identities.
- Three guiding principles for the response: transparency (to foster informed, deliberative engagement), safety (to report unvarnished and uncomfortable findings), and trust (to build an interdependent community).
- Planned initiatives: data-sharing and generative-AI policies, temporary author pseudonyms, a strategic advisory board, and new "Perspectives" and "conversations" article formats.

The editorial frames the incoming co-editors' three-year agenda as a response to interlocking grand challenges that simultaneously threaten society and the academic freedom on which business-and-society scholarship depends. The process is normative rather than causal: by anchoring the journal on transparency, safety, and trust, the editors aim to keep BAS a venue where difficult, controversial, and rigorous work can be published and debated, thereby renewing the founding "declaration of interdependence" between business and society.

**Theoretical Contribution**
As an editorial, its contribution is agenda-setting rather than theory-testing: it synthesizes six features of a contested institutional environment (drawing on the language of institutional contestation and contradictory institutional demands) and articulates transparency, safety, and trust as guiding principles for the field of business and society. It positions the journal in direct dialogue with mainstream management journals while preserving its boundary-pushing, agenda-setting identity.

**Practical Implication**
The editorial announces concrete journal practices: required data statements and encouraged data/code deposition (e.g., via OSF), a forthcoming generative-AI use policy, optional temporary author pseudonymity for at-risk scholars, a new strategic advisory board, and new "Perspectives" and "conversations" formats — all intended to support scholars working on increasingly controversial topics such as climate change, DEI, and human rights.

**Future Research**
The editors identify research avenues tied to each of the six environmental features — institutional contestation and the "post-truth" era, holistic business-nature relations, the path forward for equitable opportunity amid DEI backlash, the links between business and democratic backsliding, social and environmental inequality, and the consequences of restricted human mobility — and invite agenda-setting work on these grand challenges.
