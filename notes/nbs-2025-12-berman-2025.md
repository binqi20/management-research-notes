# Kazakh Sharing Practices: A Critical Inquiry Into Sharing Economy Models From the Vantage of Ownership in Collective Societies

**Abstract**
The sharing economy (SE) is often promoted as a sustainable and inclusive model for resource provision, increasingly so for base-of-pyramid (BoP) contexts. However, dominant SE frameworks ground in individualistic and commodified understandings of ownership may misalign with values in collectivist societies. This article presents a case study from rural Kazakhstan to examine how ownership is conceptualized and enacted through Kazakh linguistic and cultural traditions that draw from the physical steppe ecology. Our findings reveal a relational understanding of ownership that privileges shared access and social obligation over individual control or exclusion. These principles generate forms of social value that extend beyond efficiency and economic utility. By highlighting how sharing practices are shaped by embedded ownership norms, this study challenges the universal applicability of mainstream SE models. It calls for greater attention to culturally situated ownership understanding in order to develop SE strategies that are ethically grounded and contextually responsive to BoP settings.

**Research Question**
"How do sharing practices challenge and provide alternative insights into SE models from the vantage of ownership in collectivist contexts?" The paper traces the features embedded in the notion of ownership in a collectivist society to ask how social understandings of ownership shape sharing practices and resource distribution patterns.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: relational ownership ("менің иелігінде" / *menin ieliginde*, "at my disposal") vs. privatized, commodified ownership; conventional sharing economy (CSE) vs. locally embedded sharing practices; personalized vs. depersonalized exchange; reciprocity vs. transactionality; communal responsibility vs. individual utility.
- Aggregate dimensions (Gioia): Sharing practices; Ownership; and CSE: Challenges in local context.
- Second-order themes: Sharing, Mutual assistance, Mutual responsibility, Community connection, Reciprocity and circularity, Understanding of ownership in shaping sharing practices, Local SP vs. CSE models.
- Triggering conditions: steppe ecology and pastoral/nomadic mobility that disincentivized accumulation and required collective labor for survival.
- Emergent outcomes: social value creation, social cohesion, and resource circulation rather than monetary value extraction.

In rural Kazakhstan, ownership is enacted as relational ownership — a culturally sedimented, morally infused, and socially negotiated form of access in which the right to a resource is earned through demonstrated contribution and sustained social obligation rather than legal title or payment. Sharing functions as a "moral economy" where refusal is ethically censured and reciprocity is circular rather than calculated, so platform-mediated CSE models that reduce sharing to monetized, depersonalized transactions risk eroding the very communal logics they seek to scale.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces and develops "relational ownership" as a morally grounded, socially embedded institutional logic that contrasts with the legalistic, individualized ownership underpinning SE theory, demonstrating how it derives its rationale from humans' relationship with the steppe landscape. It contributes an empirical account of sharing in a collectivist, under-researched BoP context and offers a critical corrective to universalizing models of SE application, reconceptualizing ownership as a relational and institutionalized practice that operates as a governance mechanism, a principle of resource allocation, and an ethical infrastructure.

**Practical Implication**
Deploying conventional, monetized sharing-economy platforms as a poverty-reduction strategy in BoP contexts may erode well-functioning indigenous sharing systems or result in unnecessary investments. Policy and practice should support rather than displace local logics of obligation, trust, and communal access, engaging with culturally embedded ownership structures to design SE interventions that are inclusive and contextually appropriate.

**Limitations**
The findings are based on qualitative data from a single region, limiting generalizability, since Kazakhstan's blend of nomadic heritage and contemporary economic development may not fully represent the broader diversity of BoP populations. All participants were over 40 years old, so the perspectives of younger generations are not captured.

**Future Research**
Future research should broaden the scope to comparative studies across diverse BoP populations and incorporate a more diverse age demographic, especially younger generations who may hold different perspectives on ownership and resource-sharing. Longitudinal studies tracking how sharing practices change as younger cohorts shift toward more digitalized lifestyles could illuminate the sustainability and evolution of these practices.
