We also observe a general pattern of higher willingness to pay to avoid the lowest level (‘Very Poor’) of each attribute and achieve the intermediate level (‘Fairly Good’) than for further improvements to the highest level (‘Good’). This pattern, consistent with how the levels were designed, holds across all subsamples and attributes. Thus, we find substantial demand for more sustainable clothing, particularly for avoiding the worst practices. Our results also suggest that producers could pass on a portion of their increases in costs if sustainability improvements are effectively communicated. While most consumers are not willing to pay more to reach the highest level of an attribute, there are groups of consumers who are willing to pay a premium for high sustainability standards.

**Research Question**
How much are consumers willing to pay for improvements in the social (working conditions), environmental, and health attributes of clothing, and how do these ethical trade-offs differ between consumers buying for themselves and parents buying for their children?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Attribute levels of a hypothetical T-shirt — working conditions, health protection, and environmental protection, each at three categorical levels (‘Very Poor’, ‘Fairly Good’ [reference], ‘Good’); price (continuous, 80–325 SEK).
- DV(s): Choice among three alternatives, from which marginal willingness to pay (MWTP) for each attribute level is derived.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Purchasing context (buying for oneself vs. parent buying for a child, via split samples); latent class membership; socio-economic characteristics (gender, age, education).

Utility is modeled in the random utility framework (McFadden, 1974) and estimated with a two-class latent class logit that segments respondents by preference heterogeneity, incorporating stated attribute non-attendance so that ignored attributes carry zero weight. Across both samples, health protection is valued most and environmental protection least, and MWTP is consistently larger for avoiding the worst (‘Very Poor’) level than for reaching the best (‘Good’) level — reflecting the intentionally non-linear attribute scale in which ‘Fairly Good’ corresponds to compliance with existing EU regulation. Parents display the same ranking but lower overall MWTP than the General Sample.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study provides a comprehensive, integrated analysis of consumer preferences across social, environmental, and health dimensions of clothing sustainability within a single choice experiment, whereas prior research typically examined only one or two dimensions. It documents that willingness to pay to avoid the worst practices substantially exceeds willingness to pay to reach the highest standards, linking this asymmetry to risk aversion / negative-label effects and to a non-linear, regulation-anchored attribute design.

**Practical Implication**
There is substantial consumer demand for more sustainable clothing, especially for avoiding the worst practices, suggesting that sustainability labeling (e.g., under the EU Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation) emphasizing avoidance of the most harmful practices would resonate with consumers. Producers could pass on a portion of cost increases for moderate sustainability improvements if these are clearly communicated, and a subset of consumers forms a target market for premium high-sustainability products.

**Limitations**
The hypothetical nature of the choice experiment means hypothetical bias cannot be ruled out despite oath-script and budget-reminder mitigations. Respondents were given clear sustainability information that is rarely available in real purchasing situations, and the latent class model did not converge for the Parents Sample when interacting attributes with socio-economic variables, limiting heterogeneity analysis in that sample.

**Future Research**
The authors note that their experimental design does not allow direct examination of compensatory decision-making or moral-licensing heuristics across sustainability attributes, pointing to further work on how consumers trade off multiple sustainability dimensions. Estimating producer-side costs of sustainability improvements is left beyond the scope of this study.
