# Pollution havens versus gates of hell: Environmental pollution and multinationals' foreign investment

**Abstract**
We find evidence in a panel of US multinational corporations from 2007 to 2020. A firm's investment in a host country is decreasing in the host country's environmental pollution, especially if the firm faces heightened constraints in finding alternative labor inputs and material supplies or is highly dependent on these resources.

**Research Question**
How do polluting environmental conditions alter firms' cost structures and, in turn, shape their global strategies? Specifically, how does a host country's environmental pollution affect multinational corporations' foreign investment in that country?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Host country environmental pollution (100 minus the average of EPI Climate Change [PCC] and Environmental Health [HLT] scores; alternative measures: GHG emissions, PM 2.5)
- DV(s): MNC investment (log of one plus the number of an MNC's subsidiaries in a host country in a year; alternatives: new investment, divestment, sales percentage, location choice)
- Mediators: None tested directly; the proposed mediating channel (pollution-induced operational cost via labor costs and material supply constraints) is examined through moderators
- Moderators: Host country skilled labor market depth (−); rail transport network (−); labor-intensive industries (+); consumer goods industries (+) — signs denote whether they attenuate or amplify the negative pollution effect

The central mechanism is the pollution-induced operational cost: environmental pollution in a host country raises MNCs' labor costs (by thinning the skilled-labor supply and reducing workforce productivity) and tightens material supply constraints (by contaminating or depleting input materials and disrupting transportation networks). These rising operational costs erode profit margins and diminish the investment attractiveness of pollution havens, so when compliance pressure is held constant, more-polluted host countries draw less MNC investment. The effect is buffered where countries offer deep skilled-labor markets or strong rail networks and is intensified for labor-intensive and consumer goods industries that are most exposed to these inputs.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances three literatures. It extends pollution haven research beyond compliance-cost arbitrage by showing that environmental degradation imposes direct operational costs that can push foreign investment out rather than pull it in. It complements the corporate sustainability literature's emphasis on intangible reputational loss by documenting the tangible operational costs—elevated labor and material expenses and supply-chain disruption—of operating in degraded environments. And it advances MNC foreign-investment research by establishing environmental pollution, a direct outcome of weak regulation, as an overlooked determinant of location decisions, with contingencies tied to host-country resource availability and firms' resource dependence.

**Practical Implication**
For managers, the findings show that investing in pollution havens creates both compliance-arbitrage opportunities and rising operational costs, with labor-intensive and consumer goods firms most exposed; these costs are attenuated where skilled labor and advanced transport networks are available. For policymakers—particularly in high-pollution developing countries—relaxing environmental regulation to attract FDI may yield unsustainable growth, since unchecked degradation eventually inflicts operational costs that erode the very business opportunities sought, so reducing pollution supports long-term economic growth and sustainability.

**Limitations**
The study focuses only on the negative effect of host-country pollution on foreign investment and does not examine other strategic decisions (e.g., R&D investment, new product development) that pollution might drive or hinder. It isolates operational costs from labor and supply constraints but does not capture other costs of operating in pollution havens, such as climate-risk exposure or the consequences of environmental violations. The sample is limited to MNCs headquartered in the United States.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine whether environmental pollution shapes other MNC strategic activities, including R&D investment, clean-technology development, and global sourcing from polluted countries. Scholars could explore additional financial and nonfinancial costs of exploiting pollution havens, including climate risks such as extreme weather and rising sea levels. Researchers could also test whether the findings generalize to MNCs from non-US countries, especially developing-country firms operating in different institutional contexts.
