# Platform Regulation: Beyond Power and Size

**Abstract**
Not reported in paper

**Research Question**
On what basis, and how, should digital platforms be regulated — the editorial frames the issue around the WHY (why platforms differ from ordinary firms), the WHAT (which platform-specific harms to target), and the HOW (how to craft dynamic, context-aware governance) of platform regulation.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Platforms are "inverted firms" / market architects, not merely large technology firms — they are simultaneously the infrastructure and the governors of digital markets, deriving structural power from network effects, data accumulation, and complementor activity rather than size alone.
- WHY: Near-zero marginal costs and reinforcing feedback loops between user participation, data, and complementor activity create tipping points, barriers to entry, and ecosystem-level market power that the Structure-Conduct-Performance paradigm and traditional antitrust cannot capture.
- WHAT: Regulation should target specific practices (self-preferencing, tying, unfair revenue sharing, "killer acquisitions," data entrenchment, manipulation via social-justice narratives) rather than blunt proxies like firm size or market share, including harms arising on smaller platforms (e.g., child-data privacy).
- HOW: Effective governance combines ex-ante rules (e.g., the EU Digital Markets Act) with adaptive, context-sensitive mechanisms (certification/inspection models, a dedicated Digital Authority, self-regulation backed by credible intervention) while pursuing global coherence.

The central process the editorial advances is a paradigm shift from a static, size-focused view of competition toward a dynamic, ecosystem-level view: because platform power is fluid, context-dependent, and distributed across actors, regulation must continuously monitor evolving sources of control and preserve generative innovation while curbing extractive ("bad") gatekeeping.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The piece reframes platforms as a new mode of economic organization — an ecosystem or inverted firm orchestrating others' production — rather than a new type of firm, and argues this distinctiveness renders traditional antitrust metrics (market share, the Structure-Conduct-Performance paradigm) incomplete or misleading. It synthesizes the Special Issue's contributions into an integrated WHY/WHAT/HOW agenda for platform-regulation scholarship and calls for a "new regulatory imagination."

**Practical Implication**
For policymakers and antitrust authorities, the editorial urges moving beyond static, homogeneous models toward dynamic tools that assess platform-specific harms (self-preferencing, killer acquisitions) and protect nascent competitors. For platform leaders, it stresses proactive and ethical governance, arguing that long-term viability may depend as much on perceived fairness and trustworthiness as on technological prowess, and points toward participatory models where firms, users, and complementors cocreate the rules.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The editorial poses open questions for the field: how to design governance that curbs extractive power while nurturing generative innovation ("good" vs. "bad" gatekeeping), how to balance global coherence with local needs, what role managers, users, and complementors should play, and whether the primary objective of platform regulation should be to preserve competition or to preserve value creation and user welfare.
