# When and how simplified nutrition labels improve fast-food choices

**Abstract**
Simplified nutrition labels, such as Nutri-Score, are increasingly used in grocery stores, but their effects on out-of-home consumption, their psychological mechanisms, and potential moderators remain poorly understood. An initial online experiment showed that Nutri-Score leads fast-food customers to select meals (and especially desserts) of higher nutritional quality by helping them identify the healthiest options, ruling out three other theory-driven potential mechanisms. A field study involving 200,039 fast-food meals replicated the positive effects of Nutri-Score in a middle-class metropolitan area but found that it backfired in a less affluent provincial town. A second online experiment, which recruited a nationally representative sample, replicated the nutritional improvement for middle-class people living in metropolitan areas, but found no effect among those with a lower socioeconomic status living in provincial towns. Nutrition importance was the only one of the nine factors tested that moderated the effects of Nutri-Score and explained dietary differences across these groups.

**Research Question**
Whether, when, and how adding the Nutri-Score simplified nutrition label leads fast-food customers to choose meals of higher nutritional quality, including the distinct psychological mechanisms underlying its effects and how those effects vary across socioeconomic status and place of residence.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Presence of the Nutri-Score label on the fast-food menu (binary treatment, coded ½ / −½)
- DV(s): Nutritional quality of the chosen meal (energy-weighted FSAm/HCSP "FSA" score; lower = healthier); secondary outcomes meal energy (kcal) and spending (€)
- Mediators (tested): ease of identifying the healthiest product (the supported pathway); ease of evaluating taste/fillingness/cost; importance of nutrition and other eating goals; risk aversion; regulatory focus (the latter four not significant)
- Moderators: importance of nutrition as an eating goal (the only significant moderator); subjective socioeconomic status, household income, and degree of urbanization of residence

Nutri-Score improves fast-food nutritional quality by facilitating the evaluation of nutritional quality — making it easier to identify the healthiest options — rather than by shifting eating goals, risk attitudes, or regulatory focus. Because the effect operates through making healthiness legible, it only changes behavior for consumers who already attach importance to nutrition. Since nutrition importance is lower among low-SES consumers and those living in provincial towns, the label benefits middle-class metropolitan consumers but has no effect (or backfires) for lower-status provincial consumers, thereby widening pre-existing nutrition inequalities.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the health-nudge literature by documenting consumer heterogeneity along socioeconomic and geographic lines in response to behavioral interventions and by showing that nudges can increase nutrition inequalities. It advances food-labeling research by demonstrating that simplified labels work by facilitating evaluation of nutritional quality rather than by changing eating goals, and by isolating nutrition importance as the mechanism that both moderates label effectiveness and explains socioeconomic and geographic differences in fast-food choices.

**Practical Implication**
For fast-food chains, adopting Nutri-Score was inexpensive, did not create negative inferences about taste or other attributes, and did not hurt (and sometimes increased) revenue, making it attractive for restaurants serving healthier-profile foods such as sandwiches. For policymakers, because labels help only consumers who already prioritize nutrition, complementary strategies (e.g., displaying portion weight to counter the belief that healthy items are less filling) may be needed to avoid widening the socioeconomic diet gap.

**Limitations**
The studies were conducted with a single French sandwich chain (Pomme de Pain) and with the official French label, limiting generalizability to other cuisines, chains, and countries. The online experiments measured consumption intentions rather than actual choices, which can inflate socially desirable responding, and in the field study Nutri-Score recall was imperfect, so label visibility and attention could not be cleanly measured.

**Future Research**
Future work should use eye-tracking to measure the role of label visibility and attention in real-world settings, examine whether socioeconomic differences exist in how consumers process simplified labels, and probe why provincial residents attach less importance to nutrition. Researchers should also study how labeling affects prices, product reformulation and degree of processing, competition across distribution channels, and the influence of other individual characteristics such as political ideology.
