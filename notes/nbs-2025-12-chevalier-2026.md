# Fixed capital and growth imperatives: Is commercial aviation trapped in a treadmill?

**Abstract**
Roadmaps to make commercial aviation net-zero face major challenges, including the lack of time needed to mass-produce sustainable aviation fuels, the growing risks of biomass appropriation, and the persistent inability to decouple sectoral growth from greenhouse gas emissions. Consequently, demand-reduction strategies for aviation emissions will remain ineffective unless the supply-side growth imperatives embedded in the capitalist organization of the industry are addressed. The proposed framework also offers broader insights into the inertia and self-reinforcing metabolic dynamics of capitalist economies.

**Research Question**
The paper examines the supply-side lock-ins linked to growth imperatives that can limit demand-side decarbonization strategies and create inertia in commercial aviation, asking whether the industry is structurally trapped in a growth "treadmill" that makes net-zero unattainable under its current capitalist institutional structure.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core construct: Capital lock-in — investments in large-scale, long-lived fixed assets create structural inertia, with existing material stocks commanding future financial and metabolic flows.
- Four lock-in effects: two financial (the yield constraint *q − c*; the constraint of expanding maximum production capacity) and two physical (minimum operational utilization, e.g., refinery turndown rates; minimum circulating capital for maintenance/repair).
- Defensive investment: firms reinvest to avoid social devaluation of fixed capital from competitive pressure, which converts the yield constraint into a microeconomic growth imperative.
- Emergent outcome: rising organizational and material complexity raises the minimum financial and metabolic throughput thresholds, driving ever-higher production, passenger volumes, resource use, and GHG emissions (a socio-metabolic growth treadmill).

The process integrates an institutionalist Kaleckian model of accumulation (Crotty, 1993; Eichner, 1976) with the sociometabolic nexus framework (Haberl et al., 2021; Pineault, 2023): fixed capital is theorized as a coupling point between the socio-economic accumulation treadmill and its sustaining biophysical throughput. Because each oligopolistic firm is coerced into reinvesting by competitors, micro-, meso-, and macro-level drivers reinforce one another into a structural growth imperative that anchors aviation in a fossil-fuel-based socio-metabolic regime and narrows the "socio-metabolic corridors" available for an ecological transition.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates institutionalist post-Keynesian/Kaleckian theories of the firm with the social-ecological theory of material stocks and flows to model fixed capital as the mechanism linking capital accumulation to a self-reinforcing growth imperative. It extends the carbon lock-in and treadmill-of-production literatures by specifying four micro-level financial and physical lock-in effects, and offers a framework transposable to other capital-intensive industries and to the macroeconomic level.

**Practical Implication**
Demand-reduction measures (flight limits, behavioral shifts such as flygskam, efficiency, fuel substitution, offsets) cannot decarbonize aviation while the supply-side growth imperatives of its capitalist organization remain intact. The authors argue decarbonization requires structural transformation of production and ownership institutions — postcapitalist, degrowth-oriented solutions including a planned wind-down and closure of capacity.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The authors call for research on how ownership and governance of aviation assets could be restructured to escape the growth imperative, what regulatory frameworks could cap or reduce aviation growth while ensuring just transitions for workers and regions, and through what democratic, multi-level governance processes societies could define "essential" versus "non-essential" air travel without reproducing existing power imbalances.
