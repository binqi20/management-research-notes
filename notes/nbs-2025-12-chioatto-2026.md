# “Local Heroes”: Construction firms pioneering circular innovation

Local market orientation exerts a strong positive effect: firms generating at least 50 % of their turnover locally are 15 percentage points more likely to adopt a circular innovation. Regulatory factors are not significant in aggregate, but become decisive for specific circular practices such as selective demolition and the use of sustainable inputs. Theoretically, the study demonstrates that circular innovations follow different adoption dynamics than traditional innovations in the construction industry, with proximity-based networks and local material flows playing a central role. Policy-wise, findings highlight the need to support R&D, especially in smaller firms, strengthen targeted regulatory incentives, and foster local inter-firm collaborations to reduce transaction costs and overcome secondary material quality risks.

**Research Question**
Why do some construction firms adopt Circular Economy Innovations (CEI) while others do not, and how do regulation, R&D expenditure, firm size, and local market orientation shape that adoption? A second question asks whether local market orientation — contrary to its documented negative effect on conventional innovation — positively drives CEI adoption.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Green public-procurement participation (GR_REGULATION), formal R&D investment (RED), firm size (log employees, SIZE), local market orientation (LOCAL, % turnover from local markets)
- DV(s): Adoption of at least one of six circular-economy innovations (aggregate CEI dummy); six practice-specific dummies (sustainable inputs, waste reduction, selective demolition, decontamination, reuse, transfer of waste)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Not reported in paper (heterogeneity examined by disaggregating CEI into six practice-specific models rather than via interaction terms)

R&D investment and firm size are strong, significant predictors of aggregate CEI adoption, with R&D showing the larger marginal effect (0.239) over size (0.126). Local market orientation is positive and highly significant: firms anchored in local markets are more likely to adopt circular practices, especially selective demolition and waste transfer — practices that hinge on inter-firm exchange of secondary materials. The proposed process is that proximity-based local networks lower transaction costs, mitigate quality risks of secondary materials, and ease knowledge exchange through short, close-knit supply chains, so that localization becomes an enabler of (rather than a brake on) circular innovation. Regulation is insignificant in the aggregate but becomes decisive for specific practices (sustainable inputs, selective demolition, decontamination, waste transfer), reflecting the targeted design of Italian green public-procurement rules.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study provides what the authors describe as the first firm-level investigation of CEI adoption determinants in the construction sector, extending general construction-innovation theory to circular-specific practices. Its most original claim is that circular innovation follows different adoption dynamics than conventional innovation: local market orientation — associated with lower innovativeness in prior construction-innovation work — is here a positive driver of CEI, because circular material flows and knowledge exchange are best organized through proximity relationships and short supply chains.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should expand public support for R&D, particularly in smaller firms, through funding programs, tax incentives, or grants targeting resource-intensive CEIs such as selective demolition and decontamination. Regulatory frameworks should reflect the multidimensional nature of CEIs with targeted (not one-size-fits-all) incentives, and should reinforce the creation of inter-firm networks, collaborations, or clusters to leverage local markets, reduce transaction costs, and improve secondary-material quality control.

**Limitations**
The dataset, though rich and original, covers only a single period, constraining the ability to capture the dynamic impact of circular-innovation determinants over time. The evidence is also limited to Emilia-Romagna construction firms, restricting the generalizability of the findings to other territorial contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research should expand both the temporal and geographical scope — moving beyond a single cross-section and a single region — to provide a more comprehensive and robust understanding of the drivers of CEI adoption across different territorial spaces.
