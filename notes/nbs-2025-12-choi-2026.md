# Human Capital Disclosure and Labor Market Outcomes: Evidence from Regulation S‐K

**Abstract**
We examine the labor market consequences of the 2020 Regulation S-K requiring human capital disclosure in 10K filings. Using large-sample joblevel data and a Generative Large Language Model (GLLM), we observe that public firms subject to the regulation increase their disclosure of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) information in job postings relative to a matched sample of large private firms. The increase in job-posting disclosure is more pronounced among firms facing greater external pressure to increase their workforce diversity. **Research Question**
What are the labor market consequences of the 2020 Regulation S-K, which mandates human capital disclosure (and, prominently, DEI information) in 10K filings — specifically, does the regulation change firms' DEI disclosure in job postings and their realized workforce diversity?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Treatment × Post — being a public firm subject to Regulation S-K (vs. matched large private firm) interacted with the post-amendment period (August 2020 onward)
- DV(s): Extent and length of DEI disclosure in job postings — d(DEI) and IHS(DEI Length); workforce gender and ethnic/racial diversity (overall and senior); recruitment outcomes (filling days, interviews, offers tendered/accepted/declined)
- Moderators: Firm's pre-regulation external pressure (low gender diversity, high ESG-fund ownership, greater analyst DEI attention); credible DEI commitment (presence of a DEI officer; magnitude of change in job-posting DEI disclosure); labor-supply availability (geographic/industry presence of women candidates)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper

Because including DEI information in 10Ks is credible and salient, firms facing external pressure are incentivized to raise actual workforce diversity; to do so they first signal commitment by disclosing more DEI information in job postings to attract diverse candidates (a signaling logic in which forthcoming vs. omitted disclosure reveals firm type, per Milgrom [1981]). However, short-term inelastic labor supply of diverse candidates lengthens recruitment, so disclosure rises immediately while measurable diversity gains emerge only about four quarters later and concentrate among firms with credible internal commitment.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study documents that securities regulations can reach beyond capital markets to shape labor market outcomes through disclosure, contributing to the literature on the real effects of disclosure regulation. It extends work on labor-market disclosure and on firm responses to diversity issues by isolating the role of mandated securities disclosure (rather than social movements such as George Floyd's death or #MeToo) in altering firms' hiring and recruiting practices, while underscoring the structural constraints (inelastic labor supply) that limit short-term diversity change.

**Practical Implication**
Principles-based human capital disclosure mandates can prompt firms to change labor-market disclosure and, eventually, workforce composition, but meaningful diversity gains take time and depend on credible internal commitment (e.g., a DEI officer). Regulators and practitioners should anticipate lagged, heterogeneous effects and recognize the supply-side limits of using disclosure rules to shape workforce diversity.

**Limitations**
The authors observe only one state of the world in which a regulatory change and strong social movements occurred within a few months of each other, complicating clean attribution; they mitigate this with a narrow event window, pre-trend tests, and a #MeToo falsification test. Workforce demographics from Revelio Labs are inferred via machine learning rather than self-reported, introducing potential measurement error in diversity measures.

**Future Research**
Future studies could investigate whether Regulation S-K leads to further changes in HR management beyond DEI — such as employee turnover or workplace safety — given that Safety & Health and Employee Development are other value-relevant human capital topics in 10Ks. Such work can inform practitioners and regulators on the consequences of a more prescriptive, rule-based human capital disclosure mandate.
