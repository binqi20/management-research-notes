# Board gender diversity, innovation ambidexterity, and firm performance

**Abstract**
Moderated-mediation models show that the impact of board gender diversity on innovation ambidexterity via prudent experimentation and trickle-down effect is (a) lower in firms in which female directors have greater monitoring responsibilities, and (b) higher in firms in which directors' compensation is tied to longterm goals. Innovations produced by gender-diverse boards contribute positively to firm fundamentals but are undervalued by financial markets. Our study provides a deeper understanding of how governance structures contribute to innovation ambidexterity and organizational performance.

**Research Question**
Does board gender diversity enable innovation ambidexterity—the simultaneous pursuit of innovation efficiency and radical innovation—and through what mechanisms and boundary conditions does this effect operate? The paper also asks whether capital markets correctly value the innovations produced by gender-diverse boards.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Board gender diversity (Wom_pct, lagged proportion of female directors), instrumented by the fraction of male directors who serve on other boards that include at least one female director (Male_onothwomboards_pct).
- DV(s): Innovation ambidexterity, operationalized through four composite measures (High_Both, High_Both_Z, Continuous_Z, Ln_Combined) built from seven patent-based innovation-efficiency and radicalness proxies; firm financial performance (ROA); and long-term innovation-related buy-and-hold abnormal returns (AnnualBHAR).
- Mediators: Prudent experimentation (proxied by patent abandonments, Ln(Abandonments)); organizational trickle-down effect (proxied by the proportion of female inventors, PropFemaleInv).
- Moderators: Monitoring responsibilities of female directors (proportion of committee memberships held by female directors, PropFemaleComm); long-term orientation of compensation (share of director compensation tied to long-term goals, MeanPropLTIP).

Drawing on upper echelons theory, the authors argue that female directors bring distinct behavioral (self-transcendence values, prudent risk assessment), cognitive (information elaboration, divergent thinking), and social (network ties, homophilic role-modeling) characteristics that shift board decision-making toward balanced exploitation and exploration. Two mechanisms convert diversity into ambidexterity: prudent experimentation—initiating uncertain projects while terminating failing ones through patent abandonment—and a trickle-down effect that raises the share of female inventors in gender-diverse R&D teams. These mediating pathways are attenuated when female directors are loaded with monitoring duties (which crowd out strategic and advocacy effort) and amplified when director pay rewards long-horizon value creation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances accounting and governance research by recasting board gender diversity as an enabler of innovation ambidexterity rather than a direct driver of financial performance, and by identifying the specific mediating mechanisms (prudent experimentation, trickle-down) and governance boundary conditions (monitoring load, compensation horizon) that prior upper-echelons work left as a black box. It also extends the accounting literature on (mis)valuation of intangibles by showing that market valuation inefficiencies extend to the governance processes that generate intangible output—markets systematically underreact to innovations produced by gender-diverse boards.

**Practical Implication**
Boards and nominating committees should avoid disproportionately allocating female directors to monitoring-heavy committee roles, which suppresses the innovation benefits of diversity, and should tie director compensation to long-term performance to amplify those benefits. Firms may also need to provide disclosure and guidance to capital-market participants to counter persistent negative stereotypes about women's innovation capabilities that lead markets to undervalue diversity-driven innovation in the short run.

**Limitations**
The study relies on archival patent-based proxies for ambidexterity, which capture behavioral capacity only imperfectly and may overlook some aspects of the contextual "behavioral capacity to simultaneously" pursue incremental and radical innovation. The analysis is confined to gender diversity and to U.S. public firms with patenting activity, and some constructs (e.g., differential director expertise) cannot be fully tested with the available data.

**Future Research**
Future analytical work could examine whether an optimal level of board gender diversity maximizes innovation ambidexterity, and whether the findings generalize to racial, ethnic, and cultural diversity. Researchers could also investigate additional firm-level and environmental factors that moderate the mediated effect, and study how organizations might correct the market's persistent undervaluation of women-led innovation through disclosure.
