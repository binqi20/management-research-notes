**Research Question**
How may we understand resistance in accounting as a fluidity of affects, and what are the implications of this affective grounding of resistance for our understanding of accounting—particularly in donor–NGO relationships within the international development assemblage?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: hybridity and affective ambivalence (Bhabha); parallel attraction toward and repulsion from donors’ managerial and accounting practices in the colonial encounter.
- Triggering conditions: donors’ funding conditionalities, capacity assessments, audit checklists, and shifting (e.g., sustainability) agendas imposed on a grassroots Southern NGO.
- Process / phases: initial attraction via overlapping human-rights commitments → rejection of imposed managerial/bureaucratic paradigm → renewed attraction as the NGO embraces professionalization → ongoing negotiation, re-signification, and dissent.
- Emergent outcomes: resistance reconceptualized as a non-strategic "flux of affects"—the NGO survives, gains legitimacy, and mobilizes its political mission while remaining inside the assemblage.

The paper argues that resistance is not a pre-planned binary opposition but an affective "push-and-pull" in a liminal "third space" of enunciation. Accounting acts as an ambivalent tool: pushed onto the colonial "inferior," it is appropriated, translated, and re-signified by the NGO, simultaneously a device of donor authority and a medium through which the subjugated enact agency. Affective ambivalence is the driving force that converts emotional discomfort into action, producing a hybrid identity that is neither the original activist organization nor a Northern technocratic NGO.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study reconceptualizes resistance in accounting research as a dynamic flux of affects marked by transitions between emotional states, rather than rigid macro/micro strategies of binary opposition. It extends NGO accounting and accountability literature by showing the affective—not merely managerial or legalistic—nature of donor–NGO funding relationships, and theorizes accounting as a product of postcolonial hybridity that mediates the aspirations of both the powerful and the subjugated.

**Practical Implication**
The analysis suggests Southern NGOs can navigate donor conditionalities by selectively appropriating, translating, and re-signifying managerial and accounting practices to serve their own political missions—using compliance and a strong administrative reputation as leverage to negotiate more horizontal, better-funded relationships rather than rejecting donor practices outright.

**Limitations**
The paper does not engage with the political meaningfulness of resistance acts toward emancipation; the disruptive acts observed, whether or not they constitute productive resistance, may not amount to a truly emancipatory program, since they remain anchored in the status quo of the development aid system and the assemblage that defines the limits of oppositional strategy.

**Future Research**
Future studies may explore when acts of resistance contribute to articulating genuinely alternative development and accounting programs, and may dive deeper into the intersection of accounting and culture—for example, contrasting Northern and Southern perceptions of "formality" in accountability.
