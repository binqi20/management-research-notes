# The Unintended Carbon Impacts of Large-Scale Electricity Storage

**Abstract**
Methodology/results: We develop a model to analyze the emission impact of profitmaximizing large-scale storage agents in a competitive electricity market.

**Research Question**
What are the primary drivers of the unintended carbon emissions induced by large-scale electricity storage, under what market conditions does storage increase or decrease emissions, and how can regulators mitigate these emissions while still enabling storage agents to maximize profits?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Although storage operations are directly carbon-neutral (scope 1 ≈ 0), profit-maximizing price arbitrage shifts the dispatch of conventional generators, producing non-zero indirect (scope 2) market-level emissions that can be positive or negative.
- Key constructs: round-trip efficiency (η); the cost gap between cleaner and more-polluting generators; the transaction (buy-sell operation pair) as the unit of emission accounting; the minimum sufficient carbon levy.
- Boundary conditions: results derived as tight worst-case lower/upper emission bounds robust to uncertainty in demand and renewable generation; technical constraints (start-up costs, minimum load, ramping) examined separately and shown not to overturn the core conclusions.

The paper formalizes, in a multiperiod auction-based market model, how a storage agent's arbitrage alters which generators are scheduled and thus how much CO2 the system emits. It proves that a "perfect" (η = 1) storage does not raise emissions only when more-polluting generators also have higher marginal costs; with η below one or when dirty generators are cheaper, even efficient storage can raise emissions. Counterintuitively, higher round-trip efficiency can increase system emissions (by unlocking more polluting transactions), so a steeper marginal-emission-cost curve — and a higher carbon levy — may be required to keep emissions below a target, and a higher levy does not always lower observed emissions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper is the first to extend the prior two-period analysis of storage-induced CO2 emissions to a multiperiod setting and to analyze emissions at the transaction level, deriving tight analytical bounds on the marginal emission rate of storage that depend on the generation mix and storage type. It formalizes analytically the minimum carbon levy sufficient to prevent a storage agent from increasing emissions beyond a target, where prior work only showed levy effects numerically, and it shows that a higher levy can paradoxically increase storage-induced emissions.

**Practical Implication**
The framework lets market participants and regulators assess, interpret, and potentially control the unintended carbon impacts of storage operations. It implies that carbon levies for storage may need to be substantially higher than typical cap-and-trade prices, that there is no monotonic relationship between round-trip efficiency and worst-case emission impact, and that indirect emissions depend on whether solar or wind dominates the renewable mix.

**Limitations**
Real-world factors such as network congestion and multimarket participation are excluded, a simplification the authors note facilitates clear, generalizable insights but limits realism. The robustness of the bounds to uncertainty introduces conservatism, and the analysis focuses on worst-case indirect emissions of storage.

**Future Research**
The authors suggest relaxing the conservatism of the worst-case robust bounds through probabilistic approaches better suited to regulators wishing to target more flexible standards, and extending the framework to incorporate excluded real-world features such as network congestion and multimarket participation. They also point to developing mechanisms that manage emissions at finer temporal resolution.
