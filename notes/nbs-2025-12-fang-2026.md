# Political Segregation in the Labor Market and the Persistence of Pay Gaps

**Abstract**
Many scholars assume that initiatives to promote gender and racial pay equality are largely performative, helping firms appear socially responsible while making little progress. However, the extent to which such initiatives are genuinely supported may depend on the ideological leaning of the field in which they occur. We propose that the higher the ratio of liberals in an industry or occupation, the more its members will be guided by logics that support the equal distribution of resources between gender and racial groups. Using large-scale archival samples of U.S. employees and recent data on U.S. firms and nonprofit organizations, we find support for our theory. The results indicate that membership in liberal-leaning industries and occupations is associated with stronger support for the use of pro-equality practices. Liberal-leaning industries and occupations also showed greater progress toward gender and racial pay equality over time. Notably, we controlled for various alternative explanations, such as demographic and geographic differences between fields. Thus, this paper suggests that the political composition of a field may give rise to logics and norms that shape how efforts to promote group equality are understood and pursued.

**Research Question**
Why have gender and racial pay gaps persisted despite increasingly common organizational equality initiatives? The paper asks whether the political composition (the ratio of liberals to conservatives) of an industry or occupation shapes whether pro-equality logics and norms emerge, and thereby whether equality initiatives are genuinely pursued and pay gaps narrow.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Industry liberalism and occupational liberalism (average self-reported political ideology of a field's members, reverse-scored so higher = more liberal)
- DV(s): Support for pro-equality practices (preferential hiring/promotion of women and Blacks); gender and racial (Black–White) pay gaps in annual wages
- Moderators: Proportion of women / racial minorities in a field; geographic (state) ideology
- Controls: Personal liberalism, age, gender, race, education, region/state fixed effects, industry/occupational polarization, work-related variables; industry and occupational fixed effects

Drawing on field theory, the authors argue that industries and occupations function as fields whose ideological composition is reproduced and reinforced through interorganizational "hubs" (trade associations, conferences, unions, professional groups). A higher ratio of liberals lets pro-equality logics—shared understandings that gender and racial equality are valued and expected—emerge, spread, and become legitimate, so members support pro-equality practices and pay gaps narrow. In conservative fields, equality-supporting members encounter fewer like-minded peers and efforts challenge a meritocratic status quo, so pro-equality logics emerge weakly or not at all and gaps persist.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends research on pay gaps, organizational ideology, and political polarization by theorizing ideology as a field-level (industry/occupational) norm rather than only an attribute of individuals, teams, or firms. It shows that shared ideological logics can emerge even in loosely connected settings through informational hubs, and it identifies political segregation in the labor market as an overlooked driver of societal polarization, reframing slow equality progress as the uneven distribution of pro-equality fields rather than uniformly symbolic effort.

**Practical Implication**
Initiatives to reduce pay gaps should be more effective in liberal fields (where equality is more widely valued) and face greater barriers—but greater room for change—in conservative fields. Efforts are likely more effective when directed toward influential hubs such as industry associations, professional conferences, training centers, and accreditation agencies that can normalize fair-pay and objective-hiring practices across a field.

**Limitations**
The authors could not directly measure whether promoting equality is a logic or norm for each field, so they cannot fully confirm that differing liberal/conservative logics explain the findings. The data lack information on individuals' specific job levels, limiting analysis of pay gaps among similar roles at different hierarchical levels, and other omitted controls may refine the understanding of pay inequality. Data are limited to the United States.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how actors embedded in multiple intersecting fields (e.g., an HR director in the energy sector) navigate conflicting ideological pressures, and whether powerful actors (executives, boards) exert disproportionate influence over a field's ideological leaning. Researchers could measure pro-equality norms directly, incorporate finer-grained job-level indicators, and conduct cross-cultural studies to test whether the patterns hold where institutional support for equality and political polarization differ.
