# Linking Multisite Sex Ad Data at the Individual Level to Aid Counter-Trafficking Efforts

**Abstract**
Methodology/results: Our process leverages concepts and techniques from network science, information systems, and artificial intelligence to link ads across sites at the level of an individual or unique posting entity.

**Research Question**
How can the high volumes of disparate, noisy data from multiple adult service websites be collected, linked at the level of an individual or unique posting entity, and filtered of erroneous links to produce actionable intelligence for counter-trafficking operations?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Graph representation of multisite ASW data; same-user probability threshold (δ) used for edge filtering
- DV(s): Giant-component size reduction, increase in number of extracted components (individuals/posting entities), and computational (elapsed) time
- Mediators: ASW-tailored sentence-transformer embeddings + cosine similarity feeding a logistic-regression "same user" classifier that decides whether to remove an edge
- Moderators: Site characteristics (e.g., data volume, duplication, share of vertices in the giant component) that condition relative performance versus the betweenness-centrality benchmark

The process builds artifacts (post text, perceptual image hashes, contact info) into an undirected graph in which a giant component emerges from generic and misappropriated data. By recognizing this giant component as a known network-science phenomenon, the authors project it to post-text vertices and iterate over edges, using a sentence transformer fine-tuned on ASW posts to score same-user probability and remove low-probability (erroneous) edges without deleting data. This decomposes the giant component into many smaller components, each likely corresponding to one individual or posting entity, yielding more actionable leads than a betweenness-centrality method while running far faster.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper's central contribution is recognizing the emergence of a giant component in the graph representation of sex ad data, establishing a connection between counter-trafficking analytics and network science. It introduces a data-driven, language-model-based edge-filtering procedure that decomposes the giant component without data loss and a novel scheme for generating training labels from nongiant components in the absence of ground truth.

**Practical Implication**
The process transforms large volumes of disparate ASW data into summary and detail outputs that law enforcement and nonprofit partners can use directly to initiate contact with potential victims, minimizing volunteers' exposure to explicit content. Refined over years of practitioner collaboration, it has already supported operations identifying more than 60 potential trafficking victims and can process millions of ads in under an hour for daily updates.

**Limitations**
Not all ads on ASWs are associated with trafficking victims, and the process does not attempt to predict whether any individual is being trafficked. ASWs are only one channel (others include social media and the Dark Web). Because the procedure avoids data loss, scam ads are allowed to persist, and a residual (smaller) giant component often remains whose composition is not fully understood.

**Future Research**
A better understanding of how scam ads manifest in the graph representation is needed. The authors also call for improved tools to discover emerging ASWs and shifts in volume within a highly fluid ecosystem, and for techniques that exploit images to detect erroneous links despite the low-resolution imagery prevalent on these sites.
