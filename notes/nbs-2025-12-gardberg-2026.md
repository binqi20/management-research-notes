# Evaluating Organizations in an Age of Absurdity: How Fake News Disrupts the Relationship Between Business and Society

**Abstract**
Fake news—false or misleading information presented as news—now abounds. As information about firms becomes decreasingly credible, can stakeholders make effective use of their influence to drive firms to engage in socially responsible behaviors? We assembled this special issue to explore the extent to which the social evaluations that underpin stakeholder cognition and action have been compromised and to assess how this affects firm behavior in this era of fake news. The five articles comprising this issue elucidate the strategies used to spread fake news and show how it influences stakeholders. They find that emotionality proves effective in spreading and countering fake news. However, even when fake news is successfully countered, a negative residue remains. **Research Question**
As information about firms becomes decreasingly credible, can stakeholders still make effective use of their influence to drive firms toward socially responsible behaviors—and to what extent does the rise of fake news compromise the social evaluations (reputation, legitimacy, status, stigma, celebrity) that underpin stakeholder cognition and the use of social control to align business with society?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Social evaluations function as a mechanism of social control over business only if three interrelated conditions hold—credible, verifiable signals from accountable intermediaries (antecedent), judgment aggregation toward consensus (formation), and predictable market reward/punishment (outcome).
- Disruption: Fake news laden with emotional appeals corrodes all three conditions—lay experts produce near-costless signals with no reputational stake, signal convergence fails amid algorithmic echo chambers, and ideological/identity-based prior beliefs override organization-specific evaluations.
- Emergent dynamics: Individual entrenchment plus collective fragmentation make evaluations polarized and unstable; emotionality outperforms facts, a strong prior reputation becomes a liability rather than a buffer, and a negative "illusory" residue persists even after fake news is debunked.
- Boundary conditions: Vulnerability likely varies by stakeholders' information asymmetry, direct experience with the firm, valence of that experience, and whether a firm's offerings are search versus experience goods.

The editorial synthesizes five studies to argue that fake news shifts social-evaluation management from substantive to symbolic, crisis-driven impression management. Because emotionally resonant narratives can sustain stakeholder support despite objectively problematic firm conduct—and because moral- and emotion-based evaluations (stigma, celebrity) resist rational correction more than rational ones (reputation)—the disciplinary function of stakeholder disapproval weakens, loosening the tie between firm behavior and social outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial advances a revised perspective on social evaluations that integrates the uncertainty introduced by fake news, specifying how the antecedent, formation, and outcome conditions underpinning social evaluations break down in a compromised information environment. It reframes signaling, stakeholder, and information-asymmetry accounts by distinguishing organization-specific prior beliefs from broader ideological/identity-based priors, and theorizes the conditions under which different social evaluations (reputation vs. stigma vs. celebrity) diverge in their susceptibility to, and recoverability from, fake news.

**Practical Implication**
Firms can no longer rely on a strong prior reputation as a buffer; countering fake news requires moving beyond fact-checking and debunking to manage the emotional intensity and legitimacy crisis that fake news creates, combining verifiable disclosure with emotionally resonant responses while recognizing that defensive narrative management may crowd out substantive reputation-building.

**Limitations**
As a conceptual editorial introducing a special issue, the claims rest on synthesis of five studies rather than new systematic evidence. The contributing papers focus mainly on micro-level evaluators, and differences across stakeholder groups in exposure and vulnerability to fake news remain largely unexplored.

**Future Research**
The authors propose questions across three levels: stakeholder-level (who is vulnerable, how information asymmetry, direct experience, and search vs. experience goods moderate fake-news effects), social-evaluation-level (how fake news differentially affects reputation, stigma, and celebrity given their rational, moral, and emotional bases), and societal-level (which mechanisms can restore the integrity of evaluation processes and whether some evaluations are more resilient or correctable than others).
