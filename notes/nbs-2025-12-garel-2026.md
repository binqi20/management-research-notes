# Firm-Level Nature Dependence

**Abstract**
We employ the scores in two applications. First, the scores relate positively to measures of downside risk, with effects stemming mostly from high dependencies on water-related ecosystem services. Second, the scores predict nature-related incidents that arise when high nature dependence leads firms to damage nature, overuse resources, or trigger disputes with local communities.

**Research Question**
How can a firm's dependence on ecosystem services be quantified at scale, and do these dependencies have financially material consequences for investors and firms? The paper builds new firm-level NatureDep scores and asks whether they are reflected in corporate action, disclosure, investor engagement, stock-market downside risk, and real (incident) outcomes.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): NatureDepOverall (revenue-weighted average dependence across economically relevant ecosystem services) and NatureDepHigh (count of high revenue-weighted ecosystem dependencies, score ≥ 4)
- DV(s): Downside risk (value at risk [VaR] and lower partial moment [LPM]); occurrence of nature-related ESG incidents (RepRisk); likelihood of BlackRock biodiversity engagement; CDP nature actions; earnings-call biodiversity mentions
- Mediators: Nature-related incidents (impacts on ecosystems and biodiversity) as the observable channel linking dependence to tail risk
- Moderators: Asset tangibility (PPE/assets — amplifies downside-risk effect) and occurrence of an incident year (strengthens the dependence-downside-risk relation); diversification tested but non-amplifying

NatureDep scores translate global ENCORE Dependency Materiality Ratings into firm-level exposure to physical nature risk via revenue-segment weighting. Because the loss of a critical ecosystem service is hard to substitute, strong reliance on a few services (NatureDepHigh) generates non-linear tail risk: it predicts downside risk (driven by water-related dependencies) but not average returns. High dependence also raises the likelihood of damaging nature, producing ESG incidents that link physical and transition risks and concentrate downside outcomes in incident years.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the nascent biodiversity and nature-finance literature by introducing novel, scalable firm-level measures of nature dependence and demonstrating a "double materiality" of nature loss — firms with greater impact on nature also tend to depend on it more. It shows that nature dependence is financially material through tail-risk and incident channels rather than average returns, and that investors (e.g., BlackRock) have begun pricing this exposure while corporate action and disclosure lag.

**Practical Implication**
Investors can use NatureDep scores to identify firms most exposed to physical nature risks and address them via divestment (exit) or engagement (voice). Firms that mitigate or hedge nature dependencies may see financial-market benefits as investor awareness grows, and the metrics can help firms meet upcoming nature-related disclosure requirements.

**Limitations**
The measures capture exposure to nature risks rather than risk realizations: they reflect global dependence assessments and do not account for local ecosystem deterioration, the likelihood of disruption, or firms' adaptive capacity, because asset-location data are not yet available at scale. Downside-risk evidence is indirect since the authors cannot condition on specific risk realizations, and ENCORE's annual frequency imperfectly captures the dynamics of living organisms.

**Future Research**
The authors anticipate refinement of the measures, particularly by linking geospatial firm asset-location data with localized ecosystem-service conditions. Further work could examine how nature-related shocks propagate through supply chains, how firms adapt to dependencies, sharpen tests of financial implications with more granular data, and develop easy-to-use dependence metrics for corporate disclosure.
