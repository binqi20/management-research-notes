# Corporate Nature Risk Perceptions

**Abstract**
We survey portfolio companies of a large asset owner to explore the evolving landscape of nature risks. Nearly half of all companies (48 percent) view nature risks as financially material, and 43 percent of those perceive nature-related physical risks, and 27 percent transition risks, as having financial effects already today. Three-quarters of companies experiencing nature-related investor engagement view these interactions as value-generating.

**Research Question**
How do companies perceive nature-related financial risks (their exposure, management, and disclosure), and how do they perceive and respond to investor interest and engagement on these emerging risks?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Financially material nature-risk exposure (transition-risk exposure; physical-risk exposure); experience of nature-related investor engagement; whether engagement influenced strategy/operations.
- DV(s): (Planned) corporate nature-risk actions — impact/dependency assessments, mitigation, offsetting, adaptation, hedging, stress tests, technological innovation, and public disclosure; perceptions of whether investors consider nature risks.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Risk type (transition vs. physical) differentiates effects (e.g., physical—not transition—exposure predicts more hedging).

The paper treats nature risks through the lens of financial materiality (IFRS, 2024): risks matter when they substantially affect a firm's financial position in ways that influence investor decisions. Survey responses show that perceived material exposure is strongly associated with undertaking assessment, mitigation, and disclosure actions, and that investor engagement is associated with higher propensities to act on dependency assessments, risk management, and disclosure—evidence the authors read as supporting causal effects of ESG engagement that are hard to document with archival data. Drawing on feedback-effects reasoning (Bond, Edmans, & Goldstein, 2012), the authors argue a perceived horizon gap and a perceived disconnect between investor attention and cashflow/cost-of-capital analysis could impede efficient incorporation of nature risks into prices.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes early, survey-based evidence to the nascent finance literature on biodiversity and nature risks, documenting corporate (rather than investor or academic) perceptions of an emerging risk type. It surfaces a "fuzziness of the objective function" problem—companies perceive investor attention to nature risks that does not translate into traditional valuation channels (cashflows, discount rates)—posing a challenge for theorists modelling nature risk in corporate finance and asset pricing.

**Practical Implication**
Because financial effects originate from dispersed sources across the entire value chain, no single one-size-fits-all metric (unlike carbon emissions for climate) can capture nature-risk exposure, so risk-management and reporting frameworks must accommodate sectoral and regional heterogeneity. Investor engagement on nature appears value-generating and behavior-changing for many firms, and because climate and nature are widely seen as intertwined, the authors suggest integrating rather than sequencing climate and nature strategies.

**Limitations**
The sample is likely tilted toward companies with higher awareness of and stronger credentials in nature risks, since such firms are more disposed to participate, and the overall response rate is below 6 percent. Because the surveying asset owner is also a major shareholder engaging on nature, responses may be subject to acquiescence bias; the authors run several tests (state/sovereign-fund top-5 indicator, cross-channel t-tests, personal-experience correlations) that show little systematic bias but cannot fully rule it out.

**Future Research**
The authors call for theoretical work on the financial (cashflow and risk) and non-financial channels through which nature risks affect firms, development and integration of metrics capturing nature-risk complexity into risk management and reporting standards, and incorporation of nature-risk features into asset-pricing and valuation models. More empirical work is also needed on how investor engagement contributes to better identification, assessment, and management of financially material nature risks.
