# Towards a Justice-Oriented Human Rights Due Diligence Approach for Migrant Labor

**Abstract**
This article reconceptualizes the structural injustices faced by migrant workers in transnational labor systems characterized by fragmented governance and exploitative recruitment practices. While frameworks like the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights promote corporate human rights due diligence (HRDD), their practical implementation often falls short in addressing the systemic nature of exploitation. We argue for a conceptual shift from "global supply chains" to "Human Supply Networks" to capture the decentralized, relational dynamics of migrant labor sourcing that diffuse accountability and dissipate the force of risk-based HRDD. Drawing on Young's Social Connection Model, we develop a relational responsibility framework that extends beyond liability to include complicity in structural injustice. We conclude by outlining five propositions for a justice-oriented HRDD approach that centers the lived experiences of migrant workers and promotes more meaningful corporate accountability in transnational labor governance.

**Research Question**
How should corporate human rights responsibility be conceptualized in transnational labor contexts, and what constitutes effective HRDD in addressing the structural injustices faced by migrant workers across fragmented "Human Supply Networks"?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Risk-based HRDD rooted in the UNGPs' causation/contribution/direct-linkage trichotomy presumes traceable agent-centric pathways and therefore cannot reach the systemic, diffuse harms produced across Human Supply Networks (HSNs).
- Key constructs: Human Supply Networks (HSNs); dependence connection; existential connection; attributability vs. accountability; "normativity of the normalized"; strategic vs. communicative rationality.
- Five propositions: HRDD must let a firm account for (1) dependence connections and (2) existential connections to unjust structures; (3) respond to both isolated norm violations and normalized systemic practices; (4) engage in stakeholder deliberation to promote ethically reflexive practices; and (5) respond to both embedded processes and the lived outcomes experienced by migrant workers.
- Boundary conditions: Peripheral actors often fall beyond even large MNEs' governance reach, yet still contribute to aggregate outcomes; responsibility is temporally contingent and varies with a firm's capacity to influence, monitor, and orchestrate.

Building on Young's Social Connection Model, the authors relocate corporate responsibility from fault and causation to structural connectedness: firms that benefit from or depend on exploitative migrant-labor arrangements bear a collective, forward-looking responsibility to transform the background conditions that sustain harm. Because structural injustice is reproduced through routine, individually permissible actions whose normalization erodes moral urgency, a justice-oriented HRDD must couple a process focus with accountability for outcomes and balance strategic rationality against communicative, dialogical engagement with affected workers.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances five interrelated contributions: it reconceptualizes transnational labor processes as Human Supply Networks; it grounds corporate human rights responsibility in relationality rather than attribution by distinguishing dependence and existential connections; it builds a justice-oriented HRDD framework around five interacting propositions; it extends Young's Social Connection Model with the under-theorized "normativity through normality" mechanism that morally invisibilizes structural harm; and it foregrounds the strategic-versus-communicative rationalities shaping corporate behavior in HSNs.

**Practical Implication**
HRDD frameworks should move beyond linear, compliance-driven supply-chain audits to map both formal and informal HSN actors, open channels through which migrant workers articulate risks and demand remedy, and evaluate lived outcomes (e.g., wage continuity, protection from retaliation). The framework presses for reforms such as visa portability, employer-pays recruitment, systemic fee abolition, and worker-led monitoring, and calls on regulators and standard setters to revise guidance to reflect this expanded, outcome-oriented understanding of responsibility.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that substantial theoretical and practical work remains and that operationalizing a network perspective spanning the full migration life cycle presents analytical and governance challenges. As a conceptual article, the framework is illustrated through interpretive cases rather than empirically tested, and the HSN construct is not yet empirically mapped.

**Future Research**
They call for empirically and conceptually mapping HSNs (via comparative case studies, network analyses, and ethnography); refining governance tools, metrics, and benchmarks to fit the migration life cycle; designing and evaluating migrant-worker voice and knowledge mechanisms that move beyond tokenism; studying how normalization erodes moral urgency; examining how instrumental and communicative rationalities interact in corporate decision-making; and pursuing interdisciplinary, cross-sectoral work on sharing accountability across decentralized systems.
