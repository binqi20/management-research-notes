# Introduction to the special issue: ‘Biodiversity and finance: Risk, disclosure and double materiality’

**Abstract**
Not reported in paper

**Research Question**
How can biodiversity be integrated into financial logic, given that the financial sector has remained largely unresponsive to accelerating biodiversity loss despite the two-way relationship between biodiversity-related risk and the performance of financial assets?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: The financial sector channels capital through lending, investment, insurance, and asset management, yet has remained largely unresponsive to biodiversity loss.
- Key construct — biodiversity-related risk: Biodiversity loss poses both physical risks (degraded ecosystems reducing returns on nature-dependent sectors) and transition risks (regulatory, legal, or market changes).
- Two-way relationship: Financial flows can themselves exacerbate biodiversity loss (e.g., financing deforestation-linked infrastructure, insurance underwriting for high-risk extractive industries).
- Structural barriers: The difficulty of monetizing ecosystem services, the public-goods nature of biodiversity, and misaligned fiduciary incentives keep biodiversity marginal in financial decision-making.
- Proposed reframing — ecological finance: Treating economies as open subsystems of the biosphere, subject to biophysical constraints, limits to substitution between capitals, and ecological timescales.

The introduction motivates the special issue by arguing that biodiversity remains marginal in financial decision-making despite a documented two-way relationship: biodiversity loss undermines the long-term viability of underlying economic activities and thus the value of financial assets, while financial flows can directly and indirectly accelerate that loss. Drawing on ecological economics, which views the economy as fundamentally embedded in nature rather than external to it, the editors call for moving beyond narrow risk framing toward an "ecological finance" that confronts the systemic challenge of integrating biodiversity into financial logic, while acknowledging the public-goods obstacle that makes private financial returns from biodiversity conservation difficult to capture.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The piece advances the framing of an emergent field of "ecological finance" grounded in ecological economics, extending the view of economies as open subsystems of the closed biosphere to the financial system, and positing that if global financial returns are subject to biophysical constraints, the financial system would have an interest in conserving nature.

**Practical Implication**
It signals to investors, insurers, and policymakers that biodiversity-related physical and transition risks materially affect financial assets, and that policy efforts to "crowd-in" private finance for biodiversity conservation must contend with the public-goods nature of biodiversity's economic benefits.
