# Do sustainability reports contain financially material information?

**Abstract**
Recent years have witnessed significant growth in corporate sustainability reporting. Yet existing research provides mixed evidence on the information content of these reports for investors. We examine the stock market reaction to the announcement of a sample of US corporate sustainability reports incorporating Sustainability Accounting Standards Board metrics that are intended to provide financially material information to investors. Using standard measures of information content, we cannot find compelling evidence that these reports provide a significant amount of new information to investors. Further analysis of a subset of common metrics indicates that they are either financially immaterial or preempted by traditional financial disclosures. Finally, we show that most firms target their sustainability reports at a broad set of sustainability-oriented stakeholders rather than a narrow set of financially oriented investors.

**Research Question**
Do corporate sustainability reports that incorporate SASB metrics — designed to be financially material and decision-useful — actually convey new, financially material information to investors, as measured by stock-price and trading-volume reactions to their announcement?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Announcement of a standalone corporate sustainability report containing SASB metrics (vs. non-announcement trading days; and benchmarked against the nearest quarterly earnings announcement); cross-sectional moderators I(Inaugural SASB Report), I(Related Form 8-K Filed), I(External Assurance).
- DV(s): Three-day cumulative abnormal return (CAR_t-1,t+1), Abnormal Volatility, and Abnormal Volume around the announcement date; 60-day post-announcement CAR drift.
- Mediators: None modeled.
- Moderators: Inaugural-report status, related Form 8-K filing, external assurance.

Adapting Beaver's (1968) information-content paradigm, the authors test whether the release of SASB-aligned sustainability information moves prices or volume. Abnormal Volatility (1.046) and Abnormal Volume (0.069) are statistically indistinguishable from their no-information benchmarks, whereas the matched earnings announcements show large, highly significant reactions (Abnormal Volatility 3.757; Abnormal Volume 1.328). Supplemental tests trace the null to two mechanisms: the metrics (e.g., monetary losses from legal proceedings) are typically financially immaterial, and in the rare material cases the information is preempted by mandatory Form 8-K/10-K disclosures. Document-type and website-positioning analyses show most firms target the reports at a broad stakeholder audience rather than investors.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides the first direct event-study evidence on the information content of periodic corporate sustainability reports containing SASB metrics, reaching the opposite conclusion to Beaver's (1968) seminal finding for earnings. It reconciles prior mixed value-relevance results by showing that associations documented elsewhere need not reflect incremental decision-usefulness, and it reframes sustainability reporting as primarily addressed to impact-materiality concerns of broad stakeholders rather than to financial materiality for investors.

**Practical Implication**
Because US SASB-aligned sustainability reports do not appear to deliver significant new financially material information, standard setters (SASB, TCFD, ISSB) and securities regulators cannot justify mandatory sustainability disclosure on financial-materiality grounds alone; an impact-materiality focus (as adopted by the GRI and the EU CSRD) may better serve actual users. Investors and reporting firms should reassess the costs and benefits of producing and analyzing such reports.

**Limitations**
Results are specific to the sample of US sustainability reports examined and may not generalize. Firms' decisions about whether, where, and how (e.g., with a press release) to disclose SASB metrics are endogenous, and the sample-selection requirements may bias toward reports containing relatively less information. SASB standards and the financial consequences of the underlying impacts are nascent and evolving.

**Future Research**
The financial materiality of sustainability metrics may rise as standards improve and as governments impose greater financial penalties (e.g., carbon taxes) for negative impacts, warranting re-examination over time. The evidence speaks only to financial materiality and is silent on impact materiality, leaving open the assessment of sustainability reports against impact-materiality objectives and broader stakeholder uses.
