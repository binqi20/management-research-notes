# How negative accounting news events, voluntary ESG assurance, and assurance provider influence consumer purchasing intentions

**Abstract**

**Research Question**
Do conscientious consumers treat negative accounting news events (financial restatements) as surrogate indicators of the reliability of a firm's unobservable ESG product-quality claims, thereby lowering consumer purchasing intentions (CPI), and can voluntary ESG product-quality assurance — and the choice of assurance provider — mitigate this effect?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Restatement type (no restatement / error / irregularity); ESG product-quality assurance and assurance provider (no assurer / USDA government agency / financial statement auditor / non-financial-statement audit firm) — a 4 × 3 between-subjects design
- DV(s): Consumer purchasing intentions (CPI), measured on a seven-point Likert scale
- Mediators: Organizational legitimacy (OL) perceptions (restatements → lower OL → lower CPI)
- Moderators: Presence/type of ESG assurance, which moderates the "backside" OL → CPI path (PROCESS Model 14)

Building on organizational legitimacy theory and the credence-goods literature, the paper argues that because ESG claims are credence attributes consumers cannot verify, consumers rely on surrogate signals; a financial restatement signals diminished organizational legitimacy and is generalized into doubt about the firm's ESG claims, eroding CPI more severely for intentional irregularities (fraud) than for unintentional errors. Voluntary third-party ESG product-quality assurance acts as a more direct, reliable surrogate of production quality, blunting the indirect negative effect of restatements on CPI by weakening the OL → CPI link. However, when the firm's own financial statement auditor provides the ESG assurance, that provider's proximity to the restatement undermines perceived assurance reliability — though under fraud, all providers are discounted because management's willingness to deceive lowers assurance reliability in general.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study bridges accounting, ESG, and marketing research by extending organizational-legitimacy and credence-goods theory to consumers — an audience accounting research has largely overlooked in favor of shareholders, creditors, auditors, and employees. It shows that accounting events function as surrogate information for consumers' beliefs about a firm's ESG claims and that assurance-provider identity conditions the protective value of ESG assurance, demonstrating a transfer of (un)reliability perceptions across an auditor's distinct assurance services.

**Practical Implication**
Executives and boards should recognize that negative accounting events — even those unrelated to ESG claims — depress consumer purchasing intentions, and that voluntary ESG product-quality assurance can shield brand image and minimize market-share losses. Boards should obtain such assurance for credence claims and select providers carefully, generally avoiding the firm's own financial statement auditor as the ESG assurance provider when restatement risk exists.

**Limitations**
It is difficult to determine how consumers weigh negative accounting events against other factors such as product loyalty and purchasing habits. The study examines voluntary (not mandatory) assurance and ESG product-quality assurance (akin to an agreed-upon-procedures engagement) rather than broader ESG/sustainability-report information assurance, so effect magnitudes may not transfer. The sample comprises consumers willing to pay a premium for cage free eggs, so findings may not fully generalize to other consumers or non-credence goods.

**Future Research**
Future work could quantify how consumers weigh accounting news against loyalty and habit, assess the costs of establishing and maintaining certification, and test whether effects hold in a mandatory-assurance regime. Researchers could also contrast the impacts of ESG disclosure assurance versus ESG product-quality assurance, extend the design to non-credence goods, and examine how different consumer segments respond to assurance claims.
