# Reimagining Growth Futures: Overcoming the False Binary between Green Growth and Degrowth

**Abstract**
We find that there are substantial disagreements within as well as agreements between green growth and degrowth. In consequence, we argue that the literature is caught in a false binary. To constructively move the debate forward, we propose giving up the paradigmatic and polarized approach and instead embracing a multidimensional plurality of imagined growth futures.

**Research Question**
The paper asks whether green growth and degrowth are genuinely mutually exclusive, internally coherent, competing paradigms, or whether the debate is "caught in a false binary" that obscures substantial intra-paradigm disagreements and cross-paradigm overlaps in how a green transition can be imagined.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Green growth and degrowth are better understood as "orientational paradigms" (a thinner version of Kuhnian paradigms) rather than distinct, internally consistent scientific paradigms or opposed ecopolitical projects.
- Key constructs / dimensions: Nine inductively derived dimensions of variation, grouped into three meta-categories — national institutions (agents of change, mechanisms of change, economic system, political institutions), world order (international economic integration, global applicability, international redistribution), and scientific cosmology (technological progress, understanding of history).
- Process / method: An integrative literature review maps each position along a continuum (extreme A to opposite B) within both green growth and degrowth, surfacing internal inconsistencies and mutual overlaps.
- Emergent outcome: A shift from a two-pole dichotomy to a multidimensional landscape of possible growth futures, revealing common ground (e.g., shared appeals to stronger global governance, technologically neutral Green New Deal thinking).

By demonstrating that each paradigm spans wide internal variation (e.g., green growth running from neoliberal carbon pricing to green industrial policy; degrowth running from local self-sufficiency to digitally enabled planning), the authors show the binary is "false." The straw-manning by which each side mischaracterizes the other reproduces an illusory paradigm; re-reading both as overlapping orientational paradigms within ecological economics builds the case for pluralizing the debate.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper re-conceptualizes green growth and degrowth as overlapping "orientational paradigms" rather than competing, internally coherent ecopolitical projects, and offers a nine-dimensional analytical framework (spanning national institutions, world order, and scientific cosmology) for mapping the range of standpoints. This moves theorizing of growth futures from a restrictive dichotomy toward a multidimensional landscape, and contributes to the disciplinary distinction between ecological and mainstream economics.

**Practical Implication**
The authors hope the framework can inspire policy proposals and reforms that combine elements previously assigned to opposing camps — for example, pairing planning with carbon pricing and industrial policy to move from green capitalism toward green mixed economies or ecosocialism — to deliver a rapid and just green transition.

**Limitations**
The nine dimensions cover only identified actual disagreements, not all possible ones, and the authors explicitly do not consider them the final or ultimate framework for understanding sustainability and growth. The mapping is illustrative rather than a comprehensive or representative review of the literature, and examples are offered to support points rather than as exhaustive evidence.

**Future Research**
Future scholarship can use the proposed dimensions to avoid assuming homogeneity within, and difference between, the two positions, and can extend or revise the framework with entirely different dimensions and categorizations. Researchers could also probe more deeply the repercussions of treating green growth and degrowth as orientational paradigms for ecological economics, and further establish how this reframing affects the relationship between ecological and mainstream economics.
