# Can objective information update subjective beliefs on sustainable farming? Evidence from a randomized experiment with Japanese rice farmers

**Abstract**
Participants are randomly assigned to one of three groups: a control group receiving only qualitative information (C), a treatment group receiving average income change information about OF and HCR (T1), and a treatment group receiving distributional income change information about OF and HCR (T2). We estimate the effect of the information by eliciting farmers’ subjective beliefs about income changes associated with adopting OF and HCR both before and after the treatment. The results suggest that subjective beliefs can be updated by objective information, particularly when farmers receive favorable information. Additionally, the findings from a choice experiment indicate that both the subjective mean and subjective standard deviation contribute to farmers’ willingness to adopt sustainable farming practices. These findings show the potential of offering objective information to revise subjective beliefs as an effective strategy for promoting the adoption of sustainable farming practices.

**Research Question**
Can providing objective information about the income changes experienced by current adopters update conventional rice farmers' subjective beliefs (mean and variability) about the profitability of environmentally sustainable practices, and do these beliefs in turn shape willingness to adopt?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Information treatment arm (control = qualitative only; T1 = average income-change information; T2 = distributional income-change information); the perception gap between objective information and the farmer's prior subjective belief; their interaction (Info × PercGap), which identifies the learning rate θ.
- DV(s): Update in subjective mean and subjective standard deviation of income change (post − pre) for Organic Farming (OF) and Half Chemical Reduction (HCR); and, in the choice experiment, the willingness/utility to adopt OF or HCR versus conventional farming.
- Mediators: Updated subjective mean and standard deviation are the channel through which information affects stated adoption intentions (subjective beliefs interacted with adoption indicators in the mixed logit).
- Moderators: Whether the information is favorable ("good news") versus unfavorable ("bad news"); elicited CPT risk/loss-aversion preferences; respondent age and gender (robustness).

The mechanism is partial Bayesian-style belief updating: farmers shift their prior subjective distribution toward the objective information at a learning rate θ that depends on the prior-information gap, but updating is incomplete (θ = 0.268 for the OF mean, 0.123 for the HCR standard deviation) because the population-average information is not seen as fully applicable to a farmer's own circumstances. Updating is asymmetric — it occurs only for favorable information (higher OF profitability, lower HCR variability) and not for unfavorable information, consistent with optimism bias and information-avoidance accounts. Because higher subjective means raise and higher subjective variances lower adoption intentions, information that revises beliefs upward translates into greater willingness to adopt, with simulations showing information provision is roughly equivalent to a 4,000 JPY/10a subsidy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper is the first to elicit farmers' subjective beliefs about the opportunity costs (income changes) of adopting sustainable practices and to show experimentally that such beliefs can be updated by objective information, extending the literature that treats perceptions as fixed barriers to adoption. It documents asymmetric ("good-news/bad-news") belief updating among farmers in the agri-environmental domain and links updated beliefs about both the mean and the variance of income to adoption decisions, integrating belief-updating and risk-preference (Cumulative Prospect Theory) perspectives.

**Practical Implication**
Information campaigns that credibly convey the realized profitability of current adopters can cost-effectively raise adoption of sustainable practices, with an estimated policy effect equivalent to a 4,000 JPY/10a subsidy. Because updating is asymmetric and credibility-dependent, policymakers should pair information with peer-to-peer learning, early-adopter "reference points," and demonstration projects, and design transparent, contextually appropriate messaging.

**Limitations**
Subjective beliefs were elicited about an inherently unobservable outcome, so incentive-compatible mechanisms were infeasible and some measurement error may remain. The choice experiment was hypothetical (mitigated only by a cheap-talk script), and the online-panel sample is younger and more male than the national rice-farmer population, limiting external validity. Post-treatment beliefs were measured immediately, so persistence is unknown.

**Future Research**
Field experiments and incentive-compatible designs could test belief updating and actual adoption behavior in real-world settings. Studies should examine other farmer populations, crops, and countries and additional sources of heterogeneity (education, crop type, prior familiarity, regional conditions) to assess generalizability. Longitudinal designs with short- and long-term follow-ups are needed to learn whether belief changes persist and translate into adoption.
