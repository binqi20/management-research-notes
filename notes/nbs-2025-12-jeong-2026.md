# Bottom‐up effects of female strategic leadership: Firm performance effects through employees

**Abstract**
We propose a complementary, bottom-up view: female top managers foster employee participation, which is positively associated with firm performance. Using a unique panel dataset from South Korea, we find that female representation in top management is linked to higher employee participation, which mediates its relationship with firm performance. The benefits of employee participation are especially pronounced in firms with innovation-oriented or labor-intensive strategies. By integrating micro-level employee behaviors with macro-level firm outcomes, our study shows how female strategic leaders contribute to firm performance through a top-enabled, bottom-up process.

**Research Question**
How — not whether — does female representation in top management influence firm performance? The authors ask whether female top managers improve firm performance through a bottom-up pathway in which they foster employee participation, complementing prior top-down and perception-based explanations.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Female representation in top management (number of female top managers / im-won)
- DV(s): Firm performance (Return on Assets, ROA, winsorized at 5%)
- Mediators: Employee participation (firm-level aggregate of employee voice, inclusion in decision-making, and autonomy)
- Moderators: Female representation in middle management (on IV→mediator, H2, unsupported); firm innovation intensity (R&D/sales) and firm labor intensity (labor costs/sales) (each on mediator→DV, H4 and H5, supported)

Drawing on upper echelons theory and strategy-process research, the authors argue that female top managers' more democratic, participative leadership style raises employee participation, which activates bottom-up autonomous strategic processes (Burgelman, 1983a). These processes enhance firm performance by enabling exploration of new competencies and by fostering employee "buy-in" and commitment. Consistent with microfoundations theorizing, employee participation mediates the macro-level link between female top-management representation and firm performance (indirect-only mediation), and the participation–performance link is amplified where firms emphasize innovation or are labor-intensive.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends upper echelons theory by opening the "relational black box" between top managers and employees, showing that TMT composition shapes not only top-down decision processes but also bottom-up autonomous strategic processes operating through employees. It contributes employee-level microfoundations to research on female strategic leadership and firm performance, and argues the "business case" and "social justice case" for gender diversity are more compatible than previously thought, since female leaders broaden employee voice (social justice) which in turn benefits performance (business case).

**Practical Implication**
Firms can realize performance benefits from female strategic leadership by empowering and soliciting voice from all employees, thereby activating bottom-up autonomous strategic processes. Because the business and social-justice rationales for gender diversity are complementary, organizations need not choose between "what women can do for firms" and "what firms can do for women and all employees."

**Limitations**
The theory is tested only with South Korean data, where female representation in top management is very low and gender equality ranks comparatively low, so generalizability to other contexts is untested (likely a conservative setting). Despite GEE, instrumental-variables regression, and coarsened exact matching, endogeneity is not fully eliminated; the authors interpret results as robust associations rather than causal evidence.

**Future Research**
Future studies should test the bottom-up model in varied national and institutional contexts to assess generalizability, and should adopt experimental methods and other robust designs for triangulation to further address endogeneity. The authors also encourage broader adoption of multi-wave, multi-source datasets like the HCCP to examine cross-level macro–micro mechanisms.
