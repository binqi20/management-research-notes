# Alienable or inalienable, and how? Individual property rights in commons governance

**Abstract**
By assessing social-ecological performance and conducting a qualitative analysis of transaction costs, we analyze how these three institutions evolved and what outcomes they produced. We find that quota alienability doesn't increase grazing intensity.

**Research Question**
How do community institutional arrangements evolve to integrate or resist market mechanisms within commons and support sustainable and equitable resource governance? Specifically, how should individual grazing rights be made alienable (and through which legal protection — property rules, liability rules, or inalienability) within a common property regime?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / entitlement types: inalienability; property rules (voluntary transfer at consented price); liability rules (transfer compensated at a price set by an authoritative third party — here the village collective).
- Process / phases: Stage 1 (2009-2013) inalienable quotas with informal "iron yak" reciprocal lending; Stage 2 (2013-2018) alienable quotas under property rules with bilaterally negotiated prices; Stage 3 (since 2018) alienable quotas under liability rules via collective-monitored compensation.
- Triggering conditions: pre-2009 overgrazing/"internal open access" prompts the quota system; "poor sells cheap" inequities and low/zero negotiated prices under property rules trigger the shift to liability rules.
- Mediating mechanism: transaction costs (information, negotiation, enforcement) — collective monopolization of trading lowers search/negotiation costs while keeping monitoring costs low.
- Emergent outcomes: alienability does not raise grazing intensity; liability rules improve distributive equity and satisfaction (66.7% → 88.1%) and reduce conflict.
- Boundary conditions: efficacy of liability rules is not inherent but depends on strong collective organization, broad participation of poorer households, low monitoring costs, and local norms (oath-keeping, fairness).

The paper traces an evolutionary process in which a Tibetan pastoral community sequentially redesigns the legal protection of individual grazing quotas. Drawing on the Calabresi & Melamed framework and Coasean transaction-cost reasoning, it shows that substituting collective third-party compensation (liability rules) for bilateral bargaining (property rules) lowers negotiation and enforcement costs and counters the "poor sells cheap" problem, so that alienable rights can be integrated into the commons without ecological harm or distributive loss.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study complements the common property rights framework by foregrounding the alienability dimension of individual rights nested within commons, and demonstrates the enduring viability of the Calabresi & Melamed framework as an analytical tool for commons research. It provides empirical evidence that liability rules — rather than the more-studied property rules — can be a feasible governance pathway, and theorizes the commons as a "dual-structured" arrangement in which legally recognized private use rights and informally negotiated communal contracts are interdependent.

**Practical Implication**
Communities facing marketization pressure can retain quota exchangeability through a collectively managed compensation mechanism (liability rules) rather than fully privatizing or rejecting markets, thereby protecting alienable individual rights while safeguarding distributive equity and ecological sustainability. The approach may inform governance of other measurable, relatively homogeneous resources (e.g., grazing quotas, carbon-emission allowances, single-species fisheries) and community-based water markets that use clearinghouses to mediate pricing.

**Limitations**
The qualitative comparison of transaction costs, though grounded in long-term observation, remains somewhat subjective, and data-availability constraints prevent the authors from systematically quantifying these measurements. The findings derive from a single community in one ecological and cultural context, limiting generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future work incorporating a broader range of geographical areas and ecological contexts can address the study's contextual limitations, and more longitudinal, interdisciplinary studies would improve understanding of institutional change and governance adaptation. The authors propose for further inquiry the hypothesis that quota-based, nested property arrangements are most applicable to resource systems whose extraction units are measurable, relatively homogeneous, and directly tied to the underlying system's sustainability.
