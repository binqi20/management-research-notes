# Does an exclusive relationship with government banks matter during a climate shock?

**Abstract**
We provide novel evidence on the role of firms’ banking relationships with government banks (GOBs) during a climate-related shock when relief funds are unavailable. Using variation in the locations of rainfall shocks and firms’ banking relationships, we find that firms maintaining exclusive banking relationships with GOBs (GOB firms) secure more debt relative to other firms during rainfall shocks. We do not find such effects for firms that maintain exclusive relationships with private banks, foreign banks, or maintain multiple banking relationships. We also find that GOB relationships are particularly beneficial for firms that are more vulnerable to rainfall shocks, have long-term relationships with GOBs, and are, at the same time, healthier compared to other firms. With regard to real effects, GOB firms invest more and remain profitable than other firms during rainfall shocks. Overall, our results highlight the benefits of GOB relationships for firms during climate shocks.

**Research Question**
Does maintaining an exclusive banking relationship with government-owned banks (GOBs) help firms secure credit, invest, and remain profitable during a climate-related shock when explicit government disaster aid is unavailable?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Abnormal Rain (treated subdivision-year, extreme rainfall departure quintiles) × GOB Firm (firm with exclusive GOB banking relationship at year start)
- DV(s): Firm debt and debt net of cash (scaled by total assets); debt components (bank, secured, long-term, short-term, unsecured, trade credit); interest rate; investment; profitability (ROA)
- Mediators: Access to bank credit from relationship GOBs (the credit channel through which liquidity reaches GOB firms)
- Moderators: Firm vulnerability to rainfall (rain-sensitivity), financial constraint, relationship duration (2–4 consecutive years), and firm health (excluding distressed/zombie/opaque firms); collateralizable tangibility, low profitability, low liquidity

When relief funds are absent, rainfall acts as an exogenous local credit-demand shock. GOBs, created to address market failures and possessing proprietary information about their relationship firms, extend countercyclical credit only to firms exclusively tied to them—consistent with the relationship-banking insurance channel—while private/foreign banks hold up and multi-bank firms suffer coordination failure. Because GOBs absorb the credit-risk increase without raising interest rates and lend to financially constrained but healthier firms (no crowding out, no politically directed lending), GOB firms can invest in risky-but-profitable projects and outperform comparable firms during abnormal rainfall.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the climate-finance literature by showing that exclusive GOB relationships help firms access credit during climate shocks in a setting where government aid is unavailable—contrasting with prior evidence that GOBs ration credit during such shocks. It advances the "social view" of government banking by documenting that GOBs extend countercyclical support to constrained-but-healthy relationship firms without political direction, and it extends relationship-banking theory by showing that the bank-insurance channel (not hold-up) operates for GOBs, whereas private/foreign banks hold up and multiple transactional relationships become disadvantageous during distress.

**Practical Implication**
Maintaining a close, exclusive relationship with government banks is a potential solution for firms seeking finance during climate shocks, especially in economies where GOBs are prevalent and disaster aid is unavailable. The findings offer policymakers in similar developing economies guidance for designing climate-related policies and supporting banks to ameliorate the effects of climate change.

**Limitations**
The dataset does not allow direct observation of loan interest rates, so total interest expense scaled by total debt is used as a proxy. The empirical design is associational/quasi-experimental rather than a controlled experiment, relying on the parallel-trends assumption and the exogeneity of rainfall shocks. Data are licensed (Prowess/CMIE), constraining replication, and several supporting analyses are reported only in the Supplementary Appendix.
