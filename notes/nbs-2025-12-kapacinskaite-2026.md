# From wells to windmills: Resource redeployment and new technology investment in the energy sector

These findings suggest that firms facing industry shocks can redeploy resources into more promising sectors, but their propensity to do so may depend on the possibility of leveraging existing assets across domains.

**Research Question**
How do multi-business firms redeploy non-scale-free (financial) resources across industries following a negative industry shock, and under what conditions does such inter-industry redeployment stimulate investment in new technology in the recipient industry? The study examines oil and gas firms diversified into wind power after the 2014 oil price crash.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Wind power activity of oil and gas firms (Number of windfarms; Windfarm expenditure); presence of an oil and gas firm / windfarm (O&G firm, O&G windfarm); Post (pre- vs. post-2014 oil price drop); proximity of windfarm to nearest offshore oil and gas field (within 1, 10, 20, 30 km); their interactions with Post.
- DV(s): Oil and gas contract expenditure (mln USD); windfarm expenditure (mln USD); turbine capacity (MW) and related windfarm technical characteristics.
- Mediators: None formally modeled (process is interpreted via redeployment of "unused productive services" and asset co-location).
- Moderators: Asset co-location (geographic proximity to existing offshore oil and gas fields); country-level employment-protection strength (OECD Employment Protection Index), which attenuates redeployment.

Following the exogenous 2014 oil price collapse, a subset of wind-diversified oil and gas firms cut oil and gas spending—especially on complex offshore contracts—and redirected financial resources into wind power, consistent with diverting "unused productive services" (Penrose, 1959). These redeploying firms invested more than wind-industry rivals, and adopted newer, more powerful turbines, but only where windfarms were co-located with existing offshore oil and gas assets, indicating that the ability to leverage complementary, geographically proximate capabilities governs both whether redeployment occurs and whether it pushes the technological frontier. Stronger employment-protection laws dampened, but did not offset, the redeployment effect.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends theory on resource redeployment by linking inter-industry redeployment to technology choice in the recipient industry, showing that a negative shock (exogenously low prices) induces firms to reallocate resources to the closest marginal use—selecting challenging, higher-return projects—rather than to invest across the board, in contrast to settings where a positive shock raises cash flows (Helfat, 1997). It provides granular, large-sample empirical evidence of redeployment, foregrounds asset co-location as a driver of new-technology investment, and develops the notion of inter-temporal appropriability whereby firms capture value from new uses of existing resources.

**Practical Implication**
Only about 2.5% of firms redeployed resources from oil and gas to wind power even after a major shock, suggesting that market-based inducements alone are unlikely to drive meaningful industrial change or accelerate the energy transition. Firms are more likely to transition where they can co-locate new renewable projects with existing offshore assets; policymakers should recognize that incentives interact with firm-level capabilities, asset proximity, and labor-market institutions.

**Limitations**
The study is based on a specific two-industry setting in the energy sector, limiting generalizability. The author cannot precisely measure the specific human capital and machinery redeployed from oil and gas, and the analysis captures only one possible recipient industry (wind power), likely underestimating total redeployment.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine the competitive and technological implications of resource redeployment for rivals in the recipient industry, measuring their outcomes more directly. It could also further investigate the role of oil price fluctuations in the broader energy transition from hydrocarbons to renewables.
