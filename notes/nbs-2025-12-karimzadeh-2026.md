# Waste delinking: A pathway to degrowth?

**Abstract**
Using recent evidence on plastic, textile, and electronic waste, the article shows how closing export outlets constrains growth and can catalyze systemic transformation.

**Research Question**
Can a targeted practice of "waste delinking" — strategically disengaging high-income regions from transnational waste trade — serve not as a by-product of degrowth but as a concrete policy lever that triggers degrowth-oriented transformation of production–consumption systems?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Cross-border waste trade lets the Global North externalize the ecological and social costs of overproduction/overconsumption onto the Global South, sustaining unsustainable growth; closing this "offshore escape valve" re-imposes material limits.
- Key constructs: waste delinking (an upstream strategy internalizing environmental/social costs where waste is generated); waste colonialism; ecologically unequal exchange; ecological sovereignty.
- Deterrent mechanisms (activated when waste trade is constrained): (1) source-reduction pressure within domestic treatment capacity; (2) rising marginal costs and siting conflicts for incineration/landfill; (3) accelerating investment in prevention, reuse, repair, and safer design; (4) rising political opposition to local pollution.
- Boundary conditions / enablers: stricter enforcement of international agreements (Basel, Bamako), national bans on waste exports, and redesign of domestic waste management.

Drawing on Samir Amin's delinking and dependency theory, the paper argues that the waste trade is infrastructural to global capitalism's metabolism, allowing affluent territories to maintain a "fantasy of cleanliness" while displacing harms. Because recycling and circular-economy rhetoric grant a moral license that dulls pressure to curb throughput, downstream fixes cannot break the overproduction cycle. Removing the export option forces upstream prevention and redesign, making perpetual throughput growth physically and politically untenable and thereby rendering degrowth structurally realizable rather than merely compatible.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes two contributions: first, it repositions delinking not as a by-product of degrowth but as a practical mechanism that can actively trigger degrowth by closing avenues for externalization; second, it extends Amin's political-economic notion of delinking into the ecological realm, showing how territorial responsibility for waste can dismantle the colonial pattern of burden-shifting documented in global waste studies.

**Practical Implication**
Enacting waste delinking requires high-income countries to reconfigure production and consumption around material sufficiency via export bans, extended-producer-responsibility regimes, and material-input caps, while enabling lower-income countries to reject the historical role of sink for externalized residuals.
