# When Good Intentions Backfire: The Asymmetric Effects of Minority-Ownership Markers for Businesses on Online Platforms

**Abstract**
Our analysis of Yelp Open Data suggests that the markers have varied effects on different types of businesses, with observable benefits concentrated in specific categories. Subsequently, the results from our online experiments show that the markers may have the intended effect of increasing users’ preference rank and likelihood of choosing minority-owned businesses, but primarily among individuals who were motivated to support such businesses. For those who were not motivated to support such businesses, the markers have an inconsistent impact, even when businesses align with what people expect minorities to excel at. Even worse, the markers may have a backfire effect, resulting in a decreased preference rank and likelihood of choosing businesses that operate outside of these expectations. We discuss the research and practical implications of these findings.

**Research Question**
Do the minority-ownership markers that online platforms (e.g., Yelp, Google) display achieve their intended goal of promoting minority-owned businesses, are there unintended side effects, and what mechanisms—specifically user motivation to support the cause and stereotype-based quality expectations—explain these heterogeneous outcomes?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Presence of a minority-ownership marker (binary); business type stereotype-consistency (e.g., Soul Food / Dim Sum vs. Vegetarian / Breakfast & Brunch); user support for the underlying social cause.
- DV(s): Consumers' preference rank for a business and likelihood of choosing (Select) it.
- Moderators: User support for the cause (H2) and positive biases / stereotype-consistency of the business type (H3); their joint configuration drives the backfire effect (H4).
- Controls: Locality, star rating (High Rating), business-type appeal, display order, treatment group (Asian- vs. Black-owned).

Drawing on Cue Utilization Theory, the markers act as extrinsic cues that consumers interpret through their motivations and prior expectations. For users who support the cause and for stereotype-consistent businesses, the cue raises preference and choice. But where positive associations are absent—users who do not support the cause evaluating stereotype-inconsistent businesses—confirmation bias and ego threat lead the marker to trigger psychological reactance, producing a backfire effect that lowers preference and choice. Thus the aggregate positive effect masks an asymmetric distribution that benefits some minority businesses while disadvantaging others.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Cue Utilization Theory by showing that an extrinsic cue's effect is contingent on the interaction between user motivation and stereotype-based quality expectations, rather than being a uniform direct effect. It is the first to demonstrate that minority-marker effects are heterogeneous across business types—concentrated in stereotype-consistent domains—and to theorize and document a marker backfire effect grounded in confirmation bias and ego threat, complementing observational evidence with controlled experiments to identify the underlying mechanisms.

**Practical Implication**
Displaying minority-ownership markers to all users may be suboptimal; platforms should target receptive users via personalized recommendation rather than relying on (low) user willingness to search for such businesses. Platforms should proactively inform business owners about potential negative effects, consider opt-in deployment, account for business type, and strengthen content-moderation tools to handle off-topic or hostile reviews that markers may provoke.

**Limitations**
Marker effectiveness is sensitive to changing social, economic, and political contexts, limiting temporal generalizability. The study was conducted only in the United States, and mTurk/Prolific samples may carry selection bias toward specific demographics. The experiments simulate a specific search scenario, and the scope is limited to Black-owned and Asian-owned markers, constraining generalization to other minority groups and settings.

**Future Research**
Examine strategies to mitigate negative effects among non-supporters and to build broader consensus around supporting minority-owned businesses, including how public sentiment differs across minority groups. Investigate downstream behaviors (e.g., subsequent ratings/reviews when inflated expectations are unmet), markers for other subpopulations (women-owned, LGBTQ-owned), the role of negative stereotypes, and spillover effects on unlabeled businesses.
