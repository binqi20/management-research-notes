# Changes in the Salience of Collective Reputations: Information Disclosure and Shareholder Reactions

**Abstract**
New information about these attributes can reduce the salience of the collective reputation, helping shareholders to make more individual and accurate assessments. In 1989, the U.S. EPA published the Toxic Release Inventory (TRI), providing for the first time detailed pollution emissions data for large manufacturing facilities. We use several analytic approaches, including multiplicative heteroskedastic linear regression models, and data on 570 firms across 22 industries, to examine how the TRI data release affected shareholder evaluations of firms.

**Research Question**
Can the influence of a collective reputation vary across contexts, and if so, what changes that influence? Specifically, does the mandatory disclosure of firm-specific information reduce the salience of an industry's collective reputation, leading shareholders to evaluate peer firms more heterogeneously?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): PostTRI (post-disclosure dummy); News (industry pollution media coverage, a proxy for collective-reputation salience); their interaction PostTRI×News
- DV(s): Heterogeneity (variance) of firms' cumulative abnormal returns (CAR), and CAR mean
- Moderators: Emissions (firm-level pollution intensity = ln[emission total/sales]), via PostTRI×Emissions, tested in salient-industry sub-samples
- Mediators: Not reported in paper

Drawing on transaction cost economics, the authors argue a collective reputation acts as a perceptual heuristic that shareholders rely on when firm-specific information is scarce and the reputed quality dimension is valued—the condition of high "salience." Mandatory disclosure (the TRI release) supplies low-cost, reliable individual-firm information that decouples members' true performance from the group stereotype, lowering the reputation's salience and allowing more individualized, heterogeneous assessments. Salience thus depends on an exogenous factor (the existence of reliable information) independent of the strength of the collective reputation itself. The effect is amplified for firms whose individual performance (emissions) deviates substantially from the group, which—from a signaling view—serves as a more diagnostic cue.

**Theoretical Contribution**
Introduces the salience of a collective reputation as a new construct, showing that even a "strong" collective reputation's influence is contingent on exogenous reliable information rather than fixed once established—filling a gap in a literature that had focused on the drivers and consequences of collective reputation but treated its influence as constant. It is, to the authors' knowledge, the first study to empirically demonstrate the influence of collective reputation on firms' financial performance using stock returns, and it introduces multiplicative heteroskedastic regression as a novel methodological lens for studying evaluation uncertainty under information disclosure.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can treat information disclosure as a strategy for managing collective-reputation salience: high-performing firms in a stigmatized group may lobby for or support disclosure programs to lower the group reputation's salience and surface their individual quality (as Du Pont did for TRI legislation). Because shareholders' interpretations of disclosed information are heterogeneous and hard to predict, however, such political activities should be approached with caution and firms should carefully estimate how their own stakeholders would react.

**Limitations**
The collective-reputation measure (newspaper coverage by SIC-coded industry) may not match how shareholders actually group firms, so it may mismeasure salience. The empirical test focuses on a single stakeholder group—shareholders—whose beliefs about pollution may differ from those of NGOs or regulators. The study examines only industry-based reputation in one historical disclosure event.

**Future Research**
Examine more diverse stakeholder groups (e.g., NGOs, government regulators) with measures suited to their differing evaluations. Test alternative group categorizations beyond industry, such as geographical location or political-party affiliation, to improve generalizability. Extend the analysis to more recent TRI disclosures or equivalent environmental reports, and leverage later TRI updates and corrections for additional analytic insight.
