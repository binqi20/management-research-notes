# Leveraging Large Language Models for Hate Speech Detection: Multi-Agent, Information-Theoretic Prompt Learning for Enhancing Contextual Understanding

**Abstract**

To address these limitations, we introduce a novel prompt-learning framework for hate speech detection. Our approach offers several key innovations: (i) prompt generation is delegated to multiple language model agents, drawing upon the theory of questioning as a guiding principle; (ii) we employ an information-theoretic selection mechanism to identify the most effective prompts from a pool of candidates; and (iii) we incorporate motivation-aware instruction tuning to improve the model's capacity to capture the diverse motivational drivers of hate speech.

**Research Question**
How can prompt-learning with large language models be designed to address the domain-specific challenges of hate speech detection—namely the nuance, diversity, and heterogeneous-motivation issues—so as to improve detection performance and generalizability across diverse forms of online hate speech?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: the nuance issue, the diversity issue, and the heterogeneous-motivation issue of hate speech (three domain-specific challenges the framework is designed to address)
- Component 1 — Multi-agent prompt generation (addresses nuance): multiple language-model agents (three debaters: GPT-4o-mini, gemini-1.5-flash, Llama 3.2; two moderators: GPT-4o) engage in structured debate over questions drawn from the "question circles" (subject matter, personal response, external environment) to generate context-rich prompts.
- Component 2 — Information-theoretic prompt selection (addresses diversity): prompts are propagated via paraphrasing, back-translation, and summarization, then the subset maximizing the Shannon-entropy reduction in the [MASK]-token output distribution before vs. after instruction tuning is selected.
- Component 3 — Motivation-aware instruction tuning (addresses heterogeneous motivation): a "generate-and-tune" extension auto-generates contextual information about the motivations behind a hate-speech candidate and integrates it into the instruction-tuning input.
- Outcome: binary hate / non-hate classification, generalized as estimating a multinomial distribution over hate-related words.

The framework theorizes that prompts grounded in relevant social-science theory (the theory of questioning) elicit deeper, more context-aware responses from language models, while an information-theoretic criterion identifies prompts that universally raise output certainty across diverse cases; motivation-aware tuning then counters the distributional dissimilarity that heterogeneous motivations introduce. Across four datasets the full model achieved the highest F1 (e.g., 87.31 on WikiHate), and ablations confirmed each component contributes most to its targeted challenge.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study abstracts three previously under-articulated, domain-specific characteristics of hate speech (nuance, diversity, heterogeneous motivation) and shows how they complicate detection, then develops theory-guided design components—drawing on information theory and the theory of questioning—that resolve them. It contributes to the prompt-learning and IS literatures by demonstrating that social-science-grounded prompt design and instruction tuning outperform architecture-centric and ad-hoc approaches, and contributes a proactive IT artifact to the emerging IS literature on online hate.

**Practical Implication**
The method offers businesses—especially social media platforms that bear heavy content-moderation costs (e.g., Facebook reportedly spending over 500 million dollars a year on outsourced moderation)—an efficient automated alternative or complement to human moderation. By curbing the dissemination of hate speech, it can reduce operational expense, mitigate user defection and advertiser avoidance, and alleviate the social and psychological harms (including moderator trauma) associated with online hate.

**Limitations**
The approach operates at the text level and was not tested on person-level data (identifying individuals likely to produce hateful content), which would require longitudinal tracking of writing. Like other prompt-learning methods it relies on large language models, raising potential financial costs that can become considerable at scale. Implementation also requires balancing hate-speech regulation against preserving freedom of speech.

**Future Research**
Extend the framework to person-level prediction of hateful behavior using longitudinal data; investigate strategies to broaden applicability to smaller, open-source language models and analyze the cost-benefit tradeoffs of deployment (e.g., applying the method selectively when a simpler model is low-confidence); and apply the approach to other downstream tasks that share similar problem characteristics.
