# The Hidden Impact of Prosumers and Its Fair Mitigation

**Abstract**
We investigate the burgeoning trend of prosumers, who have transformed from traditional consumers into active renewable energy producers. While prosumers help reduce greenhouse gas emissions and reliance on fossil fuels, they often remain connected to the grid as a backup. This practice requires that utility companies reserve capacity, and conventional consumers share these associated costs. We develop a stylized model to comprehensively assess the impact of prosumers. Our findings demonstrate that, although prosumers contribute to diminishing nonrenewable energy consumption and offer potential cost savings to utility firms, they simultaneously introduce negative externalities. Specifically, they inject uncertainty into the grid, resulting in higher electricity prices and increased utility bills for regular consumers, even when fixed costs incurred by utility firms are not considered. As the intermittency of prosumer energy generation increases, the socially optimal proportion decreases while the self-selected equilibrium proportion of prosumers increases. Furthermore, we examine the potential implications of a conventional linear incentive scheme for prosumers, exemplified by the 2023 U.S. federal tax credit for solar panel installation costs. We find that such schemes may exacerbate social disparity. To address this issue, we propose a reverse-linear subsidization approach, which paradoxically requires less funding to achieve equivalent prosumer adoption rates and results in smaller social disparity.

**Research Question**
Should energy authorities incentivize or charge prosumers, and how should the benefits and costs of distributed solar power be allocated equitably across the community? The paper centers on the adoption of green technology — specifically rooftop solar panels — and the ramifications of a growing prosumer population for traditional consumers.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core constructs: prosumer proportion (γ); generation intermittency (σ); mean solar efficiency (μ); roof size s (heterogeneity); regulated utility price set by total expected cost over net demand; installation costs (fixed I0, variable I); differential social benefit α.
- Three optimal/equilibrium proportions compared: socially-cost-minimizing (γc*), consumer self-selection equilibrium (γd*), and egalitarian max-min fair proportion (γ0).
- Key result: more prosumers raise the coefficient of variation of net demand, so the electricity price rises in γ, μ, and σ even with no fixed costs — a negative externality on regular consumers.
- Divergence under intermittency: as σ rises, the socially optimal proportion falls while the self-selected (and egalitarian-fair) proportion rises, risking a death-spiral dynamic that nonetheless stabilizes via roof-size heterogeneity.
- Proposed intervention: a budget-neutral "reverse-linear" subsidy (larger per-unit subsidy for smaller roofs) that reaches γc* via self-selection while reducing unfairness and requiring a smaller levy than the conventional roof-size-proportional tax credit.

Because prosumers stay grid-connected as backup, their intermittent generation injects demand uncertainty that forces the utility to hold reserve capacity; the regulated break-even price then increases in the prosumer share even absent grid-maintenance fixed costs, shifting cost onto non-adopters. Conventional proportional subsidies (e.g., the 2023 U.S. federal solar tax credit) disproportionately reward large-roof consumers who would adopt anyway, widening the welfare gap, whereas a reverse-linear subsidy schedule attains the same socially optimal adoption with less funding and smaller disparity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper complements the operations-management literature on prosumers, renewable energy, and electricity-price fairness by endogenizing the utility firm's operational (capacity and pricing) decisions and theoretically showing that the electricity price can rise even without any fixed cost — isolating intermittency-driven operational uncertainty as a distinct driver of price hikes and consumer unfairness. It connects prosumer intermittency to competition-/inventory-risk mechanisms in operations and proposes a budget-neutral reverse-linear subsidy as a fair mitigation mechanism.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should account for negative externalities from prosumers' intermittent generation — not only fixed-cost reallocation — when designing interventions. A uniform proportional tax-credit ratio (e.g., 30%) exacerbates social unfairness; instead, regulators should direct larger subsidies to consumers with relatively small roof sizes, which can raise prosumer adoption to the socially optimal level while mitigating unfairness and requiring less total funding.

**Limitations**
The model abstracts away energy storage, assumes uniformly distributed roof size with deterministic demand for tractability, focuses on a single-supplier (monopoly) market rather than deregulated competition, and abstracts away intraday fluctuations of solar power by working at an aggregate level of intermittency.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how battery storage systems that enable prosumer self-consumption affect electricity prices, relax the uniform-distribution/deterministic-demand assumptions to a more general distribution of consumer heterogeneity, extend the model to deregulated markets with supplier competition, and study solar variability at the operational (intraday) level rather than only in aggregate.
