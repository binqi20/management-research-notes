# The Ripple Effect of Reputation Spillover: How Corruption Fugitives Shape Consumer Perceptions of Fugitives’ Host Countries and Their MNEs?

**Abstract**
The relocation of corruption fugitives raises an important question: how does their presence in host countries affect the brand credibility of multinational enterprises (MNEs) from those destinations? Drawing on the reputation spillover perspective, this study theorizes and tests a ripple effect of reputational damage. Consumers in fugitives’ home countries attribute corruption-related reputational damage to the host country’s image and extend it to MNE brands originating there. This spillover effect is moderated by perceived corruption in consumers’ home country and by national animosity between the home and host countries. By uncovering how corruption fugitives indirectly shape foreign brand evaluations, this study broadens the understanding of nonmarket actors as carriers of reputational contagion across borders. **Research Question**
How does the relocation of corruption fugitives affect the reputations of MNEs from their host countries — that is, how does the presence of fugitives in host countries affect the brand credibility of MNEs originating from those destinations?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Frequency of being corruption fugitives' host country (FHF); corruption-related damage to host-country reputation (image damage)
- DV(s): Corruption-related damage to host-country reputation (H1); brand credibility of host-country MNEs (H2)
- Mediators: Country-image damage was tested as a mediator between FHF and MNE brand credibility but found NOT to mediate (no significant mediation; multiple antecedents coexist)
- Moderators: Consumers' perceived home-country corruption (H1a, supported in Study 1 but not Study 2); consumers' national animosity toward host countries (H2a, supported)

A sequential "ripple" process is theorized: the settlement of fugitives abroad triggers social disapproval, which activates *entitativity* perceptions whereby observers cognitively bundle host-country institutions, society, and firms into one coherent unit. High entitativity lets negative judgments about fugitives spill over first to the host country's image and then to MNEs headquartered there, eroding perceived brand credibility even absent any direct firm–fugitive link. Perceived home-country corruption and national animosity amplify the strength of these cross-level transfers.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces "transnational reputational contagion," showing corruption can have outbound, cross-national reputational effects rather than being confined within borders, and that the corruption–institution relationship is mutual (institutions shape corruption, but corruption can also reshape institutions abroad). It identifies corruption fugitives as a new class of nonmarket actors who generate reputational risk for firms without any direct engagement, and provides a multilevel account linking macrolevel societal trust in governance to microlevel consumer evaluations of corporate brand credibility.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should recognize that hosting corruption fugitives may yield short-term capital inflows but carries long-term reputational costs for the country's MNEs, and should maintain transparent legal frameworks and cooperate with international anti-corruption initiatives. MNE managers must proactively manage country-level reputational associations through defensive measures (targeted advertising, consumer education, public relations) and collective efforts such as joint CSR programs or cross-firm campaigns to signal integrity and rebuild consumer trust.

**Limitations**
The absence of a mediation effect indicates unknown factors shape the host-image-to-MNE relationship, and the analysis is limited to home-country consumers, narrowing generalizability. Methodologically, the experiments were not preregistered and lacked explicit manipulation checks, raising concerns about transparency, interpretive validity, and potential researcher bias.

**Future Research**
Future work should test whether firms with stronger ethical reputations are more resilient (a possible "halo effect"), and examine collective action among stakeholders, third-country audiences, and the mediating role of media exposure and public awareness. Longitudinal experiments, large-scale surveys/secondary data, and cross-national comparisons (e.g., contexts varying in institutional integrity, media freedom, and corruption tolerance) are recommended, along with preregistration and direct manipulation checks.
