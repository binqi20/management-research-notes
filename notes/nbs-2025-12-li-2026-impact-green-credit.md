# The impact of green credit guidelines on green lending and environmental outcomes: Evidence from Chinese banks

**Abstract**
To enhance the effectiveness of GCGs, we recommend that the GCGs be supplemented with mechanisms that promote practices across the banking sector.

**Research Question**
The study poses two central research questions: (1) Do Chinese banks increase their green lending in response to the Green Credit Guidelines (GCGs)? and (2) Do all banks respond equally to the implementation of the GCGs?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): GCG implementation (Post dummy, =1 for 2012 onward); mandatory green-lending disclosure exposure (treat); state control (SCB); local pollutant (SO₂/CO₂) intensity; provincial green lending ratio; green-bond policy onset (2015)
- DV(s): Bank green lending ratio (GLR = green lending / total loans); bank profitability (net profit ratio NPA, net interest income ratio NIIA); future provincial SO₂ and CO₂ emissions
- Mediators: None formally modeled
- Moderators: State control (SCB) and environmental-regulation intensity moderate the GCG → green-lending relationship; state control and high-GLR moderate the GCG → profitability relationship

The GCGs operate as a "carrot-and-stick" institutional mechanism: compliant banks receive regulatory and financial incentives while non-compliant banks face penalties, compelling banks to expand green lending to secure legitimacy (institutional theory). The aggregate increase in green lending is driven predominantly by state-controlled banks, which—through closer government ties, superior information access, and resource capacity—can better evaluate and profit from green projects (relationship banking theory). Increases in provincial green lending under the GCG framework are in turn associated with lower future provincial SO₂ and CO₂ emissions, indicating the policy advances its environmental objectives.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study shifts the analytical focus of the green-credit literature from borrowing firms to lending institutions, examining the underexplored impact of the GCGs on banks. It provides evidence consistent with both institutional theory (regulatory pressure induces banks to expand green lending for legitimacy) and relationship banking theory (informational and resource asymmetries explain why state-controlled banks engage and profit more), highlighting the uneven distribution of policy benefits across bank ownership types.

**Practical Implication**
Because GCG benefits concentrate among state-controlled banks while non-state-controlled banks face barriers, policymakers should supplement the GCGs with mechanisms that broaden participation across the banking sector—such as structured information-sharing systems to reduce informational asymmetries and targeted training and resource-capacity support for non-state-controlled banks—to maximize the environmental effectiveness of green credit frameworks now adopted by over 60 countries.

**Limitations**
GCG impact is identified primarily through a time dummy (Post), which may capture other contemporaneous policies or economic events, and the proxy variables for mechanisms are constrained by data availability (e.g., bank ESG-team size and expertise are unobserved). Green-lending data are disclosed voluntarily, raising potential self-selection bias; although checks suggest missing data appear random, self-selection cannot be fully ruled out, limiting generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future work could conduct comparative cross-country analyses to test generalizability across institutional and regulatory contexts, and use more granular data (internal ESG staffing, regulatory evaluation metrics, loan-level information) to isolate the precise channels behind banks' green-lending profitability. Incorporating borrowing-firm characteristics (e.g., debt reliance, environmental performance) and a wider range of financing sources could further illuminate the mechanisms underlying profitability differences.
