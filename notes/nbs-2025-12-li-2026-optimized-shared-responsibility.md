# An optimized shared responsibility accounting framework: unveiling the big black shadows behind small emitters in global value chains

**Abstract**
Our findings show that under conventional accounting frameworks that inadequately consider carbon transfers induced by investment, trade, and sequential production, carbon emissions responsibilities of small developed economies with huge FDI outflows, as well as GVC downstream sectors, have been significantly underestimated. Behind these so-called "smaller emitters" in the conventional sense lie big black shadows (i.e., large volumes of carbon footprints in GVCs). Our study provides a new perspective on carbon emissions responsibility sharing along GVCs, highlighting the importance of integrating investors and global sequential production decisions into the responsibility-sharing system and emphasizing the role of multinational enterprises in greening global supply chains.

**Research Question**
How can carbon emissions responsibility be fairly and reasonably allocated among countries and sectors when global value chains involve sequential production and cross-border investment, and how does incorporating investors (alongside producers and consumers) change the "real" emissions responsibilities of economies conventionally classified as small emitters?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core construct: tripartite carbon emissions responsibility (responsibility as producers + responsibility as consumers + responsibility as investors) allocated along sequential GVC production
- Comparison frameworks: PBA, CBA, FIBA, SBA (conventional) vs. OSBA (proposed)
- Key inputs: OECD AMNE enterprise-heterogeneous ICIO tables distinguishing domestic enterprises (DOEs) and foreign-invested enterprises (FIEs); IEA sectoral emissions; OECD TiM labor-compensation shares
- Allocation rule: emissions responsibility of MNEs' foreign affiliates is shifted to the FDI home country and recorded as that country's responsibility as investors; upstream emissions are secondarily reallocated to downstream sectors
- Diagnostic ratio: E_OSBA/E_PBA, where values > 1 indicate PBA underestimation

By extending Lenzen et al.'s (2007) shared responsibility logic to an enterprise-heterogeneous MRIO model and adding an investor dimension, the OSBA framework reattributes carbon footprints embodied in cross-border investment and sequential production. Because conventional PBA credits the carbon of MNEs' foreign affiliates to the producing country rather than the investing home country, small FDI-exporting economies (e.g., Luxembourg, Switzerland, the Netherlands, Hong Kong) and downstream sectors (e.g., construction, computer/photovoltaic manufacturing) appear "clean" while concealing large GVC carbon footprints. OSBA surfaces these "big black shadows" by routing investor and downstream responsibility back to the actors that induced the emissions, yielding a more equitable basis for climate negotiations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops the first tripartite carbon emissions responsibility accounting and allocation framework that simultaneously encompasses production, consumption, and investment within the whole GVC network, addressing the gap that prior frameworks (PBA, CBA, SBA, CTBA, FIBA) each ignore either investment-related or sequential-production-related carbon transfers. It reconceptualizes responsibility sharing by integrating investors as macro-level agents and by passing carbon responsibility upstream and downstream along sequential production, thereby revealing systematic underestimation of small developed economies' and downstream sectors' "real" emissions responsibilities.

**Practical Implication**
The authors recommend establishing a multi-actor (producer-consumer-investor-investee) carbon responsibility accounting and sharing criterion worldwide to break the climate-negotiation deadlock; designing industrial policies and indirect-emission/carbon-market mechanisms that make downstream sectors share upstream abatement costs; and requiring large MNEs to manage Scope 2 and Scope 3 emissions across their entire supply chains, incorporating suppliers' carbon-reduction performance into procurement decisions.

**Limitations**
The study does not account for tax havens when estimating economies' responsibilities as investors (because the AMNE database lacks this information), likely underestimating the investor responsibility of high-income Western economies such as the US and the UK. Additionally, the producer-consumer-investor responsibility-sharing ratios are computed solely from economic benefits (value-added shares), ignoring economies' and sectors' differing carbon-emission characteristics and supply-chain roles, which may understate the responsibility of lower-income, higher-emission economies and sectors.

**Future Research**
The authors plan to use additional methods to optimize the responsibility-sharing coefficient and to compare the advantages and disadvantages of different optimization methods, moving beyond a purely value-added-based sharing rule.
