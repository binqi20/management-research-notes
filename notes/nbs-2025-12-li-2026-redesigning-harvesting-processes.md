# Redesigning Harvesting Processes and Improving Working Conditions in Agribusiness

**Abstract**
We collaborate with a leading mushroom producer in North America to investigate the potential benefits of redesigning agribusiness operations, specifically, harvesting processes, to enhance both firm performance and working conditions of employees. We consider two harvesting protocols: The dominant status-quo practice, namely Harvest-all and an alternative, namely Selective Harvesting. In the Harvest-all protocol, workers pick all crop units in the areas assigned to them, regardless of the crops’ size and maturity, resulting in higher productivity but lower average value (quality) of the harvested produce. Workers also remain in demanding physical postures for longer periods of time, resulting in ergonomic stress. In contrast, under the Selective Harvesting protocol, workers take multiple rounds to completely harvest the assigned area, picking in each round only the select crop units that are near their peak monetary value. This results in lower productivity but higher average quality of the harvested produce and better ergonomic conditions. We develop mathematical models to analyze the two harvesting protocols, provide a complete analytical characterization of the optimal managerial decisions under each protocol, and examine how these decisions are influenced by relevant contextual factors. In addition, we characterize the performance of each harvesting protocol along three key performance metrics of interest: Firm profitability, worker monetary welfare, and worker ergonomic welfare. Using both analytical and calibrated numerical methods we show that adopting Selective Harvesting over Harvest-all can create win–win scenarios where both the firm and workers are better off. However, our analysis reveals that the firm’s and workers’ interests may not always be fully aligned. We subsequently demonstrate that the misalignment can be reduced by making adjustments to the compensation structure so that workers’ earnings match the maximum potential value while having minimal impact on firm profitability. Our models illustrate the benefits of careful process redesign in creating better working conditions for employees and advancing firms’ social responsibility practices.

**Research Question**
From the firm's perspective, what are the optimal operational decisions (harvest start time, and the proportion to selectively harvest) under the Harvest-all and Selective Harvesting protocols, how do the two protocols compare on firm profits and worker welfare (economic and ergonomic), and are there win–win scenarios where both the firm and workers are better-off under Selective Harvesting relative to Harvest-all?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: Firm's expected profit (Π), workers' monetary welfare (earnings S), and workers' ergonomic welfare (Hand Activity Level, HAL).
- Decision levers: harvest start time (t₀); proportion selectively picked (X); compensation structure — time-based rate (c) and performance-based revenue share (α).
- Contextual factors: crop biological characteristics (growth rate k₁, deterioration rate k₂, pop-up window T, growth duration P); worker efficiency (pick/observe/relocate times εy, εo, εx); worker compensation structure.
- Process / regions: comparing protocols yields win–win, lose–win, and lose–lose regions in X, governed by wage thresholds (c̃, ĉ).

The economic core is a time–quality trade-off: picking all units quickly (Harvest-all) lowers labor time but harvests many units off-peak and raises ergonomic stress, whereas selectively picking near-peak units (Selective Harvesting) raises average produce value and lowers HAL but increases time spent moving between bins. Because the value-based pay component and time-based pay move in unison for workers but in opposition for the firm, the selective-harvesting fraction that maximizes worker earnings (Xw*) exceeds the fraction that maximizes firm profit (X*), creating a misalignment that the firm can shrink by reweighting performance-based versus time-based compensation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a new class of "Harvesting Models" that jointly incorporates heterogeneity in crop maturity times and the human (ergonomic) factors of harvesting — elements the prior operations literature had not considered together. It broadens the conception of worker welfare in operational planning beyond monetary earnings to include ergonomic welfare, showing analytically that embedding ergonomic considerations in supply-chain operations can advance a firm's triple bottom line rather than trade off against it.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can use the analytical decision support to choose harvesting protocols by crop type and operating context: Selective Harvesting is most beneficial for fast-deteriorating crops (e.g., raspberries, organic mushrooms) and when labor is abundant and cheap, while Harvest-all remains preferable for slow-deteriorating crops (e.g., corn) or when labor is scarce and expensive. In the calibrated TMG study, Selective Harvesting raised firm profit by 20.9%, worker earnings by 20.5%, and lowered HAL by 31.6%; modest compensation adjustments let workers reach their maximum earnings with only a ~0.7% profit reduction, easing managerial buy-in.

**Limitations**
The analysis assumes a piecewise-linear monetary value curve and treats the number of crop units per bin (N), the growth duration (P), and the number of selective rounds (R) as fixed/known, with stochastic extensions relegated to appendices. The grounded validation relies on a single collaborating mushroom producer (TMG), so calibrated magnitudes are context-specific even though the analytical structure is general.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine the interplay among the three performance metrics for other crops and its implications for protocol choice, and extend the model to firms that explicitly embed social objectives into decision-making (e.g., social enterprises or mission-driven organizations), for instance via a multiobjective framework that evaluates harvesting protocols on a weighted combination of financial and social outcomes.
