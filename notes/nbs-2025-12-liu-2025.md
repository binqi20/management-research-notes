# Financial Value of Nature: Coastal Housing Markets, Mangroves, and Climate Resilience

**Abstract**
As coastal risks increase due to development and climate change, a tangible benefit of nature is the protection it offers against storm damage. Many studies from the risk industry and others assess the direct effects of wetlands for reducing damage during storms. However, the value of wetlands for coastal protection could extend to many other benefits, including home prices in areas where storms are common. We use property-level housing transaction data from Zillow and show that proximity to mangroves in Florida moderates home price decline and dispersion following major hurricanes.

**Research Question**
After a major hurricane, do properties near mangroves experience smaller price declines than properties farther from mangroves, even though both are equally exposed to hurricane risks? More broadly, what is the private financial value of mangroves as a nature-based protection against climate-related extreme weather, as revealed through coastal housing markets?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Property's distance-to-mangroves group (0–2, 2–4, 4–8, 8–16, >16 km), interacted with hurricane-timing indicators; mangrove forest width as an intensive-margin moderator.
- DV(s): Log of property sale price (level effect) and the ZIP-code coefficient of variation of prices (dispersion effect); derived outcomes are the probability of losing ≥25% of value (pVaR) and willingness to pay for mangrove protection.
- Mediators: Perceived flood-risk reduction priced into homes; homebuyer learning about mangrove protection (especially in off-path counties, with ~1-year lag).
- Moderators: FEMA flood-risk zone (effect concentrated in medium-risk areas); on- vs. off-hurricane-path counties; mangrove forest width.

Identification exploits the differential price response to hurricanes across properties with differing mangrove proximity, using repeated-sales variation with property fixed effects (absorbing location, amenities, flood exposure) and year-month fixed effects (absorbing common trends). Because mangrove proximity correlates with coastal proximity, the analysis is restricted to medium-flood-risk properties with similar inherent risk and explicitly orthogonalizes mangrove distance against distance to the coast. The reduction in post-hurricane price loss attributable to mangroves is translated into a pVaR and then into willingness to pay, conditional on a hurricane occurring.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper proposes a market-based methodology to quantify the private financial value of nature-based solutions, measuring mangroves' protective benefit through revealed housing-price dynamics rather than engineering-based direct-damage-avoidance estimates. It is one of the first studies to measure the mitigating effect of mangroves (and of nature-based solutions more generally) on flood-risk-related housing value loss, contributing to the literatures on valuing ecosystems and biodiversity and on the costs and benefits of flood-risk reduction.

**Practical Implication**
Because mangroves benefit property owners, developers, insurers, and financial institutions, these private agents have a financial stake in protecting and restoring mangroves; the paper's dollar estimates and methodology can be embedded in financial instruments and public–private partnerships that mobilize private capital for coastal nature conservation, easing climate-adaptation costs for governments while delivering ecosystem and biodiversity co-benefits.

**Limitations**
The estimates capture only the housing-value component of mangroves' benefits and do not constitute a full cost–benefit analysis that would include flood protection of non-housing assets, biodiversity, carbon sequestration, and fisheries co-benefits. The willingness-to-pay figures are conditional on a hurricane occurring; computing unconditional WTP would require beliefs about climate scenarios and their effect on hurricane damages, which is beyond the paper's scope. The mangrove measure is time-invariant (coverage changed little over the period), no systematic data on gray infrastructure are available, and ZTRAX transaction data were later discontinued by Zillow.

**Future Research**
The authors point toward incorporating their private-benefit estimates into the design of structured financial instruments and public–private partnerships for funding mangrove protection and restoration, and toward broader cost–benefit analyses that account for the many additional co-benefits of nature-based solutions (flood protection of other assets, biodiversity, carbon offsets, fisheries) in coastal climate-adaptation policy.
