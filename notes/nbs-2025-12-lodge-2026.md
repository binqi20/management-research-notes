# From Paralysis to Publicization: How Victims of the UK Post Office Horizon IT Scandal Experienced and Confronted Organizational Harm

**Abstract**
The publicization of organizational harm is critical for enabling accountability and justice. While existing research offers valuable insights into how audiences label and sanction harm as misconduct, we know comparatively little about the early stages of such publicization—how knowledge of harm first moves from the private sphere to broader outsider awareness. In this study, we address this undertheorized phase in the misconduct literature by examining the UK Post Office Horizon IT scandal, in which more than 4,000 subpostmasters were wrongly accused of theft and false accounting over many years due to faults in an IT system. Our analysis reveals how a series of organizational moves produced a state of "response paralysis" among victims, leaving them unable or unwilling to speak out, thereby stalling publicization. We then show how this paralysis was gradually broken down, enabling victims to take the first steps toward making their experiences public. By foregrounding these early dynamics in the process of publicization, our study offers a more complete understanding of publicization and the broader lifecycle of organizational misconduct. Overall, the study enables scholars to better understand why some organizational harm remains hidden for years—or never comes to light at all.

**Research Question**
The paper asks two interrelated questions: (1) "What impedes victims of organizational harm from publicizing the harm they have experienced?" and (2) "How can victims of organizational harm attempt to move toward publicization?" It thereby theorizes the undertheorized earliest stage of publicization—how knowledge of organizational harm first moves from the private sphere to outsider awareness.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering acts of harm: systemic faults in the Horizon IT accounting system, falsely attributed to individual subpostmasters as theft/false accounting
- Organizational moves (impeding): (1) treating systemic harm as isolated incidents; (2) projecting false hope of resolution; (3) defending processes while keeping them opaque; (4) diffusing an organizational account that dominates the public narrative
- Key construct / barrier: "response paralysis" — four co-constructed types: self-driven (isolation-based, doubt-based) and other-driven (despair-based, shame-based)
- Enabling processes (breaking down paralysis): gaining awareness of other victims (reduces isolation), recognizing systemic harm (reduces doubt), developing a belief in efficacy (reduces despair and shame)
- Emergent outcome: victims break silence, enabling publicization that makes harm available for audience evaluation and sanction
- Underlying abstraction: organizationally produced informational asymmetries and reputational asymmetries

Organizational moves interacted with victims' lived experience of victimization to co-construct a "response paralysis" that kept harm contained in the private sphere. Rather than fear of retaliation (as in whistleblowing accounts), isolation, doubt, despair, and shame prevented victims from recognizing their experiences as systemic or shareable. Over time, three sequentially interdependent processes—awareness of others, recognition of systemic harm, and a collective belief in efficacy—dismantled this paralysis, enabling the first steps of publicization. The authors abstract these dynamics to two organizationally produced asymmetries: informational asymmetries that distort victims' access to accurate information, and reputational asymmetries that grant the organization disproportionate credibility.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the organizational misconduct literature by theorizing the earliest, undertheorized stage of publicization—how information about organizational harm first enters the public domain—and redirects attention from external audiences and bystander whistleblowers to victims themselves as central but underexamined actors. It identifies "response paralysis" as an organizationally constructed (not merely psychological) barrier to publicization, abstracts it to informational and reputational asymmetries, and offers a complementary account of why organizational harm can persist for years through four specific organizational moves.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and policymakers should not interpret silence as the absence of harm, since silence can be actively produced and sustained by organizational actions, narratives, and (dis)incentives. Because disclosure depends on a prior, less visible process of recognizing harm, organizations and policymakers should invest in mechanisms for collective reflection and pattern recognition (e.g., peer forums, facilitated discussions, trusted intermediaries) and ensure internal grievance structures operate as more than false promises.

**Limitations**
The findings rest on a single in-depth case whose scale of harm, long time horizon, and technological context may not transfer automatically to other settings, and the identified forms of paralysis may manifest differently across organizations, sectors, or cultures. The case involves a large number of victims, making collective organizing and belief in efficacy more feasible than in cases with fewer victims, and response paralysis is not the only mechanism explaining victims' silence (e.g., deliberate silencing or explicit threats under forced labor or undocumented work). The study also focuses on insiders, so dynamics may shift for outsider victims such as communities or customers.

**Future Research**
Scholars should examine when and how collective action remains viable in contexts with far fewer victims, and how solo individuals establish connections or are reached by wider support networks. Research could explore how alternative silencing mechanisms (e.g., fear, coercion, NDAs) intersect with or diverge from response paralysis, and how organizations influence victims' sensemaking in ways that inhibit recognition and disclosure. Further work could investigate how external victims (communities, customers) confront organizational harm when insider knowledge is absent and access to redress is limited.
