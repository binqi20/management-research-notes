# Who should pay for ESG ratings?

**Abstract**
When all investors are socially responsible, the model coincides with a model of credit ratings, explaining why credit ratings are sold to issuers while most ESG ratings are sold to investors. Ratings have a positive effect on firms’ ESG performance, which is maximized when investors’ preferences are heterogeneous.

**Research Question**
When would we expect an ESG rating to be sold to firms rather than to investors, and why are credit ratings paid by issuers while most ESG ratings are paid by investors? How do the presence of an ESG rating agency and the choice of its business model impact firms' ESG performance and stock prices?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: The defining difference between an ESG and a credit rating is that ESG information is valued by only a subset of investors (socially responsible / warm-glow investors, mass ω), whereas credit-rating information is valued by all; this single distinction drives the rating agency's choice of business model.
- Key constructs: "issuer pays" vs. "investors pay" business models; "green" (low expected emissions e̅) vs. "brown" (high e̅) firms; fraction ω of socially responsible (SR) investors; rating-agency bargaining power λ; stock-market liquidity (noise-trader variance σ_z).
- Boundary conditions / limit cases: when ω → 1 the model becomes isomorphic to a credit-ratings model (issuer pays dominates); a positive price impact of transparency is necessary but not sufficient for "issuer pays" to be more profitable; results extended to nonzero cash-flow/emission correlation, information leakage, and an alternative RA outside option.
- Emergent outcomes: rating-agency business-model choice; firm's expected equilibrium stock price; firm's endogenous emission-abatement investment.

In a noisy rational expectations equilibrium, ESG ratings reduce the risk SR investors face, so they trade more aggressively. When SR investors dominate and the firm is green they hold long positions, so disclosure raises the expected price and the firm is willing to pay for a public "issuer pays" rating; when normal investors dominate and the firm is brown, SR investors short-sell and disclosure depresses the price, so the agency instead sells information privately under "investors pay." As in Grossman and Stiglitz (1980), "investors pay" profits are capped because informed trading impounds information into prices, whereas "issuer pays" profits are not capped, so issuer pays prevails only when the SR fraction exceeds a threshold increasing in the firm's brownness. Endogenizing abatement, the rating agency's presence raises firms' incentive to cut emissions, with an inverse-U impact in ω that is largest when investor preferences are most heterogeneous.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper proposes what it frames as the first model of the determinants and implications of an ESG rating agency's choice of business model, nesting credit ratings as the limit case in which all investors value the information. It shows that the fraction of investors who care about rating content suffices to rationalize the otherwise puzzling fact that credit ratings are sold to issuers while most ESG ratings are sold to investors, and it links the agency's business model to asset-pricing effects (green-versus-brown stock price reactions) and to firms' real emission-abatement incentives.

**Practical Implication**
"Issuer pays" rating agencies should optimally specialize in green-industry firms traded on less liquid markets in regions where socially responsible investing is prevalent, while brown firms on liquid markets are better covered under "investors pay." As SR investing grows (e.g., in Europe) the model predicts a shift toward "issuer pays," whereas the U.S. ESG backlash should delay it; transparency regulation faces opposition from fossil-fuel-linked parties because ratings tend to lower brown firms' expected prices.

**Limitations**
The firm manager is assumed to have no private information about realized cash flows or emissions, which the authors acknowledge is unrealistic and adopted solely for tractability. The model abstracts from issues central to the credit-ratings literature (moral hazard, conflicts of interest, reputation, rating shopping) and assumes the agency reports truthfully (no greenwashing). The case with short-sales constraints, and parts of the nonzero cash-flow/emission correlation analysis, cannot be solved analytically because they break the CARA-normal framework's tractability.

**Future Research**
The paper points toward testing its cross-sectional and time-series predictions—e.g., that ratings on issues concerning a larger fraction of investors are more likely issuer-paid, and that rising SR investment shifts agencies toward "issuer pays." It also flags richer settings the baseline abstracts away from, including allowing the rating agency to add noise to (manipulate) the information it sells and incorporating short-sales constraints, as avenues that would alter tractability and outcomes.
