# The Role of Information, Rewards, and Convenience in Take-Back Programs for Clothing

**Abstract**
Across all experiments, we find that providing generic information that collected items will be diverted from the landfill significantly decreases the reward required by consumers to return their used clothing.

**Research Question**
What is the effect of providing information about collected clothing's intended use — and of mentioning a specific circular economy strategy versus generic information — on consumers' participation in a clothing take-back program, and how does the convenience of the return process affect participation and interact with the information provided?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Information condition (Blind/no information, Generic, OpenLoop recycling, ClosedLoop recycling, Resale, Donation); convenience of the return process (Convenient home pickup vs. Inconvenient drop-off).
- DV(s): Consumers' willingness-to-accept (WTA) — the minimum reward required to return used clothing (online experiments); number of items brought (field experiment).
- Mediators: Consumers' perception that the retailer is explicitly profiting from the returned clothing (aversion to retailer profit; supported by a mediation analysis in Online Appendix B).
- Moderators: Convenience of the return process (Hypotheses 6a/6b — the information effect strengthens as returns become more convenient).

Drawing on the operational transparency literature and the Theory of Planned Behavior, the authors argue that consumers value the sustainability signal of a take-back program and will accept a lower reward when told their clothing avoids the landfill. Specific circular economy strategies do not add value beyond generic landfill-diversion information; crucially, resale (and, for younger subjects, closed-loop recycling) triggers a negative response because consumers dislike the retailer profiting from their returned garments, whereas donation does not. Greater convenience lowers the required reward and substitutes for information, since an easy return resembles the low effort of landfilling.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper is the first to study consumer return behavior for durable products such as clothing and consumers' perceptions of different circular economy strategies, extending the operational-transparency and closed-loop-supply-chain literatures (which focus largely on consumer electronics and purchasing decisions rather than return decisions). It shows that more specific sustainability information is not necessarily better and can backfire, identifying aversion to retailer profit as the mechanism driving negative reactions to resale.

**Practical Implication**
Retailers can raise participation simply by communicating that collected clothing will be kept out of the landfill; more specific information about recycling or resale need not help and may reduce collection. If a retailer intends to resell returned clothing, it should be transparent but offset the negative reaction with a higher reward or a more convenient (e.g., home-pickup) return process, whose added cost may be recovered through the lower reward consumers then require. Donation can achieve high participation for a very small reward.

**Limitations**
The work combines online experiments, a laboratory study, and a field study conducted with a student population rather than with a retailer's actual consumers, which secures high internal validity and clean causal identification but limits external validity. WTA is elicited hypothetically (with an incentive-alignment mechanism) rather than observed in a real retail setting, so the elicited rewards are not intended to prescribe precise reward levels for practice.

**Future Research**
Future studies could run a field experiment in partnership with a retailer or use secondary data to infer the actual rewards to use in practice. Other retailer decisions — such as the form of the reward (cash/coupon vs. future-purchase discount) or the type of convenience offered — warrant investigation. Researchers could also examine whether take-back programs that reward consumers inadvertently accelerate disposal and new clothing purchases, requiring models of dynamic consumer decisions and preferences for variety and novelty.
