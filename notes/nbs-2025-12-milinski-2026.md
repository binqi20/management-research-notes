# Climate extreme events and climate change are enforced by extortionate freeriders overloading those who mitigate – An economic experiment

**Abstract**
“fair players”, who consistently contributed their share, and “extortioners”, who free-rode and forced others to compensate for their lack of contribution. Groups were randomly composed of participants, whose observed behavior revealed between 0 and 5 individuals acting as extortioners. When extortion became overwhelming, fair players failed to absorb the deficit, groups missed the target and steadfast extortioners incurred losses too. Extortioners disregarded losses and accumulated a large gain while fair players earned little. Nonetheless, fair players' best response was to accede to extortion and cover the deficit. Extortion decreased mitigation across all conditions. Our findings may help to illustrate mechanisms also relevant in the real world where individual actions e.g., leisure travel on giant cruise ships or running power plants with coal, and much else, ultimately prevent collective success. In 2024, global CO2 emissions and temperatures reached record highs, stressing the inability of fair players to compensate the deficit caused by freeriders. This dynamic suggests that the persistence of climate change stems from the unwavering behavior of extortioners in contexts outside of the laboratory.

**Research Question**
Does the threat of repeated, avoidable intermediate climate-damage events increase mitigation effort and the likelihood that groups avert a final simulated climate catastrophe in a collective-risk social dilemma? And if intermediate events fail to raise success rates, what behavioral mechanism explains that null effect?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Loss structure of intermediate climate events (between-subjects treatments: `control` with no intermediate losses; `constant fee` with uniform £1 losses; `increasing fee` with escalating £1/£4/£8/£16 losses at rounds 2/4/6/8); number of extortioners per group (randomly 0–5, exogenous to treatment).
- DV(s): Per-round and total contributions to the climate fund; probability of reaching the £120 final target; per-player payoff; "overcompensation" (net contribution above the threshold).
- Mediators: Fair players' compensatory contributions offsetting the "extortion mean" (combined deficit produced by free-riders).
- Moderators: Fair players' finite compensatory capacity — escalating losses in the `increasing fee` treatment erode their ability to cover the deficit when extortioners are numerous.

Participants split endogenously into time-consistent "fair players" (contributing at least the £20 fair share) and "extortioners" (steadfast low contributors). Intermediate events transiently raised contributions but did not raise final-target success, because reaching the target was governed instead by extortion: fair players' best response is to accede and cover the extortionate deficit, which masks the effect of intermediate losses. Success fell only when the number of extortioners (typically up to 4–5 of 6) exceeded fair players' capacity to compensate, at which point groups missed the target and even steadfast extortioners lost their funds.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the collective-risk social dilemma framework (Milinski et al., 2008) and extortion theory (Press & Dyson, 2012; Milinski et al., 2016) by introducing *repeated*, temporally patterned climate-damage losses rather than a single end-of-game catastrophe, and shows that strategic heterogeneity — specifically the exogenous, random presence of steadfast extortioners — rather than the loss schedule is what determines collective mitigation outcomes. It reframes the persistence of climate change as being effectively "enforced" by a stable minority of extortioners whose behavior is unresponsive to escalating, anticipated losses.

**Practical Implication**
Because extortioners do not adjust behavior even as anticipated losses escalate, voluntary individual-level mechanisms (e.g., merely increasing the frequency of decision opportunities) are insufficient to sustain cooperative climate mitigation. The authors suggest governments, institutions, and policy could curb such behavior through systemic measures linking individual effort to collective action — for example, a global carbon tax.

**Limitations**
The design deliberately abstracts away real-world complexity (no communication, fixed thresholds, a student subject pool), so it isolates mechanisms rather than replicating field conditions. The authors note they cannot formally test a "give-up" effect (whether players stop compensating once the threshold is unreachable), and that incorporating governments/institutions would have required adding layers of variation that risked introducing confounding factors.

**Future Research**
The authors point toward modeling the role of governments, institutions, and policy instruments (such as a global carbon tax) that link individual efforts to collective action, while managing the added experimental variation this would introduce. More broadly, the results invite investigation of how the exogenous, randomly distributed presence of steadfast extortioners can be detected and counteracted in real-world coordination problems.
