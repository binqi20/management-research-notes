# Stakeholder Political Ideology, Purpose of Business Beliefs, and Responses to CEO Activism: Introducing an Asymmetry Model

**Abstract**
CEO activism refers to public stance-taking by CEOs on politically polarized issues. The prevailing view is that liberal and conservative stakeholders respond symmetrically to CEO activism, like how liberal and conservative voters have been found to respond to ideological causes. Specifically, both groups are thought to similarly favor ideologically aligned CEO activism, and disfavor ideologically misaligned CEO activism and nonactivism. We question the assumption that liberal–conservative symmetry in national politics translates to stakeholder preferences. Extending research indicating that liberals scrutinize the perceived benefits and costs that businesses impose on society more than conservatives due to differences in purpose of business beliefs, we theorize that liberal stakeholders respond more favorably to aligned activism, and less favorably to misaligned activism and non-activism. We corroborate our ideological asymmetry model in three preregistered experiments examining job seekers and consumers. Crucially, conservatives, unlike liberals, prefer non-activism over aligned activism, even when conservatives feel strongly about the underlying issue.

**Research Question**
Do liberal and conservative stakeholders respond symmetrically to CEO sociopolitical activism (and a lack thereof), as the stakeholder alignment model assumes, or do liberal–conservative differences in beliefs about the purpose of business produce asymmetric responses to ideologically aligned activism, misaligned activism, and non-activism?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Stakeholder political ideology (binary: conservative = -1, liberal = +1); CEO activism condition / ideological (mis)alignment (ideologically aligned, ideologically misaligned, no CEO activism)
- DV(s): Intention to pursue employment (Experiments 1a, 1b); product purchase intention (Experiment 2)
- Mediators: Liberal–conservative gap in beliefs regarding the purpose of business (manipulated as the proposed explanation in Experiment 2's double-randomization mediation design)
- Moderators: The ideology x (mis)alignment interaction is the focal effect; the purpose-of-business manipulation moderates (attenuates) the asymmetry

The proposed process is that, because liberals more than conservatives believe firms should pursue societal well-being beyond core profit-generating functions, liberal stakeholders scrutinize the broader societal benefits and costs of a CEO's activism more intensely. Consequently, liberals react more positively to ideologically aligned activism (H1) and more negatively to misaligned activism (H2) than conservatives, and react more negatively to CEO silence (H3). Conservatives, viewing activism as a deviation from the firm's core functions, can prefer non-activism even over ideologically aligned activism. Experimentally narrowing the liberal–conservative gap in purpose of business beliefs causes the asymmetries to dissipate, supporting the proposed explanation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces an "ideological asymmetry model" of stakeholder responses to CEO activism that challenges the dominant "stakeholder alignment model" assumption of liberal–conservative symmetry, showing that symmetry observed among voters in national politics does not translate to the firm–stakeholder domain. By using a double-randomization mediation design it provides causal evidence that the asymmetries stem from political differences in purpose of business beliefs, contributing to literatures on ideological fit in organizations, corporate purpose (by exposing stakeholder heterogeneity in purpose beliefs), and firm–stakeholder relations, while offering a stakeholder-demand explanation for the rarity of conservative CEO activism.

**Practical Implication**
CEOs should recognize that the strategic payoffs of activism are asymmetric: liberal activism can attract liberal stakeholders, but conservative activism appears invariably costly because even conservative stakeholders prefer silence over conservative stance-taking. CEOs navigating polarizing issues can use these asymmetric stakeholder preferences to calibrate whether, and on which causes, to speak up given their firm's stakeholder base.

**Limitations**
The studies focus on stakeholder political ideology while acknowledging that stakeholders vary on many other dimensions, and they examine stakeholders with the potential to form a relationship with the firm rather than those who already identify with it, whose reactions could differ. The experimental, scenario-based design uses hypothetical firms and behavioral intentions rather than realized behavior; effects of activism vividness and field/real-world dynamics were not directly tested in the main studies.

**Future Research**
Future work could test the ideological asymmetry model among other stakeholder types (e.g., stakeholders who already identify with the firm, or informational intermediaries such as the media), examine how the vividness of CEO activism amplifies the observed asymmetries, and apply social media field experiments in the startup context where stakeholder bases are not yet established. Scholars could also investigate whether instances and success of corporate activism ebb and flow with the relative political and cultural power of conservatives versus liberals.
