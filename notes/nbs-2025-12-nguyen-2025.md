# Engaged and Responsible Scholarship: Why Qualitative Researchers Should Not Embrace GenAI

**Abstract**
We argue that placing a generative artificial intelligence model between qualitative researchers and their subjects of inquiry fundamentally displaces and distorts the conditions under which meaningful and epistemologically sound knowledge can emerge. We further argue that such mediation raises ethical implications that are incompatible with the principles of responsible research that underpin business and society scholarship.

**Research Question**
Should qualitative researchers in business and society scholarship "embrace" generative AI tools (as Anis and French, 2023, advocate), and is the uptake of GenAI a simple matter of balancing utility against researcher oversight? The authors answer in the negative, asking what placing a GenAI tool between researchers and their subjects of inquiry does to the epistemological and ethical foundations of qualitative inquiry.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: placing GenAI between qualitative researchers and their subjects of inquiry displaces and distorts the conditions under which epistemologically sound knowledge emerges, and is incompatible with responsible-research principles.
- Key constructs / counter-claims: rebuts the three virtues claimed by Anis and French (2023) — (1) efficiency is the wrong scientific value; (2) GenAI fabricates rather than explicates, because it cannot comprehend the social world; (3) GenAI is exploitative rather than equitable, facilitating epistemic exploitation under the guise of empowerment.
- Boundary condition: the authors are not rejecting technological innovation per se, but the deliberate, instrumentalist insertion of GenAI into the interpretive core of qualitative inquiry.

The authors marshal Tsui and McKiernan's (2022) principles of responsible research to argue that qualitative knowledge is produced through slow, embodied, and relational practices that require meaningful engagement with people's lived experiences. Delegating sense-making to "decontextualized algorithms" reconfigures participants as disembodied "data objects," generates untraceable outputs that preclude an audit trail, and cedes epistemic and scientific responsibilities to systems that lack contextual understanding, reasoning, and reflexivity. They reframe how researchers conduct research as itself a normative act, urging scholarly communities — rather than commercial developers — to set the boundaries between algorithmic outputs and qualitative findings.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The commentary recasts the adoption of GenAI in qualitative research as a normative and epistemological question rather than a question of utility and oversight, directly countering the instrumentalist framing of Anis and French (2023). By grounding its critique in Tsui and McKiernan's (2022) principles of responsible research, it positions human interpretation as a situated, reflexive, meaning-making practice that algorithmic systems cannot replicate, and frames the defense of qualitative inquiry as a commitment to science as a public good.

**Practical Implication**
For qualitative researchers, the challenge lies less in competing with GenAI's supposed efficiencies than in reaffirming the distinctive contributions of human-centered inquiry. Scholarly communities — not commercial developers — must actively set and maintain the boundaries between algorithmic outputs and qualitative findings so that reflexivity, context, and social responsibility remain central to teaching, mentoring, and scholarship.

**Future Research**
The authors urge the business and society community to lead and shape this scholarly conversation, beginning by refusing to cede epistemic responsibilities to technological solutions and reaffirming scholarly practices that promote the production of meaningful, socially engaged knowledge.
