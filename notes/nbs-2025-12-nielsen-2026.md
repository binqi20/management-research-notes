# Impact, Interrupted: How and When Thwarted Prosocial Impact Undermines Employee Performance and Retention

**Abstract**
To attract and motivate employees, many organizations convey a compelling mission and design jobs with the apparent opportunity to make a prosocial difference. Yet, this opportunity may not always be fully realized despite the best efforts of employees. In our research, we examine experiences of “thwarted prosocial impact”—the belief that the organization is limiting the prosocial impact one could have through their work. Drawing from psychological contract theory and the relational job design literature, we suggest that thwarted prosocial impact undermines the performance and retention of individual employees because it leads employees to perceive that their ideological contract with the organization is not being sufficiently fulfilled. Further, we theorize that these deleterious effects are particularly strong for those with high levels of contact with beneficiaries in their jobs. We test these ideas in a large sample of fully employed alumni from a public university. Results indicate that thwarted prosocial impact predicts low levels of ideological contract fulfillment, particularly for those in high-beneficiarycontact jobs. These effects were associated with lower coworker-rated boosterism behavior and greater self-rated counterproductive behavior directed at the organization. Through serial mechanisms of ideological fulfillment and counterproductive work behavior, thwarted impact significantly predicted voluntary turnover one year later.

**Research Question**
How and when does "thwarted prosocial impact"—the belief that one's organization is limiting the prosocial impact one could otherwise have through one's job—undermine employee performance (boosterism, counterproductive work behavior toward the organization) and retention (voluntary turnover)? The paper asks whether ideological contract fulfillment is the explanatory mechanism and whether beneficiary contact exacerbates these effects.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Thwarted prosocial impact (belief that the organization limits one's potential prosocial impact)
- DV(s): Boosterism (coworker-rated OCB–O), counterproductive work behavior directed at the organization (CWB–O, self-rated), and voluntary turnover (one year later)
- Mediators: Ideological contract fulfillment (primary mediator); CWB–O as a second-stage mediator in serial paths to turnover
- Moderators: Beneficiary contact (hypothesized exacerbating factor on the thwarted-impact → fulfillment link); gender examined post hoc

Drawing on psychological contract theory and relational job design, the authors argue that thwarted impact signals that the organization has reneged on the *ideological* dimension of the psychological contract—its implied obligation to advance a valued cause. Reduced ideological contract fulfillment then triggers reciprocity-driven withdrawal: less boosterism, more CWB–O, and ultimately voluntary exit. Because beneficiary contact makes the implications of thwarted impact more salient, employees in high-contact jobs experience a stronger negative effect on fulfillment, magnifying the downstream behavioral consequences.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces thwarted prosocial impact as a construct distinct from job opportunity for impact and perceived prosocial impact, showing that current impact perceptions say little about whether employees feel the organization is constraining them. It is among the first studies to identify an antecedent of ideological contract fulfillment and to use that fulfillment to explain how an organizational experience translates into performance and retention outcomes. It also reframes beneficiary contact—usually treated as a motivator—as a double-edged relational characteristic that can amplify negative reactions when prosocial potential is blocked.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should monitor whether employees, especially those in high-beneficiary-contact roles, feel their prosocial impact is being thwarted, for example via "stay" interviews and voice solicitation. Because the strongest specific driver was organizational rules and procedures, relaxing or clearly justifying unnecessary bureaucratic constraints—and designing policies that authentically match the organization's stated mission—can protect motivation and retention.

**Limitations**
The field-survey design supports temporal precedence (one-year turnover lag) but not strong causal inference, and the study had a low response rate that could skew results if nonresponse related to study variables. The sample is occupationally diverse but Western, and the model may apply more readily to service than to manufacturing contexts; coworker ratings of boosterism may be deficient, and left-censored turnover data were ill suited for hazard analysis.

**Future Research**
The authors call for work examining whether thwarted impact operates through non-ideological channels (e.g., perceived organizational care), how industry, sector (nonprofit vs. for-profit), and national culture moderate the effects, and whether interventions targeting rules/procedures or beneficiary contact attenuate or unexpectedly worsen thwarted impact. They also suggest sampling newcomers for hazard analysis and studying reactions targeted at supervisors as proximal agents of the organization.
