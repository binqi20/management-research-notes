# When the Time Never Comes: Temporal Mobilization and Temporal Tensions in a Nascent Solar Energy Field

**Abstract**
Studies show how actors successfully engage with temporal mobilization, understood here as purposeful attempts to harmonize temporal perceptions across different field communities, to foster cross-community engagement with rising issues. These studies predominantly focus on established fields where organizations are prone to adopt similar rhythms and timeframes for action. Less is known about how temporal tensions emerge and develop between communities over temporal mobilization attempts and how they influence mobilization processes. We address this through a four-decade qualitative investigation of temporal mobilization in a nascent solar energy field. Our findings show how temporal mobilization polarizes asynchrony in community engagement, based on the “imprinting” of early temporal mobilization attempts and the emergence of conflicting perceptions of the nature of progress in the field among communities.

**Research Question**
How do temporal tensions emerge and develop during temporal mobilization attempts in nascent fields? How do temporal tensions influence mobilization processes?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Temporal mobilization (harmonizing temporal perceptions across communities); polarizing asynchrony (community perceptions of opportune time drifting apart along the past–present–future spectrum); recurring asynchrony; mobilization breakdowns.
- Communities / actors: Visionaries (scientists, entrepreneurs; future-oriented, long timeframes); modest anticipators (innovation intermediaries, NGOs, politicians; mid-range timeframes); present-driven actors (incumbents, vested policy domain; short timeframes).
- Process / phases: Three macro-trend-driven sequences — Sequence 1 "Solar as an alternative to oil" (1973–1988, demonstrating short-term feasibility); Sequence 2 "Solar as a solution to climate change" (1989–2007, promoting long-term potential); Sequence 3 "Solar as an expanding global market" (2008–2016, promoting short-term opportunities). Each begins with collective engagement and ends in asynchrony and breakdown.
- Inter-community mechanisms: (1) Imprinting — early temporal mobilization attempts mold expectations and cause delays in later engagement; (2) Conflicting perceptions of the nature of progress — visionaries perceive circularity (a "deep" present orientation projecting past disappointments), new entrants perceive linearity (a "shallow" present orientation).
- Emergent outcomes: Self-sustaining short-termism, repeated mobilization breakdowns, and the local field's marginalization in global comparison despite its early pioneering role.

Across four decades, each community articulates an "opportune time" to engage by comparing the field's expected maturity (shaped by prior experiences) against its preferred engagement timeframe; misalignment produces asynchrony. Because early hyped expectations and disappointment (e.g., the "Solar Hamlet" demonstration) imprint subsequent attempts and because pioneers come to read repeated breakdowns as circular non-progress, asynchrony recurs and polarizes unintentionally, repeatedly stalling mobilization and reproducing short-termism.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper expands understandings of synchronized engagement in fields by theorizing (a)synchronies as timeframe-based community interpretations (expected vs. preferred timeframes) rather than as planned, one-time outcomes imposed by dominant actors. It extends imprinting from single organizations back to the field level, showing how formative temporal mobilization efforts shape engagement across communities over decades, and it theorizes short-termism as a collectively constructed, self-sustaining group process driven by the malleability of temporal perceptions and orientations under shifting macro-economic trends.

**Practical Implication**
For advocates mobilizing nascent sustainability fields, early hyped expectations and bold short-term demonstrations can backfire by imprinting lasting stigma and caution that suppress later engagement; sustaining momentum requires managing how communities interpret the field's expected maturity and avoiding self-reinforcing circularity perceptions among long-engaged pioneers.

**Limitations**
The study is contextually bounded: it examines a single emerging technological field (solar energy) in one country (Finland) with unusually harsh geographic conditions and entrenched incumbent energy sources, where recurring short-termism partly reflects that dominant position. Findings on the interpretive aspects of asynchrony are most applicable to settings of high uncertainty about an issue's importance and maturity timeframe.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine conflicting maturity interpretations during issue emergence and their effects on group dynamics; how temporal mobilization is enabled and constrained by unfolding macro-economic trends; and how challengers in non-dominant positions can employ temporal mobilization to resolve temporal conflicts and propel collective action ("How can challengers employ temporal mobilization to resolve temporal conflicts successfully in field settings...?").
