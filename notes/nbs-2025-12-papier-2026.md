# Forced Labor in Labor Supply Chains: Contracting and Information Asymmetry

**Abstract**
To implement incentive-compatible contracts that deter coercive labor outcomes, the buyer may need to allocate informational rents to the agent by foregoing a portion of its surplus. We find that information asymmetry regarding the agent's true recruitment capability necessitates that the buyer offers a "menu of contracts" to prevent the risk of forced labor. This menu benefits exactly one type of agent, depending on the earnings differential between the available contracts, but always leaves a financial surplus for agents with strong recruitment capabilities.

**Research Question**
How do market and economic factors influence the occurrence of forced labor in labor supply chains, and how can a socially aware buyer design optimal contracts (price and audit level) to deter a profit-maximizing labor agent from coercing workers when the agent's true recruitment capability (labor pool size) is private information?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: A buyer cannot directly observe an agent's recruitment capability; an agent with insufficient labor pool has financial incentive to substitute costly coercion for fair wages, so the buyer must use contract price and pre-contract auditing as substitute levers to make coercion unprofitable.
- Key constructs: labor pool size K (recruitment capability / agent type), coercion level g, fair wage w0 = D/K, audit level a and audit cost ca, buyer contract price p, post-contract NGO scrutiny.
- Boundary conditions / cases: low audit cost (buyer pays fair wage, audits heavily, extracts full surplus) vs. high audit cost (buyer pays above fair wage, audits minimally, leaves agent a surplus); full information vs. asymmetric information (menu of contracts); single agent vs. multiple agents (sequential Dutch reverse-auction menu).
- Emergent outcomes: incentive-compatible menu of contracts; informational rent to the high-capability agent; coercion-free equilibrium.

Modeling the buyer-agent interaction as a Stackelberg game solved by backward induction, the paper shows that price and audit are substitutes for deterring coercion. Under full information the optimal coercion-free contract depends on the audit cost. Under asymmetric information about the agent's labor pool size, the buyer must offer a menu of two contracts satisfying incentive-compatibility and individual-rationality constraints; the earnings differential between contracts determines which single agent type benefits from the information asymmetry, but the high-capability (large labor pool) agent always retains a positive surplus regardless of audit cost. Extending to many agents, a sequential menu mimicking a Dutch reverse auction selects the agent with the largest labor pool while remaining coercion-free, though the surplus-retention ("protection") effect diminishes as the number of competing agents grows.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends supply-chain contracting and mechanism-design theory to the previously underexplored problem of forced labor, focusing on private information about an agent's *recruitment capability* (labor pool size) rather than the more common private information about a supplier's behavior or effort. It shows that, contrary to full-information intuition and to prior responsible-sourcing models, the optimal menu offers a *lower* price and *higher* quantity to the more capable agent, and that information asymmetry can let a low-capability agent use coercion as a strategic "threat" to extract rents.

**Practical Implication**
Buyers should pay labor agents above the minimum fair wage to reduce coercion incentives, especially when audit costs are high, and should design incentive-compatible menus of contracts rather than a single one-size-fits-all contract, which the paper shows fails to eliminate forced labor. Because addressing information asymmetry is costly (the buyer must concede informational rents), buyers can complement auditing by investing in learning about agents' true recruitment capabilities; in the numerical illustration the menu reduces the buyer's expected costs by more than 60%.

**Limitations**
The model assumes the worker's reservation wage is uniformly distributed and that the audit detection probability is the product of audit level, pre-contract information, and the share of coercion—assumptions made for tractability. It also analyzes a single-period interaction, ignoring potential effects of long-term buyer-agent relationships, and the buyer selects exactly one agent. Estimating precise parameter values is difficult in contexts involving potentially illegal activity, so the numerical experiments illustrate rather than quantify.

**Future Research**
The authors suggest extending the setting to cases where the buyer must select multiple agents, establishing the findings empirically from field data, and investigating how buyer competition moderates the results.
