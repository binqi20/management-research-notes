# Who’s Afraid of the Minimum Wage? Measuring the Impacts on Independent Businesses Using Matched U.S. Tax Returns

**Abstract**
A common concern surrounding minimum wage policies is their impact on independent businesses, which are often feared to be less able to bear or pass on cost increases. We examine how these typically small and medium-size firms accommodate minimum wage increases along product and labor market margins using a matched owner-firm-worker panel data set drawn from the universe of U.S. tax records over a 10-year period, and using state minimum wage changes as identifying variation. We find that on average, firms in highly exposed industries do not substantially reduce employment—they do not lay off workers but moderately reduce part-time hiring. Instead, these firms are able to fully finance the new labor costs with new revenues, leaving average owner profits unchanged. Higher wage floors, however, forestall entry, particularly for less productive firms, reducing the number of independent firms operating in these industries by roughly 2%. Yet these industries do not shrink; instead, incumbent responses and strong positive selection among entrants reshape industries that rely heavily on low-wage workers, yielding fewer but more productive firms after the cost shock.

**Research Question**
How do independent (predominantly small and medium-size, pass-through) U.S. firms accommodate minimum wage increases along product- and labor-market margins, who ultimately bears the economic burden of higher wage floors, and how are potentially vulnerable low-earning and young workers affected?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): State minimum wage increases (19 increases of at least \$1 beginning 2013-2016 across 17 states, Washington DC, and Chicago; firm operating in a treated state = treat_jc indicator)
- DV(s): Firm employment relationships, wage bill, revenue, COGS/other nonlabor inputs, owner profits, value added per worker, firm entry/exit; worker-level annual earnings, employment probability, retention, and across-firm transitions
- Mediators: Reduced firm entry and strong positive selection of entrants (demand reallocation toward more productive surviving firms); price pass-through to consumers; higher worker retention and reallocation from independent firms to large C-corporations
- Moderators: Industry exposure (restaurants vs. other/retail); baseline firm productivity, size, and reliance on low-wage labor; worker age (teens vs. older) and baseline employer type

Incumbent firms absorb the labor-cost shock not by laying off workers (they only trim very part-time, mostly teenage hiring) but by financing higher wage bills with new revenue, leaving owner profits intact—so consumers, not owners, bear essentially 100% of the incidence. At the extensive margin, higher wage floors deter entry (~2% fewer firms), and the entrants who remain are positively selected on productivity and cost efficiency; incumbents also become leaner and more productive. The result is an industry-level reshaping toward fewer but more productive firms in which workers receive a larger slice of a larger revenue pie, while worker reallocation toward C-corporations buffers the modest hiring reductions so individual employment stays flat and earnings rise.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study provides the first comprehensive, multi-margin documentation of minimum wage incidence for a large set of U.S. firms—jointly tracing product-market, labor-market, and productivity responses using administrative tax data—and shows that minimum wages mainly redistribute from customers to workers rather than from owners to workers. By identifying off clean never-treated control states (rather than baseline exposure), it estimates novel extensive-margin effects, showing how reduced entry and selection among entrants shift the productivity distribution of exposed industries, akin to how new technology, trade, or recessions reallocate activity toward more productive firms.

**Practical Implication**
The findings undercut the common rationale for small-business exemptions from minimum wage hikes: even small and medium-size independent firms accommodate phased-in increases without layoffs or profit losses, financing higher pay through added revenue. Policymakers can expect higher wage floors to raise low earners' incomes (~18-22%) with negligible disemployment, while the principal real cost is somewhat dampened new-firm entry, partly borne by would-be entrepreneurs.

**Limitations**
The estimates capture short- to medium-run effects (four years out) of phased-in increases; longer-run responses may differ if entrants or incumbents reconfigure away from low-wage labor. Because the tax data do not separately report prices and quantities, the authors cannot empirically distinguish price pass-through from demand reallocation as the source of revenue gains, nor can they observe hours, so reductions in employment relationships cannot be fully translated into changes in jobs.

**Future Research**
Future work could assess the longer-run effects of minimum wage increases as firms adapt production technologies and input mixes, and examine margins not estimated here—such as reduced workplace amenities or deferred equipment maintenance. Separating price from quantity responses and estimating effects on hours and tasks would sharpen the interpretation of how independent firms and their workers absorb wage-floor policies.
