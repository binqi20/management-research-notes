# Local development based on non-timber forest products: Revisiting the case of São Francisco do Iratapuru in the Brazilian Amazon

**Abstract**
Using information from former studies as well as own empirical data from interviews, a representative household survey and focus groups, we show that the quality of life in the village has improved markedly over the last decades while the forest has been conserved. We observed an increase in real income, a reduction in income inequality and better access of households to marketed as well as public goods and services, except eletricity.

**Research Question**
To better understand whether and how strategies for NTFPs can be effective in achieving the dual goals of local economic development and forest conservation, the study analyses in-depth the long-term economic, social, and environmental impacts of NTFP-based development interventions in the case of São Francisco do Iratapuru.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering conditions: chronic land disputes, dependence on barter middlemen, lack of marketing and education access, prompting 22 families to found the village (1992) and the Comaru cooperative.
- Intervention strategies (facilitators): public procurement of Brazil nut biscuits (Amapá State), infrastructure investment plus protected-area creation (federal PPG7), and a guaranteed-purchase company-community partnership (Natura), with an incidental hydroelectric-dam intervention (CEJA).
- Key constructs / tensions: local institutions (cooperative/associations) vs. external interventions; value-added processing vs. raw-product sale; income growth vs. income inequality and elite capture; productive vs. community infrastructure.
- Process / phases: timeline reconstruction (1992–2023) of initiatives related to selected economic, social, and environmental indicators, then probing causal results chains via triangulated qualitative and secondary data.
- Emergent outcomes: ~three-fold real-income increase, reduced inequality, near-universal access to goods/services (except electricity), and zero deforestation in the reserve.
- Boundary conditions: low external deforestation pressure, low-perishability/hard-to-substitute NTFPs, well-defined property rights, women's involvement, and pre-existing local organizations.

The central process is that durable, locally-rooted organizations channel and sustain external interventions: investment in productive infrastructure with technical assistance and guaranteed NTFP purchases raised value-added and unit prices (harvesters received ~187% more than middlemen prices), tripling real income without higher extraction rates and thereby strengthening residents' incentive and capacity to conserve the forest. The authors argue that external interventions were necessary but not sufficient — local social cohesion (e.g., Comaru predating any state intervention) was the decisive condition enabling the interventions' viability, longevity, and recovery from shocks such as the 2003 factory fire.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study supplies rare long-term, multi-dimensional proof of concept that NTFP-based development can reconcile socio-economic improvement with forest conservation in the Brazilian Amazon. Against a literature that attributes NTFP success chiefly to outside intervention, it foregrounds the centrality of locally created and managed institutions, and surfaces a substantive mismatch between researcher-defined indicators and the development dimensions (e.g., community infrastructure, education, oil-extraction productivity) that local resource users themselves value.

**Practical Implication**
NTFP-based development should be promoted where specialised extraction, capable local organizations, women's involvement, low-perishability/hard-to-substitute products, secure property rights, and controlled deforestation pressure coincide. Financing mechanisms (e.g., the Tropical Forest Forever Facility) should channel funds directly to local communities, and public investment must complement production by securing water, electricity, and other services and by effectively protecting the forest base.

**Limitations**
The analysis is limited by the scope of available indicators — for example, the environmental indicator relies on forest cover rather than forest quality or extraction sustainability. The findings rest on a single, exceptionally well-documented case situated in an area where external pressure to deforest remains low, constraining direct generalization.

**Future Research**
Whether and to what extent NTFP extraction is a viable economic option for sustainable development of Amazonian communities, including under higher deforestation pressure, requires further long-term scientific study. The authors call for robust databases with panels of communities and interventions monitored over time, and for defining and measuring criteria and indicators jointly with local communities, including via local "citizen scientists."
