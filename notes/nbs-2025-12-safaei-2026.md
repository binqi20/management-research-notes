# No News About Climate Action is Good News for Low-Polluting Firms

**Abstract**
Media plays a crucial role in shaping public perception of firms, significantly impacting their operations and performance. Firms often attempt to influence media channels to showcase their climate action initiatives, aiming to enhance their public image, reputation, and stakeholder trust. While firms cannot directly control their media coverage, intentional or unintentional dissemination of their climate action efforts can thrust them into the media spotlight. The implications of this media spotlight remain uncertain. While firms may anticipate positive public relations benefits from such exposure, it may also raise environmental expectations for firms and risk highlighting less favorable aspects of their environmental practices. Despite previous research on the impact of media exposure on firms' financial outcomes, a notable gap exists in understanding how the media spotlight on firms' climate actions affects their operational and financial performance. A clear hurdle is the lack of a systematic and objective measurement of such a spotlight. Our study addresses this research gap by developing a machine learning-based framework to derive a climate action vocabulary as a novel information artifact. The vocabulary is subsequently used to measure the intensity of media attention on a firm's climate actions. Our research reveals that an increased media spotlight on climate actions, regardless of their sentiment, has an adverse effect on firms' financial performance, primarily due to the rise in operational costs. Furthermore, we find that the impact of media spotlight is heterogeneous. Low-polluting firms experience negative financial consequences as the downside of the heightened media spotlight. In contrast, high-polluting firms and those operating in polluting industries may witness an overall positive financial impact. We also find that firms' market orientation (business-to-business vs. business-to-consumer), durability classification, index constituency (i.e., S&P 500), and greenwashing status significantly moderate the relationship. Our research highlights key considerations for corporate leaders with respect to the drawbacks for low-polluting firms in seeking media attention for their climate actions. Given that the media captures society's limited attention, our research suggests that firms should refrain from promoting superficial narratives about climate change that distract society from more impactful sustainability conversations.

**Research Question**
Whether and how climate action-related news spotlight affects firms' financial performance, and how variations in firms' environmental performance (and related firm/industry attributes) influence this relationship.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): News Spotlight — weighted count of climate-action-related news articles for a firm-year, derived from a machine-learning climate-action vocabulary (instrumented with the county-level 2000 "Democratic Voting" ratio); positive/negative spotlight split via FinBERT.
- DV(s): Firm financial performance and market value — ROA, ROS, ROE, ROI, NPM, M-B, Tobin's Q.
- Mediators: Operational costs — Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) and Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A) expenses.
- Moderators: Firm environmental performance (CO2-equivalent emissions), industry pollution status ("dirtiness"), market orientation (B2B vs. B2C), durability classification, S&P 500 index constituency, greenwashing status.

Increased media attention on a firm's climate actions raises environmental expectations and stakeholder scrutiny, compelling firms to undertake costly operational adjustments (supplier vetting, sustainable transitions, intensified marketing, reporting, and audits) that inflate COGS and SG&A and thereby depress financial performance. Mediation analysis shows COGS and SG&A together fully mediate the spotlight-to-ROA effect (25% and 75%, respectively). The effect is heterogeneous: low-polluting firms incur net financial harm because additional environmental investment yields marginal gains at substantial cost, whereas high-polluting firms (and firms in "dirty" industries) — starting from a low reputational baseline with cheaper improvement opportunities — can see the financial sign "flip" to positive.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a novel, automated, interpretable machine-learning climate-action vocabulary as an information artifact for measuring overall media spotlight on firms' climate actions, moving beyond the prior literature's reliance on isolated environmental events and proprietary datasets. Substantively, it isolates the long-term financial impact of the climate-action news spotlight itself (while controlling for actual environmental performance via CO2 emissions) and documents counterintuitive, heterogeneous effects across pollution levels and firm types, advancing research on automated content analysis, firm environmental practices, media coverage, and public relations in operations management.

**Practical Implication**
Firms should tailor their media strategy to their environmental profile: low-polluting (cleaner) firms should be cautious about seeking climate-action attention given its associated costs, while high-polluting firms may derive financial benefits and use the spotlight as a driver for cost-effective improvement. Managers should shift from superficial virtue signaling toward genuine environmental action, and from a societal standpoint media attention is better directed at high-polluting firms with greater potential for environmental strides.

**Limitations**
The findings primarily address firms' financial performance and do not capture intangible, long-term benefits of media spotlight on climate actions that financial measures may miss.

**Future Research**
Future work may further explore the complex dynamics between media spotlight and financial performance amid evolving global sustainability priorities to better understand managerial and operational responses to media scrutiny. Another direction is to examine climate-action-related spotlight in social media and its effect on firm performance.
