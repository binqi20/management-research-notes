# Socio-Ecological Inequalities in Housing Consumption: How Income, Urban Form, and Tenure Drive Carbon Footprints

**Abstract**
We draw on a unique combination of sources—register data about the Dutch population and housing stock, a mobility survey, and life cycle inventory data—to reveal that a) the carbon footprint per person differs starkly between richest and poorest groups; b) the low-density locations and income of richer households drive mobility emissions; c) rental units show larger carbon footprints related to lower quality housing stock

**Research Question**
How and to what extent do income, housing tenure, building type, and urban form impact per capita carbon footprints of housing? The paper develops a multidimensional model to assess socio-ecological inequalities in housing consumption — the overall use of material and energy in the provision and operation of housing.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Household socio-economic status (income decile, top income percentile; housing tenure — owner-occupied, private rental, social rental); physical building properties (building typology, construction period, dwelling size); urban context (address density/urbanity, distance to train station and amenities)
- DV(s): Per capita consumption-based GHG carbon footprint of housing and its components (car use, residential gas, grid electricity, building materials, heating)
- Mediators: Mobility behavior and car use/ownership, through which low-density urban form translates income into emissions; floor area per person, through which household size and detached typology drive energy use
- Moderators: Tenure (degree of resident control over efficiency investment); urban density and proximity to amenities (conditioning the income–car-use link)

Housing footprints in the Netherlands already exceed the per capita Paris-aligned emissions ceiling across all income and tenure groups, with the budget projected to be exhausted by 2033. Inequalities between income groups are driven mainly by car use — when car use is excluded, footprints are remarkably similar across social groups except the top 1%. Higher-income households suburbanize into low-density locations that lock in car dependency, while building-related emissions of the poorest 30% resemble those of the top 10% because of poor insulation and fossil-fuel dependence in older rental stock. Tenure structures who can invest in efficiency: private renters face weak landlord incentives, so suburbanization offsets emissions-saving investments concentrated in owner-occupied stock.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates social and environmental critiques of housing by linking quantitative analyses of environmental footprints to socio-spatial dynamics, opening a niche that treats housing as a key site of socio-ecological inequality. It advances a political-ecology and degrowth-oriented framing of housing consumption, showing that footprint assessments based on household budget surveys and monetary proxies miss the material differences (dwelling size, gas use, kilometers traveled) and tenure dynamics that structure who appropriates housing resources and who is responsible for emissions.

**Practical Implication**
Without a multidimensional understanding of housing's socio-ecological inequalities, some sustainability measures (e.g., carbon pricing) will be socially regressive and must be coupled with support mechanisms (e.g., an ETS2 Social Climate Fund). The authors call for densification, reductions in per capita floor area, progressive income- or asset-based property taxation, substantial investment in social-rental stock and public transport, and land-use planning that curbs suburban sprawl and car dependency.

**Limitations**
The model does not account for the specific car fleet (size/weight) of each housing profile, likely underestimating inequalities since richer households own larger, heavier cars. It lacks information on the retrofitting and energy efficiency of buildings, so residents' energy-use norms cannot be cleanly isolated from building efficiency. Part of the population was excluded due to missing data or suspected dwelling-sharing registration errors, which may overestimate private-rental footprints; income and tenure were analyzed separately despite their interrelation.

**Future Research**
The authors call for further interdisciplinary research into ways to provide socially just and environmentally sustainable housing. They specifically suggest studying interactions between income and tenure, which were analyzed separately here, and extending the model — adjustable to land and material footprints — to compare other affluent countries with more sprawling urban forms or residualized social-housing stock.
