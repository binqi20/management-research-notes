# Climate change mitigation and green energy investment: A stock-flow consistent model

**Abstract**
only slows the low-carbon transition but also depresses aggregate demand, wages, and employment. The paper examines possibilities for reducing the carbon intensity of production and provides insights into the interactions between ecological, macroeconomic, and distributive factors during the transition to a green economy.

**Research Question**
The paper addresses a research gap concerning the interactions between demand-side dynamics and environmental factors, investigating how demand-driven dynamics shape both economic and environmental outcomes during the green energy transition, and how the speed of that transition influences economic and environmental variables. Its central hypothesis is that a combination of targeted public policies and progressive taxation can achieve the low-carbon transition without triggering degrowth or exacerbating income inequality.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Share of energy-sector investment allocated to green technologies (governed by a logistic diffusion process whose speed depends on government mitigation spending); tax rates on rentier income and on wage income; investment sensitivity to GHG emissions
- DV(s): GHG emission flows and carbon intensity; aggregate output, employment, real wages, and consumption (energy and non-energy)
- Mediators: The investment channel — accumulated/flow GHG emissions feed back negatively into non-energy investment; government balanced-budget mitigation spending that accelerates the green diffusion path
- Moderators: Effectiveness of government mitigation spending; investment sensitivity to emissions; the progressivity of the tax mix (rentier vs. wage taxation)

The model couples Kalecki-Steindl-Minsky investment dynamics and Goodwin-type conflicting-claims distribution with an ecological dimension: as energy firms raise their green investment share, emissions decline toward a net-zero steady state, while emission flows feed back into investment. A negative shock to the green investment share slows the transition and depresses aggregate demand; progressive taxation (taxing rentier rather than wage income) funds mitigation spending that sustains demand and employment while still reducing emissions, so decarbonization need not require degrowth.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to demand-led (post-Keynesian) climate macroeconomics by building a tractable, medium-scale two-sector ecological SFC model that integrates aggregate demand, income distribution, and ecological constraints, rather than the supply-side neoclassical growth frameworks that have traditionally dominated climate economics. It advances the literature by endogenizing environmental conditions in investment decisions and by making the speed of the low-carbon transition responsive to fiscal policy, capturing feedback effects between distribution, demand, accumulation, and emissions that standard models omit.

**Practical Implication**
Government mitigation spending and the speed of public response are crucial for reaching net-zero emissions and avoiding degrowth; delayed or weakened mitigation effort produces fossil-fuel lock-in and persistent emission overshooting that propagate through the investment channel. Financing mitigation through progressive taxation — taxing rentier income more heavily than wages — can reduce emissions while sustaining wages and employment, so the cost of the transition need not fall on workers.

**Limitations**
The model incorporates only a single energy-transition policy instrument — a balanced-budget government expenditure on transition-accelerating technology — and abstracts from alternatives such as emission limits and carbon taxes. It is a short-run business-cycle model that abstracts from long-run growth determinants, physical/biophysical constraints, and relative-price and cost differentials between green and brown energy; parameters are calibrated for illustrative purposes rather than estimated, so results indicate the direction of impacts rather than precise magnitudes.

**Future Research**
Future work could explore alternative policy instruments such as emission limits and carbon taxes, whose implications lie beyond this paper's scope. It could also extend the framework by endogenizing the long-run growth trend and incorporating alternative transition pathways as well as innovation dynamics.
