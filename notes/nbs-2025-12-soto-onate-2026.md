# Post-growth meets polycentric governance: Toward an interdisciplinary research program

**Abstract**
the paper produces a conceptual matrix that reveals research avenues, identifies testable hypotheses, and guides practitioners designing post-growth interventions. The result is a coherent roadmap for empirical and theoretical work on governing beyond growth.

**Research Question**
How can societies adjust their economic scale to ecological boundaries without sacrificing democracy, and specifically, how can a polycentric approach to governance be built, maintained, and operationalized to coordinate post-growth transformations while preserving democracy and self-governance at lower political scales? The article asks whether post-growth and polycentricity cohere, and what an integrated research program connecting them should contain.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: post-growth scholarship (which holds that ecosystem limits entail general limits to growth) and polycentric governance (multiple, formally independent decision units nested within an overarching institutional system) share coherent positive grounds, normative underpinnings, and boundary conditions, making their integration into "polycentric post-growth" theoretically consistent.
- Key construct — "polycentric articulation": the process by which rights and responsibilities are polycentrically rearranged to align governance with socio-ecological problems (matching the "shape" of the governance system to the "shape" of the problem, i.e., institutional fit).
- Transitional processes (four rearrangement pathways, distinguished by vertical direction and locus of sovereignty): pool, disaggregation, cession, and appropriation.
- Boundary conditions: both frameworks are circumscribed to societal solutions to collective problems; post-growth is more restrictive on societal goals while polycentricity is more precise on governance.
- Five analytic components mapped into a conceptual matrix: post-growth concerns; autonomous actors; coordination mechanisms; overarching institutional framework; and political-economy dynamics.

The central process is the articulation and disarticulation of jurisdictions: autonomous lower-scale actors and overarching institutions rearrange rights and responsibilities (bottom-up or top-down) to build governance systems whose scope matches the socio-ecological problem, while retaining residual sovereignty at lower scales under the subsidiarity principle. By breaking polycentric reality into its components and the dynamic processes that connect them, the framework yields a transdisciplinary "map" that identifies research avenues, testable hypotheses, and design guidance for governing beyond growth.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper integrates two previously disconnected scholarships — post-growth/degrowth and Bloomington-School polycentricity — into a coherent research program, demonstrating their consistency and formulating the novel concept of "polycentric articulation of post-growth aspirations." It supplies a typology of four (dis)articulation processes and a five-component conceptual matrix that together delineate the domain, research avenues, and testable hypotheses for governing post-growth transitions, addressing the gap whereby degrowth authors invoke local autonomy yet default to top-down, nation-state-centered policies.

**Practical Implication**
The conceptual matrix is intended to guide practitioners designing post-growth interventions by clarifying how the structure of a socio-ecological problem (its scale, rights/responsibilities, and complexity) should shape the corresponding governance architecture and the direction of jurisdictional transfer (pooling, cession, disaggregation, appropriation). It signals where coordination must be built across scales (e.g., for global commons) while preserving democratic self-governance and autonomy at lower scales.

**Limitations**
As an explicitly conceptual, agenda-setting article, it develops no empirical test and acknowledges that central aspects (e.g., how autonomy is granted, supported, and protected; redistributive polycentric architectures; conflict dynamics beyond mutual gain) are only scratched at the surface and remain underexplored. The authors note that complexities emerging specifically from integrating post-growth and polycentricity are still underdeveloped and that, following Elinor Ostrom, "there are no panaceas."

**Future Research**
The article proposes a research program organized around its five components: characterizing post-growth concerns through a polycentric lens; the features and systems formed by autonomous actors; deliberate coordination mechanisms and institutional-design principles; the role of the overarching institutional framework and the state; and the political economy of transition (pathways, conflict, evolutionary dynamics). It calls for work on the enablers, obstacles, and conditions for the emergence of functional polycentric systems that can politically address post-growth concerns.
