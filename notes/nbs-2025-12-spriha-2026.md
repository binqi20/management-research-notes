# How Employees Who Have Made Upward Social Class Transitions Get Heard in the Workplace

**Abstract**
Individuals can move from lower social class origins to higher social class through education and entry into prestigious or well-paying jobs. These individuals, known as upward transitioners, have several strengths. Yet, research suggests that their strengths often go unrecognized in the workplace. We highlight a particular strength that does translate into tangible benefits for upward transitioners. Drawing on theories of social class, cross-cultural adaptation, and power, we propose that upward transitions foster a distinct open-minded communication style in individuals, which we term voice crafting. This style involves acknowledging the fallibility of one's viewpoints and staying receptive to managerial input when speaking up about work issues. It emerges from upward transitioners' experiences navigating unfamiliar higher-class cultural contexts, which build humility and a learning orientation. We argue that voice crafting helps upward transitioners get their ideas and concerns implemented, especially when they are engaging with powerful managers who expect interactive conversations. We find support for our theory across survey, experimental, and qualitative interview studies with diverse samples. In the process, we highlight how upward transitioners, because of their inclination to voice craft, are in unique positions to exercise influence within organizations.

**Research Question**
How and when do the strengths of upward social class transitioners translate into real workplace benefits? Specifically, do upward transitions foster a distinct open-minded communication style (voice crafting), and does that style help such employees get their voice implemented, especially when interacting with highly powerful managers?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Upward social class transition, operationalized as the interaction of social class origins and current social class (lower origins moving to higher current class); manipulated voice crafting (high vs. low) in Study 2C.
- DV(s): Voice crafting (Hypothesis 1 outcome); voice implementation (managers championing and acting on employees' ideas).
- Mediators: Voice crafting (mediating the indirect effect of upward transitions on voice implementation).
- Moderators: Manager power (control over organizational resources, rewards, and punishments).

Cultural retooling during an upward transition forces individuals to question taken-for-granted "doxa," cultivating humility and a learning orientation that together constitute open-mindedness; this is behaviorally expressed as voice crafting—speaking up while acknowledging one's fallibility and inviting managerial input. Drawing on the situated focus theory of power, the authors argue that highly powerful managers are more sensitive to how voice is presented and, via a reciprocal open-mindedness effect and a participation effect, respond more strongly (implementing ideas) when voice is crafted. Thus upward transitions exert a positive indirect effect on voice implementation through voice crafting that is amplified under high manager power.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends social class research by identifying open-mindedness/voice crafting as a strength that is neither inherited from origins nor destinations but emerges from the transition experience itself, and it shows this strength yields tangible workplace influence. It introduces the construct of voice crafting to the voice literature, reframing voice as a two-way co-construction of ideas rather than a one-way persuasion attempt. It also nuances power research by showing that high manager power does not uniformly increase voice implementation but heightens managers' sensitivity to how voice is delivered.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should deliberately create opportunities for upward transitioners to speak up and should recognize and recruit for the value these employees bring. Upward transitioners should recognize that their open-minded conversational style resonates especially well with powerful managers and engage leaders rather than hesitating out of an ingrained reluctance toward authority figures. More broadly, voice crafting can foster mutual open-mindedness that improves problem-solving and helps bridge divides in polarized settings.

**Limitations**
Upward transitions were measured rather than experimentally manipulated, so unobserved characteristics driving mobility cannot be fully ruled out. The studies did not measure the proposed reciprocal open-mindedness mechanism or alternative explanations such as threat reduction, and Study 2's effective samples were reduced by preregistered data-quality exclusions. The qualitative study (Study 3) lacked downward transitioners and lower-class non-transitioners and relied on retrospective accounts.

**Future Research**
Panel studies could track how changes in social class over time affect voice crafting, and research could examine how voice crafting unfolds when subjective and objective class indicators diverge. Scholars could test moderators of the transition–voice crafting link (e.g., just-world beliefs, time spent in the new class, psychological adjustment) and probe whether voice crafting is a habit versus a strategy by examining learning versus instrumental motives. Additional outcomes—informal leadership, creativity, and potential emotional exhaustion from continuously absorbing input—also warrant study.
