# The information value of energy labels: Evidence from the Dutch residential housing market

**Abstract**
This paper examines the information value of energy labels using administrative data on all transactions in the

**Research Question**
Do energy labels provide additional information not readily available to the market, and is this information reflected in housing prices? Does the voluntary or mandatory nature of energy labels affect their information value?

**Mechanism Process**
- DV(s): Natural logarithm of transaction price per square meter (and the binary RDD treatment outcome at Energy Index cutoffs)
- IV(s): Energy label class (A–G categorical scale); the binary "above/below the Energy Index cutoff" treatment dummy in the RDD; label-system regime (voluntary 2008–2014 vs. mandatory 2015–2017)
- Mediators: None (the design probes whether labels carry information *beyond* directly observable energy efficiency rather than a mediating pathway)
- Moderators: Labeling regime (voluntary vs. mandatory); also probed against alternative energy-efficiency measures (Energy Index, electricity and gas use), neighborhood, dwelling type, building year, and housing-market conditions

The authors define a label's "information value" as the extent to which information conveyed by the label *beyond* directly observable features is capitalized into prices. Conventional hedonic models show large premiums for better labels, but these conflate the label with collinear, observable energy efficiency. To isolate any incremental label effect, the sharp RDD compares dwellings just above versus just below the Energy Index thresholds that mechanically determine the discrete label, where dwellings are otherwise similar. For the mandatory system (which lacks the underlying Energy Index), the authors instead use "artificial labels"—checking whether dwellings later labeled already commanded premiums before obtaining a label. Finding no premium at the RDD margins, and substantial pre-label premiums, implies the observed premiums largely reflect unobserved-by-researcher dwelling characteristics that buyers can perceive, so the labels' marginal information value is limited.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to the literature on the market capitalization of energy efficiency by separating the price premium attributable to a label from the premium attributable to readily observable energy efficiency, and by comparing a complex-voluntary against a simple-mandatory disclosure design within one market. It shows that hedonic-pricing premiums for labels are largely an artifact of collinearity and inadequate locational controls, and that better-labeled dwellings already attracted premiums before being labeled—evidence that the introduction of labels per se need not be what drives observed price differences.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should not treat label price premiums as proof that labels themselves add information, nor rely on labeling as the sole instrument for informing the public about energy efficiency; labeling should be one part of a larger information system. Disclosure designs with a more thorough, expert-based evaluation process have greater potential to convey additional information to homebuyers, so the latest (2021) Dutch reform holds promise for improving labels' information function.

**Limitations**
The information value is defined only via transaction prices, so effects on insulation investment or buyer welfare are not assessed, and the asymmetric-information dynamics of the rental market are not examined. The RDD requires the continuous Energy Index, which the mandatory system does not produce, so the mandatory-system conclusions are less direct; the data span 2000–2017, a period of relatively stable Dutch energy prices. The underlying transaction data are confidential.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine the broader information value of energy labels—including effects on insulation investments and the value buyers create—and the role of labels in the rental market and incentive compatibility between landlords and tenants. Researchers could also compare outcomes between periods of stable versus sharply rising energy prices, given heightened post-2022 attention to energy efficiency in the Netherlands.
