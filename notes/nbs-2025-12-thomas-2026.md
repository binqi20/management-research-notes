# Decommodifying Cacao: Matchmaking between Producers and Buyers of Fine Flavour Cacao from Peru

**Abstract**
If decommodification of cacao is to benefit vulnerable smallholder producers, institutions and trade relationships are needed that allow more producers to meet the more stringent requirements of a diversified, high-quality cacao sector.

**Research Question**
The study examines the alignment between fine flavour cacao (FFC) buyers and producers in Peru — focusing on Piura (Blanco de Piura) and Cusco (Chuncho cacao) — assessing the extent to which different producer types match the requirements and interests of different buyer types, and how the matchmaking could be improved so that smallholders benefit from cacao decommodification.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Producer-type attributes (cacao variety, farm management, yields, certification, post-harvest practices, association membership, marketing channel) and buyer-type requirements (quality standards, supplier attributes, business relationships, marketing resources)
- DV(s): Producer-buyer matching scores (attribute matching scores, overall matching scores, percentage matching scores) capturing how well each producer type satisfies each buyer type's demands
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

Buyers were grouped via MCA + HCPC into four types and producers via PCoA + PAM into distinct types per region; weighted attribute-importance scores per buyer type were combined with producer compliance proportions to compute matching scores. The mechanism is that decommodification through FFC shifts trade terms favourably but imposes more stringent quality, ecological, and socio-economic requirements, so benefits accrue selectively to producers able to meet them — potentially reinforcing exclusion of less-equipped smallholders unless institutions and trade relationships close the gaps.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the concept of decommodification beyond its conventional association with common-property regimes and collective action, showing that in a telecoupled resource system like cacao — where long distances separate production and consumption — trade and market exchange remain central, so specialization and value-chain trade are the likely institutional outcome rather than ideal-typic common-property arrangements. It identifies three insights (institutional interplay, the continued role of trade, and patterns of inclusion/exclusion) characterizing decommodification in historical commodity sectors.

**Practical Implication**
The matchmaking methodology lets public authorities, NGOs, farmer organizations, and buyers identify concrete actions (e.g., diversifying buyers, formalizing contracts, enabling credit access, acquiring certifications, increasing wet-cacao marketing, making women's value-chain contributions visible) that specific producer types can take to expand and stabilize their market opportunities and access higher FFC prices.

**Limitations**
Applying the matchmaking methodology at the producer- and buyer-type level risks oversimplifying reality because these groups are not always homogeneous, and connecting specific buyers and producers may require more tailored interventions. North American and Asian buyers may also be underrepresented because buyer interviews were conducted at European-centred trade fairs.

**Future Research**
Future research is needed to test whether different matching scores account for differences in the socio-economic and ecological outcomes of FFC production.
