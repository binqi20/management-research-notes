# “Taking Wendake Beyond Wendake”: Economic Development and Legal Tenacity in the Wendat Community

**Abstract**
The economic self-sufficiency of Indigenous communities has become a widely recognized debate in Canada. This article analyzes how the Indigenous community of Wendake, located in Québec, is working toward the cultural resurgence and recognition of its identity—in order to combat the negative consequences ensuing from the Indian Act of 1876. The latter resulted in Indigenous populations declared legally irresponsible to own their land. Drawing on Axel Honneth’s concept of the struggle for recognition, we show how the Wendat community relied on what we call its inherited capital of recognition (notably its ancestral political and economic dispositions) to peacefully assert itself in two key areas. First, the legal right to have treaties signed with colonial powers from past centuries recognized; and second, economic development through the creation of a financial system adapted to Indigenous specificities. Our analysis points to a process of peaceful struggle for the resurgence and recognition of Indigenous identity in what at first glance appears to be a process of neocolonial subjugation to the market economy.

**Research Question**
In a broader context of neocolonial economic development, how does an Indigenous community pursue peaceful struggles for the resurgence of its culture and the recognition of its Indigenous identity?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: inherited capital of recognition (accumulated ancestral self-esteem); Honneth's three spheres of recognition — love, legal rights, social esteem; tangibility of achievements
- Triggering condition: the Indian Act of 1876 denying Indigenous dignity and declaring populations legally irresponsible to own their land, superimposed on historic treaties
- Practices / peaceful struggles: (a) legal struggles to re-establish old treaties (e.g., the 1990 "Sioui case" recognizing the 1760 Murray Treaty; framework self-determination agreement); (b) economic development by creating an Indigenous-adapted financial apparatus (SOCCA, founded 1992) and a tourism/cultural-identity project (Hôtel-Musée, Pow-Wow, off-reserve exhibitions)
- Emergent outcome: strengthened legal rights and social esteem, consolidating the Nation's overall capital of recognition

The paper argues that because ancestral cultural specificities were recognized and experienced as rewarding, the Wendats built self-esteem that became an inherited capital of recognition — the foundation for contemporary, non-violent yet assertive struggles. Rather than radical rupture (assertive recognition) or passive dependence (reconciliation), the community pursued a "third way," asserting treaty rights through the courts and building a financial system adapted to the unseizability of reserve land. Tangible legal and economic achievements furnished recognition through the gaze of the non-Indigenous "other," reinforcing self-respect and social esteem without dissolving Indigenous identity into the market economy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper's main contribution is the concept of inherited capital of recognition — historically accumulated self-esteem (conveyed through historical books and oral narratives) on which contemporary emancipatory struggles are grounded and which, when struggles succeed, strengthens the community's overall capital of recognition. It extends Honneth's theory of recognition to an Indigenous community navigating neither rupture with nor passive subjection to the state, and underlines the role of tangibility in establishing recognition. The authors also frame their work as a pragmatic (not merely theoretical) contribution that could serve the agenda of Indigenous revitalization.

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest that Indigenous communities look to their pre-colonial history to identify cultural markers and restore self-confidence as a basis for recognition struggles, and that awareness, reappropriation, and diffusion of these markers can be facilitated through training programs. They argue legal struggle and economic development—pursued peacefully—can restore dignity and self-respect even where the prospects of full victory remain uncertain.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge their analytic journey is incomplete and not without limitations: proximity to cities may significantly affect development potential, and the Wendat case is not mechanically transferable to communities (e.g., the Sioux) whose conditions make restoring traditional ways of life more complex. As non-Indigenous researchers with initially low understanding of First Nations' quests, they note their study required intensive reflexivity, co-construction of the object of study with the community, and vigilance against the threat of neo-colonialism; fieldwork was also fragmented across stays.

**Future Research**
Future research could further investigate the potential for resurgence and identity recognition in conjunction with work on communities in South America, Australia, and New Zealand—including more precise identification of sources of cultural self-esteem rooted in a community's history, arguments for the legal consolidation of Indigenous rights, and economic strategies closely linked to cultural markers. The authors also point to open questions about which cultural representations are chosen for tourist consumption and which "accommodations" are acceptable in hybridizing tradition with modernity.
