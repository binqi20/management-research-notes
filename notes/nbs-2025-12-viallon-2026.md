# Resource regimes and the success of local renewable energy projects

**Abstract**
More specifically, public landownership and revenue sharing increase the chances of project success, as this entails the involvement of local democratic institutions and supports the resilience of project promotors against political contestation.

**Research Question**
Under what configurations do property rights and public policies jointly explain the success or failure of local renewable energy projects (REPs)? The study tests whether four conditions — public landownership, revenue sharing, strategic planning, and environmental legislation — are necessary and/or sufficient for REP success.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key conditions (property rights): public landownership (PUBLIC) within the REP area; revenue sharing (RESOURCE) with local public and/or private actors.
- Key conditions (public policies): strategic planning (PLAN) prior to local development plans; environmental and landscape legislation (ENV), where the absence of use restrictions favors success.
- Outcome: degree of REP success, fuzzy-set coded (0 = abandoned, 0.3 = on hold due to contestation, 0.7 = ongoing administrative/judicial procedure, 1 = operating).
- Causal logic: configurational (multiple conjunctural causation, equifinality, asymmetry); four sufficient paths identified via Boolean minimization (intermediate solution).

Drawing on the institutional resource regime (IRR) approach, the paper argues that ownership and policy regulation are interdependent in shaping resource use. Public landownership lets authorities simultaneously control planning and land, resolving "complexity traps," while revenue sharing incentivizes local actors to support a project. Public landowners (e.g., communes, cantons) prove more involved in REP initiation and more resilient against political contestation than private landowners, who may judge contested projects too risky and withdraw support.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the institutional resource regime approach by explicitly bringing property rights into the analysis of REP implementation, which prior literature on local acceptance and policy integration treats only through proxies. It shows that public policies alone are insufficient and that property-rights conditions — especially public landownership and revenue sharing — are central to explaining REP success, contributing to debates on energy democracy and energy citizenship.

**Practical Implication**
Because landownership matters, policymakers should review means of securing public land control (expropriation, pre-emption, emption rights) in areas targeted for REPs, and consider redistributing project revenues locally to build support. Authorities may also use land-policy instruments to support private landowners, including co-landownership arrangements with private investors.

**Limitations**
The number of cases (twelve) is limited, so replication with additional cases is needed to refine the results. Scope conditions apply: the cases are located in wealthy, liberal Western democracies, and institutional differences (legal systems, levels of government) are not explicitly modeled, which may limit generalizability.

**Future Research**
Ongoing cases should be reconsidered later in light of legislative, judicial, economic, and technological changes. Further work could study the governance arrangements of REPs — involved actors, ownership, management, and decision rules — and how restructuring the distribution of property rights (e.g., delegating disposition and use rights over energy land to sub-national authorities) might accelerate the energy transition.
