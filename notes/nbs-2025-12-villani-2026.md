# The Grey Shades of Green Jobs: Unpacking the Occupational Approach to Green Employment

**Abstract**
Second, we discuss the methodological problems of this approach, including inconsistent categorisations, outdated classifications, and the assumption that the content of occupations remains constant across time and countries.

**Research Question**
Is the occupational (task-based) approach — the dominant framework used by academics and institutions to measure green employment — theoretically and methodologically valid for capturing the labour-market impact of the green transition, and does the green employment it estimates actually track environmental outcomes?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Share of "green jobs" / green labour input in a country (and industry), measured via the O*NET occupational approach — binary categorisation (GID/GES/GNE) and the continuous Vona et al. (2018) "greenness" index
- DV(s): Green and environmental performance indicators (GEI) — GHG emissions per capita, EGSS output share, plus EPI, Green Future Index, firm-level GHG targets, and sectoral renewables/recycling shares
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Not reported in paper (specifications instead vary by timing — contemporaneous, lagged, and first-differenced — and by country/year fixed effects)

The occupational approach infers a job's environmental contribution from its occupational title or task description rather than from what the worker or firm actually produces. This construct mismatch generates type I errors (workers in "green" occupations producing non-green outputs, e.g., robotics technicians in oil refining) and type II errors (essential contributors such as accountants or pesticide handlers in green firms going uncounted), and is compounded by arbitrary, outdated, US-anchored classifications requiring lossy aggregation. Because occupational structures are stable, the measure shows essentially flat green employment in Europe (2011-2022) and no robust correlation with environmental indicators — reflecting the framework's flaws rather than a failure of the green transition itself.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides the first systematic scrutiny of the occupational approach to green employment that runs from its theoretical foundations through to empirical quantification, showing that the framework is misaligned with the very concept of green employment (which depends on outputs and processes, not occupational titles). It demonstrates that this construct-validity problem, not measurement noise alone, makes occupational measures unsuitable for benchmarking the green transition, and argues for output- and process- (organisation-) based alternatives.

**Practical Implication**
Because accurate green-employment measures are central to reskilling, employment support, and regional industrial policy, statistical bodies such as Eurostat, Cedefop, and national institutes should develop EU-specific, regularly updated, output/process-based definitions rather than relying on the US-anchored O*NET occupational approach, which risks misguiding workforce policy and eroding confidence in green policies precisely when robust evidence is most needed.

**Limitations**
The empirical validation deliberately implements the (flawed) occupational approach itself, so its flat trends and null correlations illustrate rather than test alternative measures. The environmental and policy indicators used as benchmarks have their own limitations — greenwashing in firm-level targets, the composite/indirect nature of indexes such as the EPI, and structural changes unrelated to green-job creation — which the authors acknowledge.

**Future Research**
Future work should pursue output- and organisation-based frameworks that identify sectors generating green outputs and trace back all contributing workers (including supporting roles), drawing on granular firm-level and administrative datasets to map green value chains. The task-based approach retains value for mapping the tasks and skills of green new and emerging (GNE) occupations to inform targeted training and curricula.
