# From Grand Challenges to Bold Solutions: Investor Perspectives on Financing Urban Climate Change Adaptation

**Abstract**
The analysis is based on 51 semi-structured interviews as well as secondary information on UCCA policies and finance documents. The interview respondents indicate that market imperfections, including incomplete and asymmetric information, positive and negative externalities and imperfect finance markets, as well as path-dependent practices and views, hinder investment. Overall, the study contributes to an emerging body of scholarship that seeks to understand the conditions under which public and private actors may collaborate in generating enabling conditions for financing UCCA.

**Research Question**
'What are the perspectives of investors regarding the challenges and opportunities for transforming the current finance regime for urban climate change adaptation?' The paper examines how private investors perceive challenges and opportunities for financing city-level innovations in urban climate change adaptation (UCCA), and how they interact with niche innovations and the broader finance regime.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: niche innovations versus the prevailing finance "regime"; "conforming" (fitting within existing finance structures) versus "stretching" (transforming the regime) interventions, with a hybrid "adjusting" category; "soft" (behavioural — education, networks, advice) versus "hard" (structural — policies, laws, taxes) interventions.
- Regime attributes analysed: Industry Structure, Policy, Market/Finance, Knowledge, and Technology — forming the "selection environment".
- Triggering conditions / barriers: three root market imperfections — incomplete/asymmetric information, positive and negative externalities, and imperfect markets (short-termism, market power) — plus path-dependent practices, mitigation bias, and risk-averse investor beliefs.
- Process / phases: (I) constructing case timelines (2008–2022); (II) interviews and focus groups with investors; (III) mapping 42–43 interventions across regime attributes into an interventions matrix to classify their transformative potential.
- Emergent outcomes: across all three cities, "conforming" and "adjusting" interventions dominate while "stretching" interventions are rare, signalling a finance regime poorly equipped to mobilise capital for UCCA.

Drawing on sustainability transitions (ST) and strategic niche management (SNM), the paper argues that finance regimes change when niche innovations reshape the perceptions of influential actors (investors, associations, regulators) through nurturing, empowerment, narrative-building, and political negotiation. Mobilising adaptation finance depends on whether innovations conform to existing finance structures or push institutions to "stretch" — addressing market imperfections, internalising externalities, and instilling a "sense of urgency" and industry leadership. Because investors treat UCCA as a contested public good with unattractive risk/return profiles, regime "stretching" requires regulatory, fiscal, and market-rule changes that codify responsibilities and de-risk investment.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study develops a new conceptual framework (an interventions matrix) extending Smith and Raven's (2012) and Geddes and Schmidt's (2020) work to categorise "conforming," "adjusting," and "stretching" interventions for financing UCCA, foregrounding the neglected role of finance in sustainability transitions. It empirically documents investor perspectives across three capital markets and offers a scholarly critique of overly optimistic views about private-sector investment's transformative role absent structural changes to address market imperfections.

**Practical Implication**
Mobilising private finance for UCCA requires "stretching" interventions: new regulations and fiscal instruments (e.g., resilience pricing, tax exemptions, adaptation subsidies), de-risking via blended finance and public guarantees, standardised disclosure and impact-measurement tools, and stronger industry leadership from actors such as State Investment Banks. Shifting the spotlight from governments to finance bodies, the authors stress codifying investor responsibilities and building track record, trust, and co-investment to close the gap between cities' adaptation plans and available private finance.

**Limitations**
The three exemplar cases (large, low-lying coastal capital markets) and the unique configuration of conditions in each city make generalisation a challenge. The secondary data was guided by interviewees and provides a snapshot rather than a comprehensive dataset, and government-representative interview data was collected but not analysed. Interventions in the future matrix (Fig. 6) are placed by independent features without a systems analysis of their interrelationships.

**Future Research**
Future research could expand the framework to additional cities in both developed and emerging economies, allowing greater cross-case learning and refinement of the conceptual model. Further investigation is needed to determine the interrelationships among interventions more precisely, and to examine connections (excluded here) between sustainability-transitions and adaptation scholars' perspectives on transformation, as well as decentralised, community-driven, and alternative financing mechanisms.
