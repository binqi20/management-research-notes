# Biodiversity finance: What has been achieved and where is it heading

**Abstract**
capiatl markets may be resistant to financing nature. Financial markets are great innovators, evidenced by the number of nature-related instruments available, showing that intellectual capital is being applied to this issue. We propose ways in which the academic community can be instrumental in directing the necessary financial flows into nature recovery solutions.

**Research Question**
Why are financial flows into nature-based instruments well behind targets despite a clear case for protecting nature, and how can the academic and scientific community help direct the necessary financial flows into nature recovery solutions?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: nature and biodiversity are materially significant to the economy, yet capital flowing into nature-based solutions is well below required levels — the paper diagnoses why and prescribes a remedy.
- Eight obstacles to financing nature: setting priorities; aligning time scales; educational shortfall; providing the framework; measurement of biodiversity; relying on technology and innovation; political considerations; and externality mentality.
- Key constructs: double materiality (impacts on a company and a company's impacts on nature/climate); the nature financing gap; nature-negative subsidies/outflows that dwarf nature-based-solution inflows; and unpriced externalities.
- Proposed lever: the academic and scientific community accelerating market urgency by working on the intersection of nature-related risks/opportunities and financial markets, building benchmarks and data, and helping reduce nature-negative subsidies.

The paper argues that biodiversity loss is a market failure rooted in unpriced externalities and a perceived absence of frameworks, data, and benchmarks. It contends that the necessary resources, instruments, and roadmaps already largely exist, so the binding constraints are prioritization, timescale mismatch, education, measurement, and politics rather than scarcity. Academics can be instrumental by reframing nature as risk-and-opportunity for investors, bridging short-term capital horizons with nature's long-term recovery dynamics, and informing policymakers and accounting bodies to internalize externalities.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper frames under-investment in nature as a financial-markets coordination and materiality problem rather than a resource-availability problem, organizing the obstacles into eight analytically distinct mechanisms (priorities, timescales, education, frameworks, measurement, technology reliance, politics, externalities). It extends the "double materiality" concept from climate to biodiversity finance and articulates a normative agenda positioning the academic community as an instrumental actor in redirecting capital toward nature-positive outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Financial institutions should treat nature as a risk-and-opportunity issue warranting higher priority, exploit existing instruments, roadmaps, and data tools (e.g., TNFD disclosures, ENCORE, WWF Risk Filter Suite, BIOFIN catalogue) rather than waiting for a single benchmark, and design dual-purpose instruments that align returns with biodiversity's rebound. Academics can deliver near-term value by helping reduce nature-negative subsidies and clarifying externalities for policymakers.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The author calls for academics to analyze the timescale mismatch between short-term capital markets and long-term natural systems, to help shape more accurately dual-purpose nature-based financial instruments, to align new studies with financial preferences and available data sources, and to work with accounting bodies and regulators on incorporating nature and reducing nature-negative subsidies.
