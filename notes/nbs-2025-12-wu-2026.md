# Activism risk and corporate self‐regulation: Investigating how anti‐ SLAPP laws impact firms' institutional corporate social performance

**Abstract**
By analyzing how US firms respond to the enactment of laws that prevent Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation (anti-SLAPP laws), we find that in anticipation of activist attacks, firms enhance their institutional corporate social performance (institutional CSP). This proactive response is more evident in firms facing a heightened risk of activism, such as those with substantial media exposure for socially irresponsible outcomes. We suggest the looming institutional risk of activism prompts firms to engage in self-regulatory adaptation in enhancing their institutional CSP, hopefully mitigating the risk of being targeted by stakeholder activists.

**Research Question**
Whether and how do firms preemptively self-regulate—by enhancing their institutional corporate social performance—in anticipation of (rather than in reaction to) stakeholder activism, when the institutional risk of such activism rises following the enactment of anti-SLAPP laws?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Anti-SLAPP law enactment (state-level dummy = 1 once the firm's headquarters state has enacted such a law by year t)
- DV(s): Institutional CSP (MSCI/KLD additive strengths-minus-concerns score in the Community, Environment, and Human Rights categories)
- Moderators: Media coverage of corporate social irresponsibility (CSI), standardized count of RepRisk news articles
- Controls/FE: firm size, ROA, leverage, industry competition (HHI), state GDP, state liberalism, state disasters; firm, state, and year fixed effects

Anti-SLAPP laws strip firms of litigation as a tool to silence critics, lowering activists' perceived legal risk and creating an environment-level opportunity structure that raises firms' ex-ante activism risk. Anticipating heightened targeting, firms self-regulate by improving institutional CSP—performance beyond their formal boundaries that builds moral capital and normative legitimacy, making it harder for activists to frame them as morally culpable "safe bets." This preemptive response intensifies for firms whose firm-level vulnerability is amplified by media coverage of CSI, where environment- and firm-level opportunity structures interact to create layered vulnerability. Empirically, enactment raises institutional CSP by about 0.22 CSP strengths (~0.21 SD), with the effect stronger under high CSI coverage and emerging ~1 year post-enactment, persisting up to six years.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances scholarship on corporate strategies to preempt activism risk, shifting from the dominant backward-looking (reactive) view to a forward-looking one and generalizing preemptive self-regulation beyond location-sensitive sectors to institutional CSP as a broadly relevant tool. It theorizes the interdependence of multi-level activism opportunity structures, showing how environment-level (anti-SLAPP laws) and firm-level (CSI media coverage) conditions interact to shape firm adaptation. It also extends research on how institutions shape CSP from cross-national comparison toward subnational (state-level) institutional variation, heeding Ioannou and Serafeim's call to understand why institutions affect some CSR actions more than others.

**Practical Implication**
For policymakers, activist-friendly legal reforms such as anti-SLAPP laws can drive meaningful improvements in firms' institutional CSP—not by mandate, but by altering the stakeholder environment firms operate in. Safeguarding civic freedoms and protecting public dissent thus carries an additional payoff: when firms lose the ability to suppress critics through litigation, they become more likely to improve institutional CSP to avoid becoming activist targets, generating broader societal benefits.

**Limitations**
The analysis does not capture variability in how stakeholders actually receive these self-regulatory efforts, leaving stakeholder receptivity outside the model. It also cannot observe firms' nuanced internal decision-making processes, as executive interviews would be compromised by social desirability bias (executives framing CSP improvement as altruistic rather than strategic). Finally, institutional CSP is unlikely to be the only preemptive tactic firms use against would-be activism.

**Future Research**
Future studies could integrate stakeholder receptivity into models of preemptive strategy and develop indirect methods that navigate social desirability bias to recover the true strategic motivations behind CSP improvement. Researchers could also examine other preemptive tactics firms deploy against would-be activism and the effects of other activism-friendly legislation (e.g., whistleblower protection laws) on firms' stakeholder-oriented adaptation.
