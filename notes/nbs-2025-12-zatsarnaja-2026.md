# Nudging grid-friendly electric vehicle charging: Different shades of social framing and the power of individual factors

**Abstract**

These findings provide insights into factors that are related to grid-conscious charging behavior, contributing to power system stability, renewable energy integration, and a successful transition to sustainable electric mobility.

**Research Question**
How does nudging through social framing—with or without social transparency of one's own decision, and with or without a donation-based gamification opportunity—affect EV drivers' willingness to charge during grid-friendly times (RQ1, RQ2), and how do grid-friendly charging adoption factors (RQ3) and individual characteristics (RQ4) relate to that willingness?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Treatment condition (control vs. social framing without social transparency vs. social framing with social transparency vs. social framing with donation-based gamification)
- DV(s): Binary willingness to charge within the grid-friendly charging timeslot (no = 0, yes = 1)
- Adoption factors (covariates): Prior knowledge of grid-friendly charging; flexibility to integrate grid-friendly charging into routine; importance of economic benefit
- Individual characteristics (covariates): Biospheric values; sensitivity to social norms; morality toward grid-friendly charging; gender; age

Social framing was expected to recruit social-norm and reciprocity mechanisms—adding visibility of individual contributions to counter free-riding on the shared grid, and adding donation-based gamification to combine reward anticipation with prosocial generosity. Empirically, none of the three social-framing variants increased grid-friendly charging relative to control; donation-based gamification actually reduced willingness relative to the other framing conditions, consistent with a dilution effect from addressing too many motives at once and a weakened behavioral-reward link given an abstract, delayed donation. Willingness was instead driven by routine flexibility, stronger moral values, valuing economic benefit, and being female.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study shows that social framing—even when augmented with social transparency or donation-based gamification—does not reliably promote grid-friendly EV charging, contradicting prior findings that removing behavioral anonymity or adding gamification motivates pro-environmental behavior. It foregrounds intrinsic moral values and perceived routine flexibility, rather than externally imposed social-norm signals, as the psychological mechanisms underlying EV drivers' grid-conscious charging, and surfaces a potential detrimental ("dilution") effect of combining donation-based gamification with social framing.

**Practical Implication**
For charging service providers and policymakers, social framing techniques that highlight collective impact or expose individual users' actions appear ineffective, and adding donation-based gamification to such framing may backfire. Because willingness was tied to moral values and routine flexibility, providers should instead enhance temporal flexibility (e.g., convenient off-peak charging at workplaces and destinations), design more tailored and interactive gamification, deploy smart-charging/V2G infrastructure, and run educational campaigns that cultivate personal responsibility.

**Limitations**
The online experiment and survey create a potential intention-behavior gap, so stated intentions may not translate into real-world charging. Reliance on a single vignette limits ecological validity and the ability to examine contextual trade-offs, and the text-based nudges with restricted donation options were not very engaging. The Norwegian setting—with its unique energy landscape and highly developed electric mobility—limits generalizability to other contexts.

**Future Research**
Future work should validate the findings through multiple-treatment field studies and observational data, and apply multi-criteria scenarios varying charging urgency, opportunities, and incentive forms. Researchers could test more engaging and personalized gamification and non-monetary strategies (e.g., mobile apps with rewards and challenges, community feedback, real-time grid-condition messaging), investigate smart-charging and V2G technologies, and assess policy instruments such as time-of-use rates and dynamic tariffs.
