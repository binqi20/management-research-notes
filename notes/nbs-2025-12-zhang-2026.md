# Transparency and divestment: the impact of a public database about insurers’ carbon-intensive investments on their portfolio choices

**Abstract**
In 2016, the California Department of Insurance introduced a public database of carbon-intensive holdings of certain California-licensed insurers. The database lowers the cost to external stakeholders of assessing insurers’ carbon investments by removing the need to analyze complex statutory filings. Using a difference-in-differences design, I find that, after the introduction of the database, affected insurers (i.e., those included in the database) reduce their carbon-intensive holdings by approximately 14% relative to unaffected insurers. The reduction is more pronounced for insurers with harder-to-process statutory filings, greater public visibility, or operations in states with more climate-conscious stakeholders. In terms of insurers’ divestment consequences, I find that affected portfolio firms reduce bond offerings and capital expenditures following the database’s introduction. Overall, my findings suggest that enhancing transparency on institutions’ carbon-intensive investments helps redirect capital away from the carbon economy.

**Research Question**
Does enhancing transparency about insurers’ environmentally irresponsible (carbon-intensive) investments — by reducing the processing costs of identifying them through a public database — influence insurers’ portfolio choices and, in turn, the financing and investment decisions of their carbon-intensive investees?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Treat × Post (inclusion in the CRCI database after its 2016 introduction); for investee tests, Treat_Investee × Post (carbon-intensive investees previously financed by treatment insurers, post-introduction)
- DV(s): Insurer carbon-intensive investments (CarbonInvest, carbon-intensive securities scaled by total investments); investee bond offerings (likelihood and amount) and capital expenditures (CAPX)
- Mediators: None formally tested; the proposed channel is reduced information processing costs raising stakeholder awareness and pressure (regulators, customers, NGOs)
- Moderators: Portfolio complexity / statutory-filing processing costs (number of private securities, portfolio dispersion, filing size), insurer public visibility (Google Trends, advertising), and stakeholder climate-consciousness in operating states (DemoPremium, GovCarbon)

By centralizing processed summary statistics of insurers’ carbon-intensive holdings, the CRCI database reduces the awareness and acquisition costs that previously made these positions hard to infer from complex statutory filings. Lower processing costs let climate-conscious stakeholders and regulators identify environmentally irresponsible insurers and pressure them via boycotts, divestment campaigns, reputational damage, and regulatory scrutiny. To the extent this anticipated pressure imposes economic costs, treatment insurers shift away from carbon-intensive assets (a ~14% relative reduction, ~$10 million per insurer). Because bonds make up ~90% of these holdings, the resulting demand shock propagates to investees, who reduce bond issuances and capital expenditures.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the insurer-investment literature by being the first to show that enhanced transparency (specifically, reduced processing costs) of insurers’ carbon-intensive holdings can drive portfolio decarbonization, complementing work on mandatory qualitative climate disclosures and responding to Blankespoor et al.’s (2020) call for research on the broader implications of processing costs. It also contributes to the debate on the efficacy of investor exit/divestment strategies by demonstrating that bond investors’ divestitures influence targeted firms’ financing (lower bond issuances) and real investment (lower capital expenditures).

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest that policymakers and regulators seeking to redirect capital away from the carbon economy can use low-cost transparency interventions — publicizing processed data on institutions’ carbon-intensive holdings — to prompt portfolio decarbonization across the broader financial sector, not only among insurers.

**Limitations**
The documented decline in carbon-intensive investments could partly reflect contemporaneous decarbonization pressure from California insurance regulators, which is difficult to fully disentangle because the database may itself amplify such pressure by increasing regulatory awareness. Given California's leadership in promoting portfolio decarbonization, the author cannot rule out that other concurrent California regulatory efforts partially contribute to the observed divestment.
