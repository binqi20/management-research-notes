# Do Nature‐Loving CEOs Make the World Greener?

**Abstract**
This study examines the relation between CEOs’ preferences for environmental protection (“nature-loving preferences”) and corporate environmental actions. Drawing on social ecology research, I develop a proxy for CEOs’ nature-loving preferences based on their childhood exposure to greenspace and validate it using their positive discussions of nature during earnings conference calls. Findings show a significant positive association between CEOs’ nature-loving preferences and their firms’ participation in Clean Development Mechanism projects. This effect remains robust after addressing firm–CEO matching and omitted variable concerns. Cross-sectional tests reveal stronger effects when local environmental demands are high

**Research Question**
Whether—and under what conditions—CEOs’ personal nature-loving preferences (an intrinsic preference for environmental protection) translate into corporate environmental actions, after weighing those preferences against organizational performance pressures and personal career considerations.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO nature loving — the per-capita greenspace area in the CEO's hometown during childhood (before age 11), used as an exogenous, childhood-rooted proxy for an intrinsic preference for environmental protection.
- DV(s): Firm participation in Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) projects — CDM dummy (≥1 project in a year) and log(CDM number); plus estimated carbon reduction and one-year-ahead change in Trucost GHG emission intensity.
- Moderators: Local environmental demands (public environmental attention; natural-resource scarcity); CEO decision-making power (CEO–employee salary gap); ownership/affiliation (non–central-SOE, no Communist Party affiliation); supplier/customer concentration; appreciation- vs. consumption-oriented childhood nature exposure (Tree-Planting Campaign vs. Down-to-the-Countryside Movement).

Building on social ecology research—which holds that childhood appreciation-oriented interactions with nature cultivate a durable adult preference for environmental protection—the paper argues that CEOs imprint this personal preference onto firm policy. Because translating environmental values into corporate action requires reallocating managerial attention and firm resources (at the expense of short-term profitability), the effect surfaces most strongly where CEOs have discretion (greater decision-making power) and where preferences align with local stakeholder interests (high public environmental concern). The childhood-greenspace measure predates careers, mitigating endogeneity, and CEO-turnover tests rule out systematic environmental-preference-based hiring.

**Theoretical Contribution**
First, the paper extends the "manager effect" literature by isolating environmental preference as a distinct CEO trait—operationalized through a novel, childhood-greenspace measure exogenous to later-life professional influences—that is empirically separable from general prosocial tendencies and CEO risk preferences. Second, it extends the CSR literature, which has emphasized external stakeholder pressure, by showing that CEOs as key insiders incorporate personal environmental preferences into corporate decision-making, highlighting the multifaceted determinants of CSR.

**Practical Implication**
The childhood-greenspace measure offers boards and researchers a tractable tool for identifying personal value–based drivers of executives' environmental decisions. Because nature-loving CEOs are associated with greater corporate carbon reduction yet draw no significant market reaction at appointment, the findings suggest such preferences advance environmental and societal welfare without a detectable shareholder-value penalty or premium.

**Limitations**
The setting is China-specific, relying on the hukou system to infer CEOs' childhood hometowns and on hand-collected city greenspace data subject to measurement error (e.g., pre-1986 reporting gaps, addressed via winsorizing and exclusions). The actual-emissions (Trucost) test rests on a small sample (limited Chinese coverage), requiring extension of the sample window to 2006–2017 to gain statistical power.
