# Biodiversity Co-Benefits in Carbon Markets? Evidence from Voluntary Offset Projects

**Abstract**
We compile the largest extant dataset of voluntary carbon offset projects and link them to high-resolution satellite-derived measures of habitat integrity in local ecosystems. Contrary to asserted co-benefits, we find carbon offset projects are associated with a 3.7 percent increase in habitat disturbance, as measured by the Human Influence Index. Land-use analyses show that these projects tend to accelerate the conversion of biodiverse shrublands and less-dense forests into pastures and simplified landscapes, implying reduced habitat integrity and ecological trade-offs. We examine impact heterogeneity by ecosystem condition, certifications, stated co-benefits, protected area overlap, registry, and rating coverage, but still find no evidence of improved habitats. Nor do alternative habitat measures, the Bioclimate Ecosystem Resilience Index and the Biodiversity Habitat Index, show measurable improvement following offset projects. We conclude that voluntary carbon markets have ecological performance gaps and information frictions relevant to both investors and offset market governance.

**Research Question**
Do voluntary carbon offset projects that advertise biodiversity co-benefits actually improve local habitat quality, or are these claimed ecological co-benefits unrealized in practice across the voluntary carbon market (VCM)?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Carbon offset project establishment (PostEstablishment indicator equal to one for years following project initiation); interactions with project characteristics (baseline HII, biodiversity disclosure, protected-area overlap, registry, third-party rating status, pre/post-2014 IPBES timing).
- DV(s): Habitat disturbance measured by the Human Influence Index (HII); robustness with the Biodiversity Habitat Index (BHI) and Bioclimate Ecosystem Resilience Index (BERI); land-cover change (Δ area of pasture, shrubland, forest types).
- Mediators: Land-use/land-cover conversion—systematic increase in pasture (≈45.9 ha/year) offset by losses of shrubland and other forest—as the pathway linking project implementation to rising human pressure.
- Moderators: Baseline ecosystem condition, certification/disclosure status, protected-area overlap, registry affiliation, rating coverage, and implementation timing—none of which reverses the adverse direction of effect.

Using an interrupted time series design that exploits stable, flat pre-treatment HII trends, the authors interpret post-establishment deviations as causal local effects of offset projects. Rather than catalyzing natural regeneration, projects are associated with a prompt ~3.7 percent rise in HII (driven by conversion of structurally diverse shrublands and forests into simplified pasture), so the "win-win" of joint carbon-and-biodiversity gains theorized in ecology is not systematically realized in current VCM practice. The pattern echoes information-friction/"market for lemons" concerns: co-benefit claims and ratings are carbon-centric and not validated against habitat outcomes, leaving investors exposed to unpriced ecological risk.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides the first systematic, large-scale empirical evidence on the biodiversity (habitat) impacts of the voluntary carbon market, advancing debates on the effectiveness of market-based conservation, additionality, and the financialization of nature. By documenting that stated and realized ecological performance diverge—and that disclosure, certification, and third-party ratings fail to identify better-performing projects—it reframes VCM co-benefit claims as an information-frictions and governance problem in the market for biodiversity-linked environmental assets.

**Practical Implication**
Findings call for stronger biodiversity safeguards within VCM standards, more transparent ecological (habitat) reporting, and third-party verification of co-benefit claims. Investors should demand quantitative disclosure of habitat outcomes alongside carbon metrics, and policymakers can leverage improving, strategically-unbiased satellite-derived measures within MRV systems and frameworks such as the CBD and the Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures.

**Limitations**
Results reflect outcomes within developer-disclosed project boundaries (not surrounding spillovers) and are restricted to nature-based projects from 2000–2020 with adequate geospatial documentation, which skew newer, larger, and toward Australia—limiting external validity to the full VCM universe. HII proxies human pressure rather than species directly and can produce false negatives, and boundary imprecision plus partly subjective verification may introduce measurement error.

**Future Research**
Longer time series, ground-based verification, and more comprehensive project coverage are needed to validate and extend the findings, since some ecological benefits (e.g., species recolonization, successional recovery) may unfold over decades. The authors also flag general-equilibrium structural models to address a broader set of VCM counterfactuals, including how projects in one area affect project siting elsewhere.
