# Toxic neighbors: E-waste dumps and the decline of social capital

**Abstract**
We study how living near electronic waste dumping sites shapes the social fabric of communities in Ghana and Nigeria.

**Research Question**
How does living near electronic waste dumping sites affect the multidimensional social capital of nearby communities, and through what mechanisms does this environmental exposure erode community cohesion?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Proximity to an e-waste dumping site, operationalized as the interaction Post × distance (km) — a continuous DID treatment where smaller distances after site establishment indicate greater exposure.
- DV(s): Composite social capital index and its four dimensions — trust in government, community participation, social insurance, and intergroup tolerance.
- Mediators: Deteriorating local living conditions (proxied by perceived improvement in basic health services) and perceived government corruption.
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

The paper argues that e-waste exposure corrodes social capital through two complementary, layered channels. First, environmental contamination and health risks degrade material living conditions, depressing subjective well-being, encouraging social withdrawal, and weakening cooperative relationships. Second, persistent hazards under weak enforcement are read as evidence of governance failure and official–polluter collusion, raising perceived corruption, damaging institutional trust, and discouraging civic engagement. Both mediators are positively associated with social capital, and entering each into the outcome equation attenuates (but does not eliminate) the proximity coefficient, consistent with partial mediation through material well-being and institutional trust respectively.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the e-waste literature beyond its well-documented environmental and health dimensions by empirically identifying its adverse effects on social capital, and it illuminates the mechanisms — deteriorating living conditions and weakened institutional trust — through which environmental degradation jointly erodes social cohesion. It connects e-waste exposure to the circular-economy policy agenda, bridging environmental economics, political science, and development studies to inform socially grounded environmental governance.

**Practical Implication**
Appraisal, permitting, and monitoring of waste infrastructure should internalize social costs by tracking social cohesion, trust, and civic participation alongside emissions and health indicators. Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR), with legally binding collection and recovery targets, earmarked revenues for clean recycling and community programs, and integration of informal workers, is a central lever to repair the social fabric while reducing the physical externalities of waste.

**Limitations**
Afrobarometer randomly displaces field coordinates for confidentiality, which likely attenuates the distance gradient toward zero, so estimated effects may be conservative. The mediation analysis is a simple mediation-style strategy rather than a formal causal mediation design, and the "Member/attends local development association" item was not fielded in Rounds 8-9, creating missingness in the social insurance dimension.
