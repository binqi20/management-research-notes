# From Stigma to Support: “Black-Owned” Labels and Expertise Stereotypes in Cannabis and Psychedelics Markets

**Abstract**
We examine the effect of “Black-owned” labeling on cannabis and psychedelic brands, in context of the stigmatized and risky nature of the drugs category. Building on prior studies examining social justice or discrimination, we introduce an expertise stereotype framework.

**Research Question**
What are the drivers of consumer responses to Black business owners and marketers in the legalized drugs category, and can Black entrepreneurs realize the intended benefits of equity-focused policies by signaling their racial identity through "Black-owned" labeling? The paper asks whether such labeling helps or hurts, and how responses differ between in-group (Black) and out-group (White) consumers.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Presence vs. absence of a "Black-owned" (and, in Study 4, "Woman-owned") ownership label
- DV(s): Consumption/purchase intentions, willingness to pay, and ad click-through rate (CTR); perceived expertise-stereotype scores (Study 2)
- Mediators: Perceived product safety (fully mediates label × product × race effect on consumption intentions in Study 3); social justice perceptions tested but do not explain category/in-group differences
- Moderators: Consumers' racial identity (in-group vs. out-group) and product category (stigmatized/high-risk drugs vs. low-risk non-drugs)

A "Black-owned" label spontaneously activates domain-specific expertise stereotypes about the owner's social group. Because drugs are a high-consumption-risk category, out-group (White) consumers apply favorable expertise stereotypes—via quality inferences—and increase intentions for "Black-owned" drug products. In-group (Black) consumers, however, do not extend support to the stigmatized drugs category—plausibly due to stereotype threat, distancing from stigmatizing associations, and self-enhancement goals—even though they hold the same positive cannabis expertise stereotype; their in-group support appears only for low-risk, low-stigma products like candy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces an expertise-stereotype mechanism to the ownership-labeling literature, presenting a phenomenon in which stereotypes can yield a positive response among out-group members—diverging from previously documented in-group support, social justice, and racial discrimination mechanisms. It documents (a) race-related product expertise stereotypes (previously unestablished, alongside gender and country-of-origin) and (b) the bounds of expertise-stereotype application in consumption decisions, showing product category and stigma as critical moderators.

**Practical Implication**
In contexts like legalized drugs with a predominantly White (out-group) audience, "Black-owned" labeling can benefit Black entrepreneurs by leveraging favorable expertise stereotypes. In low-risk categories such as candy, "Black-owned" labeling is beneficial mainly when Black customers are the key target segment. Notably, Black professionals' own expectations (anticipating mostly in-group support) are inconsistent with realized consumer responses in the drugs category.

**Limitations**
The studies do not involve actual purchase or consumption of cannabis or psychedelics; ad click-through is used as a behavioral proxy, which is many steps removed from purchase and post-consumption responses. Participants' drug-use history and domain knowledge were not measured due to participant-risk considerations. Field tests (Study 4) rely on aggregate, individual-unidentifiable data and cannot confirm random assignment given platform ad-delivery optimization. The work emphasizes the positive aspects of expertise stereotypes without fully examining their potential negative consequences.

**Future Research**
Future studies could ethically collect consumption and post-consumption data, examine consumer expertise/knowledge as a moderator of reliance on stereotype cues, and explore whether "Black-owned" versus broader "Minority-owned" labels are preferable. Researchers should investigate how shifting societal attitudes toward drugs may change the documented mechanisms over time, extend to other social identities and labeling types (Table 4), and critically examine how expertise stereotypes in stigmatized domains can have adverse effects.
