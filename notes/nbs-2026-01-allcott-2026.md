# An Economic View of Corporate Social Impact

**Abstract**
Growing discussions of impact investing and stakeholder capitalism have increased interest in measuring companies’ social impact. We conceptualize corporate social impact as the welfare loss that would be caused by a firm’s exit. To illustrate, we quantify the social impacts of 74 firms in 12 industries using a new survey measuring consumer and worker substitution patterns combined with models of product and labor markets. We find that consumer surplus is the primary component of social impact, suggesting that consumer impacts deserve more attention from impact investors. Existing environmental, social, and governance (ESG) and social impact ratings are essentially unrelated to our economically grounded measures.

**Research Question**
How can a company's social impact be defined and measured in a theoretically grounded, economically consistent way — and how do such measures compare with existing ESG and social-impact ratings? The paper answers by conceptualizing a firm's social impact as the social welfare loss that its exit would cause.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core construct: corporate social impact defined as the welfare loss from a firm's exit (individual impact) and the firm's Shapley-value share of the welfare loss from the whole industry's exit (share of industry impact).
- Components of total surplus: consumer surplus, worker surplus, firm profits, and externalities/internalities (e.g., CO2, smoking harms), aggregated as a Pareto-weighted sum of utility with social marginal welfare weights inversely proportional to income.
- Key drivers (IV-like): consumers' and workers' willingness to substitute away (own-price and labor-supply elasticities), consumer/worker income levels, and the magnitude of negative externalities/internalities.
- Outcome (DV): dollar-valued social welfare loss from exit, by firm and by industry.
- Identification: a 3,500-person stated-preference survey supplies firm-level price-response and labor-supply-response shares, feeding a random-coefficient logit demand system, Nash-Bertrand markup recovery, and integration under residual labor supply.

A firm has larger social impact when its consumers and workers are less willing to substitute away, when it serves lower-income consumers or employs lower-ability workers (who carry higher welfare weights), and when its products generate fewer negative externalities and internalities. Profits contribute little because most are business-stealing and accrue to high-income owners with low welfare weights. Empirically, consumer surplus dominates; the largest positive impact is Walmart's grocery business (~$151B/year) and the most negative is Philip Morris (~-$17B/year).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides an economically grounded, welfarist definition of corporate social impact — the causal welfare effect of a firm's exit — and shows that the standard toolkit of welfare economics (consumer/worker surplus, externalities, Shapley-value apportionment) can be assembled into an internally consistent, dollar-denominated measure. It reframes "impact" as a partial-equilibrium counterfactual and demonstrates that consumer surplus, not profit, is the primary component.

**Practical Implication**
Impact investors and rating agencies should give more attention to consumer surplus, especially for low-income consumers, rather than focusing narrowly on profits or conventional ESG dimensions. Because prominent ESG and social-impact ratings are essentially uncorrelated with the economically grounded measure, the framework offers firms, investors, workers, and consumers a transparent way to quantify and compare social impact.

**Limitations**
The welfarist moral philosophy and the conceptualization of impact as the effect of exit may omit characteristics stakeholders value (e.g., diversity and inclusion, governance, lobbying). The empirical implementation relies on restrictive static partial-equilibrium assumptions (ignoring supplier surplus, innovation spillovers, and competitor entry), uses hypothetical stated-preference surveys to identify elasticities, and requires strong functional-form and contested externality/internality assumptions; estimates are a proof of concept, not final measures.

**Future Research**
Dynamic product-entry models could capture the time path of exit effects that the static framework approximates only roughly. The approach could be extended to many more markets and used to address limitations in existing corporate social impact and ESG measurement systems, and to refine the (normative) distributional weights and externality magnitudes used in the quantification.
