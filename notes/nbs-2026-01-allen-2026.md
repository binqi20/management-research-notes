# Thinking-Being With a Garden: Developing Ecocentric Ethics for Sustainable Organising

**Abstract**
In this paper, we turn to gardens to offer a new ethics of being with the world that can inform sustainable organising. For this, we develop an ecocentric ethics perspective informed by the writing of Karen Barad, in particular her notion of ‘agential cuts’. By reviewing gardening literatures, and exploring our embodied gardening practices, we attempt to ‘step out’ of institutionalised ways of thinking and working to reach for different embodied and relational ways of being with earth-others. We contribute to debates by: (a) tracing multispecies mutualities of ‘cutting-together apart’ within the gardens we tend, to show how such cutting can develop understandings of an entangled ethics in which human involvement can at times be peripheral; and, (b) showing how garden(ing)s can help enrich understandings of ‘ethico-onto-epistemologies’ by situating embodied practices of organising within planetary processes and rhythms which are variously consequential for sustaining forms of life. These contributions stemming from garden(ing)s can help to inform ecocentric thinking-being in business organisations by developing possibilities for (re)conceptualising: how businesses practices, and connected imaginaries, are entangled in creating conditions that can diminish and enhance earth-others existences; and, how the fluidities and temporalities of specifically located webs of life in which businesses activities are part necessitate sensitivities to working with unknowing.

**Research Question**
In the context of considering sustainable ways of organising businesses, the paper pursues the question: "How do we develop the contours of an ecocentric ethics that is rooted in living socio-material entanglements?" The aim is to move beyond human-centred, anthropocentric framings of nature toward an embodied, relational ethics of "being with" earth-others.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: agential cut / "cutting-together apart"; "ethico-onto-epistemology"; "naturecultures"; "earth-others"; habitat vs. boundary; "ecology-in-place"; knowing vs. unknowing.
- Process / phases: mapping the cross-disciplinary garden(ing) literature → introducing Barad's ethico-onto-epistemology and the agential cut → weaving two autoethnographic vignettes (Sheffield allotment; Berlin cherry tree) → moving from perceiving individual species toward perceiving living assemblages and habitats.
- Practices: organic/allotment gardening, zoning, digging a pond, pruning, wildlife-camera observation — read as material-discursive boundary-making practices.
- Emergent outcomes: recognition that humans can be peripheral or decentred participants in webs of life; a shift from intimate "knowing" relations to an ethics of "unknowing"; sensitivity to planetary rhythms and temporalities.

Through a Baradian lens, the authors argue that gardens come into being as phenomena through intra-active "agential cuts" that materially and discursively produce distinctions (e.g., garden vs. wild, plant vs. soil, present vs. absent species). By tracing how these cuts both separate and connect humans and earth-others across seasonal and planetary rhythms, they develop an ecocentric ethics in which responsibility is reframed as "response-ability," human involvement is sometimes peripheral, and "unknowing" becomes a valued epistemic dimension of sustainable organising.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends relational posthuman and ecocentric ethics in business ethics / MOS by grounding them in concrete, located naturecultures (gardens) rather than abstract or single-species framings, thereby answering critiques that ecocentric theorising stays too general and must be rooted in an "ecology-in-place." It develops Barad's "ethico-onto-epistemology" and the agential cut to show how cutting-together-apart produces species and habitats as co-emerging, and contributes the insight that humans are not always active participants in the entanglements that sustain earth-others — decentring the human further than prior ethics-of-care accounts.

**Practical Implication**
For sustainable business practice, the authors sketch propositions translating ecocentric ethics into reflexive questions managers might ask — e.g., "How do we understand that our business activities are participating in ecosystems?", "Who else's thriving do we make possible?", and how organizing practices attend to planetary rhythms — positioning an "ecology-related unknowingness" as a source of livelihood (re)creation rather than something to be eliminated through control-oriented data and compliance (e.g., EU CSRDDD/non-financial reporting).

**Limitations**
The authors note they have "not (yet) outlined possible consequences for business ethics," offering only sketched propositions in their closing remarks. The account is built from the authors' own two allotment gardens (Sheffield and Berlin) and their individual experiences of particular naturecultures, so its claims are situated and experiential rather than generalisable, and the move from intimate gardening relations to organisational/business contexts is acknowledged as highly challenging.

**Future Research**
The paper invites further engagement with the "rich socio-materialities and embodied multispecies practices that gardens offer" and with developing an ecocentric *business* ethics that takes planetary boundaries and Anthropocene volatilities seriously. It proposes pursuing reflexive questions about how organising practices cut earth-others' webs of life together and apart, and about how a practice of "unknowing" and embodied listening can reshape sustainable organising.
