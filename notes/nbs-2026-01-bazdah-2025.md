# Making Sense of Conflicts During Management Consulting Missions: A Values-in-Practice Approach

**Abstract**
This study investigates to what extent and how consultant–client relationships generate conflicts, in particular conflicts of values. Based on 50 case studies of management consulting missions, our research identifies four client–consultant relationship dynamics, of which three are marked by relational, methodological, or values conflicts. Our results: (1) highlight the situated and dynamic nature of values conflicts, which are shown to be associated with the consultant’s or client’s relational practices, as they violate norms of respectful business interactions; (2) demonstrate how values—referring to what practices are and are not desirable from the consultant’s and client’s points of view—are called upon to make sense of situations that are experienced as highly stressful or emotional and that disrupt expectations of a business relationship; and (3) complement Jehn’s (1992) definition of relational conflict by specifying that it can be associated with various conceptions of business relationships. From these results, we suggest two sets of practical recommendations to limit the risk of conflicts and improve both clients’ and consultants’ well-being during consulting projects.

**Research Question**
To what extent do the practices of the consultant and the client generate conflicts and conflicts of values during management consulting missions? How do these conflicts emerge, and what are their consequences for the consultant and/or the client?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: relational practices vs. methodological practices; relational, methodological (process), and values conflicts; "expected values" (trust, benevolence, recognition, respect) vs. uncooperative relational practices; positive vs. negative emotionality.
- Process / phases: four temporal dynamics of the consulting relationship — (1) fluid relational and methodological agreement; (2) methodological (process) disagreement; (3) relational disagreement; (4) methodological disagreement that evolves over time into a values conflict.
- Triggering conditions: a methodological disagreement expressed through relational practices that violate respected norms (e.g., verbal violence during meetings) is reframed by actors as a values conflict.
- Emergent outcomes: low negative emotionality (frustration, distrust) for relational/methodological disagreements; high negative emotionality (stress, anger, exhaustion) for values conflicts, which can lead an actor to quit the mission.

Drawing on a values-in-practice approach (Aadland, 2010) and Jehn's conflict typology, the authors show that values conflicts are not confrontations between two stable value systems but are temporally and dynamically situated in the actors' interactions, always preceded by a methodological disagreement. Through a Weickian sensemaking lens, actors invoke "values"—rather than contextual or psychological explanations—to make sense of practices they perceive as inappropriate and morally accountable, anchoring abstract values in concrete relational practices retrospectively described during interviews.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three theoretical contributions: it shows that values conflicts are situated and dynamic, arising from relational practices that violate norms of respectful business interaction rather than from clashing value systems; it demonstrates that "values" function as discursive resources participants call upon to make sense of stressful, expectation-disrupting situations; and it complements Jehn's (1992) definition of relational conflict by specifying its source—differing conceptions of and expectations regarding the business relationship.

**Practical Implication**
Consultants should clarify and discuss their intervention mode and methodological practices with clients at the outset, and both parties should articulate their relational expectations and a relational framework to limit deterioration. Because such frameworks are not sufficient, the authors also recommend involving middle management to give consultants and clients space to discuss relational difficulties and to support consultants in managing the emotional work of missions.

**Limitations**
The COVID-19 pandemic prevented the planned observations and data triangulation, so the study relies on interview accounts alone. The research did not capture the consultant's and the client's views of the same mission (no matched dyads), and the authors caution that the effectiveness of their managerial recommendations remains uncertain given the intensity and evolution of disagreements.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine the emotions and emotional work conflicts generate (e.g., non-verbal behavior; Hirschman's exit-voice-loyalty for post-conflict coping), study the consultant–client dyad on the same mission to surface asymmetric conflict perceptions, and extend the analysis to other service relationships such as care settings. Developing a measurement scale for conflicts and their emotions is also proposed as an avenue for quantitative studies.
