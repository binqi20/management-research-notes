# Perceived Air Pollution and Innovative Work Behavior: An Empirical Examination Based on Conservation of Resources Theory

**Abstract**
Innovative work behavior is crucial for organizational sustenance and growth, and scholars have uncovered many determinants of the same. However, the majority of predictors are either in the work or organizational domain. It is not known whether environmental factors like perceived air pollution can influence innovative work behavior, and if yes, why and when. Our study aims to address this gap by leveraging the conservation of resources theory, which posits that individuals experiencing stressors try to protect their limited resources by withdrawing from work outcomes. The authors utilized a multi-wave multi-source research design and collected data from 506 supervisor–subordinate dyads working in research and development units of 17 companies in India. We found that emotional exhaustion mediated the negative relationship between perceived air pollution and innovative work behavior, and that this relationship was buffered (accentuated) at high (low) levels of supervisor support. Theoretical and practical implications are discussed.

**Research Question**
Can an environmental factor like perceived air pollution influence innovative work behavior (IWB), and if so, why (through what mechanism) and when (under what boundary conditions)? The study examines whether emotional exhaustion mediates the perceived air pollution–IWB link and whether supervisor support moderates it.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived (subjective) air pollution
- DV(s): Innovative work behavior (IWB), supervisor-rated
- Mediators: Emotional exhaustion (indicator of resource depletion)
- Moderators: Supervisor support (on the air pollution → emotional exhaustion path, and the overall indirect path)
- Controls: Objective air pollution (AQI), subordinate age/gender/education, supervisor age/gender

Drawing on COR theory, the authors argue that perceived air pollution depletes or threatens multiple valued resources (personal health, money, health of loved ones, time, energy, sense of control), precipitating emotional exhaustion; emotionally exhausted employees then withdraw from resource-intensive activities like IWB to conserve remaining resources, a logic reinforced by the goal disengagement perspective. Supervisor support, itself a COR resource, buffers this process: at high supervisor support the positive air pollution–exhaustion link is attenuated (and the negative indirect effect on IWB turns nonsignificant), whereas at low support it is amplified. All five hypotheses were supported.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces a novel resource-depletion mechanism (emotional exhaustion) linking perceived air pollution to a positive employee performance outcome (IWB), extending a literature that had focused on destructive outcomes such as CWBs and abusive supervision. It identifies a flexible, controllable boundary condition—supervisor support—in contrast to prior static or uncontrollable moderators (trait self-control, cloudiness), and positions perceived air pollution as a novel, organization-external antecedent of IWB, expanding the construct's nomological network.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should target emotional exhaustion (e.g., positive work reflection, CBT, meditation, humor) and empower supervisors to grant work-from-home arrangements, actively listen, and adjust workloads for employees affected by air pollution. Training should be directed at units with low supervisor support. For policy, governments are urged to treat perceived air pollution as a psychosocial risk factor under occupational health and safety frameworks and to sponsor supervisory training.

**Limitations**
The multi-wave multi-source survey design precludes causal inference, and data were restricted to R&D departments in Delhi NCR, India, limiting generalizability. Only supervisor support was examined as a moderator (omitting coworker/family support), IWB relied on potentially biased supervisory ratings, and the chronically polluted setting raises the possibility of desensitization effects among respondents.

**Future Research**
The authors call for longitudinal designs to strengthen internal validity, replication across polluted and relatively unpolluted cities worldwide to address desensitization, and examination of additional moderators (coworker/family support, wellness programs) and individual differences (e.g., health anxiety). They also suggest exploring whether perceived air pollution triggers green behaviors or turnover intentions, and using more objective IWB measures such as patent disclosures.
