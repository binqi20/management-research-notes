# Green Pressure, Lean Measures: Unveiling Corporate Downsizing Within the European Union Emissions Trading System

**Abstract**
In 2017, the European Union Emissions Trading System underwent a policy intervention that resulted in a surge in carbon prices. Using this setting as a quasi-natural experiment, we focus on employment, productivity, and emission outcomes among covered enterprises. Results show that emission-intensive private firms, particularly those with financial constraints, are more likely to downsize by divesting production assets, reducing both workforce and emissions. Smaller, cash-strapped listed firms are also prone to downsize by decreasing their operating leverage while maintaining emission output and asset levels. Positive productivity outcomes indicate that both private and listed firms become leaner postintervention.

**Research Question**
How do firms covered by the EU ETS respond, in terms of employment, productivity, and emission outcomes, to the increase in carbon costs following the European Council's Feb. 2017 intervention, and which firms internalize these costs through corporate downsizing and downscaling?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): EU ETS coverage interacted with the post-2017 period (EU_ETS × POST); decile-ranked industry-corrected emission intensity interacted with the post period (RANK × POST)
- DV(s): Corporate downsizing (>=5% workforce reduction in t+1); change in emissions (ΔEMISSIONS); change in total factor productivity (ΔTFP)
- Mediators: Production downscaling via fixed-asset divestment (the channel through which private-firm downsizing lowers emissions)
- Moderators: Financial constraints (low cash), firm size, ownership concentration, carbon-leakage-list status, and local employment conditions

The intervention sharply raised carbon allowance prices, making EU ETS costs material for the first time. For cash-constrained private firms, higher emission intensity raised the probability of downsizing; this response operated through divesting production assets (downscaling), which simultaneously reduced personnel and emissions (~7.8% emission drop) while raising productivity. Smaller cash-constrained listed firms also downsized, but to reduce operating leverage rather than via divestment, leaving emission output and asset levels unchanged while still improving productivity. Larger listed firms appear to absorb costs through alternative responses (R&D, cost pass-through, hedging, relocation, lobbying).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study adds climate-policy interventions and associated environmental costs as a new antecedent of corporate downsizing, and contributes to the finance literature on the drivers and outcomes of corporate environmental behavior by showing that firms weigh carbon costs against employment costs. It advances understanding of heterogeneous firm responses to the EU ETS by distinguishing private from listed firms and by formally documenting labor-market consequences across 27 countries, answering prior calls for causal evidence on the EU ETS and labor outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers intervening in emissions trading schemes should anticipate that sudden carbon-price increases impose transition costs concentrated in cash-constrained private firms and smaller cash-constrained listed firms, which downsize as emission intensity rises. Targeted instruments such as green loan investment programs, subsidies, or guarantees, and reallocating auction/innovation-fund revenue toward worker retraining, could sustain employment and dampen divestment incentives while still enabling emission reductions and productivity gains, especially for non-leakage sectors that receive fewer free allowances.

**Limitations**
The sample ends in 2020 at a carbon price of €32.57, before prices peaked near €100 in 2023, so the documented employment effects are modest (~3.5% workforce reduction for the most-polluting private firms relative to the least-polluting) and likely understate later transition costs. The matching between high- and low-emission-intensity firms remains imperfect due to limited sample size, and evidence on listed-firm coping mechanisms (R&D, relocation, pass-through) is largely anecdotal from annual reports rather than formally tested.

**Future Research**
Future work could map the skill profiles of jobs lost versus green jobs created through innovation funds to assess distributional consequences, examine voluntary turnover and whether a "cost of environmental distress" drives talent flight between strongly and weakly environmentally performing EU ETS firms, and study the persistence and longer-run magnitude of these job losses as carbon prices continued to surge after the sample period.
