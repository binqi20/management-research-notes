# Digital Resilience for the Climate Crisis: A Multi-Perspective Analysis

**Abstract**
This commentary explores multiple perspectives on the potential use of digital technologies to improve organizational resilience in the context of climate change. Such an approach is needed to address this complex problem space, especially since it encompasses a wide variety of phenomena, including floods and landslides, disruptions to global supply chains, heat waves, biodiversity loss, greenhouse gas emissions, and food insecurity. We assembled a diverse set of five scholarly teams specializing in multiple problem topics, research approaches, and theoretical perspectives on this project. Each team identified and problematized a specific facet of digital resilience for the climate crisis. The perspectives cover a range of rich narratives, including digital resilience in the context of floods and landslides in Brazil and Indonesia, conceptual development efforts incorporating the natural environment with people and technology, reconceptualization of the problem space in terms of time and type, and two applications of digital resilience in the domains of global supply chains and carbon emissions tracking. This research commentary thus presents a multiperspective examination and interrogation of digital resilience for addressing the climate crisis, out of which four transcending themes emerge: the need to integrate nature into sociotechnical thinking, the need to examine actions at both micro and macro levels, the need to include both reactive and proactive strategies, and the need to view climate crisis as a process rather than a series of events. This commentary aims to motivate other scholars who take diverse theoretical perspectives to join us in developing fundamental knowledge and practical solutions needed to achieve digital resilience for the climate crisis.

**Research Question**
How can the information systems field identify effective approaches to achieving digital resilience for the climate crisis (DRCC) — that is, how can digital technologies be used to absorb, adapt to, transform after, and proactively mitigate the root causes of climate-induced shocks across multiple entities, scales, and time horizons?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Digital resilience for the climate crisis (DRCC) must move beyond a reactive, "crisis-as-event" view of digital technologies absorbing discrete shocks toward an expanded, proactive conceptualization that integrates nature, spans micro and macro scales, combines adaptation with mitigation, and treats the climate crisis as an ongoing process.
- Key constructs: eco-sociotechnical ecosystems composed of social, technical, and natural "holons" interacting across hierarchical scales; cycle time and scale of holons; engineering resilience (control to increase scale difference) versus ecological resilience (adapt while retaining function); an "ecosystem-wide information system" (EWIS).
- Process / framework: a two-dimensional framework classifying DRCC actions by (1) adaptation vs. mitigation and (2) reactive vs. proactive orientation, illustrated through digital circular-economy supply chains and consumer carbon-incentive (CCI) programs with cross-scale "reform" and "remember" processes.
- Boundary conditions / tensions: tensions between centralized data analytics and local/indigenous knowledge; trade-offs across scales (Global North mitigation harming Global South); rebound effects and resource intensity of the digital technologies themselves.

Across five contributions, the commentary develops the process by which digital technologies can build resilience: natural, social, and technical holons operating at disparate spatial and temporal scales interact within loosely coupled ecosystems, and digital technologies that fit the scale and cycle time of the holons involved can enable either engineering resilience (mitigation that separates climate and human activity scales) or ecological resilience (adaptation that aligns ecosystem and climate scales). Because micro-level resilience actions do not automatically aggregate into macro-level sustainability, the central argument is that DRCC requires aligning micro actions with systemic macro objectives, integrating nature into sociotechnical theory, and combining reactive adaptation with proactive mitigation of root causes under a crisis-as-process perspective.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The commentary expands the conceptualization of digital resilience by integrating nature into sociotechnical thinking through an eco-sociotechnical perspective built on holons, scales, and an adapted information ecology theory, and by distinguishing engineering from ecological resilience as scale-management strategies. It contributes a framework that organizes DRCC actions along adaptation/mitigation and reactive/proactive dimensions, and it reframes the climate crisis from a series of exogenous events to an ongoing anthropogenic process, surfacing four transcending themes (integrate nature, examine micro and macro actions, include reactive and proactive strategies, and view crisis as process) intended to seed a cumulative IS research agenda.

**Practical Implication**
For those designing, implementing, and regulating climate-change solutions, the reconceptualization of ecosystems highlights the need to assess each mitigation and adaptation solution at multiple scales and to match technologies to the type of resilience being pursued (strong programming functions for engineering resilience; robust communication functions for ecological resilience). The framework points to concrete applications such as digital twins and federated open-data spaces for circular supply chains and consumer carbon-incentive programs that translate micro-level low-carbon choices into macro-level mitigation.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge that the metasynthesis embraces breadth over depth and does not claim to be exhaustive, declining to engage deeply with any single theoretical tradition; important vantage points such as critical social science and historical perspectives remain underexplored. The Brazil and Indonesia cases focus primarily on adaptation rather than the root causes of the climate crisis, and the commentary notes that digital technologies themselves are resource-intensive and can reinforce prevailing ideologies or exacerbate inequalities.

**Future Research**
The authors call for new methodologies and metrics that quantify how micro-level actions aggregate into macro-level sustainability outcomes, longitudinal studies that capture the crisis-as-process and long-term temporal dynamics, and integrated assessment models capturing interdependencies among human, technological, and environmental systems. They invite IS scholars to develop and validate integrative theories that incorporate natural ecosystems into sociotechnical studies, to investigate digital circular-economy and secondary-market governance, and to examine the second-order and cross-level effects of consumer carbon-incentive programs.
