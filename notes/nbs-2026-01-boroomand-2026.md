# Business and management research on societal wellbeing: a historic, multi-journal and large language model analysis of the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals

**Abstract**
The United Nations’ 17 Sustainable Development Goals provide a framework for categorizing research that addresses global challenges and advances societal wellbeing. Using multiple large language models, this study analyzed 47,197 articles published in 18 business and management journals, including the Journal of International Business Studies, representing five business and management disciplines over 55 years (1970–2024). The objective was to assess the extent to which scholars, including those in IB, are tackling issues related to grand challenges as captured by the United Nation’s 17 Sustainable Development Goals. Findings show that among the 47,197 articles, 17.4% relate to one or more SDGs. This percentage increased over time, from 8% before the introduction of UN Agenda 21 in 1992 to 31% after the launch of UN Agenda 2030 in 2015. SDGs 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth), 9 (Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure), and 16 (Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions) accounted for 78% of the SDG-aligned articles. The JIBS is among the leading journals contributing to these SDGs. We offer suggestions on how IB research can contribute to societal wellbeing by paying more attention to complementarities among goals and exploration of trade-offs where progress on one goal may impede advancement on another.

**Research Question**
To what extent has the increasing awareness of the need to refocus business and management research on society's "grand challenges" actually changed what is published in leading journals, and specifically how does the Journal of International Business Studies compare to other premier journals in publishing SDG-related research over 1970–2024?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: The 17 UN SDGs serve as an "analytical anchor" — a comprehensive, well-established yardstick — for categorizing five-plus decades of business and management scholarship by its relevance to societal wellbeing.
- Key constructs: the "SDG ratio" (share of SDG-related articles in a year, decade, or journal); SDGs 8, 9, 16, and 17 framed as cross-cutting "enabler" goals; complementarities versus trade-offs across goals.
- Method as mechanism: an ensemble of four AI models with a majority-voting rule converts ~50,000 abstracts into reliable SDG classifications that no human coding effort could feasibly produce (802,349 article-by-SDG assessments).
- Boundary conditions: a deliberately conservative 7/10 threshold and 3-of-4 consensus rule set a high bar, potentially underestimating coverage; several environmental and social SDGs fall outside the natural domain of business research.

The paper documents an accelerating but uneven engagement: the SDG ratio more than tripled from ~8% (pre-1992) to ~31% (2015–2024), tracking the timeline of UN sustainability agendas (Development Decades, Agenda 21, MDGs, Agenda 2030), yet remains heavily concentrated in economically oriented goals (SDGs 8, 9, 16 = 78% of aligned articles). JIBS ranks among the leaders in total SDG coverage (~24% of its articles) and in treating SDGs 8 and 9 as enablers of other goals.

**Theoretical Contribution**
By longitudinally mapping the evolution of SDG-related research across 18 flagship journals, the essay contributes to ongoing discussions about the purpose of business and management scholarship and its role in advancing societal wellbeing by addressing systemic grand challenges. It positions the flagship journals as legitimating venues able to push wellbeing topics from the "periphery" to the "mainstream," and corroborates prior findings (Walsh et al., 2003; Tsui & Jia, 2013, 2024) that welfare-oriented research has hovered around 20% historically with a modest recent increase.

**Practical Implication**
IB research, given its intrinsically cross-disciplinary and international nature and its focus on internationally operating firms, is argued to be especially well-positioned to lead cross-goal inquiry. The authors urge scholars and editors to broaden attention to under-studied SDGs (inequality, health and wellbeing, responsible consumption/production), to model inter-goal complementarities and trade-offs, and to pursue geographically nuanced, interdisciplinary research designs that translate into education, managerial practice, and policy.

**Limitations**
SDG research in the 18 journals is sharply skewed toward SDGs 8, 9, and 16 (over 78% of aligned articles), with environmental, social-inclusion, and gender goals receiving little attention. Examination of the enabling dynamics among SDGs 8, 9, 16, and 17 is very limited (only 646 unique articles, ~1.4%, show co-occurrence). The conservative 7/10 cutoff and broad goal definitions (e.g., SDG 8) may both over- and under-state genuine engagement with stakeholder wellbeing beyond firm performance, and the analysis is restricted to a US-biased set of top journals with JIBS as the sole IB outlet.

**Future Research**
Future work should explore the under-studied SDGs and theorize their relevance for international firms; map both synergies and trade-offs among goals (e.g., desalination's water–marine tension, cap-and-trade's climate–toxics tension); conduct interdisciplinary, cross-goal "cascade" studies of how economic growth, innovation, institutional quality, and partnerships spill into other SDGs; and pursue comparative work across developed and emerging markets, North and South, and subnational regions, including a finer-grained analysis of a wider set of IB journals.
