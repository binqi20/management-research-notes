# Sustainability-Oriented Institutions and the Success of Green Reward-Based Crowdfunding Campaigns

**Abstract**
Previous research has produced mixed evidence on the success of green crowdfunding campaigns. We investigate whether country-level environmental institutions help explain these inconsistencies by moderating backers’ responses. While such institutions may enhance campaign legitimacy, they can also reduce the perceived urgency to contribute by offering alternative support channels. Analyzing a large Kickstarter dataset with machine learning, we find that green campaigns are less likely to succeed, particularly in countries with stronger environmental institutions. Post-hoc analyses point to a crowding-out effect of institutional support, which dampens individual backing, and suggest that omitted variable bias may partly account for prior conflicting results.

**Research Question**
How does the environmental orientation of country-level institutions moderate the relationship between a crowdfunding campaign's greenness and its success, and can this institutional heterogeneity explain the mixed findings in prior research on green reward-based crowdfunding?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Green (dummy for campaigns classified as green via a Random Forest classifier)
- DV(s): Success (1 if the campaign met/exceeded its funding goal), with amount_collected and number of backers as alternative outcomes
- Moderators: Environmental Performance Index (EPI) as a proxy for country-level institutional sustainability orientation; green VC investments-to-GDP and green patents-to-GDP as the two underlying institutional channels
- Mediators: Information disclosed by proponents (ln_visual, text_length) mediates the greenness-success link; domestic-to-international backer ratio mediates the EPI moderation

Country-level environmental institutions exert two opposing forces on green campaign success. Stronger institutions embed green ventures in a knowledge-rich, legitimate ecosystem (proxied by green patents) that should raise perceived credibility, but they also expand alternative public and private funding (proxied by green VC), leading prospective backers to perceive their individual contributions as less needed. This crowding-out effect dominates, so green campaigns are less likely to succeed—and the gap widens—in high-EPI countries, operating principally through reduced engagement of domestic backers.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper addresses an empirical puzzle in the green-crowdfunding literature by showing that overlooked cross-country institutional heterogeneity, and a related omitted-variable bias around information disclosure, can account for divergent prior results. It contributes to work on institutions and entrepreneurial finance by uncovering an unintended detrimental consequence: strong institutional support for sustainability can crowd out bottom-up crowdfunding participation, implying crowdfunding is more effective when it fills a perceived institutional void.

**Practical Implication**
Green entrepreneurs in countries with well-developed institutional ecosystems should carefully assess whether crowdfunding is the most effective financing channel, since backers there may be less inclined to contribute. Platforms could emphasize funding gaps and the additive value of backer participation, and policymakers should promote crowdfunding as a green-finance tool especially in countries with less developed green ecosystems where contributions are perceived as more essential.

**Limitations**
The analysis is correlational and does not establish causality between campaign greenness and success. The data are limited to reward-based campaigns on Kickstarter, so generalizability to other platforms and crowdfunding types (e.g., equity crowdfunding) is uncertain. The sample covers only Europe and the United States, leaving contexts with lower institutional support for sustainability untested.

**Future Research**
Future studies could adopt experimental or other identification strategies to establish causality. Extending the analysis to other platforms and crowdfunding types would enhance generalizability. Examining contexts with weaker institutional support for sustainability would test whether the findings hold more broadly.
