# The politics of impact: How political ideology shapes perceptions of the environmental impact of individual actions

**Research Question**
Do conservatives and liberals construe the environmental impact of identical sustainable behaviors differently, and if so, what mechanism explains this ideological gap and how can it be attenuated? The paper proposes that perceived prevalence of the behavior within one's political ingroup drives ideological differences in perceived impact and, in turn, sustainable behavior.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Political ideology (liberal vs. conservative, measured continuously and dichotomously); manipulated moderators: domain type (health vs. environment), ingroup-prevalence messaging, explicit impact information
- DV(s): Perceived environmental impact of sustainable behaviors; willingness/intention to engage in sustainable behaviors and incentive-compatible choices (vegetarian recipe choice, eco pen choice, carbon-offset contribution, willingness to pay)
- Mediators: Perceived prevalence of sustainable behavior within one's ingroup (first mediator), perceived environmental impact (second mediator), tested as a serial prevalence-to-impact pathway
- Moderators: Domain framing (health vs. sustainability), prevalence-emphasizing messaging, explicit-impact communication

Because objectively estimating environmental impact is difficult, consumers rely on readily available social cues—chiefly the perceived prevalence of a behavior within their ingroup—to infer how impactful it is (an availability-heuristic logic). Conservatives observe fewer ingroup members engaging in sustainable behaviors, infer lower prevalence, and therefore perceive lower impact, which depresses engagement. The effect reflects informational (not merely normative) influence: low ingroup prevalence is internalized as valid information about a behavior's true impact. Attenuating the prevalence gap (health-domain framing, ingroup-prevalence messaging) or bypassing it (explicit impact information) eliminates the ideological difference, and the prevalence-to-impact pathway outperforms alternatives such as collective efficacy, system justification, fair-market ideology, and normative conformity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces perceived environmental impact as a distinct, ideology-sensitive driver of sustainable intentions that operates over and above climate-change beliefs, moral values, and personality traits. It identifies a novel perceived-prevalence-to-impact pathway grounded in informational social influence (Deutsch & Gerard, 1955; Kelman, 1958), showing that conservatives use the lower prevalence of sustainable behavior among their ingroup as diagnostic information about impact. It thereby extends literatures on political ideology, accuracy of environmental perceptions, and social influence by distinguishing consumer segments rather than treating perceptions as uniform.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers and marketers can raise conservatives' engagement by (a) reframing behaviors into non-environmental domains where they appear more prevalent among the ingroup (e.g., health), (b) using messaging that highlights ingroup prevalence of sustainable actions, and (c) communicating the positive environmental impact of actions explicitly. Value-aligned appeals alone are insufficient; messages must also make impact salient.

**Limitations**
Liberals' impact estimates happened to align closely with actual impact for the studied behaviors, but the authors caution they would be surprised if liberals are consistently accurate, since both groups mis-estimated certain behaviors (e.g., air travel, recycling). Multiple factors plausibly contribute to ideological differences, and the studies rule out several—but not all—alternative explanations; rationalization and other constructs may still play some role.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how perceived prevalence varies across behavioral domains and replicate the effect in other low-ingroup-prevalence contexts such as vaccinations. Researchers could study how prevalence messaging interacts with internal beliefs and values where it may provoke psychological reactance (e.g., free-speech, political-expression domains), and how combining value-based appeals with impact-focused messaging affects engagement across ideological groups.
