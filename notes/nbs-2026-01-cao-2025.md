# Detecting Gender Stereotype Biases Against Women Entrepreneurs in Large Language Models

**Abstract**
We investigate the capability of Artificial Intelligence (AI), specifically Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, to exhibit gender stereotype biases against women entrepreneurs—a critical issue as AI becomes more prevalent in entrepreneurial decision-making. Despite optimism about AI's utility, the risk of reinforcing existing gender stereotype biases poses significant ethical challenges. Our explorative research, which builds on prior studies and utilizes a regression-based method demonstrated by OpenAI, analyzes LLM responses to various scenarios. Our findings show that LLMs did not ascribe strong masculine traits to successful entrepreneurs, although some nuances still remained. Furthermore, LLMs did not rate femininity lower in general business evaluations, and explicitly identifying the entrepreneur's sex did not alter these results. These findings seemingly suggest that AI's perspective deviated from the traditional view that entrepreneurship is predominantly a masculine occupation. However, in investment-specific scenarios, LLMs exhibited a notable bias toward masculine traits over feminine traits, aligning with traditional stereotypical views of entrepreneurship. This variability in responses not only deepens our understanding of gender stereotype biases in AI but also underscores the complexity of addressing these biases, thereby contributing to discussions about AI's potential to offer less biased evaluations than human judgments and paving the way for fairer AI-driven business practices. Additionally, these findings highlight the necessity for careful integration of AI-generated data into theoretical development, considering AI's unique information processing capabilities, and the employment of suitable analytical tools to more effectively detect subtle patterns and nuances in AI-generated data.

**Research Question**
Does AI, like its human counterparts, hold gender stereotype biases against women entrepreneurs? Specifically, does AI stereotypically associate masculine traits with successful entrepreneurship, undervalue femininity, and consequently rate women entrepreneurs lower?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Gendered trait framing and stereotype-activation conditions (masculine, feminine, nullified/gender-neutral, no-stereotype control); explicit specification of the entrepreneur's sex; gendered tone of Reddit narratives; masculinity vs. femininity as regression predictors.
- DV(s): LLM trait attributions to successful entrepreneurs (BSRI words); LLM evaluations of business-opportunity attractiveness (three-item, five-point Likert scale); LLM-generated logistic-regression coefficients for investor preference.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Task stakes — bias surfaced in high-stakes investment/funding scenarios but not in lower-stakes trait-description or opportunity-evaluation tasks; model architecture produced minor variation.

Drawing on Role Congruity Theory, the authors expected LLMs to penalize women entrepreneurs whose communal (feminine-coded) traits conflict with the agentic (masculine-coded) entrepreneurial ideal. Across trait-description, experimental, and field-study tasks the LLMs instead showed balanced or slightly feminine-leaning evaluations, diverging from the theory's predictions. Only when the OpenAI-style regression method probed simulated funding decisions did masculine coefficients dominate feminine ones (e.g., GPT-4 masculinity 0.592 vs. femininity −0.047, p < 0.001), revealing that gender stereotype biases remain latent and emerge selectively under high-stakes evaluative pressure.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study raises critical questions about the applicability of human-centric frameworks such as Role Congruity Theory when analyzing LLM outputs, showing that LLMs often diverge from the theory's predictions and providing initial evidence that gender stereotype biases manifest differently in AI systems. It thereby argues for reconsidering or adapting human-centric theories to account for AI's distinct evaluative logic, and highlights the necessity of carefully integrating AI-generated data and suitable analytical tools into theory development.

**Practical Implication**
For entrepreneurs and organizations, AI generally offered balanced, less prejudiced evaluations in non-critical domains such as idea refinement and opportunity evaluation, but encoded subtle masculine-favoring biases in funding decisions. The authors call for bias audits before deployment, debiasing techniques during fine-tuning (e.g., counterfactual data augmentation, adversarial training), explainable-AI tools, and auditing or certification frameworks for AI used in accelerators, pitch competitions, and venture-capital screening.

**Limitations**
The study could not fully disentangle the three interrelated sources of bias (historical/systemic, statistical/computational, human cognitive), and convergence across models cannot be attributed definitively to shared training data, alignment strategies, or safety guardrails. Findings derive from focused studies on gender bias and may not generalize to other research questions or domains, and the work is constrained by financial and logistical limitations on data depth and breadth.

**Future Research**
Future work could systematically vary training-data inputs, fine-tuning procedures, and prompt structures to isolate the sources of bias; compare architectures, corpora, and alignment strategies to identify drivers of cross-model convergence; examine whether LLMs preserve baseline neutrality across iterative multi-turn user interactions; and investigate how AI aligns with or diverges from theoretical expectations in fields beyond entrepreneurship.
