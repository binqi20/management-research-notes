# European regions transitioning to green markets: the role of related capabilities and public procurement policies

**Abstract**
To do so, we investigate two key sets of factors. First, we consider the evolutionary capability development process whereby new specializations emerge from existing related regional capabilities, in a path-dependent way. Second, we account for green public procurement initiatives to capture path-creation efforts in the form of deliberate regional policy directed towards green market formation.

**Research Question**
How and whether do European regions transition into new green market specializations, and what roles do prior related regional capabilities (path-dependence) and green public procurement (path-creation) play in shaping these transitions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): MarketRelatedDensity (related density of a region's prior market capabilities); CrossRelatedDensity (technology-market cross-related density linking prior technological and market specializations); GreenProcurement (log count of green public procurement contracts), all lagged one time-window and standardized.
- DV(s): GreenMarketEntry — a region entering a new green market specialization (Revealed Market Advantage moving from <=1 to >1 between consecutive time-windows).
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Interaction effects tested among the three predictors; the market-relatedness x cross-relatedness interaction and the market-relatedness x green-procurement interaction are positive and significant.

The paper integrates evolutionary economic geography (the principle of relatedness) with the geography of transitions to argue that new green market specializations emerge along two logics: a path-dependent capability-development process, in which regions branch into green markets that are related to their existing market and technological capabilities, and a path-creation process, in which deliberate green public procurement articulates regional demand and legitimizes green alternatives during early market formation. Trademark-based metrics capture downstream market specializations while patents capture upstream technological relatedness, so cross-relatedness captures synergies between the two. Empirically, cross-relatedness shows the strongest association with green market entry, followed by market relatedness and then green public procurement.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the evolutionary economic geography approach to environmental innovation beyond technological relatedness by incorporating market relatedness and tech-market cross-relatedness as drivers of regional green market (not just technology) specialization. It contributes a systematic, quantitative comparative analysis to the largely qualitative geography-of-transitions literature, and demonstrates trademark data as a methodological device for capturing green market formation and its linkages to technologies at the regional level.

**Practical Implication**
Regional and EU policymakers should treat market leadership, not only technological leadership, as a target of sustainability and competitiveness policy, recognizing that some regions can lead in green markets without leading in green technologies. Green public procurement is a viable demand-pull lever for path creation, and smart specialization policies (with an added sustainability "S4") can combine "smart" and "sustainable" path development when regions intelligently leverage related existing capabilities.

**Limitations**
The analysis cannot fully control for endogeneity, so the evidence captures associations rather than identified causal effects, and quasi-experimental designs would be needed for cleaner identification of policy effects. Green procurement contracts were selected via pre-defined project tags, potentially missing relevant unlabeled procurements, and the study shares the usual caveat that patents and trademarks do not capture all technological and market activity.

**Future Research**
Future work could link green regional capabilities to the underlying availability of green skills and skill mismatch, and broaden the analysis beyond public procurement to a wider range of policy interventions and mixes. Researchers could also move beyond average patterns to examine how the identified drivers differ across region types (e.g., peripheral vs. core, urban vs. rural) and refine policy classification using deductive coding assisted by large language models.
