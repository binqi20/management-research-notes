# Can Purpose and Professional Advancement Coexist? Review of Make It Meaningful: How to Find Purpose in Life and Work by Debbie Haski-Leventhal

**Abstract**
meaningful work is both a personal quest and a collective

**Research Question**
Not reported in paper

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Purpose is framed not merely as a route to personal flourishing but as a moral imperative for ethical engagement at work, such that organizations bear a responsibility to enable employees to experience meaningful work.
- Key construct ("ethical ambition" / purpose-in-action): Career advancement and leadership need not require sacrificing one's core purpose or values but can be an expression of them — reframed as especially empowering for women uneasy about self-promotion.
- Practical framework (the "Purpose Compass"): An ikigai-inspired model organized around four questions — what do I love, what am I good at, what does the world need, and what can I be paid for — operationalized through reflective exercises.
- Contextual lens: Meaningful work as fundamentally relational, with particular resonance for female academics facing gendered expectations, structural barriers, and tensions between caregiving values and traditional metrics of success.

The review traces how Haski-Leventhal grounds the importance of purpose in ethical rather than purely instrumental terms — connecting individual meaning to stakeholder responsibility, conscious capitalism, and the avoidance of "moral disjunction" between one's values and one's role. Chavan reads the book as bridging scholarly research on meaningful work and accessible personal-development guidance, arguing that its central normative claim — individuals have a duty to seek purpose and organizations a duty to support it — is both inspirational and normatively robust.

**Theoretical Contribution**
Not reported in paper

**Practical Implication**
The review distills the book's call to action for leaders to design work environments that honor the human search for meaning — articulating clear organizational values, connecting employees' roles to a broader mission, and fostering a culture of respect and growth — and encourages professionals, especially women in academia, to reframe advancement as a platform to amplify impact rather than a compromise of their ethos.

**Limitations**
Chavan notes that the book is light on theoretical nuance and systematic empirical detail, leaning on moving anecdotes where a researcher might crave more data, and that the Purpose Compass simplifies complex constructs for a general audience — characterizing this as a deliberate trade-off of depth for reach rather than a flaw.
