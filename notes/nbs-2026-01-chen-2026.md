# Corporate Social Responsibility and Employee Information Sharing

**Abstract**
This study investigates the association between corporate social responsibility (CSR) and the informativeness of employeeshared information. Focusing on employee reviews for U.S. publicly-listed firms from Glassdoor.com, we find a significantly positive association between firms’ CSR performance and the magnitude of market reactions to these reviews. The results hold across various robustness tests. Channel analyses demonstrate that this positive association is due to reviews for firms with stronger CSR performance providing a greater volume of information and being perceived as more credible. Moreover, employee ratings for firms with stronger CSR performance exert greater influence on stock prices, are less likely to be overly optimistic, decline more sharply following irresponsible social practices, and better predict future operating performance. Review sentiment for these firms also exerts a stronger impact on stock prices. Textual analyses of employee reviews further indicate that a socially responsible culture is considered important in firms with strong CSR performance and is positively associated with the magnitude of market reactions to those reviews. The main results hold using an alternative sample of employee reviews for Chinese publicly-listed firms. Collectively, these findings suggest that strong CSR performance reflects a socially responsible culture that fosters more informative disclosures by employees.

**Research Question**
Is a firm's CSR performance associated with the informativeness of information shared by its rank-and-file employees, and if so, through what mechanisms (information volume and perceived credibility) does a socially responsible culture shape that informativeness?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CSR performance (lagged average of MSCI ESG social and environmental scores; alternative measures include CSR_GOV, Refinitiv, KLD, and Sino ratings)
- DV(s): Informativeness of Glassdoor employee reviews, proxied by the absolute abnormal return |ABRET(0,+1)| at the review posting date
- Mediators: Information volume (review length, forward-looking content, hard/quantitative information) carries part of the CSR effect on market reactions
- Moderators: CSR performance positively moderates the association between employee review ratings (e.g., overall, business outlook) and review informativeness (H2)

Strong CSR performance is theorized to proxy for a socially responsible corporate culture marked by stakeholder concern, trust, honesty, and a moral duty to "do the right thing." Such a culture deepens employee insight, fosters psychological safety and identification, reduces managerial pressure to distort disclosures, and strengthens employees' willingness to voice concerns despite retaliation risk. These forces raise both the volume and the credibility of employee-shared information, producing stronger and more accurate stock-price reactions to reviews of CSR firms relative to non-CSR firms.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the CSR–information-environment literature from top-management disclosures to bottom-up, crowdsourced, employee-generated information, identifying socially responsible corporate culture as an underexplored institutional factor shaping the value of employee disclosures. It also uncovers an unexplored mechanism linking CSR to firm value—employee-shared information that elicits greater stock-price responses—and contributes to research on the determinants of rank-and-file employee disclosure informativeness.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize an unintended economic benefit of a socially responsible culture: it enhances the informativeness of employee communications and reduces information asymmetry with the capital market. Investors should closely monitor employee voices for CSR firms on social-media platforms as early signals of firm fundamentals and future operating outcomes that may not surface through formal financial disclosures or managerial communications.

**Limitations**
CSR represents only one element of a much larger set of determinants of employee-disclosure informativeness; factors such as employee-specific characteristics, workplace dynamics, institutional conditions, and broader industry and regulatory environments also matter, so the analysis captures only part of the picture. The reliance on voluntarily posted reviews is a limitation inherent in voluntary-disclosure research.

**Future Research**
Future research could identify additional determinants of review informativeness to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the mechanisms through which employee voices contribute to information transparency and market efficiency.
