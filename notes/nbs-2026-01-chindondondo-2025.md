# Social Sustainability Practice: The Interplay between State and Community Logics

**Abstract**
This study contributes to business ethics by theorising how relational ethics, rooted in community-based values, shape the role of state intervention in driving corporate sustainability behaviour. We introduce the concept of functional compensation to explain how organisations navigate institutional complexity by shifting between state and community logics. Drawing on the African ethic of Ubuntu—which emphasises solidarity, mutual respect, and human dignity—we suggest that community logic reflects a relational ethic rooted in culturally embedded forms of moral agency. Using survey data from 276 firms in Zimbabwe, we find that state logic, centred on regulatory enforcement, drives sustainability compliance when community logic is weak. However, as Ubuntu-based community logic strengthens, it functionally substitutes for state logic, sustaining or even enhancing ethical performance in the absence of formal regulation. By integrating Ubuntu into institutional logics theory, this paper offers a context-sensitive approach to corporate ethics and social sustainability, advancing both theoretical insight and policy relevance in diverse governance environments.

**Research Question**
How do state and community logics interact to influence firms' social sustainability practices, and under what boundary conditions do state-led regulations become less central or necessary relative to voluntary, community-rooted approaches grounded in relational ethics and Ubuntu?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): State logic (perceived governmental pressure to observe social justice and environmental protection)
- DV(s): Social sustainability practice (10-item employee- and community-directed scale)
- Mediators: None tested
- Moderators: Community logic / Ubuntu (relational ethic of survival, solidarity, respect, dignity, collectivism); robustness check substitutes normative pressure
- Controls: Multinational status, firm size (employees), firm age, market logic

State logic exerts a positive effect on social sustainability practice that weakens as community logic strengthens: the state-logic × community-logic interaction is significant and negative (b = -0.38, p < 0.001), and a strong community logic sustains high social sustainability practice even when state logic is weak. The authors theorise this substitutive pattern as *functional compensation*—a dynamic recalibration in which organisations lean more heavily on one institutional logic as another becomes less salient, legitimate, or effective. Ubuntu-rooted relational ethics internalise sustainability as a moral imperative embedded in community relationships, reducing reliance on coercive state enforcement; where community logic is weak, state logic compensates by enforcing compliance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces *functional compensation* as a novel mechanism for navigating institutional complexity, recasting organisational engagement with logics as temporally situated, inverse, and relationally modulated rather than as static blending, compartmentalisation, or hybridisation. By integrating the African philosophy of Ubuntu into institutional logics theory, it repositions community logic—often subordinated in institutional analysis—as a potentially autonomous and even superordinate force, and advances business ethics by foregrounding a relational, culturally embedded conception of moral agency over individualistic, rationalist models.

**Practical Implication**
A one-size-fits-all approach to sustainability regulation is unlikely to succeed across contexts; where relational ethics such as Ubuntu are strong, voluntary, community-based initiatives may outperform coercive regulation, and mandatory rules could include sunset clauses that phase out as relational ethics become institutionalised. Practitioners and management training programs should cultivate a sustainability mindset rooted in solidarity, compassion, and respect, and firms in community-oriented regions should integrate local cultural values into sustainability strategy.

**Limitations**
The study is contextually specific to Zimbabwe, limiting generalisability across other cultural and institutional environments. Its cross-sectional, single-instrument design offers only a temporal snapshot and risks common-method bias (addressed but not eliminated via Harman's test), and the measurement scales—especially for community logic / Ubuntu—remain incipient, with average variance extracted for the social-sustainability and community-logic scales falling below the 0.50 threshold.

**Future Research**
Future work should test whether community logic's substitutability for state intervention holds in other African, Latin American, Asian, and Global North communal settings; employ longitudinal designs to trace how relational logics and community logics themselves strengthen or weaken over time, including with rising economic well-being; examine how relational ethics can be embedded into formal corporate governance structures; and refine measurement by extending the community-logic scale into a validated "relational ethics" scale grounded in relational ontology.
