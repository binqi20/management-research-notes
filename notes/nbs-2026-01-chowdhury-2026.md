# Marginalized Stakeholder Theory: A Normative Foundation for Decolonization

**Abstract**
Whether they address issues of marginalization or study organizations in general, traditional management and business ethics scholars predominantly focus on firms' engagement with powerful stakeholders. This represents a severe problem globally, manifesting acutely in postcolonial contexts, as firms' engagement with marginalized stakeholders is either neglected or ignored. This neglect also highlights the problem of epistemic obfuscation in management and business ethics scholarship, through which existing works on decolonization and marginalized stakeholders are either overlooked or co-opted by the mainstream. To advance decolonial organizational scholarship and counter epistemic obfuscation, I propose marginalized stakeholder theory. This theory establishes new pathways for firms to revise their normative principles and decolonize their corporate activities. It enables firms to overcome colonial legacies, reject neocolonial logics embedded in instrumental approaches, and develop decolonial engagement with marginalized stakeholders. To this end, I reconceptualize three engagement approaches—proactive, reactive, and defensive—which are generally used by firms for instrumental purposes. I argue that this reconceptualization provides a starting point for the necessary structural and agentic transformation toward decolonization in epistemic and organizational practices.

**Research Question**
How can firms engage with marginalized stakeholders (MSs) in a normatively decolonial manner that transforms corporate actions toward these stakeholders from the outset, rather than treating them instrumentally or as peripheral, vulnerable parties?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Traditional stakeholder theory privileges powerful stakeholders (via salience attributes of power, legitimacy, urgency) and is built on normative foundations entangled with colonial legacies; this perpetuates "epistemic obfuscation" that overlooks or co-opts work on marginalized stakeholders.
- Key constructs: marginalized stakeholders (MSs); epistemic obfuscation and the "meta-fallacy"; moral courage, moral imagination, and "perfect/imperfect obligations"; decolonial social contracts; third-party (NGO/state) interference and misrepresentation.
- Reconceptualized engagement approaches: (1) **proactive** — MS capacity auditing and "proactive deep dialog" before firm activities begin; (2) **reactive** — "reactive deep dialog" and compromise once grievances or political opportunities emerge; (3) **defensive** — protecting MS moral and representational rights under state repression, exiting non-violently rather than supporting coercion.
- Boundary conditions: contexts vary (democratic/autocratic, postcolonial/neocolonial); marginalization can be studied beyond postcolonial settings, but MST centers colonial legacies and epistemic injustice and excludes "fringe" actors who espouse regressive worldviews.

The central process is normative rather than causal: by recognizing colonial legacies and securing MS consent and self-representation from the outset, firms revise the underlying normative assumptions of instrumental engagement, redistribute profits equitably, and reduce third-party misrepresentation, thereby enabling structural and agentic transformation toward decolonization.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper proposes marginalized stakeholder theory as a normative foundation for decolonizing corporate activity, making three contributions: (1) it shows how traditional stakeholder management perpetuates epistemic obfuscation by incentivizing attention to powerful stakeholders at the expense of MSs, and argues firms need moral courage and moral imagination to break free of (neo)colonial instrumental logics; (2) it repositions MSs as a central lens (a "dialectical" framework) for decolonizing firm-level activities and re-reading misrepresentations; and (3) it calls for breaking free of the (neo)colonial narrative of profit maximization disguised as instrumentality, reciprocity, and trust, urging scholars to revisit, redefine, or abandon concepts such as CSR, ESG, and base-of-the-pyramid.

**Practical Implication**
Firms should conduct "MS capacity auditing," establish robust social contracts and dignity criteria with marginalized stakeholders, and obtain freely given (non-coerced) consent before pursuing activities that affect them. They should reduce reliance on NGOs and government intermediaries who may misrepresent MSs, be willing to accept profit trade-offs (including leaving sites or forgoing extraction), and treat not-exploiting as a legitimate way of creating collective value and wellbeing.

**Limitations**
The author notes the viability of MST is itself constrained by entrenched neocolonial logics of profit maximization and by education systems and business schools that perpetuate epistemic obfuscation. The illustrative contexts are not representative of all political or economic systems, and MST predominantly focuses on marginalization rooted in colonial legacies and epistemic injustice rather than organizational adjustment, requiring clear scope boundaries.

**Future Research**
The author calls for further disruption of epistemic injustice so that more scholars recognize epistemic obfuscation in their own work, and for exploring additional engagement situations and approaches beyond the three reconceptualized here. He also invites work on how marginalization can be studied across diverse (not only postcolonial) contexts while maintaining MST's premises, and cautions scholars to exercise sustained care when evaluating claims of marginalization.
