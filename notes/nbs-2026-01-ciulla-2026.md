# Careless or Clueless? the Lost Promise of Facebook

**Abstract**
serves as a cautionary tale about the moral challenges that social media companies face in a complex and rapidly evolving environment and the importance of political CSR.

**Theoretical Contribution**
Reviewing Sarah Wynn-Williams' memoir as a business-ethics case study, Ciulla argues the book's most significant contribution is to the literature on political corporate social responsibility (PCSR) in the technology sector: drawing on Scherer et al. (2016) and Matten et al. (2003), she reframes Facebook/Meta as a "political actor" that began as a public good and then positioned itself at the center of political activity, jettisoning its own Community Guidelines when ethical standards conflicted with profits. The review also advances theory on ethical leadership (Ciulla, 2020), contending that technical expertise without ethical competence produces leaders like Zuckerberg who appear "more clueless than careless," and on whistleblowing, classifying Wynn-Williams under Davis's (1996) complicity theory (reporting on harms she participated in) versus Haugen's standard-theory whistleblowing.

**Practical Implication**
The review warns against allowing technology companies to design and enforce the ethical standards for their own products, since firms "excel at designing ethical safeguards, but struggle to adhere to them when they conflict with their own interests and profits" — evidenced by Microsoft, X, and Google downsizing or eliminating ethics teams. Ciulla underscores the importance of political CSR and of corporations collaborating with governments to establish responsible guidelines, while noting that all-or-nothing regulation (e.g., the U.S. State Department's stance that Facebook should accept being shut down rather than censor on Russia's behalf) is a hard pill for firms and politicians to swallow.

**Limitations**
The review notes the obvious limitation of the memoir under review: it offers only one woman's (Wynn-Williams') perspective on Facebook and Zuckerberg, a bias the author concedes by calling it a memoir, and one possibly colored by her having been fired after filing a sexual-harassment complaint against Joel Kaplan. Ciulla also observes that Wynn-Williams discloses her harassment complaint only briefly and at the end, raising questions about non-disclosure agreements and whether a settlement shaped the account.

**Future Research**
Ciulla frames the governance of ethical standards for social media and AI — and the question of who should develop those standards, firms or governments — as "one of the most pressing issues in business ethics today," leaving open whether government regulation would do a better job. She points to the unresolved challenge of reconciling the conflict between user safety and profits in an industry "operating in a regulatory wasteland, where rules are made and broken as they go along."
