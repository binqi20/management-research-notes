**Research Question**
Do public innovation subsidies and the EU Emissions Trading Scheme (EU ETS) interact synergistically to enhance green innovation at the firm level — i.e., do firms subject to both policies patent more green technologies than those affected by only one — and what are the separate causal effects of each policy on Swedish industrial firms' green patenting?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): EU ETS participation (dummy); total direct subsidies; innovation-related subsidies; subsidy size (large vs. none); their interaction (subsidy × ETS)
- DV(s): Number of green patents granted (CPC Y02 climate-mitigation/adaptation class); patent citations (quality); number of technical classes (technological scope)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Firm size (large ≥250 / medium 51-249 / small ≤50 employees); sector (energy and transport vs. all); treatment cohort and calendar time

The paper theorizes that combining innovation subsidies with emissions pricing should address two joint market failures (insufficient private R&D incentives plus weak regulatory demand), so a policy mix could direct technical change toward green innovation at lower cost than either instrument alone. A firm-level synergy, however, requires that the *same* firms respond to both policies. Empirically the authors find no synergistic effect: high-emitting ETS firms — often locked into carbon-intensive, path-dependent technologies — are largely not the firms receiving innovation subsidies or producing green patents, so the two instruments act on different firm populations. Subsidies (especially large ones, and for larger firms) are independently and positively associated with the quantity, quality, and scope of green patenting, while EU ETS participation shows no measurable effect on patentable green innovation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the policy-mix literature by isolating, at the firm level, the interaction between a specific emissions-pricing instrument (the EU ETS) and direct R&D subsidies — a test prior aggregate or cross-country work could not perform. It shows that firm-level complementarity between emissions pricing and innovation subsidies cannot be assumed from aggregate complementarity: when the policies' target populations barely overlap, no synergy emerges, and demand-side environmental regulation must be paired with strong supply-side financial support to drive green technological development.

**Practical Implication**
Because emissions pricing alone did not stimulate patentable green innovation in Sweden, policymakers should not rely on carbon markets to generate green R&D; substantial subsidy support remains essential to overcome market failures. The absence of firm-level synergy suggests prioritizing broad measures such as technology transfer and adoption incentives for high-polluting firms, while channeling sizable subsidies — which disproportionately benefit larger firms with greater absorptive and administrative capacity — to firms positioned to develop new green technologies.

**Limitations**
Due to data limitations the authors cannot control for firms' energy use or actual CO2 emissions, so they measure EU ETS participation but not firm-specific compliance costs, limiting assessment of whether the emissions-pricing/subsidy interaction differs for high- versus low-compliance-cost firms. They also cannot assess heterogeneity in EU ETS effects. Outcomes rely solely on patents, which capture only part of firms' innovation responses, and cumulative DID outcomes must be interpreted cautiously given selection and attribution concerns.

**Future Research**
Future work should revisit the interaction in more recent years as free emissions-allowance allocation is phased out and compliance costs rise. Researchers could use alternative innovation indicators (e.g., R&D expenditures) beyond patents, examine both green and non-green patents to capture bidirectional spillovers, and test whether firms under more stringent environmental regulation generate more green patents or instead redirect innovation — potentially shaped by state dependency — toward other technological domains.
