**Research Question**
When subject to public scrutiny, do firms strategically use advertising to reduce expected political costs? Specifically, do scrutinized industries direct disproportionate advertising spending toward the electorates of the politicians who oversee the congressional hearings that generate that scrutiny?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): PolAttention (industry faces 3+ congressional hearings with member testimony in year t-1), decomposed into PolAttention_ComRep (county represented by a committee member overseeing the hearings) and PolAttention_NoComRep (county not so represented)
- DV(s): AdSpending — natural logarithm of one plus dollar advertising spending by industry i in county c in year t (county-disaggregated industry advertising expenditures)
- Moderators (cross-sectional): hearing acrimony (length, negative tone, strong words, legislative words); corporate testimony characteristics (multiple firms, CEO testimony, repeated testimony in a short window, positive prepared remarks); political pressure (election year, member seniority); citizen engagement and media exposure (ANES political engagement, X posts tagging firm and politician, negative news, hearing coverage)
- Complements/substitutes: income-decreasing discretionary accruals (complement); lobbying expenditures (substitute)

Industries under expected political costs raise advertising in the electorates of overseeing politicians to ease constituents' concerns and relieve the pressure on those politicians to legislate harshly against the industry. Because only the subset of politicians on the relevant committees can impose costs, the incremental advertising in "strategically important electorates" (ComRep) over baseline electorates (NoComRep) isolates the strategic response from general reputation-building. The effect strengthens monotonically in the number of hearings and is concentrated where the political incentive to improve image is greatest (acrimonious hearings, CEO testimony, election years, senior members, engaged or media-exposed constituencies).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study provides some of the first evidence on the third Watts-and-Zimmerman (1978) channel for managing political costs — strategic communication via advertising — which had been largely undocumented relative to the well-studied lobbying and income-decreasing-accounting channels. It shows that the firm's choice among political-cost-management tools varies with the target audience, with advertising complementing income-decreasing accruals (both aimed at voters and politicians) while substituting for lobbying (aimed at politicians alone), and that advertising can curb rather than incite political activism.

**Practical Implication**
The findings document a previously unexamined channel of corporate intervention in the political process, informing the ongoing policy debate about money and corporate influence in politics: firms can quietly redirect advertising toward an overseeing politician's constituents to shape the demand for legislation, a behavior that is harder to observe than lobbying or contributions yet economically meaningful (about $60,000 of incremental advertising per strategically important industry-county-year).

**Limitations**
The authors cannot observe advertisement content, so they cannot directly verify that the advertising is intended to improve public image and must instead rely on cross-sectional variation in hearing and electoral characteristics. Selection remains a possible concern — politicians may choose to serve on committees relevant to economically important firms in their electorates — though results are robust to excluding counties/states tied to testifying firms' headquarters or operations and to controlling for a political-risk measure.

**Future Research**
Examine the content of advertisements to better understand how and when firms use strategic advertising as a commitment mechanism. Investigate how firms choose among tools (e.g., earnings management, lobbying, advertising) and the trade-offs they face in response to congressional or regulatory scrutiny, and assess the efficacy of these efforts in limiting political costs, perhaps using single-industry settings.
