# Re-enchanting Consumer Ethics Through Embodied Relationality: An Ethnographic Approach to the Attitude-Behaviour Gap

**Abstract**
Most research on the 'attitude-behaviour gap' adopts linear, rational, and individual models, or highlights notions of consumer uncertainty and confusion. We argue that different strands of the debate can benefit from an exploration of embodied relational processes shaping consumer ethics, namely an acknowledgement of the profound corporeal and interdependent nature of all consumption experiences. The aim of this paper is thus to explore the embodied relational aspects of everyday ethical consumption and cast new light on the 'attitude-behaviour gap'. We do so by drawing on embodiment in practice theories and through an ethnographic study with six families in North-East England, including observations, interviews, kitchen and walking-with tours, and participants' diaries. Our findings highlight four embodied relational practices: reconciling competing embodied states; negotiating togetherness; seeking sensorial familiarity; and calibrating attention and engagement. The paper contributes to the existing literature by offering embodied relationality as a theoretical framework to visualise the complex ambivalent embodied state consumers experience in everyday practices. We leverage this to rethink the 'attitude-behaviour gap' as a disenchanted concept, namely detached from the embodied nature of all social action and, as such, limited in its explanatory potential. We conclude the paper by outlining future research and intervention avenues to re-enchant consumer ethics scholarship, namely reconnect it with its embodied and relational nature.

**Research Question**
How do embodied relational processes — the corporeal, sensory, and interdependent aspects of everyday consumption — shape consumer ethics, and how can attending to them cast new light on the attitude-behaviour gap (ABG)?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / lens: "embodied relationality" (Mandalaki & Fotaki, 2020) read through practice theory's three devices — bodily dispositions, material elements, and embodied sense (Bourdieu; Warde).
- Practices (emergent findings): (1) reconciling conflicting embodied states; (2) negotiating togetherness; (3) seeking sensorial familiarity; (4) calibrating attention and engagement.
- Triggering conditions: competing desires/emotions in shared eating (e.g., meat reduction vs. social occasions); childhood/ancestral food memories; sensory familiarity of products and retailers; material affordances of shopping environments (supermarkets vs. alternative outlets).
- Boundary conditions: family unit of analysis; food practices; six self-assessed low-to-high ethical-engagement households.

Across family food practices, ethical (in)action is shown to emerge from pre-reflexive, corporeal states rather than linear belief-to-behaviour reasoning. Familiar foods and retailers afford an "ontological security" (Giddens, 1984) that locks consumers into comfortable-but-constraining routines, while shared sensorial practices generate belonging that is continually renegotiated. Reframing the ABG through these co-present, ambivalent embodied states yields a more nuanced account of why "unsustainable" practices persist than cognitive or attitudinal explanations allow.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper offers "embodied relationality" as a framework that surfaces neglected bodily, material, and sensory dimensions in ABG scholarship, and recasts the gap as a *disenchanted construct* — an epistemological problem detached from the embodied nature of social action — rather than a methodological or policy problem to be "bridged." It also extends practice-theory scholarship by responding to Jacobsen and Hansen's (2021) call to reconnect with first-generation theorists' attention to the embodied (not merely socio-material) dimension of practices.

**Practical Implication**
The authors propose multi-sensorial interventions that disrupt un-reflexivity and foster curiosity, address the pre-reflexive dimension of choice, and cultivate embodied belonging within local ecosystems of relationships; they advocate shifting from individual, cognitive, information-giving policies toward systemic interventions and experiential, sensory food education (especially for children).

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The authors outline four "re-enchantment" avenues: (1) methodological innovations accommodating embodied/pre-reflexive and more-than-human entanglements (e.g., neuro-gastronomy); (2) extending embodied relationality beyond the family to collective and institutional consumption spaces; (3) engaging all senses (touch, taste, smell) to study how familiarity and curiosity develop; and (4) using embodied relational methods to investigate which embodied states are conducive to destabilizing and reconfiguring constellations of practices.
