# When are Customer Boycotts Permissible?

**Abstract**
This paper examines ethical customer boycotts, instances where individuals choose to boycott for ethical reasons. By focusing on the commercial freedom of the customer, it offers a new framework for distinguishing between permissible and impermissible boycotts. It concludes that individual ethical boycotts are almost never morally impermissible. The scope of the paper is limited. It does not attempt to answer the important question of when an individual boycott decision is objectively good or bad. Rather, it attempts to answer the prior question of when a decision to boycott—made by someone who believes their decision is morally right—is doing something that is morally permissible. In defending this position, the paper distinguishes between “objective ought” and moral permissibility. The paper argues that focusing on the right of a consumer to choose is the best way to approach the question, “May I boycott this product?” The approach shifts the emphasis in the boycott debate from prevailing frameworks based on democratic values, intentional harm, and libertarianism, toward a customer’s right to choose. It proposes a cosmopolitan interpretation that relies on the default right of market participants to refrain from commercial exchanges except under extraordinary circumstances. Among other benefits, this approach addresses gaps in alternative accounts that overlook a significant portion of customer boycott decision-making. The paper explains why the threshold for boycott impermissibility is very high: (1) because any claim of impermissibility must overcome the default presumption of freedom in commercial exchange; (2) because an unusual “hard duty” follows from a determination of boycott impermissibility; (3) because a boycotter’s true intent is difficult to discern; and (4) because boycotts serve as alternatives to violence. The account offered here implies that even decisions to boycott that are objectively wrong may be morally permissible. Finally, the paper shows why certain kinds of anti-boycott laws threaten an individual’s moral right to boycott.

**Research Question**
When is an individual's decision to engage in an ethical customer boycott—made by someone who believes the boycott is morally right—morally permissible (as distinct from objectively good or bad), and when, if ever, is the default commercial freedom to refrain from buying defeated?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: An "ethical customer boycott" should be assessed through *moral permissibility* (a required/permissible/impermissible triad), not through whether the boycott is objectively good or bad; conflating the two distorts the boycott debate.
- Key constructs: commercial freedom as a default right to refrain from exchange; the distinction between "objective ought" and permissibility; an "all-things-considered permission" that holds when no all-things-considered ought conflicts with the boycott.
- Boundary conditions (four reasons the impermissibility threshold is high): (1) any impermissibility claim must overcome the default presumption of commercial freedom; (2) impermissibility implies an unusual "hard duty" to refrain-from-refraining (i.e., to buy); (3) a boycotter's true intent is opaque and contested; (4) boycotts serve as nonviolent alternatives to violence and social reform.
- Decision rule: a boycott is impermissible "only when it is likely to cause catastrophic harm or demonstrates flagrant disregard for the human dignity of an impacted stakeholder."

The paper builds a normative framework grounded in the default permissibility of market exchange: because refusing to buy is an omission protected by commercial freedom, and because defeating that right would impose a rare "hard duty" to purchase, the threshold for declaring an individual boycott impermissible is extraordinarily high. Drawing on Scanlon's permissibility framing and Mullins/Horty's "all-things-considered ought," it argues that only catastrophic-harm or dignity-violating cases (e.g., the 1920s boycott of Jewish merchants) override the default right, so even objectively wrong boycotts are usually permissible.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reframes the ethics of consumer boycotts around a customer's *commercial freedom* and the deontic concept of permissibility, rather than around democratic values (the SCEC framework of Hussain and Berkey), intentional harm and just-war/liability logic (Betz), or absolutist libertarianism (Rothbard). It offers a "cosmopolitan" account that is neither democracy-centric nor Western-centric and that accommodates clean-hands, expressive, and direct boycotts excluded by prior frameworks, including boycotts arising in non-democratic settings.

**Practical Implication**
The high impermissibility threshold has implications for lawmakers: laws that directly conflict with an individual's moral right to boycott amount to prohibiting morally permissible action, and indirect mechanisms such as loyalty oaths or anti-boycott certifications become morally suspect. Because boycotts function as nonviolent alternatives to conflict, caution is warranted when designing anti-boycott legislation.

**Limitations**
The framework's scope is deliberately limited: it does not address whether any given boycott decision is objectively morally good or bad, only whether it is permissible. It also brackets boycotts undertaken by groups, corporations, influencers, and organizers, and does not attempt a full analysis of anti-boycott laws, which would require engaging jurisprudential and political-theory questions left outside the paper.

**Future Research**
The paper points toward unresolved questions it sets aside: the moral status of boycotts by groups, corporations, and informal collectives whose distinct agency yields different rights and duties; the permissibility of influencer and organizer conduct (framed under free speech and assembly rather than commercial exchange); and a comprehensive moral and jurisprudential evaluation of anti-boycott laws.
