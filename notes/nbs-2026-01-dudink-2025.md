# A ‘Sticky’ Past: The Viscosity of Organizational Memory in Navigating Ethical Change

**Abstract**
This paper introduces viscosity as a conceptual lens to examine how organizational memory shapes ethical transformation. Based on a qualitative case study of a Dutch bank seeking to distance itself from a legacy of irresponsibility, we show how the past persists not only in discourse but also through affective, material, and temporal traces. These lingering effects create friction that complicates efforts at ethical renewal. Building on scholarship in organizational memory, historic corporate responsibility, and temporality, we theorize viscosity as the condition through which a problematic past becomes difficult to dislodge—sticking to identities, routines, and interpretations in ways that elude managerial control. Rather than framing ethical change as a matter of strategic reframing or discursive forgetting, viscosity draws attention to how history continues to be felt, inhabited, and negotiated in everyday organizational life. We show that while viscosity can constrain transformation, it may also simultaneously intensify the moral urgency for reform. In doing so, this study contributes to research on organizational memory by offering a vocabulary for understanding how organizations live with, and through, their unresolved and ‘sticky’ pasts.

**Research Question**
How does the viscosity of organizational memory shape the dynamics of ethical transformation in organizations?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key construct: *viscosity* — a condition of organizational memory in which a problematic past "persistently resist[s] resolution, detachment or reframing," producing internal friction when change is attempted.
- Process / dimensions (third-order): (1) *emotional stickiness of the past*; (2) *haunting of the past*; (3) *drag and friction*; (4) *the past as a persistent interpretive lens*.
- Triggering condition: a retrospectively problematized "sticky past" (NorthBank's conduct around the 2007–08 financial crisis and government bailout) that members cannot fully forget or reframe.
- Emergent outcomes: ethical change is slowed, made more cautious, and rendered vulnerable to external skepticism — yet the same persistence can act as a *moral intensifier* that strengthens commitment to reform.
- Boundary condition: viscosity is *not intentional* and cannot be pinpointed to a single source; it is "felt in its effects," which limits how far managers can strategically bend organizational history.

The central process is that an irresponsible past does not simply recede but folds back into present organizational life through affective, material, and temporal traces, generating "drag" that resists managerial control. Because remembering here is involuntary rather than a strategic choice, viscosity bounds the interpretive flexibility of history: there is "only so much that can be done to bend it." Paradoxically, the very inability to "move on" repeatedly reminds members why ethical change is needed, so viscosity both constrains transformation and can catalyze it as a moral compass.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances *viscosity* as a distinct theoretical lens for organizational memory, showing that the past persists not only discursively but also affectively, materially, and temporally, thereby highlighting the limits of managerial agency that prior work on strategic remembering/forgetting tends to overlook. It contributes a vocabulary for how organizations "live with, and through, their unresolved and 'sticky' pasts," distinguishing viscosity from "stickiness" and "ghostly" founder metaphors and arguing—against accounts that treat the past as pliable or unilaterally negative—that viscous memory both constrains change and can serve as a moral intensifier.

**Practical Implication**
Practitioners should treat memory and change as embodied rather than purely discursive: managers cannot assume that remembering is intentional or that a problematic past can be erased through strategic reframing. Responding to viscosity calls for patience with members' affective states (shame, guilt, fatigue), cleansing rituals of acknowledgment and mourning, and material/temporal "support structures." Most importantly, managers should recognize viscosity as an ethical compass — persistent grievances about the past signal ethical friction to be confronted, not mere resistance to dismiss.
