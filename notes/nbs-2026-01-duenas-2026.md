# The effect of extreme heat on economic growth: Evidence from Latin America

**Abstract**
Extreme heat could account for 34 %–68 % of the total projected reduction in the annual economic growth rate at mid-century due to temperature change. Our results suggest that extreme heat is one potential channel for the documented non-linearity in the impacts of rising mean temperature.

**Research Question**
Does extreme heat affect economic growth at the sub-national level in Latin America beyond the indirect effect operating through higher seasonal mean temperatures, and how does that effect vary with the intensity and duration of heat?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Annual count of "hot days" (days with daily maximum hourly heat index above given percentiles of the region-month distribution over the prior decade) and count of "heat waves" (consecutive hot days), varying in intensity (15th to 1st percentile) and duration (1 to 7 consecutive days).
- DV(s): Annual growth rate of per capita gross regional product (GRP).
- Mediators: Agriculture (share of regional employment in agriculture used to split regions into agriculture-intensive vs. not), examined as a channel rather than a formal mediator.
- Moderators: Heat intensity and duration (effects increase non-linearly in both); regional agricultural intensity.

Defining extreme heat relative to each region-month's moving historical distribution lets the measures capture unusually hot days for a given climate while accounting for acclimation to the season and local climate. By controlling for seasonal mean temperatures, the authors isolate the direct effect of extreme heat from its indirect effect through rising seasonal averages. Plausibly exogenous year-to-year, region-to-region variation in hot-day and heat-wave counts (net of region and year fixed effects) identifies the effect on GRP growth, with reduced agricultural productivity proposed as one transmission channel.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes two main contributions to the literature on the effects of extreme heat on aggregate economic activity. First, it shows that the negative effect of extreme heat on aggregate economic growth increases with both the intensity and duration of heat, using easy-to-interpret measures that allow comparisons across heat waves. Second, it provides evidence that the negative economic impacts of rising temperature will be driven primarily by extreme events rather than gradual increases in local mean temperatures, suggesting extreme heat is one channel behind documented non-linearities in temperature-growth relationships.

**Practical Implication**
Because extreme heat affects growth beyond average seasonal temperatures and accounts for 34%–68% of the total projected mid-century temperature impact, integrated assessment models that focus on mean temperature underestimate the economic costs of climate change. Adapting economic activity to extreme heat is important but challenging, since heat waves are relatively infrequent, intense, and difficult to predict in advance.

**Limitations**
The back-of-the-envelope mid-century projections must be interpreted with caution: they assume the impacts of extreme heat and seasonal mean temperatures on growth do not change over time or vary across geographies, and they ignore uncertainty about the estimated coefficients. The agricultural channel is established only as suggestive evidence via employment-share splits, and the heat-wave definitions used in the climate projections (Feron et al., 2019) differ slightly from those used in the historical analysis.
