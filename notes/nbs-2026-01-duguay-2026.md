# Mandatory Carbon Disclosure and New Business Creation

**Abstract**
This paper studies how mandatory greenhouse gas disclosure affects new business formation.

**Research Question**
How does mandatory carbon disclosure affect the birth of new businesses? The paper examines whether the U.S. Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program (GHGRP) stimulates new business formation in affected industries relative to unaffected controls, and through what channels.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Exposure to the GHGRP disclosure mandate (Post × Treat, where Treat indicates GHGRP-affected industries and Post indicates 2012 onward); a continuous treatment (ContTreat, share of facilities above the reporting threshold)
- DV(s): Business births (BusinessBirth1/2, log of new establishment counts from SUSB); secondary outcomes: incumbent production (non-combustion emissions, COGS, PROD), R&D, CAPEX, profitability (ROA, ROIC, FCF, CFO), business deaths, and emissions-related media coverage
- Mediators/Channels: Production-and-competition channel (incumbents scale back production, raise R&D/CAPEX, lose profitability); disclosure-and-information channel (proprietary facility-level data help entrants estimate demand and identify opportunities)
- Moderators: Industry carbon-emission reductions (CarbonDec), shareholder/public pressure (HighCCRisk, LowENV), proprietary-cost concerns (CommentLetters), and local facility density (LogNfac)

Using a differential-exposure difference-in-differences design, the authors find business births increase roughly 3-4% in GHGRP-affected industries after the 2012 public release of emissions data. Two mechanisms operate: mandatory disclosure pressures incumbents (via stakeholder, investor, and public scrutiny) to curb production and reallocate toward innovation, eroding their competitive position and opening market space; and the granular proprietary data disclosed (production volumes, technical inputs, cost structures) help prospective entrants identify profitable opportunities. Entry effects are stronger where incumbents actually cut emissions, where firms face greater scrutiny, and where industries raised proprietary-cost objections.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the real-effects-of-disclosure literature by shifting attention from incumbents to new business formation, documenting an unintended consequence of carbon-disclosure mandates: increased entry whose emissions may partially offset incumbents' reductions, implying that incumbent-only analyses overstate the net environmental benefit. It deepens understanding of the relation between incumbents' disclosure and new entry (extending work on financial-disclosure spillovers to GHG disclosure), and adds to the pollution-displacement literature by showing new entrants—not just incumbents—drive displacement, especially under exemption thresholds for small emitters.

**Practical Implication**
As governments expand climate-related disclosure regimes, policymakers should account for secondary effects on market structure and entrepreneurial activity: emissions-reduction credit attributed to disclosure mandates may be overestimated when new (sub-threshold) entrants are ignored, and the proprietary content of mandated disclosures can reshape competition. Designing effective, balanced disclosure policy requires attention to entry incentives and threshold exemptions for small emitters.

**Limitations**
The authors caution that evidence on the underlying channels should be interpreted as descriptive rather than causal, and that endogeneity cannot be fully ruled out because GHGRP implementation was not entirely exogenous. The analysis focuses on short-run frictions that create competitive openings and does not speak to longer-run efficiency gains from subsequent innovation. The industry-versus-local information comparison is acknowledged as conceptually and empirically difficult and should be read with caution.

**Future Research**
Future work could investigate the extent to which specific GHGRP disclosure components (e.g., inputs to emission equations) impose proprietary costs and influence competition, and could examine the longer-run efficiency and innovation consequences of disclosure-induced entry that the short-run design does not capture.
