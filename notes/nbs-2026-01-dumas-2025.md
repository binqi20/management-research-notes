# The Business–Social Paradox of ESG Investing: Responding to Persistent Tensions over Time

**Abstract**
Growing support for Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) investing over the past twenty years has not been matched by widespread implementation. Prior research has identified obstacles to ESG investing. However, little attention has been paid to its inherent paradoxical nature, to examine the persistent tensions involved and understand its complicated practical realisation. Studying why barriers to ESG investing endure offers valuable insights into how paradoxes persist over time, particularly when examining how these paradoxes interact with various response strategies. Over 32 months of field work, we collected observations of meetings, interviews and secondary data of institutional investors developing an ESG investment framework. Our analysis dissects the business–social paradox’s interconnected tensions linked to measurement, efficiency, and temporality. We identified four paradox response strategies—experimenting, ordering, anchoring, and shielding—that enabled the group to construct and work through multiple layers of complexity over time. Our findings advance ESG investing literature by theoretically reframing ESG investing into a paradoxical lens and showing how the mundane responses to paradoxical tensions, which shift over time, can support the development of more nuanced ESG tools. They also contribute to paradox theory by emphasizing the role of shielding as a dynamic enabler rather than a defensive strategy and by showing how responses gradually deepen over time.

**Research Question**
How do ESG investors, in their interactions with one another, respond to and construct persistent tensions over time to develop ESG investing tools?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: The business–social paradox, surfacing as three entangled tensions — measurement (quantitative metrics vs. expert judgment), efficiency (resource allocation vs. unproven materiality), and temporal (past indicators vs. future, short- vs. long-term).
- Paradox responses (practices): Experimenting (amplifying contradictions to spark creative solutions), ordering (structuring and defining the contradictory poles), anchoring (connecting the paradox to established external norms and principles), and shielding (parking and minimising to provide momentary relief).
- Process / phases: Three phases tracked the tool's evolution — Phase 1 "Cautious Discovery" (anchoring-dominant, measurement tension only), Phase 2 "Productive Frustration" (experimenting + shielding added, efficiency tension emerges), Phase 3 "Confident Dynamism" (rapid switching among all four responses, temporal tension surfaces, tensions become knotted).
- Emergent outcomes: Paradox "layered-ness" and "knotted-ness" — actors progressively construct the paradox in more complex ways, while the ESG tool grows from a fragile "strawman" (92 cells) into a comprehensive composite framework (~1,000 cells) combining metrics and narrative.

Over time the group moved from sequential, single responses to rapid, simultaneous combinations of all four strategies. Counterintuitively, members' confidence and comfort grew as the paradox's acknowledged complexity increased, suggesting a "learning spiral." Shielding, conventionally cast as a defensive response, instead created the conditions for sustained engagement and active experimentation, and the everyday "mundane" work of responding to the paradox yielded a more nuanced, ethical measurement tool that embraces rather than resolves contradiction.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study theorizes ESG investing through a paradox lens for the first time, shifting the focus from cataloguing and resolving obstacles to understanding how actors work through ESG's inherent business–social paradox over time. It contributes to paradox theory by extending "serial constructions of paradox" — showing how paradoxes are constructed in increasingly complex, layered, and knotted ways — and by reframing shielding as a dynamic enabler of long-term engagement rather than a defensive, vicious-cycle-inducing strategy, thereby challenging the binary of active "both/and" versus defensive responses.

**Practical Implication**
A paradox perspective offers practitioners a fresh framework for making more informed, ethical ESG decisions and may alleviate the frustrations they face. Because finance professionals tend to dismiss ESG's business–social paradox, the study highlights the importance of cultivating mindsets to embrace paradox and gaining experience with it — skills often neglected in financial education — to avoid downplaying tensions and developing inappropriate, reductive tools.

**Limitations**
The study focused on meetings and overlooked participants' activities between them, where noteworthy events might occur. The findings derive from a single collaborative, voluntary working group with little top-down control, which the authors note shapes the dynamics they observed.

**Future Research**
Future work could study settings without interruptions to examine subtle, less-active responses, and could test the framework's transferability to other business tools navigating CSR or strategy paradoxes and to more top-down or hierarchical settings. Researchers could further examine the mechanisms driving shifts between response types and elucidate how the "learning spiral" competencies for engaging paradox are cultivated at individual and collective levels.
