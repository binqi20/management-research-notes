# What Happens After Whistleblowing? A Systematic Literature Review of the Post-whistleblowing Phase

**Abstract**
In the wake of a rise in high-profile whistleblowing incidents, there has been a substantial increase in scholarly attention toward understanding the complexities of what happens after people have engaged in the act of whistleblowing. According to the current literature, this phase is often associated with significant harm for both the individuals who ‘blow the whistle’ as well as the organisations in which the wrongdoing has occurred. Given this real-world significance, together with the rapidly growing body of empirical research, a comprehensive review of the post-whistleblowing literature is warranted. The overall aim of the current review is to provide a state-of-the-art assessment of what is known about the post-whistleblowing phase and to identify how future research can advance current research and scholarship on whistleblowing. Specifically, we summarise information about current research trends and dominant theoretical perspectives evident in the post-whistleblowing literature; provide a categorisation of the different types of organisational wrongdoing; discuss the factors influencing, mediating, and moderating post-whistleblowing outcomes, and outline the methodological strengths and weaknesses of prior research. Finally, the current review highlights areas in critical need of development and paves the way for a more holistic understanding of the complete whistleblowing process, including a temporal perspective of the post-whistleblowing phase.

**Research Question**
The review is guided by three questions: (1) what theoretical and methodological paradigms have been used to study the post-whistleblowing phase of the whistleblowing process?; (2) how are different stakeholders impacted during the post-whistleblowing phase?; and (3) how does the post-whistleblowing phase unfold over time (i.e., is the process characterised by different stages and a temporal chain of events)?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core constructs: post-whistleblowing as a temporal process unfolding from short-term (immediate aftermath) to long-term outcomes for whistleblowers, wrongdoers, and other stakeholders.
- Influencing factors: organised into personal factors (demographic, personality, individual job-situation characteristics) and environmental factors (organisational, wrongdoing, and country/culture characteristics), with environmental — especially co-worker, supervisor, and top-management support — receiving most attention.
- Mediators/moderators (few studies): moral judgments mediate norms-to-ostracism intentions; distributor transaction-specific investments and market uncertainty moderate whistleblowing's effect on relationship quality; gender moderates influencing-factor-to-retaliation links.
- Outcome families (Vallerand's motivational consequences framework): cognitive, affective, and behavioural outcomes, predominantly negative (e.g., retaliation, dismissal, anxiety) but occasionally positive (e.g., policy reform, peace of mind).
- Dominant theoretical lenses: the prosocial organizational behavior (POB) model, resource dependency theory, and minority influence theory.

The review synthesises a fragmented empirical literature by mapping how organisational responses to disclosure (often retaliatory) interact with personal and environmental factors to produce a temporally evolving chain of outcomes for multiple stakeholders. It argues that proximal short-term outcomes lay the foundation for more severe distal long-term outcomes, and that power dynamics between whistleblower and wrongdoer — theorised via resource dependency and minority influence — shape the trajectory and severity of these consequences.

**Theoretical Contribution**
By focusing on the under-examined post-whistleblowing phase, the review provides a more holistic account of the complete whistleblowing process and integrates evidence on the responses of both whistleblowers and wrongdoers in the post-disclosure phase. It introduces a temporal perspective distinguishing short- and long-term outcomes and the interplay between proximal and distal consequences, and proposes additional theoretical frameworks (e.g., Job Demands-Resources, affective recognition, institutional, social identity, and corporate ethical virtues theories) to better bridge individual- and organisational-level post-whistleblowing experiences.

**Practical Implication**
A systematic assessment of post-disclosure responses generates insights into the legislation and policy needed to safeguard whistleblowers' rights after disclosure, and highlights national and cross-national variation in whistleblower protections. The authors call for strengthened practice-oriented research that enhances whistleblower legitimacy through improved legal protections and underscores corporate governance's role in combating financial crime.

**Limitations**
The reviewed evidence base is dominated by US-based, cross-sectional studies focused almost exclusively on the whistleblower's perspective (31 of 34 studies), introducing geographical, cultural, and stakeholder biases and limiting understanding of wrongdoers and other stakeholders. Considerable variation in how studies define short- and long-term, and the scarcity of longitudinal designs (only four of 34), constrain inferences about the temporal dynamics the review seeks to characterise.

**Future Research**
Future work should adopt longitudinal and temporally precise designs (e.g., panel and diary studies) with consistent reference periods, incorporate multi-source data capturing wrongdoers' and third parties' perspectives, and extend cross-cultural and cross-jurisdictional comparisons on a global scale. The authors set out a structured agenda (Table 3) pairing research questions with frameworks such as resource dependency, minority influence, Job Demands-Resources, corporate ethical virtues, social learning/cognitive, social identity, and institutional theory, and recommend mixed-method approaches and evaluations of the effectiveness of whistleblower protection laws.
