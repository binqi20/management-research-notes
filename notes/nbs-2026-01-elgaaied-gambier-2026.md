# Reframing Sustainable Consumption: Toward an Ethical Dilemma Perspective

**Abstract**
This conceptual study explores consumers’ ethical dilemmas inherent in the domain of sustainable consumption. The traditional approach to sustainable consumption tends to exclude individual interests and consumer well-being, implicitly assuming that sustainable consumption should be entirely altruistic and devoid of personal benefits. Using the triple bottom line framework adapted to the consumer level, we argue that, in addition to addressing social and environmental concerns, sustainable offerings should integrate self-profit as an equally important concern. From this perspective, sustainable consumption inherently entails dilemmas, as social, environmental, and individual benefits may conflict with one another. In this research, we propose examining sustainable consumption through the lens of ethical dilemmas, to shed light on the tensions involved in navigating these multifaceted issues. We identify nine types of ethical dilemmas related to sustainable consumption and illustrate them with concrete scenarios to demonstrate the applicability of our proposed classification. This study sets the stage for future research by highlighting key areas in which additional investigations are required to deepen understanding of ethical dilemmas and develop practical solutions for fostering sustainable consumption.

**Research Question**
How can sustainable consumption be reconceptualized once consumers' self-interest and personal well-being are admitted as a legitimate, co-equal concern alongside social and environmental concerns, and what ethical dilemmas does this consumer-level triple bottom line generate when those three dimensions conflict?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Traditional definitions frame sustainable consumption as purely altruistic and self-transcendent, treating restraint as individual sacrifice; this omission of personal benefit makes sustainability seem burdensome and helps explain the attitude–behavior "green gap."
- Key constructs (consumer-level TBL): three dimensions — planet (environmental harm reduction), people (social imperatives, rights, equality), and self-profit (the consumer's self-interest and personal well-being, extending the original "profit" pillar beyond economics).
- Boundary conditions: situations qualify as ethical dilemmas only when consumers are ethically aware, value the relevant concerns, and are not indifferent; the framing also presumes an atomistic, individual-level decision paradigm.
- Emergent outcomes: nine dilemma types arising from conflicts among (and within) the three dimensions, going beyond Elkington's three "shear zones" (e.g., Planet vs. self-profit, People vs. self-profit, Planet vs. Planet, Self-profit vs. self-profit), each illustrated with a binary-choice scenario.

By adapting the triple bottom line from the organizational to the individual level and adding self-profit as a third pillar, the paper reframes sustainable consumption as an ethical decision-making process in which planet, people, and self-profit can collide. Drawing on framing theory, the authors argue that how sustainability is defined and framed shapes consumer perceptions: surfacing personal benefits makes sustainable behavior more appealing and durable. A dynamic cognition–affect interplay (guilt, pride, empathy, anticipated regret) governs how consumers perceive and resolve these dilemmas.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study proposes a paradigm shift that incorporates consumers' self-interest and well-being as integral components of sustainable consumption rather than treating sustainability as strictly altruistic. It contributes to the sustainable-consumption literature by broadening its definition and underlying dimensions (planet, people, self-profit), and to transformative consumer research by foregrounding individual well-being. Reframing the phenomenon through ethical dilemmas yields a nine-type typology that complements, rather than replaces, attitudinal and affective accounts of the intention–behavior gap.

**Practical Implication**
Marketers and policymakers should identify the dilemmas their products or services trigger and adjust product attributes (e.g., bundling, flexible pricing, "green but affordable" options) to minimize or resolve them; surveys, focus groups, and analytics can reveal which dilemmas matter most and support segmentation. Because definitions shape evaluation metrics and advocacy, communication that highlights personal benefits and deconstructs the "sacrifice" framing can make sustainable consumption resonate with a broader audience.

**Limitations**
The framework adopts an individual-level, somewhat atomistic and rational perspective that may underweight social, cultural, emotional, and relational dynamics of consumption, and framing dilemmas through personal motivation risks downplaying the need for broader structural change toward sufficiency or degrowth. It also inherits well-known criticisms of the TBL (measurement difficulty, weak pillar integration, use as a compliance tool), and its scenarios apply best to high-involvement, slow decisions; concretizing environmental–social–individual attributes for low-involvement, signal-based choices is difficult.

**Future Research**
The authors outline five avenues: (1) assess individuals' propensity to experience dilemmas and build a consumer typology via scenario-based surveys; (2) explore consumers' underlying moral reasoning using think-aloud protocols; (3) assess downstream consequences such as dissonance, confidence, and longer-term behavior; (4) identify intervention strategies (communication, empowerment) for firms and policymakers, attending to institutional and market context; and (5) advance methodologies, including qualitative and neurocognitive/biometric approaches for greater ecological validity.
