# Do sufficiency consumption changes drive emissions down? A production network approach

**Abstract**
Our findings reveal significant rebound effects, up to 38 % for domestic emissions and 60 % for global emissions (accounting for carbon leakage), compared to estimates excluding behavioral aspects. Rebound effects from sufficiency strategies are smaller than those from energy efficiency improvements.

**Research Question**
What are the economy-wide emissions consequences of sufficiency-driven consumption changes once macroeconomic feedback effects are accounted for, and through which propagation channels in the production network do reductions in demand for emission-intensive goods actually translate into emissions reductions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Exogenous shocks to consumers' relative preference parameters (x_j) — a "Food" shock (shift to a vegetarian diet) and an "Energy" shock (reduced energy use); plus an efficiency-improvement shock for comparison.
- DV(s): Domestic (EU) and global GHG emissions, decomposed by sector; secondary outcomes are real GDP and carbon leakage to the Rest of the World.
- Mediators: Three propagation channels operating through the input–output (Leontief) network — (1) a direct demand effect with income reallocation, (2) a downstream price effect from decreasing-returns-to-scale, and (3) an upstream substitution effect.
- Moderators: Sector-specific elasticities of substitution and the structure of the underlying production network (covariance/overlap of supply chains); factor mobility (perfect reallocation vs. sector-specific primary factors).

A reduction in relative preference for an emission-intensive good lowers its relative demand and, when primary factors are sector-specific, its relative price; the price drop propagates downstream and can raise production of the very emission-intensive sectors that were targeted, generating a rebound, while substitution patterns (governed by elasticities below or above one) either magnify or dampen it. Because energy sectors occupy central, upstream network positions, a fall in energy prices spreads to all producers and drives positive carbon leakage abroad, whereas freed-up income under the Food shock is re-spent on other carbon-intensive goods (income rebound). The net emissions outcome thus depends jointly on the network structure, the carbon intensity of affected sectors, and the elasticities of substitution.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides what the authors describe as the first theoretical and numerical analysis of sufficiency-related consumption changes within a macroeconomic framework that retains a detailed microeconomic production structure. Its core contribution is disentangling three network-based propagation channels for preference shocks — a demand effect (consistent with existing input–output work) plus novel price and substitution effects derived analytically — and showing that these feedbacks, absent from standard LCA and IO studies, can be quantitatively large.

**Practical Implication**
Demand-side and integrated-assessment models that represent sufficiency as an exogenous reduction in baseline energy demand risk overstating its mitigation potential, because they omit price and substitution rebound effects that can reach 38% of domestic and 60% of global avoided emissions. Modelers and policymakers should endogenize these macroeconomic feedbacks and attend to elasticities of substitution when evaluating low-demand decarbonization pathways and designing complementary policy mixes.

**Limitations**
The framework does not specify the origin of the preference shocks (e.g., whether driven by nudges, information campaigns, or structural infrastructure change), limiting policy-specific conclusions. It is static and flow-based, abstracting from capital accumulation, savings, and investment dynamics, and it focuses on demand-side changes while disregarding endogenous production-side technological change. CES preferences with uniform income elasticity and historically calibrated, symmetric substitution elasticities are acknowledged as strong simplifications.

**Future Research**
The authors call for endogenizing a wider set of feedback effects within integrated assessment and macroeconomic models, incorporating more sophisticated (e.g., nonhomothetic CES) household-preference structures, and translating interdisciplinary evidence on low-carbon lifestyles into empirically grounded behavioral parameters. They also flag dynamic, stock-based extensions, endogenous production-technique change, a leisure-based reconceptualization of sufficiency, and — as a key step — investigating the policy drivers behind the behavioral changes modeled here.
