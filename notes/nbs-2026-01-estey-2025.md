# Toward a typology of boundaries in crisis management

**Abstract**
Why do so many organizations fail to respond to crises effectively, and what can be done to improve their crisis response? We aim to address these questions by developing a typology of boundaries that can help us understand and better prepare for future crises. Building on the existing literature on crisis management and boundaries, our typology seeks to address the critique of current crisis management models, such as these being too prescriptive and placing too much emphasis on assumptions that only the decisions made by the crisis management team (i.e., centralized decisions) will impact the crisis response. We address this critique by elaborating on four categories of boundaries that are relevant to manage crisis response, such as physical, mental, social, and temporal. Examples of these boundaries could include access to properties (physical), interpreting whether a crisis is happening (mental), shifting relationship dynamics (social), and the urgency of response time to a crisis (temporal). Our typology offers a lens to consider crisis management by taking into consideration the complexities and nuances that linear crisis management models have not yet adequately addressed. We argue that by identifying, defining, and understanding different configurations of boundaries, one can better manage crises toward a desired outcome.

**Research Question**
Why do so many organizations fail to respond to crises effectively, and what can be done to improve their crisis response? The paper asks how the concept of organizational boundaries can be used to understand and better prepare for crises in ways that linear, prescriptive crisis-management models have not.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: organizations are social constructs constituted by interacting and intersecting boundaries; locating, defining, and managing those boundaries yields a more holistic view of the organization that improves crisis decision-making toward a desired outcome.
- Key constructs (boundary definition): a boundary is "a permeable element between two or more concepts or units that supports or impedes flows across heterogeneous spaces," characterized by four core attributes — permeability, ambiguity, multifacetedness, and dynamism — each existing on a continuum.
- Four boundary types: physical (material/regulatory; order and location), social (relational interactions among internal/external stakeholders), mental (understanding, sensemaking, groupthink, mental models), and temporal (time-bound and time-sensitive aspects of a crisis).
- Two dimensions: intentional (consciously planned/managed) vs. unintentional (unplanned byproducts), yielding eight possible boundary configurations.
- Boundary relations: boundaries coexist, intersect, and interact, producing compounding "ripple effects" in which a change to one boundary creates, reinforces, transcends, or dissolves others.
- Boundary conditions: it is impossible to know with certainty how individuals or groups will react in a crisis or which boundaries will come into play; centralized and decentralized decision-making are theorized as occurring simultaneously across all organizational levels.

The central process is that crises perturb an organization's pre-crisis configuration of boundaries (aligned with Hernes' loose/medium/tight boundary configurations), and the reinforcement, transcendence, or dissolution of any one boundary cascades through the others. By making these shifting boundaries identifiable and definable, practitioners can anticipate ripple effects and enact boundary-management actions that steer a non-linear, dispersed crisis response toward a favorable resolution rather than relying on prescriptive stage models.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions: it imports the boundary-management literature into crisis management to address the limitations of overly rational, linear stage models; it bridges the internal-external divide in most crisis-management frameworks (Bundy et al., 2017) by treating within-organization dynamics and stakeholder/environment interactions through a single boundary lens; and it advances the boundary literature itself by theorizing boundaries and organizations as inseparable, mutually constituting entities, offering a definition centered on what a boundary *is* (permeable element) rather than what it does.

**Practical Implication**
By identifying and locating the physical, social, mental, and temporal boundaries present in a crisis, practitioners can make educated guesses about the ripple effects of boundary changes and enact boundary-management actions (reinforcing, weakening, or dissolving boundaries) to guide the response toward a desired outcome. Tools such as "war gaming" crisis plans and post-crisis reflective practice can surface and resolve boundary tensions before or after a crisis, and managers are advised to treat centralized and decentralized decision-making as operating simultaneously rather than as command-and-control alone.

**Limitations**
The typology is conceptual and is demonstrated only through a single illustrative case of a weather-related crisis at a US summer camp; it has not been empirically tested. The authors note that it is inherently impossible to know with certainty which boundaries will arise in a crisis or how actors will react, and that unintentional boundaries are difficult to predict and manage, constraining the predictive power of the framework.

**Future Research**
The authors call for refining the typology through empirical data collection, including manipulation of specific boundaries toward a set goal and intervention studies; developing an actionable decision-making model to help practitioners identify needed boundary-management actions; examining the long-term implications of boundary shifts (whether boundaries return to pre-crisis states or become a "new normal," and consequences for stakeholder management); and testing whether the typology applies to organizing beyond crisis contexts more generally.
