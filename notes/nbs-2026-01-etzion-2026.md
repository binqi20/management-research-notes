# Good Data, Good Questions: Leveraging Comprehensive, Direct-Observation Data Sets for Impactful Research on Organizations and the Environment

**Abstract**
Much research on organizations and the natural environment relies on self-reported corporate data of dubious reliability and accuracy. A new wave of data, originating in satellite observations, forensic genetics, hydrological maps and other sources, can help overcome these shortcomings. We identify several data sets from the natural sciences that are easily accessible and highly accurate, and provide roadmaps for management researchers to utilize them. We further explore the types of research questions these data sets enable, extending beyond the confines of environmental-financial performance relationships that have dominated previous research. By leveraging these and similar data sets, corporate sustainability researchers can obtain clearer insights into corporate environmental impact, opening doors to new avenues of inquiry.

**Research Question**
How can comprehensive, direct-observation data sets originating in the natural sciences be identified, assessed for quality, and harnessed by organizations-and-the-natural-environment (ONE) scholars to study the actual environmental impacts of corporate actions on local and planetary biophysical systems?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: ONE research has drifted toward economic performance and intermediated ESG ratings, yielding "partial and imprecise information about the direct impact organizations have on biophysical systems"; direct-observation data sets can re-anchor the field on impact.
- Key constructs / data sources: five exemplar data sets spanning water (HydroBASINS, Aqueduct, French Naïades/Hydro), forests and fire (INPE DETER/Queimadas), ocean salinity (NASA Aquarius), GHG emissions (Climate TRACE), and timber-trade provenance (ILAT, InsideWood/POWO/Tree of Life).
- Assessment framework: each data set is evaluated on accessibility, intrinsic quality, contextual relevance, and interpretability.
- Boundary conditions: attribution/attributability of biophysical change to specific firms, plus contextual relevance and interpretability, are the recurring hurdles; longitudinal and quasi-experimental designs, narrowed settings, and facility-to-parent aggregation are the proposed remedies.

By organizing natural-science data along organizational units and merging it geographically with firm activity, ONE scholars can shift the dependent variables they study from disclosed intent to observed environmental impact. The article frames this not merely as methodological innovation but as an "epistemological turn toward materiality" that lets the field test theories of organizational adaptation, ecological feedback, and system resilience grounded in biophysical properties.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The article argues that integrating comprehensive direct-observation biophysical data can transform the field's central constructs—corporate environmental performance, stakeholder salience, sustainability strategy—"from interpretive estimates to empirically grounded measures," redefining the theories the field can build "from those centered on disclosure and intent to those centered on impact and causality." It positions data work as an entry point to radical interdisciplinarity and re-anchors ONE in its founding ambition to study organizations as part of, not apart from, biophysical systems.

**Practical Implication**
ONE researchers are urged to invest effort in discovering, understanding, and merging openly available natural-science data sets, to collaborate with domain experts (e.g., environmental scientists) to specify control variables and attribution, and to favor longitudinal or quasi-experimental designs and tightly circumscribed settings where impacts are easier to attribute to specific firms.

**Limitations**
The article is an agenda-setting overview rather than an empirical study; its set of data sets and research questions is explicitly "far from exhaustive." It also cautions that alternative natural-science data sets are not a panacea and can themselves suffer from faulty observations, inconsistency over time, and poor weighting, and that several (e.g., Climate TRACE) capture only direct Scope 1 emissions or single points in time.
