# Competing With Smart Machines: The dark side of ‘conjoined agency’ in contemporary organizations

**Abstract**
While many are concerned about artificial intelligence (AI) enabled automation and the threat of mass technological unemployment, this paper contributes to a stream of organizational research that suggests semi-automation is a more probable scenario. A growing subtheme in this stream emphasizes ‘conjoined agency’, where employees collaborate with smart machines rather than be subordinated by them. In this paper, we argue that conjoined agency has a hitherto unexamined dark side. Building on an in-depth qualitative study of passenger train drivers and their managers in the United Kingdom, we demonstrate how drivers require taxing levels of stamina to successfully work with algorithms and semi-automated train systems. This is exacerbated by what we call an ideology of automation, notably threats from management that driverless trains are imminent. Drivers feel compelled to compete with these prospective robots. We term this human robotic mimesis, where workers attempt (but often fail) to perform like robots. By exploring this dark side of conjoined agency, we propose that it isn’t only the practical application of new automating technologies that is shaping organizational life but their ideological evocation within specific power relationships.

**Research Question**
How do conjoined agency, management by algorithm, and symbolic impression management play out among frontline workers when the threat of full automation is salient? The paper asks why UK passenger train drivers covertly urinate in bottles despite contractual rest breaks, using this puzzle to surface a "dark side" of human-machine collaboration.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: conjoined agency (shared human-machine intentionality); the "ideology of automation" (managerial threats of future technological unemployment that shape present power relations); human robotic mimesis (workers performing like robots).
- Triggering conditions: semi-automated cabin technologies (DSDs, "downloads," algorithmically calculated "diagrams"); combative industrial relations and managerial invocation of imminent driverless trains.
- Process / phases: first-order mimesis (robotic behaviour intrinsic to the labour process), second-order mimesis (self-imposed bodily bioregulation of food, drink, and toilet needs), third-order mimesis (symbolic signalling to a managerial audience that humans can match ATO).
- Emergent outcomes: bodily strain, fatigue, and covert workarounds (e.g., urinating in bottles) that sustain a symbolic impression of machine-like efficiency.

Drivers, faced with an antagonistic ideology of automation, treat their own bodies as machines to signal that they can perform as well as or better than the robots management claims will replace them. Because human bodies have biological limits, drivers conceal those limits through backstage "body work" rather than concede the superiority of driverless trains, exposing how power relations and ideology — not just the technology itself — shape the labour/technology interface.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the conjoined-agency framework by revealing a previously unexamined dark side: under asymmetrical power relations, semi-automation can produce roboticization of human labour rather than enrichment. It introduces "human robotic mimesis" (in three orders) and the "ideology of automation" as a mode of power, and foregrounds the embodied, visceral dimension ("cyber corporeality" / "virtual viscerality") largely absent from the disembodied management-by-algorithm literature.

**Practical Implication**
The study suggests managers and policymakers should recognize that invoking imminent full automation as a disciplinary threat imposes hidden bodily and psychological costs on skilled workers, degrading dignity, security, and safety (e.g., drivers making unsafe decisions to avoid stops). It implies that surveillance algorithms and unrealistic efficiency "diagrams" should account for embodied human needs.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The authors propose examining how semi-automation reshapes workers' subjective and cognitive qualities (political awareness, identity, ethical deliberation, intuition) even when jobs are not eliminated; how the ideology of automation functions as a mode of power and how workers resist it across occupations; and pursuing more critical, ethical, and speculative inquiry into who wins and loses as the "fourth industrial revolution" unfolds.
