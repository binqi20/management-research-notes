# Entrepreneurship and Gentrification

**Abstract**
experiencing startup entry calls for deeper reflection on the role of entrepreneurship in inequality and the sustainability of entrepreneurial ecosystems.

**Research Question**
How does growth-oriented entrepreneurship influence the neighborhoods in which startups locate — specifically, does startup entry initiate and propel the demographic, organizational, and real-estate-price changes characteristic of gentrification, and who captures the resulting value?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Growth-oriented entrepreneurship (count of startups founded in a ward over three-year windows, t-1 to t-3, from Crunchbase; VentureXpert-based VC measure as alternative)
- DV(s): Neighborhood demographic shares (white-/blue-collar, young, Black, low education); industry employment shares; share of medium-/high-priced and ethnic restaurants; annual real housing-price growth
- Mediators: Not modeled formally; argued channels are job creation, amenity upgrading, and legitimation/signaling
- Moderators: Initial housing-price level (price decile) and neighborhood home-ownership share (renting vs. owning)

Startups transform neighborhoods through three reinforcing channels: they create local jobs that draw younger, more educated, higher-income people; this influx raises demand for upscale amenities (trendier, more diverse restaurants and retail); and clusters of startups act as a signal and confer legitimacy and place labels (e.g., "Silicon Roundabout") that attract further entrepreneurs, businesses, and residents. These dynamics form a self-reinforcing cycle of renewal that bids up residential real estate prices, especially in neighborhoods that began with low prices — but because appreciation concentrates in high-renting areas, the gains accrue largely to landlords rather than to displaced long-term residents.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper identifies growth-oriented entrepreneurship as a distinct, more emergent and less centrally planned form of gentrification, contrasting with the capital-infusion and municipal-policy accounts that dominate the gentrification literature. By documenting microgeographic links between startup entry and neighborhood change, it extends work on the local economic and social consequences of entrepreneurship, surfacing an additional channel through which entrepreneurship generates a tension between economic growth and economic inequality via spatial sorting within cities.

**Practical Implication**
Although the job creation and price appreciation associated with startup clusters may tempt politicians and urban planners to promote growth-oriented entrepreneurship, these transformations impose uneven costs: rising rents displace longstanding residents and older businesses, and renters (the majority in the most-affected areas) do not share in the capital gains. Policymakers should weigh distributional consequences and the long-run affordability and sustainability of entrepreneurial ecosystems.

**Limitations**
London represents but a single case with its own idiosyncrasies, limiting generalizability. The data are at the neighborhood (ward) level and do not allow household-level measurement of home ownership or identification of who fills the new jobs or where displaced residents go. Crunchbase data are crowdsourced with uncertain provenance (partly mitigated by a VentureXpert-based measure), and the price measure does not adjust for year-to-year quality variation in the properties sold.

**Future Research**
The authors call for individual-level data to determine who benefits from these revitalizations and where displaced residents relocate, and for cross-city designs comparing places with varying degrees of entrepreneurship-driven gentrification to assess net city-wide effects. They also urge further study of the property-price/entrepreneurship relationship, including whether rising prices eventually undermine competitiveness and deter the next generation of entrepreneurs ("Silicon Valley syndrome").
