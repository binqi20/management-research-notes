# “If I Accomplish Nothing Else but Hope, That Will be Enough”: The Ethics and Morality of Market-Based Hope in Illness Entrepreneurship

**Abstract**
How do entrepreneurs embody and enact hope as a motor for ethical agency—agency that builds one's own and others' capacities and capabilities to overcome a painful present? This study puts hope at the center of theorizing around the activities of entrepreneurs who leverage it both as a way to sketch a new life for themselves and to sell it to others. Through the case of illness entrepreneurs—recovery coaches for those suffering from chronic illnesses—we observe the moral struggles involved when hope becomes framed within the logic of the market. Recovery coaches at once embody hope (as formerly sick people who have recovered), neatly package and sell it (as entrepreneurs), and manifest it (opening up new spaces of possibility for themselves). The work involved in balancing these threads requires constant realignment of the entrepreneurs' own hopes for a new life and their care for others. We show how, through these moral struggles, hope opens up new spaces of possibility and imbues people—both entrepreneurs and their clients—with the agency to move toward those new horizons. Conceptualizing the complex entanglements of hope, care, and ethical agency, our study represents a foray into recent calls for assembling and theorizing the 'architecture of hope' in organization and entrepreneurship studies. We also discuss whether this entrepreneurship, balancing hope and care to enable ethical agency, opens up new ways of understanding morality and agency in market contexts.

**Research Question**
How do entrepreneurs embody and enact hope as a motor for ethical agency—agency that builds one's own and others' capacities and capabilities to overcome a painful present? The authors further ask whether marketized hope can foster people's agency rather than trapping them in dependency, and whether the individual moral struggles of illness entrepreneurs can collectively lead to a more 'moral' market.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: hope (embodied, affective, future-oriented), care ('caring with' others), and ethical agency (building one's own and others' capacities to overcome a painful present); the central tension between self-agency and other-agency.
- Triggering conditions: biographical disruption from chronic illness (ME/CFS / Long Covid) and the perceived failure, carelessness, and false hope of mainstream medical markets, prompting former sufferers to become 'accidental' recovery-coach entrepreneurs.
- Process / phases: leaving a painful present behind → embodying hope to open spaces of possibility for others → enacting hope through self-agency (pursuing 'alignment' and a livelihood) → balancing self- and other-agency → moral struggles around marketizing and pricing hope.
- Practices: 'dosing' hope to avoid feeding 'cruel optimism' or magical thinking; flexible/sliding-scale pricing as a duty of care; bricolage of coaching, group programs, and digital content; insisting clients pay 'something' to stay 'invested.'
- Emergent outcomes: a precarious, individualized 'moral market' / 'ecology of hope' that fosters ethical agency yet remains unlikely to produce systemic change.

Through repeated moral struggles, recovery coaches realign their own hopes for a new life with their care for fellow sufferers, using hope as a 'guardrail' that gives care the impetus to move people toward new spaces of possibility. The authors theorize four interacting 'building blocks' of an architecture of hope—pain/vulnerability, care, ethical agency, and spaces of possibility—arguing that ethical agency is the 'fuel' that turns hope into forward movement, and that care and hope are most powerful when co-existent and aligned.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances two conceptual contributions: first, a new way of conceptualizing 'morality' in markets—particularly markets where shared pain, suffering, or vulnerability provide the foundation for market encounters—by arguing that the morality of marketized care services ought to include ethical agency. Second, it traces the first contours of an 'ethics of hope,' analyzing how care, hope, and ethical agency work together to move people out of painful presents toward new spaces of possibility. It extends Johansson and Wickström's (2023) definition of ethical agency from 'care for others' to a continuous balancing act between self- and other-agency, contributing to recent calls to assemble an 'architecture of hope' (Hudson et al., 2025) in organization and entrepreneurship studies.

**Practical Implication**
The findings illuminate how entrepreneurs operating in care markets (wellness, lifestyle, craft, and medical adjacencies) can 'dose' hope honestly—opening pathways while being transparent about their uncertainty—to avoid the 'cruel optimism' and magical thinking that exploit vulnerable buyers. They highlight fair-pricing and access practices (sliding scales, scholarships, group formats) as expressions of an ethics of care, and surface the limits of individualized market solutions, suggesting that genuine systemic relief for chronic-illness sufferers may require structural supports (reimbursement, social welfare) rather than markets alone.

**Limitations**
The authors caution against romanticizing the market's practices: they cannot exclude that recovered sufferers may become 'cut-throat and selfish' rather than caring entrepreneurs, and they have no way to determine whether the hope generated was not also 'cruel' or whether coaching helped people more than other 'merchants of hope.' Hope in recovery is highly personal and often spiritual—hope, belief, and magical thinking are frequently inseparable—and its marketization almost always targets the already-vulnerable, complicating any claim about the market's morality.

**Future Research**
More research is needed in other settings of markets of hope to put their morality to the test, and to study entrepreneurs motivated by very different conceptions of the spaces of possibility their entrepreneurship opens up (across illness, wellness, and lifestyle entrepreneurship). Future studies should continue building out the 'architecture of hope' in other cases and communities, delineate the relationship between an ethics of care and an ethics of hope, and be especially sensitive to the embodied and affective nature of hope—asking what a body can do when it becomes hopeful.
