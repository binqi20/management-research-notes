# Nourishing sustainability innovation: Scientific trajectories in industrial protein research

**Abstract**
These findings contribute to a view of science as a cognitive map that structures industrial research by making more explicit the underlying causal mechanisms that govern each trajectory, thereby shaping which domains are more likely to yield impactful and sustainabilityaligned innovations.

**Research Question**
Which scientific trajectories in protein research are most likely to shape follow-on innovation, and to what extent are they associated with sustainability-oriented outcomes? The paper asks how upstream science structures the technological search space for industrial R&D in the alternative-protein sector.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Four scientific trajectories derived from machine-learning topic modeling and mapped to patents — TRAJECTORY A_ANIMAL (animal-based protein), B_CELLULAR (cellular and bioprocessing), C_CHEMISTRY (protein chemistry / bioactive compounds), D_STRUCTURE (structure and stability) — coded as patent-level dummies, plus their interactions and Early/Late (split at 2003) temporal splits.
- DV(s): Forward-citation counts as a proxy for follow-on innovation (IMPACT), decomposed into sustainability-linked citations (IMPACT SDG) and non-sustainability citations (IMPACT NON-SDG).
- Mediators: None modeled (mechanism argued conceptually via codification, generality, and absorptive capacity).
- Moderators: Time period (Early vs. Late trajectory stage) and cross-trajectory interactions (notably C × D).

The paper theorizes science as a cognitive map (Fleming and Sorenson, 2004) layered onto Dosi's (1982) paradigm–trajectory distinction: trajectories grounded in codified, abstract, modular knowledge (Trajectory B, science-push) generalize and recombine across domains, producing broad and SDG-aligned spillovers, whereas trajectories rooted in tacit, context- and firm-specific knowledge (Trajectory C, demand-pull) remain locked into narrow use cases with weak diffusion. Higher codification raises third parties' absorptive capacity, enabling cumulative learning and systemic recombination, while application-bound knowledge confines learning within firm or sectoral boundaries. PPML estimates confirm B_CELLULAR is positively and C_CHEMISTRY negatively associated with forward citations, with magnitudes larger for SDG-related citations and effects that shift across early and late periods.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper refines the sustainability-innovation literature by showing that scientific trajectories differ substantially in their contribution to technological impact and SDG-aligned outcomes, challenging the assumption that scientific knowledge uniformly enhances impact (Fleming and Sorenson, 2004; Arora and Gambardella, 1994). It reframes science not as a monolithic enabler or mere source of citations but as an epistemic infrastructure whose influence depends on how codified knowledge is structured and embedded across domains, clarifying the link between scientific paradigms and technological trajectories (Dosi, 1982). Methodologically, it advances text-based innovation research with a hybrid approach combining unsupervised topic modeling of publications with supervised classification of patent texts.

**Practical Implication**
For innovation policy, the evaluation of public science should move beyond traditional academic-output metrics to consider science's capacity to shape industrial innovation paths, which is especially crucial in sustainability-intensive sectors where alignment between scientific abstraction and industrial application can determine the success of long-term transitions. The science-based-dictionary classification offers firms, investors, and policymakers a toolkit to visualize where innovation is scientifically grounded and where strategic recombination may unlock additional value.

**Limitations**
The study focuses exclusively on USPTO data, which may not fully capture global patterns of sustainability innovation or regional differences in regulatory pressures. The patent-level focus improves consistency but abstracts from firm-level strategies and organizational routines that may further shape trajectory evolution. Finally, the keyword-matching classification of SDG-related patents, while validated, may not fully capture the complexity of sustainability intent.

**Future Research**
Future work should expand the framework across sectors and countries to test the generalizability of trajectory-based patterns, including comparative studies in sustainability-critical domains such as renewable energy, circular materials, or decarbonized mobility. Fieldwork involving interviews or ethnographic studies with inventors could illuminate how firms interpret and operationalize scientific knowledge, and introducing country-level regulatory contexts would reveal the institutional contingencies of scientific trajectories. Linking trajectory patterns to firm-level outcomes such as profitability or ESG performance could bridge epistemic structure with organizational impact.
