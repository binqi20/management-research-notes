# The living lab and the cursed catalyst: Navigating the legitimacy challenges of innovation intermediaries for sustainable innovation

**Abstract**
We identify three roles—technological expert, stakeholder orchestrator, and end-user empowerer—each corresponding to one dimension of legitimacy (instrumental, relational, and moral). When combined, these roles create synergies that endow the living lab with strong active legitimacy, but also trigger dis-synergies that obscure the categorical cues required for passive cognitive legitimacy.

**Research Question**
"How do the roles assumed by living labs intersect with organisational legitimacy in sustainability projects involving frontier technologies?" The paper foregrounds legitimacy as an under-examined factor in how innovation intermediaries reconcile competing stakeholder expectations and sustainability goals.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: organisational legitimacy decomposed into instrumental, relational, and moral (active) judgments versus cognitive (passive, taken-for-granted) legitimacy; categorical identity / categorical cues.
- Roles (practices): technological expert (delivering technological advisory; designing rapidly implementable solutions); stakeholder orchestrator (engaging diverse stakeholders; harmonising goals; employing facilitation methods); end-user empowerer (building user competencies; integrating user needs; ensuring local compatibility).
- Synergies: each pair of roles is mutually reinforcing, accelerating experimentation, aligning heterogeneous interests, and strengthening trust — together producing strong active legitimacy and project success.
- Dis-synergies: performing all three roles simultaneously draws on the same scarce resources and blurs the lab's categorical identity, weakening cognitive legitimacy.
- Emergent outcome: the "Cursed Catalyst" — an imbalanced legitimacy portfolio in which active legitimacy accumulates while passive cognitive legitimacy stagnates, ultimately leading to the lab's exclusion from later project phases.

The case shows that the same role pluralism that lets an intermediary catalyse collective innovation prevents it from being recognised as a clearly bounded organisation. Because cognitive legitimacy depends on shared categorical mental shortcuts about "what the entity is," excelling across multiple roles paradoxically renders the lab's contribution invisible and its identity ambiguous. Drawing on Tost's (2011) active-versus-passive legitimacy logics and Bitektine et al.'s (2025) functional theory, the authors argue legitimacy is not a cumulative, sequential ladder but a delicate equilibrium whose dimensions can evolve in divergent—even opposing—directions, so functional effectiveness is necessary but not sufficient for survival.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances organisational legitimacy theory by showing that legitimacy dimensions can move in divergent or opposing directions, challenging the classical view of legitimacy as a cumulative, sequential ladder culminating in taken-for-grantedness. It contributes the "Cursed Catalyst" concept to the living labs and innovation-intermediary literatures, repositioning legitimacy—rather than functional prowess or structural design—as the pivotal challenge intermediaries face, and answering calls (e.g., Kivimaa et al., 2019) for a less idealised account of intermediaries in sustainability transitions.

**Practical Implication**
Living-lab managers should guard against the Cursed Catalyst by deliberately foregrounding or staging the pursuit of cognitive legitimacy: maintaining a clear, shared understanding of the lab's roles, negotiating longer-term or framework contracts, embedding explicit role descriptions and milestones, and issuing regular dashboards/briefs that make multi-role contributions visible. Curatively, managers need mid-term legitimacy "health checks" and reflective debriefs to detect under-recognition even when projects appear successful. Partners and policymakers should structure contracts and funding across the full project life-cycle and institutionalise living labs (shared guidelines, labelling/accreditation) to reduce category ambiguity.

**Limitations**
The findings rest on a single case study, limiting generalisability; comparative case studies and configurational methods (e.g., fsQCA) are needed to test the conditions that trigger the phenomenon. The MOSIS project was shaped by developed–developing-country collaboration dynamics that exacerbate power asymmetries and raise questions about whether a France-based living lab can fully understand local constraints and empower African end-users. The qualitative design also lacks the more objective, quantitative effectiveness measures that surveys or performance metrics could provide.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how legitimacy components interact dynamically across project phases, especially through comparative studies and methods capable of identifying micro-level legitimacy processes and their evolution. Researchers should test how the Cursed Catalyst phenomenon manifests in other types of innovation intermediaries and identify the boundary conditions under which intermediaries can reconcile catalytic effectiveness with the cognitive anchoring needed for long-term survival, complementing qualitative insight with quantitative assessment.
