# The Crisis of Dignity at Work: Dehumanization during Digital Transformation, Societal Upheaval, and Workforce Uncertainty

**Abstract**
Today, we witness unprecedented threats to dignity at work on a global scale, precipitated by workforce reductions, demeaning of public sector professionals and their contributions to society, funding withdrawal from important agencies and initiatives, and program closures at such a scale not thought possible until recently. This is putting progress for everything at risk, from environmental justice (access to clean water and air), to programs for the differently-abled and marginalized communities, to basic research on life-saving medical therapies. Many of these disruptions have been justified based on the promise that automation and artificial intelligence (AI) can substitute for the work of humans. In this guidepost, we ask, at what cost to society? And what can organizational scholars do about this? We call for research that rehumanizes work, recognizes the potential dark side of automation and AI, and reinstates hope and dignity during digital transformation.

**Research Question**
At what cost to society are workforce disruptions justified by the promise that automation and AI can substitute for human work, and what can organizational scholars do to protect dignity during digital transformation? The authors ask how research, policy, and practice can rehumanize work, recognize the dark side of automation and AI, and reinstate hope and dignity.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Justified by an unsubstantiated promise of AI/automation efficiencies, mass layoffs and devaluation of human capital are eroding dignity at work, producing disposability, disrespect, and alienation.
- Pathway 1 (From Dehumanization to Humanization): recognize and celebrate uniquely human capabilities (e.g., authentic empathy) that AI cannot replicate.
- Pathway 2 (From Ubiquity to Curvilinearity): investigate the potential dark side and diminishing returns of pervasive AI use on human cognitive and emotional capabilities.
- Pathway 3 (From Hopeless and Undignified to Hopeful and Dignified): apply hope theory, transparency, and professional identity refocusing to restore agency, hope, and dignity.

The guidepost theorizes that digital transformation, when framed solely around cost savings and efficiencies, detaches employees from meaning and purpose and signals their expendability, producing humiliation and ego threat. Drawing on the authors' science of dignity at work and on hope theory (Snyder et al., 1991) — which holds that hope develops when perceptions of agency and a pathway are present — they argue that leader development, transparency, and identity refocusing can rehumanize work and re-instill hope across a growing digital divide.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The piece extends the authors' integrative "dignity at work" framework to the context of digital transformation, societal upheaval, and workforce uncertainty, articulating detractors and promoters of dignity as AI and automation diffuse. It introduces hope theory as a lens for restoring agency and dignity and proposes a curvilinear (point-of-diminishing-returns) view of AI use as an empirical research agenda for the field.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders and organizations should adopt humane layoff and dismissal processes, make employees feel valued and their work meaningful, and pursue algorithmic and agent transparency to preserve human agency. Building AI as an integrated capability that creates sustainable value — rather than a bolted-on efficiency play — and supporting professional identity refocusing can protect hope and dignity during transformation.

**Future Research**
The authors call for empirical exploration of three pathways: identifying and harnessing uniquely human capabilities, uncovering the long-term and potentially curvilinear effects of AI use on human cognition, thriving, and the customer experience, and examining how leaders, transparency, hope-building, and professional identity refocusing can cultivate dignity across stages of transformation.
