# Carbon accounting quality: Measurement and the role of assurance

**Abstract**
We examine whether external assurance improves the quality of firms’ carbon accounting.

**Research Question**
Does external, third-party assurance improve the quality of firms’ carbon accounting — i.e., the extent to which reported emissions reflect a firm’s actual emissions — and through what mechanism?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Assurance (scope-specific indicator = 1 if a firm’s Scope s emissions are assured in year t); Post Assurance indicator; assurance thoroughness (limited vs. reasonable; some vs. all scopes assured); assuror type (financial auditor vs. non-financial assuror); Mandatory × Post (NFRD)
- DV(s): Carbon accounting quality, proxied primarily by Abnormal Emissions (absolute residual from a regression of logged scope-level emissions on production-output and production-technology determinants with industry-varying coefficients and industry-year fixed effects); triangulated with Manager Uncertainty and Filing Lag from CDP
- Mediators: Carbon-accounting-system indicators along the causal chain — Issues Identified, Omissions, and Revisions
- Moderators: Ex ante strength of the firm’s carbon accounting system (EPA GHGRP coverage, CDP reporting, scope-specific emissions reduction targets); thoroughness of the assurance engagement

The paper adapts Dechow et al.’s (2010) earnings-quality framework to carbon reporting, arguing that reported emissions deviate from actual emissions because of measurement-standard flaws and implementation issues; because the GHG Protocol is near-universal, the authors focus on implementation issues. Third-party assurance is theorized to improve quality primarily by identifying implementation issues (e.g., omitted sources, incorrect conversion factors) that firms then remediate, yielding fewer omissions and more revisions of prior disclosures rather than by addressing managerial misreporting incentives. The effect is predicted to be larger where pre-existing systems are weak and where assurance is more thorough.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to the ESG-reporting-quality literature by introducing a novel, vendor-independent, scope-specific measure of emissions reporting quality (abnormal emissions) that evaluates the accuracy of reported emissions rather than the breadth of disclosure. It extends the literature on the voluntary adoption of verification services by providing direct evidence that assurance — even predominantly “limited” assurance — shapes reporting quality, and it informs the broader audit literature by examining the reporting consequences of audit/assurance adoption rather than variation within mandatory regimes.

**Practical Implication**
For investors, regulators, and other stakeholders, the abnormal-emissions measure offers a tool to assess carbon disclosure quality independent of data-vendor coverage. The findings suggest that requiring or encouraging emissions assurance — even limited assurance — can improve carbon accounting quality, which is directly relevant to looming mandates such as California SB 253 and the E.U. Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive.

**Limitations**
The regression-based abnormal-emissions measure is subject to model misspecification and measurement error, and the authors explicitly do not claim it is a definitive measure of carbon accounting quality. The study does not establish causality for the voluntary-assurance results; although supplementary and NFRD difference-in-differences tests support a causal interpretation, endogeneity concerns cannot be fully ruled out. The emissions model relies on financial-statement data, so differences between carbon-reporting and financial-consolidation boundaries may add noise.

**Future Research**
Future work could build on or develop alternative measures of carbon accounting quality to corroborate and compare inferences across measures. Researchers could explore whether managerial incentives drive manipulated emissions reporting and whether assurance effects are partly driven by such incentives, and could revisit the effects of assurance mandates (e.g., California SB 253, the E.U. CSRD) as carbon reporting and assurance practices mature.
