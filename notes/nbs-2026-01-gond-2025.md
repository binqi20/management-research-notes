# Making Better Uses of Classics? Evaluating Critically the Legacy of Howard R. Bowen’s 1953 Social Responsibilities of the Businessman (1953–2023)

**Abstract**
Problematizing the use of classics as core to the advancement of scholarly fields, we analyze the purpose underlying the citation of SRB (why it is cited) as well as the breadth (what is cited) and the depth (how much is being discussed) of scholarly engagement with this classic. Combining analytical induction, quantification and a critical citation context analysis of 435 citations from 320 articles citing SRB between 1953 and 2023, we identified three main categories of use—arguing, defining, and landmarking—and evaluated the depth and breadth of such scholarly uses of SRB. Our results reveal how these uses differ across disciplinary domains and over time. Our analysis of breadth and depth confirms the continuous superficial engagement with SRB. We rely on this case to conceptualize the paradoxical legacy of Bowen, discuss ethical issues inherent to the politics of citation and evaluate what ‘fair’ uses of classics could be.

**Research Question**
For what purpose do scholars use foundational works (RQ1)? Have scholars ignored valuable ideas in foundational works (RQ2)? And to what extent have scholars disregarded the theories and arguments of classics because of their ritualized citation (RQ3)? The paper investigates these questions through the case of Howard R. Bowen's 1953 *Social Responsibilities of the Businessman* (SRB).

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: "arguing" (mobilizing SRB to build or back an argument), "defining" (relying on SRB's "page 6" definition to specify CSR or related concepts), and "landmarking" (using SRB or Bowen to signal the historical/conceptual origin of the field); the tension between formal recognition and informal neglect of a classic.
- Process / analytic layers: open coding of citation contexts into first-order codes, aggregation into second-order themes and three aggregate dimensions (Gioia method); then a quantification layer scoring per-article "breadth" (range of distinct first-order codes) and "depth" (1 = generic mention to 3 = substantive engagement).
- Triggering conditions: a classic's rising citation volume coupled with the "low-paradigm" status of business ethics, which keeps scholars reliant on classics rather than building theory from them.
- Emergent outcomes: landmarking dominates and rises over time (50–80% of citations since the late 1990s) while arguing—the deepest mode—declines; average breadth (1.12) and depth (1.33) stay low, confirming "obliteration by incorporation" of SRB's substance.

Combining citation context analysis with analytical induction lets the authors move beyond bibliometric counts to characterize *what being cited means*: a high citation count does not equal positive or substantive impact. By scoring breadth and depth and relating them to the three induced modes, they show that SRB has become a canonical reference point invoked for legitimacy rather than a source of conceptual renewal, with an ordinal logistic regression confirming that arguing citations are significantly deeper than landmarking or defining ones.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study makes a threefold contribution: it extends prior insights into the origins of CSR by theorizing the nuanced, paradoxical status of Bowen as a recognized yet neglected founding thinker; it reveals problematic "politics of citation" in business ethics and management, where ritual citation of classics raises ethical concerns about credit, knowledge accumulation, and "reinventing the wheel"; and the induced "arguing–defining–landmarking" triptych lays the ground for theorizing a repertoire of the "manifest" and "latent" functions of how scholars use (and misuse) classical works.

**Practical Implication**
For teaching, the authors encourage reading classic texts such as SRB "against the grain" in relation to contemporary debates (e.g., using its chapters on religion, power, or institutionalization), rather than citing it ritually. For research, scholars can mine SRB's overlooked institutional- and welfare-economics arguments to address current CSR puzzles, and can reuse the paper's research design to assess the appropriation of other foundational works (e.g., Freeman's stakeholder theory or Friedman's 1970 article).

**Limitations**
The citation context analysis follows the standard approach of building a hand-coded sample of papers, which limits scale; the authors note this could be extended beyond hand-coding by combining manual coding with supervised machine learning to analyze thousands of citations. A second limitation is the focus on a single book: although Bowen's case is especially significant for business ethics, the investigation remains a "unique case" of one foundational work.

**Future Research**
Future studies could scale citation context analysis via supervised machine learning to detect subtle trends across disciplines and time. They could also contrast the journeys of multiple foundational works within and beyond business ethics to theorize the dynamics of classical works' neglect and rediscovery, and build a theory of the "manifest" and "latent" functions of citing classics in management theory and the social sciences.
