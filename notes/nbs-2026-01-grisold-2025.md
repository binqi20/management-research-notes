# Guardrails for Human-AI Ecologies: Norm-Based Coordination and Design for Predictability

**Abstract**
Human-AI ecologies involve human and AI-based agents that coordinate their interactions in part by following social norms. Social norms, therefore, are important for establishing the guardrails that ensure desirable interactions in a way that is consistent with essential values, such as human safety. Managing human-AI ecologies requires specifying norms to enable coordination in known situations but also allowing for the emergence of norms to enable coordination in unspecified, unstructured situations. We integrate predictive processing theory and social norm theory to explain how existing norms are enacted and reinforced based on agents' predictive models and how new norms emerge as agents update their predictive models in response to prediction errors in uncertain coordination scenarios. Rooted in this perspective, we develop a design theory that emphasizes design for predictability and propose a set of design principles for managers and developers to encode norms to evolve in human-AI ecologies, monitor outcomes, and intervene when necessary.

**Research Question**
How do norms emerge as humans and AI-based agents interact in human-AI ecologies involving unstructured and changing situations, and how can managers and developers design these ecologies so that norm-based coordination remains consistent with essential values while allowing norms to emerge in unspecified situations?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Norms function as shared predictive models that render agents' behavior mutually predictable; "deep norms" (abstract, stable, high-level predictions with high precision) give rise to situated "surface norms" (specific, flexible lower-level predictions), and prediction errors in uncertain coordination scenarios drive surface-norm adjustment, generation, and—when reliable and value-consistent—reinforcement back into deep norms.
- Key constructs: deep norms vs. surface norms; predictive models, precision weights, and prediction errors; structured vs. unstructured coordination; coordinated interaction; low- vs. high-uncertainty coordination scenarios; roles of managers, developers, and agents.
- Design principles: DP1 design for shared norms (deep- and surface-norm encoding); DP2 design for human intervention (outcome monitoring and deep-norm reinforcement); DP3 design for predictability through sensory signals (norm-enactment communication and inference); DP4 design for predictability by enacting norms (surface-norm defaulting, adjustment, generation, reinforcing).
- Boundary conditions: agents must share the same deep norms; predictions diagnosed as deep-norm violations should not trigger surface-norm updates; the framework presumes AI-based agents can be approximated as Bayesian prediction machines, which current machines only partially realize.

The paper integrates predictive processing theory—which treats both human brains and AI learning algorithms as prediction machines that project prior knowledge onto incoming sensory signals and update models in response to prediction errors—with social norm theory, in which norms are conventions providing shared expectations that stabilize interaction. Synthesizing the two, norms become "mutually constraining error minimization processes": deep norms encode the ecology's values as high-precision high-level predictions, while surface norms instantiate them for specific coordination scenarios. When agents face uncertainty and existing predictions fail, prediction errors signal a need to adjust or generate surface norms; successful, value-consistent surface norms can be reinforced into deep norms, whereas large errors implicating deep norms should generally not update the model.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a design theory for norm-based coordination in human-AI ecologies by integrating predictive processing theory and social norm theory, proposing "design for predictability" as the organizing logic and four design principles (with subprinciples) as actionable design knowledge. It advances a more symmetrical view of human and AI-based agents—both modeled as Bayesian prediction machines—departing from the prevailing information-systems assumption that humans and AI are fundamentally distinct agents requiring constant reconciliation, and it theorizes the bidirectional dynamics between deep and surface norms as a mechanism for value-consistent emergence and governance of norms.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and developers can encode an ecology's values as deep norms and selected surface norms in agents' hierarchical prediction models (e.g., through simulation-based training), design agents to communicate and infer the surface norms they enact via sensory signals, and continuously monitor emergent surface norms to intervene and reinforce desirable ones. The principles also offer mechanisms for embedding regulatory requirements (e.g., EU AI Act principles and post-market monitoring) into the design and ongoing management of high-risk AI ecologies such as autonomous warehouses.

**Limitations**
The design principles are future-oriented and intended to inform inquiry rather than to be directly empirically validated, and they confront an "implementation problem"—current machines are not yet capable of fully implementing predictive processing—and an "update problem"—encoding and communicating new deep and surface norms requires both technical means to change agents' inner workings and means to retrain or inform large numbers of human and AI-based agents, which is challenging. The symmetrical human-AI framing is acknowledged as a broad-brush abstraction that elides important differences, such as the more embodied nature of human cognition.

**Future Research**
Future work can address the implementation and update problems by specifying how the abstracted mechanisms (e.g., reinforcement learning) can be realized across technologies, and can leverage the assumed human-AI symmetry to design overarching interventions—such as environmental manipulations that trigger prediction errors in both agent types simultaneously—relevant to impact-oriented and design-science research. Researchers can also explore how implementing values as deep norms enables simulation to predict when, how, and why surface norms will emerge, and how monitoring emergent norms can inform evolving AI policymaking and regulatory design.
