# Whose Justice? A Turn to Relational Equality from the Experiences of Ethno-Racially Marginalised Women in Australian Workplaces

**Abstract**
This paper examines how ethno-racially marginalised women in Australian workplaces continue to be constrained by enduring organisational norms that privilege whiteness, masculinity, and Western/Anglo-cultural modes of participation. Further, we show how mainstream diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives reproduce exclusion and marginalisation in unintended and new ways by misrecognising or sidelining intersectional experiences. Through an intersectional reading of survey and interview data, we show how DEI initiatives frequently reflect narrow settings for equity that privilege dominant norms while rendering others invisible or “risky”. We engage with Elizabeth Anderson’s theory of relational equality to show that the shortcomings of these initiatives result not from idiosyncratic error or oversight but from the foundational logics that underpin them, including a business-case rationale and a default to distributive justice logics. We suggest that DEI should be reimagined as a practice of enabling equal participation, mutual recognition, and interpersonal justifications within organisational life. In doing so, the paper contributes both empirically and theoretically to current debates on workplace justice, intersectional equality, and the ethics of inclusion.

**Research Question**
Within the context of Australian organisations, how do ethno-racially marginalised professional women experience their workplace? What do their accounts reveal about the relational conditions that shape inclusion and recognition in organisational life?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: distributive justice and business-case logics vs. relational equality; access/representation vs. equal standing; symbolic vs. structural inclusion; single-axis (gender-only or race-only) frameworks vs. intersectional marginalisation; the "two levers" of hard (legal/compliance) and soft (voluntary, business-case) diversity measures.
- Structural barriers (themes): organisational norms privileging Anglo/Western and masculine leadership models; institutional invisibility and underrepresentation; "epistemic neocolonialism" (co-option of marginalised knowledge; tokenism).
- Lived experience (themes): dual marginalisation / "double jeopardy" (race × gender); simultaneous invisibility and hypervisibility; stereotyping, accent- and appearance-based judgments, and microaggressions framed as humour; being treated as a "risky appointment".
- Relational capital (themes): exclusion from informal "closed shop" networks; scarce sponsorship/mentorship, especially across ethno-racial lines.
- Emergent outcomes: misrecognition and conditional standing; pressure to assimilate to dominant norms ("be a chameleon"); scaling back ambition or exiting; calls for accountability, targets, and reporting.

The paper's process logic is interpretive rather than causal: drawing on Elizabeth Anderson's (1999) relational equality, it argues that DEI initiatives grounded in distributive justice and the business case redirect attention to "what people have" (representation, pay equity, compliance) while leaving untouched the relational structures through which standing, credibility, and authority are negotiated. Because these foundational logics treat ERM women's difference as a liability to be managed or a value to be extracted, inclusion becomes a "relational burden" that requires marginalised women to adapt, advocate, and prove themselves within norms they had no part in shaping—reproducing the very exclusions it claims to remedy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes two contributions to scholarship on business ethics and intersectionality. Empirically, it centres the underexamined experiences of ERM women in Australian workplaces, showing how inclusion efforts that claim to promote justice fail to secure equal standing or meaningful participation. Theoretically, it develops a relational egalitarian framework—drawing on Anderson's account of equality as equal standing and interpersonal justification—that reframes inclusion as a matter of relational structure rather than managerial strategy, shifting the analytic focus from access to standing, from presence to participation, and from outcomes to moral accountability.

**Practical Implication**
The authors call for organisations to move beyond symbolic gestures and representational targets to interrogate how everyday norms around feedback, credibility, trust, sponsorship, and belonging are structured by unequal distributions of moral and epistemic authority. Concretely, respondents recommended holding leaders accountable for delivering on DEI policies, setting targets as an enabler of mindset change, and establishing mechanisms to measure, monitor, and report on the career progress of ethno-racially diverse women—while recognising ERM women as equal participants in reshaping leadership norms.

**Limitations**
The study notes a notable over-reliance on approaches that treat ethno-racial diversity as a discrete, fixed demographic characteristic, and reports Australia-wide constraints of limited, inconsistent, and patchy data collection on ethno-racial representation. Fine-grained regional analysis (e.g., distinguishing Asian, South Asian, or South East Asian groups) was not possible due to sample-size constraints, and the data are not publicly available due to ethical restrictions.

**Future Research**
Future research should continue to centre the perspectives of those most often excluded from organisational norm-setting—not as recipients of inclusion policies but as agents of epistemic and institutional transformation—and explore how inclusion frameworks can be reconstituted through relationally just practices (e.g., rethinking how feedback is given, how credibility is conferred, and how leadership is recognised). Further studies might examine other intersectional configurations such as migration status, linguistic background, or class position, where formal access to opportunity does not translate into relational equality.
