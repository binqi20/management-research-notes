# Nexus Effect: Unraveling the Impact of Political Patronage Connections on Corporate Investment

**Abstract**
This study investigates how political patronage influences local firms’ investment decisions in China, focusing on changes in patronage ties resulting from provincial leadership turnover. By examining prefectural officials with connections to their provincial superiors, we find that firms in these regions experience increased investment expenditures, albeit with reduced efficiency. This effect is primarily driven by stronger promotion incentives for local officials, bolstered by favoritism from provincial patrons. While political patronage helps address agency problems within political hierarchies, our findings highlight its adverse economic impact due to misaligned interests between politicians and the public.

**Research Question**
How do patronage networks among politicians — specifically school ties between Chinese prefectural officials and their superior provincial leaders — affect the investment expenditures and investment efficiency of firms located in connected prefectures, and through what mechanism?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Patronage connection status of a prefecture, operationalized as the interaction Schoolties × Incumbent (shared university ties between incumbent prefectural and provincial officials)
- DV(s): Corporate investment expenditures (Invest, t+1, scaled by total assets); investment efficiency / quality (overinvestment, innovation, total factor productivity; aggregate prefecture-level productivity)
- Mediators / channels: Stronger promotion incentives of connected prefectural officials (political promotion, official age); resource-allocation advantages (government subsidies, corporate-bond endorsement, special economic zones)
- Moderators: Network robustness (distance to alma mater, alumni-network activity); collectivist culture and weak social trust

School ties between prefectural and provincial officials build mutual trust and signal favoritism within the political hierarchy, raising connected officials’ promotion prospects. To demonstrate loyalty and improve advancement odds, these officials push local firms to invest more and channel preferential resources to them, lifting investment expenditures (a DiD-estimated ~29.85% increase). Because officials’ interests diverge from the public’s, this loyalty-driven effort distorts allocation — producing overinvestment, weaker innovation, lower total factor productivity, and negative market reactions — so patronage benefits client and patron officials at the expense of broader economic efficiency.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends research on political networks from direct politician–firm connections to interconnections among politicians, documenting a spillover of patronage onto the micro-economy that is less prone to endogeneity. It reconciles competing views in the patronage literature by showing that promotion incentives and distributive favoritism can co-exist, benefiting both client and patron officials while harming economic efficiency. It also enriches the literature on political influence over corporate investment by identifying patronage ties with higher-ranked leaders as a driver of higher but less efficient investment.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and investors should weigh the broader political factors — including officials’ patronage networks — that shape a firm’s operating environment when making investment decisions, as these networks offer a novel explanation for regional investment imbalances in China and globally. Authorities should recognize that informal personal ties within government, while reducing information asymmetry and agency problems, can distort subordinates’ incentives and produce unbalanced resource allocation.
