# Green industrial policy and latecomer catch-up: A missed green window of opportunity for domestic solar PV module manufacturers in Indonesia

**Abstract**
We apply the framework to study how industrial policies and energy policies have interacted and influenced the initial entry and early-stage catch-up of domestic solar PV module manufacturers in Indonesia in the period 2008–2023. Empirically, we draw on semi-structured interviews with representatives of domestic solar PV module manufacturers, industry informants and relevant government agencies. Based on the conceptual framework, we identify the inhibiting and encouraging factors influencing firm-level catch-up trajectories within three distinctive phases. We find that, while the catch-up trajectory of domestic solar PV module manufacturers resembled a path toward coexistence during the first and second phases, the third phase involved an aborted catch-up trajectory. However, a new catch-up trajectory toward coexistence may be emerging in relation to the recent establishment of export-oriented solar PV module production. Conceptually, the paper contributes to advancing the literature on GIPs by adopting a firm-oriented perspective and by seeking closer integration with research in development studies on the catching-up of latecomer firms.

**Research Question**
How have industrial policies and sector-specific energy policies interacted over time to influence the initial entry and early-stage catch-up of domestic solar PV module manufacturers in Indonesia, and what firm-level inhibiting and encouraging factors shaped their catch-up trajectories?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / analytical elements: industrial-policy attributes (commitment, embeddedness, discipline) and sector-specific energy-policy attributes (ambition, temporal consistency, market formation), plus their alignment.
- Firm-level conditions: firm-specific attributes and decisions (size, age, ownership, prior competences, foreign linkages, managerial strategy) that differentiate intra-industry catch-up.
- Process / phases: Phase I 'Era of ferment' (2008-2012); Phase II 'Growth' (2013-2018); Phase III 'Shakeout' (2019-2023).
- Emergent outcomes: catch-up trajectories ranging from "coexistence" (viable firms alongside incumbents) to "aborted" catch-up (stagnation, exit, diversification).
- Boundary conditions: external factors (global Chinese cost dominance, import tariffs, anti-dumping rules) that the GIP framework cannot reduce to single causal factors.

The framework treats GIP as the configuration of, and interaction between, industrial and sector-specific policies that condition whether latecomer firms catch up. In Indonesia, a 'green window of opportunity' (low entry barriers around 2008) initially encouraged domestic entry, but inconsistent energy policy, weak market formation, local-content rules undercut by permitted imports, and overwhelming Chinese price competition eroded demand, producing an aborted catch-up by Phase III. A recent export-oriented strategy (Chinese-linked production on Batam Island) may seed a new coexistence trajectory, though its developmental linkages remain uncertain.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances the GIP literature by shifting from the prevailing meso-level (industry/country) lens toward a firm-oriented, micro-level perspective that surfaces intra-industry heterogeneity and firm agency. Its novelty lies in integrating three previously unconnected strands -- development-studies work on industrial policy, policy-mix/sustainability-transition research, and firm-level catch-up of latecomer firms -- into a single processual framework that links GIP to the early-stage catch-up of developing-country firms, countering the literature's empirical bias toward the exceptional case of China.

**Practical Implication**
For policymakers in developing economies, the case shows that seizing a green window of opportunity requires sustained commitment, embedded coordination with firms, disciplined enforcement, and consistent, ambitious sector-specific policies that build genuine domestic market demand -- not local-content rules that can be circumvented by cheaper imports. It also highlights trade-offs between accelerating technology diffusion and nurturing domestic manufacturing, and warns that export/FDI-oriented strategies risk becoming isolated enclaves without linkages to the surrounding economy.

**Limitations**
As a single-country, qualitative case study focused on the early "entry and initial growth" stage of catch-up, the findings are context-specific and not intended for statistical generalization; the authors instead pursue analytical generalization and theoretical development. Company names and interviewees were anonymized, and twelve of the contacted firms did not respond, so the firm-level evidence rests on the nine participating manufacturers.

**Future Research**
The authors call for comparative applications of the framework to the same sector across countries (e.g., comparing Indonesia, Malaysia, and Vietnam) to verify its applicability, and for extensions to other sectors by combining industrial-policy analysis with the relevant adjacent policy domains. They also urge greater attention to the influence of external factors -- such as global production-network shifts, import tariffs, and anti-dumping regulation -- on latecomer catch-up under GIPs.
