# Enacting responsible leadership in cross-sector partnerships: A dynamic choreography of power

**Abstract**
How can leaders guide diverse organizations to work together on society’s biggest challenges when power is unevenly distributed? Cross-sector partnerships (CSPs) bring together companies, governments, and nongovernmental organizations (NGOs) to address complex problems, yet their success hinges on how leaders navigate competing interests and shifting power dynamics. This study examines a high-profile CSP involving a Fortune 500 company, two international development organizations, and an NGO. Drawing on Mary Parker Follett’s ideas about “power-with” and “power-over,” I show how NGO leaders combined collaborative and more coercive tactics to move the partnership forward. My analysis reveals that responsible leadership (RL) in CSPs is not a static trait or style but evolves through a dynamic choreography of power as challenges and priorities change. The findings offer practical lessons for leaders seeking to balance ethics, inclusion, and influence in multi-stakeholder collaborations, and they extend theory by reframing RL as a shifting, context-sensitive process rather than a fixed style.

**Research Question**
How are power dynamics mobilized and navigated in CSPs to enact responsible leadership (RL) in practice?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: "power-with" (jointly developed, collaborative capacity for coordinated action) vs. "power-over" (more coercive, directive control); responsible leadership reframed as the "ethical orchestration of power."
- Process / phases: three CSP lifecycle phases — Launch ("gaining the spotlight"), Pivot ("disrupting the established order"), End ("meeting project needs") — each posing a distinct situated challenge.
- Practices (seven RL tactics): differentiating the project; garnering high-level support; debunking flawed strategies; linking to partners' broader goals; cornering resistance; tailoring outcomes; mobilizing external momentum.
- Triggering conditions: marked power asymmetries among partners, entrenched stakeholder interests/inertia, and divergent organizational evaluation metrics.
- Emergent outcomes: sustained partnership momentum and concrete results (increased farmer participation, higher incomes, lower reject rates, wider market access).

Responsible leadership in the CSP unfolded as a dynamic choreography of power: NGO actors, despite lacking formal authority, sequenced and recombined collaborative (power-with) and assertive (power-over) tactics in response to evolving stakeholder configurations at each phase. Grounded in Follett's "law of the situation," influence derived not from position but from convening actors around a collectively discerned course of action, with power-over selectively mobilized — through sequencing, signaling, and external accountability — to unblock progress when collaboration alone stalled. The process demonstrates that RL is distributed across interactions rather than residing in any single leader, and that carefully exercised power-over can be facilitative rather than corrosive to stakeholder trust.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes a practice-based, empirically grounded reconceptualization of RL as the "ethical orchestration of power" — an adaptive, context-sensitive process in which actors blend power-with and power-over tactics to align interests and sustain joint action — rather than a fixed moral disposition or collaborative ideal. It extends RL theory beyond its normative, collaboration-centric roots by showing that assertive, even subtly coercive, action can be compatible with and instrumental to RL in contexts of diffuse authority, and it revives and updates Mary Parker Follett's relational view of power for contemporary cross-sector settings.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders of CSPs should shift their focus from controlling resources and people toward active engagement with stakeholders and "the situation at hand," recognizing when and how to assertively guide action without overt dominance. RL leadership-development programs should train emerging leaders in the fragile balance between collaborative (power-with) and coercive (power-over) tactics through case studies and role-play, and cultivate the foresight to anticipate distinct challenges at a partnership's beginning, middle, and end stages.

**Limitations**
As a single-case study, the findings are well-suited to theory elaboration but limited in generalizability and should be interpreted alongside other evidence; comparative case studies of multiple CSPs could strengthen external validity. The study also did not explore how the intersection of different institutional logics might lead stakeholders to interpret or enact RL differently, necessarily limiting the breadth of its insights.

**Future Research**
Comparative case studies across diverse CSP contexts could reveal common patterns and context-specific variations in RL tactics during shared phases (launch, pivot, conclusion), informing a flexible, context-sensitive typology of RL tactics. Future work could also examine the contextually sensitive boundaries or "threshold" at which power-over remains ethically facilitative, and how differing institutional logics shape divergent enactments of RL.
