# Performing Diversity: Navigating Tensions, Identity Threats, and Self-Instrumentalization in Applicant Diversity Statements

**Abstract**

By centering the applicant perspective, our study extends research on instrumental DEI framing, identity management, and organizational performativity by highlighting the self-instrumentalization required by applicants navigating ambiguous and evaluative DEI practices. Practically, our findings underscore the unintended consequences of performative DEI practices that shift the burden of proof onto individuals without institutional accountability.

**Research Question**
(1) How do applicants manage the self-presentation of their commitment to and contributions toward DEI, and what tensions do they encounter in doing so? (2) Under what conditions do these tensions arise, and what are the downstream consequences of engaging in this practice?

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Ambiguity about (a) what to include in a diversity statement and (b) what "diversity" actually means, amplified by the lack of institutional guidance and the evaluative, high-stakes hiring context.
- Core tensions: *Self-Disclosure Tension* — whether and how much to reveal personal identities and experiences in an instrumental setting; *Framing Tension* — how to package those identities and experiences to balance perceived institutional expectations with authentic self-expression.
- Central process — *self-instrumentalization*: a compelled, public, strategic, and evaluative form of identity work in which applicants must actively enact instrumental DEI framing of their own identities, with agency present but constrained by institutional expectations and power asymmetries.
- Emergent outcomes (threats): *Trivialization Threat* — the fear that one's identity is undermined and reduced to symbolic/token gestures; *Exploitation Threat* — the sense that one's identity is being used or co-opted for institutional gain, prompting discomfort, resentment, and disengagement.
- Boundary condition (preliminary): Non-White applicants more often described Self-Disclosure Tension (external pressure to highlight marginalized identities), while White applicants more often expressed Framing Tension (acknowledging privilege without overclaiming disadvantage).

Ambiguity about evaluators' expectations fuels two self-presentation tensions that reflect a conflict between strategic impression management (aligning with implicit expectations in a competitive market) and the need for authenticity (intensified by the personal and moral nature of identity). When statements are perceived as performative, these tensions amplify into threats of trivialization and exploitation. The paper theorizes self-instrumentalization as a distinct departure from prior DEI-framing work: rather than reacting to being instrumentalized by others, applicants are compelled to instrumentalize themselves, shifting the burden of proving DEI commitment onto individuals without corresponding institutional accountability.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study offers four contributions: (1) it extends research on instrumental DEI framing by shifting the focus from how individuals react to being instrumentalized by others to how they actively navigate compelled self-instrumentalization; (2) it contributes to the identity work literature by showing that strength-based identity framing—usually assumed to foster empowerment and authenticity—can feel exploitative and trivializing when publicly performed under evaluative pressure, including across multiple intersecting identities; (3) it extends research on organizational performativity by moving the analytical gaze from organizations' DEI motives to the effects of performative practices on the people they aim to include; and (4) it builds on work examining the ambiguity and subjectivity of "diversity" by showing how that uncertainty shapes applicants' self-presentation strategies and experiences of "inclusion."

**Practical Implication**
The findings underscore the unintended psychological and strategic consequences of performative DEI practices that shift the burden of proof onto individuals without institutional accountability, which can generate ambivalence, resistance, and backlash even among those most invested in DEI. The authors argue that diversity statements can be meaningful rather than harmful only with greater organizational accountability and transparency—when inclusion is not merely stated but enacted in ways that support authenticity, autonomy, and mutual accountability.

**Limitations**
The qualitative, sequential mixed-methods design limits the ability to make causal claims and to generalize, and the non-nested design with a roughly three-year temporal gap between phases means temporal shifts and field differences cannot be fully ruled out. Social desirability and identity dynamics may have shaped interview disclosures, and the focus on Management academia in the U.S. (with limited Canadian adoption) further constrains generalizability across disciplines, national contexts, and non-academic sectors.

**Future Research**
Future work could use larger, more diverse samples and quantitative methods to test causal mechanisms, assess generalizability, and explore subgroup and intersectional differences (e.g., race × gender, race × disability). Researchers could examine how evolving political and institutional contexts shape applicant perceptions over time, how these tensions manifest in other evaluative contexts (promotion reviews, award nominations, DEI task forces), and how evaluators interpret and assign value to diversity statements and whether such statements help institutions achieve equity goals.
