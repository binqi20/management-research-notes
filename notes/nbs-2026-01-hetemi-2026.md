# Knowledge ecosystem emergence: Organizing participation, identity, and actorhood to bridge the governance void

**Abstract**
To address this gap, we theorize knowledge ecosystems as meta-organizations, examining how they gradually develop organizing elements that bridge the initial governance void. Empirically, we draw on an in-depth field case study of the High-Capacity Transport ecosystem in Sweden, demonstrating how three interrelated organizational elements—participation, identity, and actorhood—emerge through an iterative, yet broadly sequential process to resolve governance void challenges in resourcing, belonging, and collective action. Furthermore, we identify discursive and performative meta-organizational practices that enable the actors to collectively enact the aforementioned organizational elements and to engage in knowledge search. We further demonstrate how the organization of knowledge ecosystems is never ‘complete’ and the governance void remains only partially resolved, given the underdefined nature of new knowledge as the ecosystem's shared goal. Overall, our process model contributes to the theory, practice, and policy of knowledge ecosystem emergence and organizing.

**Research Question**
How do actors develop the necessary organizing elements to overcome initial governance voids in the knowledge ecosystem emergence phase? The study examines how a broad university-industry-academia collaboration in Sweden gradually developed an organizational structure while pursuing innovation in High-Capacity Transport.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: the *governance void* — the lack of constituent elements of network governance (authority, conflict resolution, resource allocation) that loosely coupled, cross-sectoral knowledge ecosystems face at emergence.
- Three governance-void challenges (sequentially salient): resourcing, belonging, and collective action.
- Three emergent organizational elements (process phases): participation (T1, 2012–2016), identity (T2, 2016–2019), and actorhood (T3, 2019–2023).
- Meta-organizational practices (the means): discursive practices (contextualizing attention, discursive framing, goal consolidation) and performative practices (open-ended prospection, experimental prospection, influence structuring).
- Emergent outcomes: three knowledge search modes — problem search, integrative search, and solution search — that co-evolve with the organizing elements.
- Boundary condition: organization is never 'complete'; the governance void is only partially resolved given the underdefined nature of new knowledge as the shared goal.

The process model explains how an informal, loosely coupled group self-organizes "from the ground up": in each phase, participants jointly enact discursive and performative practices that act as "semi-structures" to bridge the focal challenge, build the corresponding organizational element, and unlock the next knowledge search mode. Participation establishes a "minimum viable" structure that mobilizes voluntary, in-kind resources and enables problem search; identity-building forges a shared purpose that enables integrative search; and actorhood consolidates goals and structures influence so the collective can act as a unified entity and pursue solution search. The ecosystem thereby graduates into a meta-organization that mitigates—but never fully closes—the initial governance void.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study develops a process model of knowledge ecosystem emergence and organizationality, showing how loosely coupled actors progressively construct the "minimum criteria" of an organization and thereby extend Dobusch and Schoeneborn's (2015) notion of organizationality to the meta-organizational level. It enriches meta-organization theory by characterizing how a meta-organization can bootstrap through informal social commitment (resourcing without a central budget) and by identifying belonging and collective-action challenges alongside resourcing. It also advances knowledge ecosystem scholarship by sequencing three knowledge search modes—introducing "integrative search" as an in-between mode that extends Järvi et al.'s (2018) two-step problem/solution logic.

**Practical Implication**
Practitioners orchestrating cross-sector collaborations should build collective mobilization early—using open participation, asset-mapping canvases, and open-ended prospection to unlock in-kind resources without dedicated funding—then purposefully shape a shared identity through joint visioning and consistent communication. Policymakers should tailor interventions to the ecosystem's developmental phase: stable long-term commitments and low-commitment signals during the participation stage, then streamlined permits, agile/matching funding, regulatory sandboxes, and inter-agency coordination as identity and actorhood emerge, especially where public funding is contingent on demonstrable progress.

**Limitations**
Reliance on a single-case study limits statistical generalizability; the emergent patterns and process model would benefit from validation across diverse innovation processes, and the proposed participation–identity–actorhood sequence reflects this case and should not be treated as deterministic. The analysis emphasizes collective dynamics and discursive/performative practices but offers limited insight into the micro-level behaviors of individual participants. The study context is shaped by a robust innovation policy and high public-private collaboration, so findings may not capture ecosystems in regions with weaker institutional support.

**Future Research**
Future studies could examine how the participation–identity–actorhood trajectory varies across innovation contexts differing in institutional or regulatory dynamics and resource environments, and how variations in institutional maturity and political economy shape meta-organizational governance choices. Research could investigate how actors build effective "innovation-discourse infrastructure," incorporate the micro-level behaviors of participants, and—given the role of coopetition—give more explicit consideration to power imbalances and competing institutional logics during knowledge ecosystem emergence.
