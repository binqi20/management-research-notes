# Trademarks and the innovativeness of social enterprises

**Abstract**
Additionally, we investigate the underlying reasons SEs choose to register (or not register) trademarks, recognizing that trademarks can serve various purposes. Our findings reveal that innovative SEs are more likely to file trademarks, primarily to protect their innovations and associated brands, pursuing market orientation, and scaling their social impact. These insights deepen our understanding of the heterogeneity among SEs and contribute to the growing conversation on the role of intellectual property rights in creating positive effects for society.

**Research Question**
The paper poses two questions: (1) "To what extent do trademarks, if used at all, relate to the innovativeness of social enterprises?" and (2) "What are the motives of SEs to register (or not to register) trademarks?"

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Number of active registered trademarks held by the SE (also tested as natural log; time-limited trademark subsamples)
- DV(s): SE innovativeness — operationalized as a website-derived innovation index and a social impact index (seemingly unrelated regressions), and as a binary survey-based innovativeness measure (logistic regression)
- Controls: Metropolis location, company age, number of employees (size categories), social impact certification, legal form (non-profit), number of patent filings, website size (log), 12 industry dummies, 17 SDG dummies
- Survey constructs: 14 motives to register trademarks and 11 motives not to register, rated on 1-5 scales

The paper argues that because SEs frequently innovate in organizational processes, business models, and services rather than in science-based, patentable artifacts, traditional indicators (patents, R&D) fail to capture their innovativeness; trademarks—being apt at signaling creative and service innovation—can fill this gap. Empirically, more registered trademarks correlate with higher website-displayed innovativeness and with innovative main products/services; each additional trademark raises the odds of being innovative by a factor of 3.6. The survey reveals SEs register trademarks chiefly to protect innovations and brands, to pursue a particular form of market orientation, and—distinctively—to protect and scale their social impact, while a minority deliberately forgo trademarks to invite imitation and scale impact indirectly.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces trademarks as a new, activity-based, readily available IP indicator for identifying innovative SEs within the heterogeneous SE population, advancing social-innovation measurement at the organizational level rather than the macro level. It shows that the trademark-innovativeness link holds across legal forms and firm ages (challenging legal form and startup status as differentiators) and that SEs combine innovation-protection with innovation-promotion and impact-scaling motives—a combination new to the trademark and hybridity literatures—thereby demonstrating that market orientation and social mission need not be opposed.

**Practical Implication**
The findings give SEs a tool to benchmark their innovativeness against peers in their industry and clarify why and how other SEs use trademarks strategically. For policymakers and impact investors, trademarks offer an accessible signal to detect innovative, scalable SEs during screening, and the results inform the broader policy debate on how intellectual property rights can amplify social benefit.

**Limitations**
Validating the trademark measure against website content is imperfect because websites are communicative devices that may present organizations as innovative for branding reasons, and because temporal discrepancies between trademark registrations and website snapshots cannot be fully ruled out despite robustness tests. The study is also confined to German SEs and to the SE population, limiting generalizability across countries and across other impact-oriented organizational forms.

**Future Research**
The authors call for applying the website-based approach in other countries and for cross-country comparisons using international trademark filings; for longitudinal designs that track trademark-innovativeness associations over time; for deeper investigation of SEs' trademarking motives and links to specific innovation stages; and for comparative studies contrasting SEs with foundations, cooperatives, and mainstream firms to test how trademark use relates to innovativeness across organizational forms and goals.
