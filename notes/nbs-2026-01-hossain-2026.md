# Rewarding Ethical Leadership: Corporate LGBTQ+ Friendliness and CEO Compensation

**Abstract**
We examine whether firms reward ethical leadership by linking corporate LGBTQ+ friendliness to CEO compensation. Using comprehensive data from publicly traded firms from the United States, we find a strong positive relation, indicating that boards actively incentivize leaders who advance inclusivity. The effect is concentrated in firms with high integrity, transparent reporting, and responsible environmental conduct, consistent with stakeholder and stewardship theories and inconsistent with rent-extraction motive. Moreover, boards with younger-generation directors amplify this relation, reflecting evolving governance norms that value ethical leadership. Overall, our evidence positions workplace inclusivity as a strategic asset that shapes executive pay design and reinforces responsible corporate governance.

**Research Question**
Do firms reward ethical leadership by linking corporate LGBTQ+ friendliness to higher CEO compensation, and does this relation reflect a genuine ethical commitment to integrity and transparency rather than managerial rent extraction?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Corporate LGBTQ+ friendliness (HRC Corporate Equality Index score at the firm level)
- DV(s): CEO total compensation (TDC1 from ExecuComp)
- Moderators: Corporate integrity, transparent financial reporting, responsible environmental conduct (lower carbon emissions); board generational diversity (Millennial/Gen Z directors); CEO power (board cooption, CEO duality) tested as a competing rent-extraction channel

The paper argues that boards reward CEOs who advance LGBTQ+ inclusiveness because such leadership signals ethical and strategic value that enhances stakeholder confidence and long-term firm value. Drawing on stakeholder and stewardship theories (supplemented by compensating wage differential, signaling, and agency perspectives), inclusiveness is framed as a costly, responsibility-laden behavior that boards compensate as recognition rather than as the product of managerial entrenchment. The relation strengthens where ethical foundations are already institutionalized and where generationally diverse boards are more attuned to ethical leadership, while CEO-power proxies do not drive the effect.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the CSR-executive pay literature by isolating LGBTQ+ inclusiveness, an underexplored CSR dimension, and showing it commands a CEO pay premium that reflects ethical recognition rather than opportunistic rent seeking. It advances ethical-governance research by demonstrating a spillover effect whereby firms with established ethical foundations are more likely to reward inclusive leadership, and by showing that board generational diversity reinforces corporate accountability and inclusivity through executive pay incentives.

**Practical Implication**
For firms, the results emphasize the strategic value of promoting LGBTQ+ inclusiveness as a factor that shapes executive incentives, not only employee engagement and reputation. For policymakers and investors, the findings inform debates over DEI initiatives and corporate governance, suggesting regulatory frameworks can encourage transparent reporting on diversity metrics and executive compensation, and aligning with the UN SDGs (notably Goal 10 and Goal 5).

**Limitations**
The measurement of LGBTQ+ friendliness, while robust, inevitably simplifies a multidimensional construct. The focus on U.S. firms may restrict the generalizability of the results to other institutional and cultural contexts.

**Future Research**
Future research could enrich this line of inquiry by conducting cross-country analyses and investigating additional mechanisms through which inclusivity shapes governance, managerial incentives, and firm performance.
