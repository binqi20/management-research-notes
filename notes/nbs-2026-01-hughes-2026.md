# Problematizing the role of artificial intelligence in hiring and organizational inequalities: A multidisciplinary review

**Abstract**
What are the implications of the growing use of artificial intelligence (AI) in recruitment and hiring for organizational inequalities? While advocates suggest that AI is a groundbreaking tool that can enhance hiring precision, efficiency, diversity and fit, critics raise serious concerns around bias, fairness, and privacy. This review article critically advances this debate by drawing on diverse scholarship across computing and data sciences; human resource, management, and organization studies; social sciences; and law. Using a hybrid review approach that combines scoping and problematizing review methods, we examine the implications of algorithmic hiring for organizational inequalities. **Research Question**
What are the implications of the growing use of AI in recruitment and hiring for organizational inequalities? Specifically, the review asks (RQ1) what key questions and concerns are being examined across disciplines about the role of AI in organizational hiring, and (RQ2) what underlying assumptions and approaches guide each discipline's treatment of algorithmic hiring and inequality.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: AI in hiring both echoes and extends Acker's (2006) "inequality regimes"; whereas inequality-producing mechanisms historically operate invisibly until made visible and de-legitimated, AI moves in the opposite direction — gaining near-ubiquitous legitimacy while becoming more invisible as an opaque "black box."
- Key constructs: an "algorithmically-mediated inequality regime" characterized by heightened algorithmic invisibility and pervasive, unchallenged algorithmic legitimacy; a "chain of knowledge" (multidisciplinary expertise) and a "chain of responsibility" (multi-stakeholder accountability).
- Disciplinary clusters problematized: computing science (CS) frames inequality as a technical "bias" problem; HRMOS frames AI as a solution to human bias and theorizes human-AI collaboration; social sciences (SS) treat inequality as systemic and contextual; legal scholarship (LS) debates the adequacy of existing law and proactive regulation.
- Boundary conditions: regulatory responses lag and are contested, varying across global, national, regional, and local contexts (e.g., the US market-/complaint-based model versus the EU rights-/preventative-based model).

The review argues that the dominance of CS and its language of "bias"/"debiasing" diffuses a "technosolution" paradigm that narrows attention to individual decision-makers and technical fixes, obscuring the structural pervasiveness of inequality. By extending Acker's framework into the digital era, the authors show how AI's opacity and growing legitimacy conceal and reproduce inequalities within routine hiring — a foundational gatekeeping process — while deflecting accountability across employers, vendors, developers, and platforms.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Acker's (2006) framework of "inequality regimes" into the digital era by conceptualizing AI-transformed organizations as sites of "algorithmically-mediated inequality regimes," in which inequality is concealed further through algorithmic invisibility and the growing legitimacy of AI solutions. It reframes AI not as a neutral tool or a solution to human bias but as a structural mechanism that participates in and transforms organizational inequality, and it introduces the paired notions of an interdisciplinary "chain of knowledge" and a multi-stakeholder "chain of responsibility."

**Practical Implication**
The authors advocate operationalizing the "chain of responsibility" across employers, algorithm designers, unions, regulators, and policy makers, since no single stakeholder can address AI-mediated hiring inequality alone. Employers can pursue participatory design and voluntary/independent audits, build cross-functional capacity (HR, designers, regulators), and embed fairness insights into hiring practices, while legal practitioners and civil-society groups push for context-tailored regulation and public accountability (e.g., using resources such as The AI Policy Sourcebook).

**Limitations**
The review is limited to English-language publications, reflecting a Global North bias and under-representing Global South perspectives. Its hybrid scoping-plus-problematizing methodology does not include explicit quality assessment, and publication practices vary across fields (e.g., CS often publishes in peer-reviewed conference proceedings rather than indexed journals). The analysis also focuses on hiring rather than other AI-altered HR processes.

**Future Research**
Future work should continue theorizing AI's role in perpetuating inequality (including whether AI is a distinct structural mechanism or embeds in existing ones), prioritize comparative work across diverse jurisdictions and the Global South, and adopt systematic reviews with explicit quality measures. The authors call for qualitative and ethnographic studies of AI's creation, use, and regulation; multi-level (micro-meso-macro) and contextualized analyses; and research on the governance of algorithmic hiring and other AI-altered HR decisions (e.g., promotions, compensation).
