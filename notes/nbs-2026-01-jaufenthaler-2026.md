# When Family Firm Reputation Backfires: The Role of Controllability in Consumer Responses to Negative CSR Incidents

**Abstract**
We apply attribution theory to examine how family ownership status shapes consumer attitudes toward companies after negative corporate social responsibility incidents, focusing on incident controllability. Two longitudinal experiments reveal that, when controllability is unknown, consumers infer lower controllability for family firms and thus hold more favorable attitudes toward them than non-family firms. However, when controllability is known, low-controllability incidents lead to similar attitudes across firm types, whereas high-controllability incidents reverse the effect, resulting in more negative attitudes toward family firms than non-family firms. These findings challenge the prevailing assumption that family firms consistently benefit from their reputation and inform stakeholder communication strategies.

**Research Question**
How do negative CSR incidents shape consumer attitudes toward family firms (vs. non-family firms), and what role does incident controllability play in this relationship?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Family ownership status (family vs. non-family); indicated incident controllability (low vs. high, manipulated in Study 2)
- DV(s): Post-incident attitude toward the company
- Mediators: Perceived controllability of the incident (Study 1, unknown-controllability context)
- Moderators: Indicated incident controllability (Study 2, known-controllability context)

Drawing on attribution theory, the authors argue that consumer responses to a negative CSR incident hinge on the controllability attributed to it. When controllability is unknown, the heightened trustworthiness and moral responsibility consumers ascribe to family firms lead them to infer lower controllability — a "benefit of the doubt" effect — preserving more favorable attitudes than for non-family firms (Study 1: full mediation through perceived controllability). When controllability is known, low controllability neutralizes this advantage (attitudes converge), while high controllability reverses it: deliberate misconduct sharply violates the elevated moral expectations and humanized perceptions attached to family firms, amplifying condemnation and producing more negative attitudes toward family firms than non-family firms (Study 2: significant ownership × controllability interaction).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper is the first to document a reversal effect in which family ownership status turns into a liability, yielding significantly more negative consumer attitudes toward family firms than non-family firms after controllable negative CSR incidents. It identifies controllability attribution as the mechanism explaining why and when attitude differences persist, converge, or reverse, and extends the application of attribution theory in family business research by showing that perceived family/non-family distinctions shape both the inferences and the consequences of controllability attributions.

**Practical Implication**
Family firms should treat their status as both an asset and a liability in CSR crises. When controllability is unknown, emphasizing family ownership can support an accidental-incident framing and preserve favorable attitudes; when low controllability is evident, doing so adds little value. When an incident is controllable, firms should avoid emphasizing family ownership at any stage and instead invest in robust internal controls, ethical accountability, and transparent post-incident communication to avoid disproportionate backlash.

**Limitations**
The studies used specific, internally caused negative CSR scenarios (a minimum-wage breach) with a single fictitious firm, so the controllability dynamics may differ for other incident types (e.g., corruption vs. environmental harm) that vary in perceived controllability. The research focused only on the controllability dimension of attribution and on a single Western (U.S.) context, leaving stability, locus, cross-cultural variation, and richer forms of family involvement unexamined.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine other incident types and audiences, additional attributional dimensions (stability, locus), and how repeated incidents affect the benefit-of-the-doubt effect over time. Researchers could study downstream reactions (accountability, anger, moral hypocrisy), antecedents of controllability perceptions, alternative outcomes (purchase intention, willingness to pay, brand loyalty), varying forms of family involvement and cultural contexts, and recovery and apology strategies for rebuilding trust after controllable incidents.
