# Symbol or Substance? Environmental Protection and International Expansion in Emerging Market Multinationals

**Abstract**
Despite growing attention to the environment decoupling behaviors of emerging market multinational enterprises (EMMNEs) in reconciling the resource dilemmas of responding to institutional pressures and achieving profit maximization, there is limited focus on how different environmental management practices affect EM-MNEs’ international expansion. We argue that symbolic environmental protection of EM-MNEs can effectively balance institutional pressures and economic efficiency, thereby facilitating their international expansion. In contrast, substantive environmental protection introduces risks of sacrificing economic benefits in response to institutional pressures, thus inhibiting international expansion. Furthermore, drawing on recent research on the multi-dimensionality of digitalization in addressing resource dilemmas, we argue that higher digitalization intensity strengthens the positive impact of symbolic environmental protection on international expansion and mitigates the negative impact of substantive environmental protection by reducing both legitimacy and efficiency issues. Conversely, a broader digitalization scope weakens the positive effect of symbolic environmental protection on international expansion and amplifies the negative impact of substantive environmental protection by increasing legitimacy and efficiency challenges. Based on a sample of Chinese listed manufacturing MNEs from 2010 to 2020, we find support for our arguments. This study contributes to the international business (IB) literature at the intersection of EM-MNEs’ legitimation strategies, environmental management practices, and digitalization.

**Research Question**
How do symbolic versus substantive environmental protection efforts by EM-MNEs differently shape their resource dilemmas as they respond to institutional pressures and strive for economic efficiency, thereby influencing their international expansion? And how do EM-MNEs' digitalization intensity and scope differently moderate the relationships between these environmental management practices and international expansion?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Symbolic environmental protection (binary disclosure of environmental concept/policy/governance structure); Substantive environmental protection (log of total environmental protection investment)
- DV(s): International expansion (count of a firm's foreign subsidiaries in a given year)
- Mediators: None directly tested (legitimacy-efficiency balance argued but not measured)
- Moderators: Digitalization intensity (depth of digital-technology embeddedness, keyword ratio in MD&A); Digitalization scope (range/types of digital technologies in MD&A)

The paper integrates sociology-based institutional theory with the economics-based efficiency perspective to argue that EM-MNEs face a legitimacy-efficiency dilemma when expanding abroad. Symbolic environmental protection confers transferable, low-cost legitimacy signals that mitigate the liability of origin while conserving resources, thereby facilitating expansion (H1 supported, β = 0.054, p = 0.015). Substantive environmental protection demands costly, poorly-transferable resource investments whose efficiency penalties outweigh legitimacy gains in foreign markets, inhibiting expansion (H2 supported, β = −0.018, p = 0.047). Digitalization intensity narrows the legitimacy-efficiency gap (strengthening the symbolic effect, H3a; weakening the substantive penalty, H3b), whereas a broad digitalization scope dilutes focus and raises coordination costs and stakeholder expectations (weakening the symbolic effect, H4a; amplifying the substantive penalty, H4b).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances institutional theory by developing an integrative framework that reconciles the long-standing legitimacy-efficiency duality in IB research, showing how symbolic and substantive environmental protection strategically balance dual tensions and extending the decoupling literature from its focus on antecedents to the consequences of decoupling strategies. It also extends the "digital paradox" debate into the IB context by disaggregating digitalization into intensity and scope, demonstrating how digitalization can either mitigate or amplify legitimacy-efficiency tensions during global expansion.

**Practical Implication**
EM-MNEs should weigh institutional and economic pressures when entering foreign markets, recognizing that symbolic environmental efforts can secure legitimacy at low cost while substantive actions face limited cross-border spillover; managers should strategically deploy digital technology—especially deepening digitalization intensity rather than broadening scope—to mitigate resource dilemmas. Policymakers in home and host countries should strengthen regulatory frameworks that enhance transparency and distinguish environmental disclosures from actual practices to curb decoupling and regulatory arbitrage.

**Limitations**
The authors do not measure the legitimacy-efficiency balance mechanism directly, nor the variation of this balance across different global markets. Substantive environmental protection is operationalized only as the amount of investment rather than its specific type, and the difference-in-differences analysis rests on a limited sample of 652 firm-year observations. The study is confined to Chinese listed manufacturing MNEs, so its findings may not generalize to MNEs from other emerging economies with different constraints.

**Future Research**
Future studies could explore the legitimacy and efficiency contingencies of these arguments, such as institutional conditions in home and host countries and the economic conditions of EM-MNEs. Researchers could examine different types of symbolic or substantive environmental protection separately and test their varied consequences, and assess the generalizability of these dynamics to MNEs from other emerging and advanced economies.
