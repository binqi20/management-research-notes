# Second-Order Knowledge Intermediaries and Multi-Country Entrepreneurial Entry into a Nascent Industry

**Abstract**
A quantitative analysis of data derived from 1,996 clean cookstove organizations operating across 113 countries (2013–2019) was conducted to assess whether a greater number of SOKIs in secondary countries was associated with increased entrepreneurial entries. Results support the model; more SOKIs were associated with more entrepreneurial entries, and this relationship was weakened when company and nonprofit SOKIs were imbalanced and when more domestic industry actors possessed overlapping knowledge. This study contributes to the literature on industry emergence by introducing a mechanism of non-firm-led, cross-border knowledge diffusion in mission-oriented industries. It also shows how local entrepreneurs in under-resourced contexts can access global knowledge—not through firms or FOKIs but through SOKIs that extend the FOKIs’ work.

**Research Question**
Can a geographically bound first-order knowledge intermediary (FOKI) support entrepreneurial entry and industry development in countries where it is not directly present, and if so, through what mechanisms and under what conditions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Count of second-order knowledge intermediaries (SOKIs) operating in a secondary country in the prior year (organizations active in both the industry's primary and secondary countries).
- DV(s): Entrepreneurial entries — the number of domestic clean cookstove companies entering a country in year *t*.
- Mediators: None modeled (the FOKI-to-SOKI learning pathway is theorized qualitatively in Stage 1, not estimated as a statistical mediator).
- Moderators: SOKI-type imbalance (|company SOKIs − nonprofit SOKIs|); SOKIs' geographic dispersion; percentage of domestic industry actors with categorically overlapping cookstove knowledge.

The two-stage process holds that in Stage 1 a FOKI accumulates an industry knowledge base in a few primary countries through carrying, co-creating, capacity building, and convening, and that SOKIs participating in these activities acquire transferable industry knowledge and industry-building capabilities. In Stage 2, motivated SOKIs re-enact those activities in secondary countries, lowering local entrepreneurs' costs of acquiring and developing knowledge and thereby enabling entry. Effectiveness depends on complementarity: a balanced mix of company (knowledge-centric) and nonprofit (actor-centric) SOKIs builds a fuller knowledge base, whereas imbalance and the presence of domestic actors holding redundant knowledge dampen the SOKI-entry relationship.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces a non-firm-led mechanism of cross-border knowledge diffusion for mission-oriented industries, extending "born global" accounts of industry emergence by showing how a geographically constrained FOKI can indirectly seed distant industries by activating SOKIs. It extends the institutional-intermediaries literature by demonstrating that knowledge intermediaries address technological, demand, ecosystem, and institutional uncertainty—not institutional uncertainty alone—across multiple national contexts, and it illuminates how under-resourced local entrepreneurs access global knowledge through SOKIs acting as knowledge "carriers."

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers, foundations, and development organizations should invest in deliberately building market-based industry knowledge rather than charity-push giveaways, since strategic, concentrated investment in a FOKI in a few hub countries can yield outsized global impact when those hubs host regionally or globally active organizations able to transfer knowledge across borders. FOKI leaders should produce codifiable-yet-adaptable knowledge outputs, select primary countries by the presence of globally active actors, and empower SOKIs as industry ambassadors through capability-building, recognition, and legitimacy.

**Limitations**
As a single-industry study of a sector with decades of prior donor-driven exposure, generalizability is limited and pre-existing conditions are hard to fully control. The data are self-reported from the Alliance's partner directory, raising the possibility of missing, inaccurate, or biased information, and the analysis is correlational, so causality cannot be established given potential endogeneity (e.g., SOKIs selectively entering countries already primed for growth). Multicollinearity among the explanatory variable and moderators may also inflate standard errors.

**Future Research**
Future work could study an industry entirely new to all participating countries to isolate early-stage diffusion from a uniform baseline, and draw on regional, subnational, or administrative (non-self-reported) data for less biased measurement. Quasi-experimental designs such as matched-sample comparisons or instrumental variables could more rigorously test the proposed causal mechanisms, and longer-horizon studies could examine whether SOKIs remain central to building the industry knowledge base after entry accelerates or whether local entrepreneurs take on that role.
