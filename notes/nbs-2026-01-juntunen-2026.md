# Contextualizing policy mixes – A configurational study on rapid transitions

**Abstract**
Three key findings emerged. First, we contextualize policy mixes to reveal how various configurations can lead to either rapid or slow transitions. Second, we contribute to the research on energy transitions by demonstrating the importance of infrastructural arrangements.

**Research Question**
How do various combinations of demand-pull policy mixes, and infrastructural, environmental, and market configurations result in either rapid or slow transitions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Three sector-specific demand-pull policy-mix pillars (economic [ECO], regulatory [REGU], soft and systemic [SOFT] instruments); remote conditions — energy-consuming weather (ENV), gas network coverage (GAS), district heating network coverage (DIST); proximate market condition — energy market price ratio (ERATIO).
- DV(s): Rapid transition, operationalized as high heat pump diffusion (heat pump market share of accessible housing stock, 2019).
- Mediators: Not reported in paper (set-theoretic configurational logic rather than mediation).
- Moderators: Conjunctural/configurational interactions among conditions (equifinality); infrastructural arrangements condition the effectiveness of policy mixes.

The paper draws on institutional theory and the socio-technical transitions literature to argue that policy mixes never operate in isolation but are embedded in a material and institutional order. Using two-step fsQCA, it separates slowly changing "remote" conditions (climate, gas and district-heating infrastructures) from more rapidly evolving "proximate" conditions (policy-mix pillars and market price ratios). Limited gas network coverage emerges as a single necessary condition for rapid transition, while no individual policy-mix pillar is necessary; multiple equifinal configurations of policy, market, and infrastructure conditions can each produce rapid diffusion. Infrastructures thus simultaneously stabilize the incumbent regime and, when reconfigured, enable change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contextualizes policy mixes by demonstrating that rapid sustainability transitions are equifinal outcomes of policy instruments interacting with market and infrastructural conditions rather than products of any single "winning" demand-pull recipe. It foregrounds materiality—especially large-scale energy infrastructures—as a central, under-theorized force in institutional accounts of socio-technical transitions, and shows infrastructures act as both barriers and enablers.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should prioritize system-level, contextually grounded policy design over isolated sectoral or stand-alone instruments, because sector-specific demand-pull instruments alone are not sufficient for rapid transitions. Addressing infrastructural lock-ins through destabilizing instruments (e.g., carbon taxes, fossil-fuel phase-outs) and improving the cost competitiveness of clean technologies are emphasized.

**Limitations**
The outcome measure captures the speed/extent of technological diffusion but not the full range of socio-technical change, and the 20-case data set means some configurations rest on a single case, limiting the achievable frequency cut-off. QCA also prevented a configurational analysis at the instrument-category level within each policy-mix pillar, and the study omits organizational/individual-level conditions and the middle (strategies, values, behaviours) layer of the transition-pathways framework.

**Future Research**
Future studies should incorporate instrument-interaction effects within policy-mix pillars, add income/purchasing-power and regional-level data, separate climate and energy policy instruments from aggregated market conditions, and examine equifinal transition outcomes that are not technology-driven. Researchers are also encouraged to extend the temporal scope beyond 2019, study other sectors and innovation types, and move beyond the European context.
