# When Do Gender-Diverse Teams Engage More Investors? Evidence of Threshold Alignment Benefits at Techstars

**Abstract**
We find that the number of participating investors has a positive downstream impact, increasing the amount of funding that the venture raises. Results are robust to the inclusion of controls for venture, founder, and mentor characteristics, with clustering at the Techstars cohort level. Qualitative insights from 20 semistructured interviews reveal how interteam gender alignment facilitates interpersonal attraction, whereas intrateam gender diversity enables resource acquisition. We discuss the theoretical and practical implications of this phenomenon for internal-external team interactions in venture funding and beyond.

**Research Question**
How do internal (founder-team) and external (mentor-team) gender-diversity thresholds interact to shape resource acquisition — specifically, the number of investors a venture engages — in the male-dominated context of accelerator-based venture funding?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Founder-team gender diversity (presence/absence at the 30% threshold); mentor-team gender diversity (presence/absence); their interaction; an "alignment" dummy (matched vs. misaligned founder–mentor gender composition)
- DV(s): Number of participating investors engaged by the venture (with downstream amount of funding raised as a further outcome)
- Mediators: Interpersonal attraction (interteam) and resource acquisition (intrateam), surfaced qualitatively rather than formally tested
- Moderators: The founder × mentor gender-diversity interaction itself functions as the core contingency (alignment as the conditioning structure)

Drawing on critical mass theory and the literature on homophily and entrepreneurial tie-building, the authors argue that neither founder-team nor mentor-team gender diversity raises investor counts as a standalone factor; instead, aligned thresholds matter. Founder teams paired with mentor teams of matched presence (or absence) of gender diversity engage more investors than misaligned pairings, and the aligned presence of gender diversity yields the highest investor counts. The proposed process is a "homophily of diversity" advantage: interteam consistency supplies the coordination, communication, and trust of homophily while intrateam diversity supplies the breadth of knowledge, resources, and networks, jointly offsetting bias against gender-diverse teams in venture funding.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces team-level diversity-threshold alignment as a novel form of homophily operating between internal founder teams and external mentor teams, extending critical mass theory beyond standalone teams into a paired internal–external framing. It qualifies the boundary conditions of mentorship effectiveness, showing that mentorship benefits are unevenly distributed and depend in part on gender-composition similarity between founder and mentor teams, and it links homophilous interorganizational partnerships to organizational-level performance outcomes for ventures.

**Practical Implication**
Accelerator managing directors can improve program efficacy by intentionally assigning and training mentor teams whose gender composition aligns with that of founder teams, since aligned pairings engage more investors. Because gender-diverse founder teams are otherwise often "overmentored and underfunded," threshold-aligned mentor matching offers a practical lever to help such teams compete for funding, with downstream benefits for policymakers financing accelerators and regional economic development.

**Limitations**
The observational, nonrandomized design yields lower internal validity than random assignment, so the authors do not make definitive cause-and-effect claims and acknowledge a lack of control over team assignment and outcome observation. They caution against generalizing beyond having one woman on a founder team, to larger founder teams, or to other demographic dimensions such as ethnicity, given the sample distribution and data-access restrictions; female-dominated team combinations were too rare to retain for comparison.

**Future Research**
The authors call for large-scale randomized controlled trials that experimentally intervene via random assignment and capture pre- and post-intervention measures, ensuring sufficient sample sizes for underrepresented (e.g., female-dominated) team combinations. They also suggest accounting for shared backgrounds such as ethnicity, studying the likelihood of initiating homophilous versus heterophilous ties, and testing whether the findings generalize beyond early-stage tech ventures to other advisor–advisee relationships.
