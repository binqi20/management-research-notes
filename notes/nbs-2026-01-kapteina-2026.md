# Why Do Companies Engage in the Creation of Private Governance? A Systematic Literature Review and Research Agenda

**Abstract**
Many companies create private governance—collectively designed arrangements such as ecological or social standards that guide corporate conduct. Yet explanations for why firms engage in such governance creation remain fragmented across a wide range of studies and disciplines. This article synthesizes existing research into a comprehensive framework that integrates macro-level institutional conditions with mesoand micro-level drivers and barriers, thereby clarifying the antecedents of corporate engagement in the creation of private governance. We also develop a research agenda addressing these gaps within debates on political corporate social responsibility (PCSR). While PCSR articulates normative ideals of legitimate corporate engagement, empirical findings show governance creation often serves instrumental reasons. Our framework clarifies why and under what conditions firms engage in the creation of private governance.

**Research Question**
Why do companies engage in the creation of private governance — that is, why and under what conditions do for-profit firms initiate, design, or co-develop collectively designed environmental and social standards that regulate corporate conduct?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Firms' engagement in private governance creation is driven not by isolated factors but by interrelated antecedents distributed across three analytical levels (macro, meso, micro).
- Macro-level antecedents (institutional conditions): institutional differences, change in societal expectations, regulatory uncertainty, political contestation, trust in market-based solutions, and competition law (a barrier).
- Meso-level antecedents (firm/industry characteristics): structural drivers (firm size, supply chain structure, industry maturity), relational drivers (network participation, knowledge spillover, stakeholder exchange, reputational building/protection, risk mitigation, opportunity seeking), and economic drivers, alongside collective-action barriers (free riding, shirking).
- Micro-level antecedents (individual agency): managers' values, managers' belief in win–win solutions, and leadership behaviors (addressed in only 7 of 127 articles).
- Central tension: Whereas PCSR frames governance creation as a morally driven response to regulatory voids, empirical studies overwhelmingly identify instrumental motives (reputation, risk, regulatory and market gain).

The review's central synthesis is that corporate engagement in private governance creation emerges from a multilevel, processual configuration of institutional conditions, organizational resources and relationships, and managerial agency. PCSR supplies the normative foundation — firms act as "norm-makers" or political actors filling governance gaps where public authority fails — but the synthesized empirical record shows that legitimacy-, risk-, and opportunity-based instrumental logics dominate observed behavior, exposing a persistent disconnect between PCSR's moral aspirations and the strategic realities of governance creation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions: it provides the first systematic and integrative review of the antecedents of corporate engagement in creating private governance, synthesizing fragmented interdisciplinary findings into a comprehensive multilevel framework (macro institutional conditions; meso and micro drivers and barriers). It develops a targeted research agenda addressing nine conceptual and empirical shortcomings (e.g., insufficient cross-level theorization, absent microfoundations, neglected moral motivations, nonengagement, implementation/internalization, narrow Western empirical focus). Finally, it reconnects the empirical private-governance literature with PCSR's normative aspirations, advancing PCSR as a normatively ambitious yet empirically attentive theory.

**Practical Implication**
By clarifying that firms most often create private governance for instrumental rather than moral reasons, the review cautions managers, NGOs, and policymakers that participation in multi-stakeholder standards can serve reputation rather than reform and may remain symbolic unless deeply embedded in organizational routines, monitoring, and decision-making. It flags a "dark side" in which voluntary standards can delay, dilute, or preempt stronger public regulation and skew participation toward powerful actors while marginalizing workers, smallholders, and local communities — signaling that the substantive credibility and democratic legitimacy of private governance initiatives must be actively assessed rather than assumed.

**Limitations**
The systematic approach prioritized published, English-language, peer-reviewed research, possibly missing insights from non-English, practitioner, gray literature, or policy reports, and it reflects biases in the existing record—especially the overrepresentation of the Global North and high-visibility industries. The heterogeneity of cases and methods hindered comparisons of specific drivers or factors, and overlaps between antecedent categories can occur despite the authors' efforts to maximize between-category and minimize within-category variance.

**Future Research**
The authors propose a research agenda clustered around contextual factors, motivations, manifestations, and consequences of private governance creation: building integrated multilevel frameworks linking macro pressures, meso forces, and micro factors; strengthening micro-level theorizing of leadership values, moral identity, and micropolitics; revisiting moral motivations and theorizing nonengagement as a meaningful organizational decision. They further call for studying the depth and internalization of implementation (symbolic vs. substantive), expanding empirical horizons beyond Western consumer-facing sectors, and examining the systemic consequences of private governance for democratic legitimacy, institutional feedback loops, and the potential "dark side" of private regulation.
