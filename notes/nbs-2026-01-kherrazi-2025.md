# Organizing Open Social Innovation For Societal Challenges: Unlocking the Potential of Open Spaces

**Abstract**
Open Social Innovation (OSI) is gaining increasing attention in discourses among scholars and practitioners as a new approach to addressing grand challenges. However, little is known about how to organize such OSI efforts. To address this gap, this article explores the role of open spaces, especially fabrication laboratories (fab labs), in orchestrating and scaling OSI efforts. Using a qualitative investigation, the findings reveal key mechanisms enabled by fab labs to orchestrate the OSI process and scale its outcomes. Implications for research, society, and practice are then discussed.

**Research Question**
How can OSI be effectively organized and enabled to tackle grand challenges? Specifically, the paper examines how open spaces — particularly fab labs — enable the orchestration of the OSI process and the scaling of its outcomes.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering condition: Grand challenges are too complex and cross-sectoral to be solved by a single focal organization, motivating a society-centered "OSI 2.0" mode of organizing that cannot be fully designed a priori.
- Orchestrating mechanisms: (1) (re)framing the idea/project to align stakeholders; (2) resourcing — acquiring key resources and inviting stakeholders via global fab lab networks; (3) community building — setting rules, mobilizing volunteers, and fostering inclusion.
- Scaling mechanisms: (1) integrating (and evolving) across local, European, and international communities; (2) commons sharing under open-source/MIT-charter practices; (3) spinning off the initiative into an autonomous values-driven OSI venture.
- Emergent dynamics: The six mechanisms are interdependent (e.g., resourcing with integrating; reframing with spinning off) and shift across the process — community building early, commons sharing later — so the OSI 2.0 process emerges and evolves rather than being predetermined.

The case shows that fab labs act as hybrid open spaces (the "Middleground") that orchestrate and scale OSI by mobilizing dispersed actors and resources, lowering barriers to participation, and enabling commons-based co-production, ultimately catalyzing emergent, multi-stakeholder social innovation around disability.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study offers an updated framework for OSI research, providing empirical insight into the cross-sectoral and multi-stakeholder OSI 2.0 perspective and highlighting the emergent, dynamic nature of its processes versus the organization-centric OSI 1.0. It uncovers interdependencies among orchestration and scaling mechanisms that prior literature overlooked, and enriches the literature by exploring the intersection of open innovation and disability — including spinning off as an understudied element of empowerment distinct from business and social venturing contexts.

**Practical Implication**
For social entrepreneurs, fab labs offer platforms to design interventions, build networks, and rapidly prototype to scale impact. Policymakers, philanthropists, and universities should secure fab labs' financial viability and tools through funding and public-private partnerships. Firms and managers can use fab labs for low-volume repair/customization, to empower disabled employees, and to reinforce CSR, while fab managers gain a life-cycle toolkit of mechanisms from (re)framing to spinning off.

**Limitations**
The exploratory research is grounded in a single pioneering case study, which constrains generalizability of the derived framework. The finding that spinning off is a critical element of empowerment is noted as requiring further investigation.

**Future Research**
Future studies could employ multiple case studies to validate the framework derived from these findings. More research on open spaces is needed, and the role of spinning off in social and disability contexts — distinct from business and social venturing — warrants deeper investigation.
