# Social Purpose Formation and Evolution in Nonprofit Organizations

**Abstract**
Whereas leaders are commonly seen as guarding organizational purpose from drift, our study shows how members in nonprofits protect purpose from irrelevance and inefficacy.

**Research Question**
What dynamics shape the formation and evolution of social purpose in nonprofit organizations? The study moves beyond "inside-out" (leader-driven) and "outside-in" (stakeholder-driven) views toward a processual, multi-actor account of how purpose forms in nascent nonprofits.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / triadic conception: social purpose as leaders' purpose *narratives*, members' *understandings*, and *enactment* in organizational practices.
- Triggering conditions: founders begin with an abstract, future-oriented vision; frontline volunteers engage directly with beneficiaries and form more concrete, present-oriented understandings.
- Productive tensions: discrepancies emerge across "what" the desired impact is, "who" the beneficiaries are, and "how" impact is pursued — rooted in distinct commitments (leaders to the outward narrative; members to beneficiaries) and divergent temporal orientations.
- Process / phases: left unaddressed, discrepancies can produce conflict and purpose fragmentation; channeled, they fuel a distributed search that reorients purpose toward a resonant, achievable social impact.
- Emergent outcomes: purpose is coconstructed and realigned (at "Inclusion," labor-market integration became central while technology entrepreneurship receded).

The paper develops a grounded model in which social purpose is emergent, dialectical, and coconstructed by leaders and members through shifting alignments among narratives, understandings, and practices. Founders' abstract visions generate early enthusiasm and create space for experimentation, while members' beneficiary-grounded understandings drive evolution toward feasibility and impact. Whereas leaders protect purpose against drift, members protect it against irrelevance and inefficacy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends research on organizational purpose beyond corporate and social-enterprise settings to nascent nonprofits, theorizing the dynamic tensions between leaders' abstract, future-oriented vision and members' concrete, impact-focused understandings. It reconceptualizes purpose formation as emergent, dialectical, and coconstructed, challenging "heroic-founder" and purely institutional-conformity accounts, and foregrounds frontline members as pivotal agents who reshape purpose through sustained attention to beneficiaries.

**Practical Implication**
Nonprofit leaders should treat discrepancies between articulated vision and frontline practice not as dysfunction but as a generative resource, granting members autonomy to surface beneficiary-grounded knowledge. Prematurely locking in an "aligned" purpose risks "mission deflation" and blindness to evolving beneficiary needs; sustaining a more ambitious, evolving, sometimes-contested purpose can ultimately create greater social impact.

**Limitations**
The grounded model rests on a single longitudinal case and assumes a significant degree of continuity in purpose narratives over time, stemming from leaders' commitment or stakeholder demands. A broader sample of nonprofits could unpack dynamics under change and disruption (e.g., abrupt funding shifts), which this single-case design cannot fully capture.

**Future Research**
Comparative designs across corporate, social-enterprise, and nonprofit contexts—and between nonprofits and idealistic start-ups—could clarify how organizational nascency shapes the coconstruction of purpose and when generative tensions emerge. Further work could examine purpose alignment/misalignment effects on legitimacy, authenticity, and resource attraction, and investigate "ideological inertia" and "solutionism" driven by entrepreneurial ideology in nonprofit settings.
