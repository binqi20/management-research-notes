This is a book review and does not develop its own causal mechanism. As a critical reading, it foregrounds the process Bullough describes: Britain's post-imperial reinvention as a global financial "Butler," driven by both voluntary "push" factors (lax regulatory frameworks, minimal enforcement) and exogenous "pull" factors (the financial deregulation pressures of the 1980s and 1990s). Innovations such as London's Eurodollar market and offshoring nodes (e.g., the British Virgin Islands, Gibraltar, Scottish Limited Partnerships) sustain an "ethically gray system," while underfunding and a box-ticking compliance culture leave law enforcement unable to investigate sophisticated financial crime.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The review connects Bullough's journalistic account to business-ethics scholarship, arguing that the UK's "butlering" activities violate two core tenets of moral philosophy — fairness and equality (Greenberg & Bies, 1992) — and that they harm global development and good governance by spurring inequality, corruption, and crime. Krammer positions "butlering" as an under-examined ethical phenomenon warranting more academic scrutiny, calling for ethics research that moves beyond conceptual and descriptive approaches toward practical policy solutions.

**Practical Implication**
The review argues for reform: closing regulatory loopholes, enhancing tax transparency, ensuring effective enforcement, and eliminating inconsistent laws and preferential treatment for certain UK territories. It cautions that such reform is counterbalanced by powerful opposing interests and the UK's economic over-reliance on these activities, and notes that organizations often pair tax-minimizing and secrecy strategies with CSR or greenwashing to deflect societal criticism.

**Future Research**
Krammer proposes three directions for ethics scholarship: (1) examine the drawbacks of minimal regulatory oversight and secrecy and the relationship between financial deregulation and societal deficiencies (corruption, crime, inequality); (2) investigate the trade-offs organizations face when combining opportunistic location-specific strategies with CSR or greenwashing; and (3) better leverage civil society and stakeholder mechanisms — including transparency in penalty enforcement and social-media activism — to improve corporate accountability.
