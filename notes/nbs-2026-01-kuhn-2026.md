# Should We Talk About the News? Coworker Discussion and Affective Well-Being in a Polarized Society

**Abstract**
In a sharply polarized society many business leaders discourage workplace discussion of current events for fear of disruptive disagreement. Yet authenticity and openness are widely believed to foster friendly relationships which benefit employees and organizations. We explore this tension by investigating when and why employees discuss politicized news events—news which liberals and conservatives tend to perceive, process, and react to differently—and the proximate effects of their doing so across three studies using different methods and measures. **Research Question**
When and why do employees in a highly polarized society discuss politicized news events with their coworkers, and what are the effects of doing so on their affective well-being and coworker interactions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Within-person state affect (positive/negative); emotional reactions to breaking politicized news; perceived political similarity with coworkers; perceived demographic similarity; work location (on-site/hybrid/remote)
- DV(s): Coworker discussion of politicized news; end-of-day emotional exhaustion; mood effect of discussion; receipt of person-focused support; coworker incivility; burnout
- Mediators: Politicized news discussion (links perceived political similarity to person-focused support); social sharing as an emotion-regulation pathway
- Moderators: Extent of news discussion buffers the within-person link between mid-day negative affect and end-of-day exhaustion; emotional valence of the news event moderates the effect of discussion on mood

Drawing on emotion regulation and social sharing of emotion, the authors argue that emotionally charged politicized news functions as an affective event prompting employees to share their reactions. In a polarized society this sharing is selective: employees disclose mainly to coworkers they perceive as politically similar and expect to react congruently (consistent with the similarity-attraction paradigm), so the discussion that actually occurs tends to commiserate or celebrate rather than spark conflict. This selectivity provides a plausible explanation for why such discussion is, on balance, associated with beneficial affective outcomes—buffered exhaustion, improved mood, bonding, and greater person-focused support.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the social sharing literature beyond work-related events to politicized societal events, showing such discussion can bolster employees' affective resources and well-being. It highlights the underexplored role of shared (and perceived) political identity in coworker interactions, demonstrating that perceived political similarity predicts politicized-news discussion better than surface-level demographic similarity, and connects findings to societal spillover, emotion regulation in context, identity expression, and attraction-selection-attrition (ASA) processes.

**Practical Implication**
Because social sharing of breaking politicized news can aid affective well-being and elicit person-focused support, attempts to ban such discussions may be counterproductive and impractical, and could risk backlash with limited effect since many employees already self-restrict. At the same time, organizations have valid reasons to develop respectful communication guidelines and to be alert that openly partisan managers and broader societal forces may harm some employees' ability to thrive and contribute to burnout.

**Limitations**
As an initial exploration the studies focused on proximate outcomes, so long-term effects of discussing sociopolitical issues remain unknown. Study 3 assessed only the frequency (not breadth) of supportive and uncivil behaviors, and Study 1's reliance on overt-conflict measures plus high pandemic-era remote work may have limited detection of incivility. The research was restricted to the United States, an outlier in affective and ideological polarization.

**Future Research**
Longitudinal research could reveal long-term and dynamic effects of political identity and news discussion on coworker relations and workgroup emotional climates. Distinctions between fear- and anger-provoking news, finer-grained analysis of discussion content, the role of physical proximity amid remote/hybrid work, and other emotion-regulation strategies (news avoidance, doomscrolling) merit study. Cross-national work in countries with different polarization, media environments, and multi-party systems is needed to assess generalizability.
