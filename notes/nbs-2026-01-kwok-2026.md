# Firm ownership and pollution

**Abstract**
We provide a theoretical micro foundation for how much pollution (negative externalities) a firm

**Research Question**
How much of a firm's pollution externality will the firm internalize, and how does this depend on the distribution of ownership shares among its shareholders (and, aggregated up, on a country's wealth concentration and ownership individualization)?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Each shareholder bears abatement cost in proportion to their share but enjoys the (equal) environmental benefit as a citizen, so a smaller owner supports any project with a lower benefit-to-cost cutoff; an owner with share 1∕N has preferences aligned with a social planner (Proposition 1, Corollary 1).
- Decision rule: Under weighted majority voting solved as a Coalition-Proof Nash Equilibrium, the firm passes a project iff b/c exceeds the share of the weighted-median owner α_φ (Proposition 2); firms with many equal owners therefore pollute less.
- Boundary condition: With unequal shares, an inequality-reducing "Robin-Hood transfer" produces three offsetting forces — a distribution-of-power effect (less pollution) versus receiver and giver preference effects — so more equal ownership can counterintuitively raise pollution (Proposition 3); transfers to non-owners weakly reduce pollution (Corollary 4).
- Country-level aggregation: Environmental performance increases in wealth equality (λ) and in ownership diffusion (p); concentrated or individualized ownership raises national pollution (Proposition 4).

The central process is that a firm's abatement is set by its weighted-median shareholder, who — as both a cost-bearer and a beneficiary of the environment — internalizes the externality only in proportion to their stake. Because a 1∕N owner internalizes exactly like a social planner, a firm owned equally by the entire population would abate optimally, which is why representative-agent models of environmental externalities are shown to be internally inconsistent or under-specified.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper supplies a micro foundation linking a firm's ownership distribution to how much of its pollution externality it internalizes, formalizing the role of the weighted-median shareholder and showing that ownership inequality and individualization map onto higher pollution. It demonstrates that standard representative-agent externality models in environmental economics and macroeconomics are either internally inconsistent or not fully specified, since equal population-wide ownership would yield social-planner-level abatement.

**Practical Implication**
The model implies the entry of small, environmentally focused activist owners should improve a firm's environmental performance, since they shift voting power toward stronger abatement preferences. It also suggests the framework applies better to local air and water pollutants than to climate change, because for global externalities even many small shareholders capture only a minuscule fraction of the benefit and thus retain weak incentives to abate.

**Limitations**
The model is deliberately kept as simple as possible and abstracts from features such as buying and selling of shares, alternative (e.g., non-weighted or democratic) voting structures, voter abstention by small shareholders, and voting on bundles of projects under a budget constraint. Empirical testing is constrained because cross-country comparisons are notoriously problematic and a firm's ownership structure may be correlated with confounders such as industry.

**Future Research**
The authors suggest the cleanest empirical test would exploit shareholdings and voting-behavior data on individual owners of individual firms, checking whether small owners vote for environmental projects more often than large owners within the same firm. They flag extensions including share trading with preference change, other voting structures, voter abstention, budget-constrained voting on project bundles, and implications for FDI and the pollution-haven effect.
