# Changing beliefs or changing behavior? Understanding the belief‐to‐behavior process and intervening to curb the impact of misinformation

**Abstract**
We also reviewed individual and social-­structural interventions that are best suited for changing beliefs versus behavior, conceptually integrating interdisciplinary work on behavior change with the psychology of belief change.

**Research Question**
The paper addresses two questions: (1) When should one target belief versus behavior? and (2) what interventions should one use to change belief versus behavior, given that the average belief–behavior correlation is modest and variable?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Beliefs influence behavior only under specific cognitive conditions, so the choice between belief-change and behavior-change interventions should be function-driven rather than defaulting to belief correction.
- Key constructs: belief-­to-­behavior inference; behavioral goal; inferential-chain length (outcome vs. existence vs. descriptive beliefs); memory coupling of the belief–behavior association.
- Boundary conditions: a belief influences behavior when it is connected to an activated behavioral goal, when the reasoning chain from belief to behavior is short, and when the belief–behavior connection is retained in memory.
- Intervention typology: individual interventions (correction, prebunking, shifting cognitive feelings, narrativization, bypassing, self-affirmation, motivational interviewing, skills training, habit formation) versus social-structural interventions (legal/administrative sanctions, institutional trustworthiness, social norms, monitors/reminders, material incentives, social support, access).

The framework synthesizes the belief-to-behavior inference model with interdisciplinary work on behavior change. Because behavioral attitudes, intentions, and habits can decouple from the beliefs that produced them, correcting a belief is not always sufficient to change behavior; interventions must be matched to the intended outcome (belief accuracy versus behavioral impact) and to the cognitive architecture linking the focal belief to the target behavior. Outcome beliefs, which involve short inferential paths, are more behaviorally consequential than existence or descriptive beliefs, so behavior-focused corrections should target outcome beliefs, emphasize behavioral relevance, or include direct calls to action.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends Albarracín, Fayaz-Farkhad, and Granados Samayoa's (2024) framework by incorporating belief-change interventions alongside behavior-change interventions and by applying the belief-to-behavior inference model to the misinformation domain. It brings together a dispersed literature to articulate when belief correction will and will not propagate to behavior, theorizing the previously undertheorized process that initiates a connection between a belief and a behavior.

**Practical Implication**
Interveners should first identify the intended outcome—belief accuracy or behavior change—and select strategies accordingly: fact-checkers and journalists may prioritize correction regardless of behavioral consequence, whereas those seeking behavior change should target outcome beliefs, use prebunking and behaviorally relevant corrections, or deploy behavior-focused strategies such as bypassing, social-structural changes, habit formation, and improved access.

**Limitations**
The paper is explicitly conceptual and analyzes no original data, so its propositions about which interventions best change belief versus behavior await direct empirical testing. The authors note that several reviewed strategies (e.g., bypassing, narrative correction) rest on still-developing or inconclusive evidence bases and should be assessed with actual behavioral measures.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine the behavioral impact of narratives with actual behavioral measures, test whether bypassing is more effective than correction for existence or descriptive beliefs (rather than only outcome beliefs), and investigate the temporal dynamics by which beliefs formed without behavioral goals are later reactivated at decision points to influence behavior.
