# Impact of digital transformation technology adoption on worker wages

**Abstract**
This study examines the impact of digital transformation technology (DTT) adoption on individual worker wages

**Research Question**
How does the adoption of a broad spectrum of digital transformation technologies (robots, AI, cloud computing, blockchain, etc.) at the industry level affect individual worker wages, and how do these wage effects vary across task types (routine/non-routine, cognitive/manual), education levels, and age groups?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Industry-level DTT adoption intensity (employment-weighted share of firms adopting any of nine DTTs), and its interactions with task-type, education, and age dummies; also purpose-specific DTT intensity (product/service development-sales, marketing, production process, organizational management).
- DV(s): Log of real hourly wages of individual workers (CPI-adjusted).
- Mediators: Not reported in paper (no formal mediation tested; the mechanism is labor-demand reallocation via displacement vs. reinstatement effects).
- Moderators: Task type (non-routine manual, routine manual, routine cognitive, non-routine cognitive), education (college vs. no college degree), age group (15-34, 35-64, 65+), and sector (manufacturing vs. non-manufacturing).

DTT adoption acts as an industry-level labor-demand shock: where technology displaces workers it lowers labor demand and wages, and the displacement effect is found to outweigh the reinstatement effect. The wage reductions concentrate in cognitive tasks (a 10-percentage-point rise in DTT intensity lowers routine-cognitive wages ~1.14% and non-routine-cognitive wages ~2.07%), while manual-task wages are largely unaffected. The negative impact is stronger for less-educated and older (middle-aged) workers, consistent with their tasks being more substitutable by recent software-driven DTTs such as AI and big data.

**Theoretical Contribution**
By leveraging firm-level adoption data on a broad spectrum of DTTs (rather than a single technology such as robots) matched to individual worker wage panels, the study shows that the recent wave of digital transformation exerts downward wage pressure on cognitive tasks-including non-routine cognitive work traditionally considered resilient to automation-contrasting with prior robotics/automation studies that found negative effects mainly on routine tasks. This broader, more recent measurement reframes the macroeconomic labor-market implications of technological change and helps reconcile mixed findings across the automation literature.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should design future labor policies to mitigate wage-reduction effects for workers in vulnerable task categories (especially cognitive tasks) as DTT adoption rises, providing support to relieve adverse welfare impacts. They should also reconsider education policies-reforming educational content and rebalancing workforce composition-to better prepare workers for shifting labor-market demand as DTTs substitute for certain task types.

**Limitations**
The task classification mapping occupations into task types is based on existing literature but involves a degree of subjectivity, and possible occupational-task mismatch (routine tasks misclassified as non-routine cognitive) may partly account for the observed wage effects. Although the study considers a wide range of DTTs, it cannot separately analyze the effect of each individual technology due to data limitations, and DTT intensity is constructed largely from larger firms while most KLIPS workers are in small enterprises.

**Future Research**
Future research would benefit from developing more standardized and scientific task-classification frameworks. As more detailed data become available, researchers should examine the impacts of individual DTTs separately, given that different technologies may exhibit varying degrees of substitutability or complementarity with labor, to yield deeper insights into how specific technologies affect employment, wages, and skill demands.
