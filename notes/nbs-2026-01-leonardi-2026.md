# Knowing Enough to Be Dangerous: The Problem of “Artificial Certainty” for Expert Authority When Using AI for Decision Making and Planning

**Abstract**
These findings reconceptualize process expertise as a distinct form of interpretive work that helps maintain useful levels of uncertainty in the face of growing pressures toward artificial certainty.

**Research Question**
How do process experts maintain authority and manage uncertainty when AI-generated representations make complex future outcomes appear definitively knowable?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: "artificial certainty" (the illusion that inherently uncertain futures are definitively knowable); process expertise as interpretive/representational work; the tension between transparency/participation and the preservation of useful uncertainty.
- Two competing orientations: *enhancement* (Ocean) — amplifying the technology's detail, immersion, and apparent authority so representations appear to speak for themselves; *modulation* (Mountain) — limiting detail, maintaining abstraction, and positioning the model as subordinate to expert judgment.
- Three representational practices: controlling the level of detail, shaping stakeholder engagement, and constructing the model's meaning.
- Emergent outcomes: enhancement triggers a recursive loop in which stakeholders mistake representations for reality and expert authority erodes; modulation sustains an epistemological gap that keeps uncertainty alive and preserves the expert's mediating role.
- Boundary / configuring conditions: early design choices (in-house vs. external modelers; early vs. delayed stakeholder input) set in motion divergent self-reinforcing feedback loops.

Through a comparative ethnographic study of two urban planning organizations using the same AI simulation tool (UrbanSim), the authors show that artificial certainty is not an inherent property of AI but emerges from how process experts create and deploy AI-generated representations. When experts enhance the technology, highly detailed and immersive outputs lead laypeople to treat the model as an independent epistemic actor, displacing the expert's capacity to make their interpretive knowledge visible. When experts modulate it, they keep representations provisional and preserve their authority as mediators between the knowable and the unknowable.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to organizational theory in at least two ways: it reconceptualizes process expertise as a distinct form of interpretive, representational work — the curation, framing, and calibration of technological outputs — and it reveals how different orientations toward representations (enhancement vs. modulation) shape decision making under uncertainty. Building on this, it develops a critical distinction between representations *of* the future (which encourage treating the future as knowable and decisions as mere implementation) and representations *for* the future (scaffolds for deliberation that surface assumptions and sustain reasoning under persistent uncertainty), offering a new way to theorize decision making as organizations increasingly deploy sophisticated AI.

**Practical Implication**
Experts who must present AI-generated simulations should calibrate representations rather than choose between engagement and uncertainty: pairing immersive, high-detail visuals with explicit narration of assumptions, limitations, and scenario contrasts can sustain stakeholder attention while leaving room for contingency and preserving the expert's authority as a mediator of uncertainty.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The authors suggest several strategies warranting exploration in future research — for example, planners making the limitations of AI representations more explicit to stakeholders, and developing representations as tools that allow actors to explore possibilities rather than implement predetermined solutions — repositioning uncertainty from a problem to be eliminated to a resource to be embraced.
