# Kafka and Organization Studies

**Abstract**
Organisation scholars frequently refer to Franz Kafka to shed light on various dark sides of organisations, in particular the dysfunctional aspects of bureaucratic organisations commonly associated with the word “Kafkaesque.” In this essay, we take the hundredth anniversary of Franz Kafka’s death as an occasion to revisit his work and life as a source of imagination for organisation scholars. We start by introducing Kafka’s “office writings,” produced during his daytime job as an accident insurance lawyer for industrial workers and, so far, largely overlooked by organisation scholars, as well as facets of his biography. We then propose that a more comprehensive analysis of Kafka’s oeuvre offers organisation scholarship a unique perspective on two pressing, contemporary challenges of organising. First, Kafka’s work and life illuminate the inherent contingency and futility of organising in the face of uncertainty, while also highlighting its necessity. Second, his writing provides a nuanced understanding of enigmatic, inescapable organisations that resonate with today’s digital and algorithmic forms of organising. Through his work, we find examples of individual and organisational acts of resilience and resistance, including a leveraging of bureaucratic institutions to fight inequality and injustice. These themes directly speak to current debates on the role of organisation in times of crisis and disruption, marked by the erosion of democratic institutions, the rise of digital and algorithmic organizing, and ecological collapse.

**Research Question**
How can a fuller engagement with Franz Kafka — reading his literary fiction together with his largely overlooked "office writings" and his biography — expand the imagination of organisation studies beyond the standard "Kafkaesque" critique of dysfunctional bureaucracy, and what does this reveal about contemporary dilemmas of organising under uncertainty and digital/algorithmic control?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Restricting Kafka to a critique of bureaucracy is impoverishing; a joint reading of his literary work, office writings, and biography reveals a deeper, generative dilemma of organising.
- Key constructs / tensions: the simultaneous *impossibility and inevitability* of formal organisation; contingency, futility, and necessity of organising risk; "organisationality" of enigmatic, unaccountable systems.
- Kafka as "bureaucrat-novelist": critic of bureaucracy yet also its defender and reformer, who used bureaucratic and legal tools to protect industrial workers.
- Contemporary extensions: (1) perpetual organising to ensure the uninsurable/unimaginable amid overlapping crises and planetary-boundary breaches; (2) inescapable, enigmatic digital and algorithmic organising (machine learning, AI, "algorithmic bureaucracies") that shifts power away from accountable bureaucrats.
- Emergent stance: the tools of oppression can become tools of defiance, resistance, and emancipation — bureaucrats and scholars as sources of democratic control.

The essay's central process is interpretive and aesthetic rather than causal: by reading Kafka's fiction "less as an object of analysis than a generative mode of thought," the authors argue that organising is at once impossible and inevitable, creating fragile illusions of security that are nonetheless necessary. This dilemma, sharpened in Kafka's treatment of accident insurance as an "oxymoron," is shown to recur today in re-insurance gaps, sustainability disclosure regimes, and opaque algorithmic decision-making that produces organisationality while evading appeal — while Kafka's own humanism and professional activism model reflexive resistance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper repositions Kafka within organisation studies, moving beyond the dominant "Kafkaesque"/dysfunctional-bureaucracy reading by foregrounding his "office writings" and reformist activism. It theorises organising as marked by a fundamental dilemma — its impossibility and simultaneous inevitability — and connects this to contemporary debates on the organisation of risk, securitization, digital and algorithmic organising, and the erosion of democratic institutions, thereby "expanding the imaginary of organisation studies."

**Practical Implication**
The authors suggest organisation scholars can use their professional expertise to expose how democratic institutions are gradually dismantled (e.g., by AI tools that evade democratic governance) and to defend the protective potential of formal organising and regulation often dismissed as "too bureaucratic." They invite scholars to practice, theorise, and diffuse — including through teaching and "writing differently" — methods of resistance to a technocratic order.

**Future Research**
The essay invites organisation scholars to engage more deeply with Kafka's oeuvre and life to "think organisations differently, or anew," in at least two directions: studying the perpetual, self-referential dynamics of organising risk and uncertainty, and examining inescapable algorithmic/digital organising. It asks how writing can be defended as a generative practice against AI tools and what further methods of resistance to a technocratic order scholars can theorise and diffuse.
