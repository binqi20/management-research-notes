**Research Question**
How does an increase in downstream market demand within one technological domain affect the rate and direction of technological progress among firms in adjacent (non-demand-relevant) domains, by reshaping the competitive landscape for skilled knowledge workers?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Affected (adjacent-domain) firm × After-ZEV-mandate interaction (exogenous demand shock for EV technologies)
- DV(s): Firm invention outcomes — patent count (total, core, peripheral), count of new technological areas explored, average patent originality; and net inventor loss
- Mediators: Net loss of knowledge workers (inventors) from affected firms to EV firms (column-decomposed into loss to policy-report EV firms and to other EV-patenting firms)
- Moderators: Affected firm's technological domain (growing vs. declining; renewable-energy domains), firm size, and firm age

Following an exogenous rise in downstream demand for EV technologies (the 1990 CARB ZEV mandate), EV firms increased EV inventive activity and competed more intensely for skilled knowledge workers, whose short-run supply is inelastic. Firms in adjacent technological domains—whose inventors possessed knowledge transferable to EV technologies—disproportionately lost those inventors to EV firms. Because knowledge workers drive both core technological capabilities and the capacity to diversify, the affected firms subsequently produced fewer inventions, concentrated their decline in core areas, and became less likely to explore new technological areas, with effects strongest for firms in growing domains and for smaller, younger firms.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends demand-pull innovation research by identifying an indirect negative spillover: demand growth in one domain can erode technological progress in adjacent domains through competition for a shared, inelastically-supplied innovation resource (knowledge workers). It bridges the demand-pull and worker-mobility literatures to show how a focal firm can be harmed by market and technological changes in seemingly unrelated industries, and it contributes to research on firms' reallocation of innovation resources by demonstrating that resource losses—not just opportunistic gains—shape which firms can diversify.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers designing demand-side innovation policies (subsidies, procurement, mandates) should account for indirect spillovers, since boosting one domain can siphon scarce inventors away from other emerging domains such as renewable energy, undermining overall policy efficiency. Firms should monitor the threat of losing knowledge workers to non-product-market competitors operating in technologically related domains and think more broadly about their boundaries and underlying technical capabilities.

**Limitations**
Inferring inventor mobility from changes in patent-assignee information may miss inventors who moved but stopped patenting (false negatives), though the author argues this biases estimates toward underestimation. The findings may also reflect idiosyncratic features of the 1990s emerging-EV setting (a large talent gap, an unanticipated sales mandate), so negative spillovers could be tempered in technology-push contexts or when labor supply is more abundant. EV firms were identified ex post from CARB policy reports rather than by their ex ante diversification decisions.

**Future Research**
The author calls for work on whether negative spillovers affect all firms equally, the short-term versus long-term effects of such spillovers, how firms can mitigate the risk of losing innovation resources and knowledge workers, and how policymakers might mitigate these negative spillovers when designing innovation policy.
