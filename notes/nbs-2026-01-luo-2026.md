# Stakeholder Satisfaction Interdependencies in 10-K Reports

**Abstract**
While shareholder primacy views emphasize the maximization of shareholder value, stakeholder theory (STHT) conceptualizes business as a process of joint value creation across multiple stakeholder groups. Yet, empirical evidence on how managers interpret and represent stakeholder relationships in practice, particularly whether stakeholder interests are framed as competing or mutually reinforcing, remains limited. We analyze management narratives in 10-K reports to examine how firms discuss the satisfaction of key stakeholder groups. Using data-driven causal structure estimation via the LiNGAM method, we uncover a hierarchical structure of reported stakeholder satisfaction that flows from external to internal stakeholders, with all relationships exhibiting positive interdependencies. Our results challenge the assumption that stakeholder interests are inherently conflicting and suggest that managers frame stakeholder relationships as mutually reinforcing, with shareholder outcomes emerging as a consequence of broader stakeholder engagement rather than as a primary objective. By providing empirical evidence on how managerial discourse communicates positive stakeholder interdependence rather than trade-offs, our study helps bridge normative stakeholder theory with observable corporate practice.

**Research Question**
How do firms represent the interdependencies among stakeholder satisfactions in their 10-K reports — in particular, are the satisfaction levels of different stakeholder groups (customers, suppliers, employees, shareholders) framed as competing/conflicting or as positively reinforcing, and what causal structure links them?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Stakeholder orientation as expressed in 10-K narratives, operationalized as the average hedonometer sentiment toward each of four "definitive" stakeholder groups (customers, suppliers, employees, shareholders)
- DV(s): Reported stakeholder satisfaction for each group, and the estimated causal structure (directed acyclic graph) of interdependencies among the four satisfaction scores
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

The paper develops a two-step construct-validity argument: 10-K sentiment reliably captures management's expressed stakeholder orientation (Step 1, grounded in Affect Infusion and signaling theory), and that orientation translates into realized stakeholder satisfaction (Step 2, grounded in Expectation Confirmation Theory and prior empirical evidence). Applying Direct-LiNGAM to the standardized sentiment scores yields three findings: stakeholder satisfactions are systematically interdependent (H1), all observed relationships are positive (H2), and satisfaction flows hierarchically from external stakeholders (suppliers, customers) to internal ones (employees, shareholders), with shareholder satisfaction positioned as a causal descendant (H3). Managers thus frame stakeholder interests as mutually reinforcing rather than zero-sum, with shareholder outcomes emerging as a consequence of broader stakeholder engagement.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances stakeholder theory by explicitly modeling stakeholder-satisfaction interdependencies across multiple groups rather than in isolation, introducing the concept of a "stakeholder satisfaction hierarchy" that captures the relative salience and directional influence among groups as reflected in disclosures. It supplies large-scale empirical evidence challenging the presumption that stakeholder conflicts are inherent, suggesting instead that trade-offs are contingent on managerial framing, and it links core stakeholder-theory principles (interest alignment, joint value creation) to observable managerial practice in business ethics.

**Practical Implication**
The identified hierarchy, flowing from external to internal stakeholders, offers managers a strategic lens for prioritization: by recognizing which groups exert broader network influence, managers can focus on actions that generate positive spillovers, supporting resilience, legitimacy, and long-term value creation. For policy and governance, disclosure-based stakeholder-network analysis could give regulators and investors a complementary tool to monitor shifts in corporate governance and stakeholder orientation over time and to flag early signals of strategic misalignment.

**Limitations**
LiNGAM relies on assumptions that may not always hold: a linear data-generating process, non-Gaussian residuals, the absence of hidden confounders, and an acyclic (no-feedback) structure, even though real-world stakeholder interactions may be reciprocal or cyclical. The analysis does not distinguish whether observed stakeholder alignments reflect ethical commitments or instrumental motivations, and incorporating firm-level governance characteristics reduces the sample by roughly 70%, limiting the representativeness of that extended DAG.

**Future Research**
Future work could combine causal text analysis with linguistic or discourse-based methods to examine moral framing, strategic justification, or value-based reasoning, and link patterns to external indicators (ESG ratings, regulatory environments, reputational outcomes) to disentangle ethical conviction from pragmatic adaptation. Researchers could also examine temporal dynamics of stakeholder narratives more systematically, apply richer large-language-model interpretations of disclosures, and extend the text-based causal framework to other domains such as corporate strategy, governance, and organizational communication.
