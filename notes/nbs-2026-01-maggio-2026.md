# Second Chance: Life with Less Student Debt

**Abstract**
We exploit an episode of plausibly random debt discharge due to the loss of paperwork for thousands of defaulted borrowers to examine the effects of private student debt relief on borrower outcomes. We find that borrowers who receive debt relief (treated) experience declines in debt balances and delinquency rates on other accounts, and increases in mobility and income relative to those who bear the costs of default like wage garnishment and collections (control). Borrowers in both groups contribute to our findings through different mechanisms. While our estimates may not directly apply to blanket student loan forgiveness, they speak to the benefits of forgiveness in reducing the consequences of debt burden for distressed borrowers.

**Research Question**
What are the causal effects of discharging private student debt on distressed borrowers' credit and labor market outcomes, and through which mechanisms do these effects operate?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Debt Relief × Post — an indicator that a borrower's private student loan was discharged (treated) interacted with the post-discharge period; identification comes from National Collegiate losing collection lawsuits owing to its inability to prove chain of title.
- DV(s): Other (non-student) debt balances; delinquency rates and distress (bankruptcy, foreclosure, medical default); geographic, job, and industry mobility; income and income growth; wage composition and hours worked.
- Mediators (channels): Relaxation of liquidity/credit constraints; debt-overhang relief affecting long-term labor decisions; psychological-distress relief raising labor productivity; possible substitution toward hidden/informal income.
- Moderators: Whether the control group is already adjudicated / liquidity-constrained (ex post and ex ante collections); above- vs. below-median debt-relief amount.

Case adjudication moves the two groups in opposite directions: it discharges roughly $7,400 of student debt for treated borrowers while subjecting control borrowers to wage garnishment, so both groups jointly drive the estimated differentials. The control group contributes via tighter liquidity constraints and debt overhang (garnished wages reduce incentives to earn income that would accrue to creditors), while the treated group actively deleverages, becomes less delinquent, and—relieved of debt overhang and psychological distress—relocates, changes jobs, and earns more. The student-loan debt-overhang logic mirrors the standard corporate-finance problem in which equity holders under-invest when income accrues to debt holders.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides plausibly exogenous, granular evidence that private student-debt relief generates benefits exceeding the relief amount itself, with positive spillovers onto other creditors—dimensions absent from the policy debate. It extends the debt-relief and debt-overhang literatures (previously focused on mortgages and credit cards) to student loans, a uniquely non-dischargeable liability, and disentangles the distinct contributions of the relieved (treated) and default-bearing (control) groups, showing that liquidity-constraint, debt-overhang, and psychological-distress channels jointly produce the observed effects.

**Practical Implication**
Targeted forgiveness for distressed, liquidity-constrained private student-loan borrowers in default (but not yet in bankruptcy) can reduce non-student indebtedness, delinquency, and bankruptcy while improving mobility and income, with persistent benefits and positive externalities for other lenders. The authors argue these results support imposing income-driven repayment for private student loans, easing access to such options for federal loans, and making deferment/forbearance accessible for private loans—while cautioning that the findings likely do not extend to blanket loan forgiveness.

**Limitations**
The data do not directly observe consumption, savings, or hidden/informal income and assets, so some mechanisms (e.g., substitution toward informal income) cannot be formally tested. Wage and income data are available only for a subsample employed by Equifax-subscribing firms, and borrowers can drop out if they switch to non-covered employers, raising potential selection concerns the authors probe in the Internet Appendix. The authors also cannot estimate the moral-hazard costs of debt relief.

**Future Research**
The authors hope future policy evaluations of targeted student-debt discharge incorporate the positive deleveraging externalities for other creditors and the persistence of beneficial effects that they document. They note their setting speaks to targeted relief for borrowers similar to their sample rather than to blanket forgiveness, leaving the effects on broader borrower populations (including federal-loan borrowers) for further study.
