# Tech Ethics, Organizational Values, and Ethical Work: Insights from a Qualitative Study of Five UK-Based Digital Technology Businesses

**Abstract**
The ethical challenges raised by the reach of digital technologies into all aspects of life highlight the moral role of the technology industry in modern society. While there is great potential for good, irresponsible development and commercialisation efforts can result in serious harm. As digital technologies and artificial intelligence become increasingly ubiquitous, technology companies face pressure from government and civil society to take their moral responsibility seriously. This paper takes a novel, virtue-ethical approach to the role and contribution of the values of technology firms. Rather than focusing on regulation, it considers how organisational values might support ethical decisions and moral conduct among employees. The paper presents findings of a qualitative study, which reveals how enacted organisational values within five technology companies support the establishment of an ethical culture that guides moral conduct. Based on our reflective thematic analysis of 30 interviews with UK employees, we identify three themes for the effective use of organisational values and the establishment of an ethical workplace culture: (i) “A lot of really, really good shiny examples”, (ii) Values should ‘talk the walk’, and (iii) “You can’t have a value if you don’t believe in it fully”. According to participant accounts, technology companies can successfully encourage moral conduct through effective use of organisational values.

**Research Question**
Within a broader inquiry into the character strengths most important in the UK digital technology sector, the paper pursues two exploratory questions: (1) Which virtues or character strengths are considered most important for ethical leadership in these tech companies? (2) What organisational practices, both implicit and explicit, cultivate or limit the expression of these virtues in the workplace?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: espoused versus enacted organisational values; internal reputational coherence; moral alignment between personal and organisational values; exemplars vs. anti-exemplars; virtue vs. vice
- Themes (process): (i) "A lot of really, really good shiny examples" — leaders and followers act as lived exemplars of values; (ii) Values should 'talk the walk' — values must authentically articulate and reflect the existing culture; (iii) "You can't have a value if you don't believe in it fully" — personal-organisational value resonance underpins adoption into conduct
- Practices: bringing values into everyday "corridor" conversations; challenging the status quo; questioning and dialogue; embedding values into appraisal so enacting them becomes "part of the job"
- Emergent outcomes: cultivation of epistemic and moral virtues (humility, helpfulness, open-mindedness, curiosity, courage, epistemic generosity); strengthened moral agency; counteracting of vice (hubris, ego)

When organisational values are deeply embedded and enacted—rather than merely espoused—they articulate a tangible culture that guides moral conduct. Through a Neo-Aristotelian virtue-ethical lens, exemplars elicit admiration and emulation of virtues, while value resonance between employees and the firm lets individuals "fully believe" in the values and sustain moral integrity, supporting habituation of virtue and engaged moral agency in an ethically ambiguous sector.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper offers a novel virtue-ethical, internally focused account of how organisational values operate at the local level to guide ethical conduct, shifting attention from external regulation and compliance-oriented "tech ethics" toward enacted values and ethical culture. It empirically substantiates conceptual proposals (e.g., de Rooij & de Bruin, 2022) on collective epistemic virtue, showing how exemplars, anti-exemplars, and personal-organisational value alignment foster virtue cultivation and moral agency within technology firms.

**Practical Implication**
Technology firms can encourage moral conduct by selecting values that reflect human (not merely "corporate") virtues, ensuring espoused values are genuinely enacted and authentically reflect lived culture, surfacing both positive exemplars and counter-examples in everyday conversation, and embedding values into meaningful appraisal processes so that enacting them becomes part of the job. Senior leaders should support employees in developing the epistemic, moral, and service-oriented virtues their roles require.

**Limitations**
Participant recruitment relied on organisational gatekeepers, and framing the study around "character strengths," "values," and "leadership development" may have introduced social-desirability, self-deception, and self-confirmation biases, partly mitigated by including negative/contradictory accounts and assuring confidentiality. Researcher bias is possible, addressed via reflexivity, bracketing, and blind peer review. Data were collected (June 2021-March 2022) before the widespread adoption of generative AI/LLMs such as ChatGPT, so the study could not directly address LLM-specific ethical issues.

**Future Research**
The authors note that the ethical questions raised by the recent prevalence of LLMs and generative AI are now a major focus for ongoing research in the field (e.g., Zhao et al., 2025) and invite further work extending their findings to this rapidly evolving context. They also frame their study as putting "empirical meat onto the bones" of conceptual proposals about collective epistemic virtue, inviting further empirical development of virtue-ethical approaches to organisational values.
