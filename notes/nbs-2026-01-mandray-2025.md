# Distinguishing Three Concepts of the Societal Good: The Managerial Case for Adopting the Thomistic Concept of the Common Good

**Abstract**
How exactly individual flourishing and societal flourishing are connected is a central question in social sciences in general and in business ethics in particular. We argue that the main theories of flourishing have developed along a trajectory going from Aristotle to Aquinas and then to the current, prevailing understanding of the relation between individual and societal flourishing. This relationship has been theorized in terms of the pursuit and achievement of the "societal good." This concept of "societal good" includes three distinct concepts: mutual advantage, the common good, and the collective good. In this paper, we meticulously distinguish these concepts, tracing the concept of mutual advantage back to Aristotle, the common good to St. Thomas Aquinas, and the collective good to Enlightenment and post-Enlightenment thinkers. Our failure to be clear about the significant differences among these three concepts means we have not always understood exactly what is at stake in favoring (explicitly or implicitly) one notion over another. We argue that many business ethicists have adopted a collectivist approach, which, in subordinating ethics to politics, poses a significant threat to human dignity and thus to flourishing at both the individual and community level. Other business ethicists have argued for the adoption of an Aristotelian understanding of eudaimonia or flourishing, but that approach, too, may threaten human dignity. Although Aristotle stresses a politically embedded individual who possesses the authoritative agency to achieve his or her own flourishing while pursuing interests shared with other members of the polis, Aristotle's approach may be less effective than that of Aquinas when it comes to safeguarding the worth of each human being in the modern economy and society. Adopting a Thomistic approach to the common good, an approach which does not make ethics subservient to politics, offers a dignity-respecting and community-enhancing way for ethicists to rethink key elements of business management, including leadership, strategy, organization theory, human resources, and entrepreneurship.

**Research Question**
What are the conceptual differences among mutual advantage, the common good, and the collective good as conceptions of the "societal good," and why should business ethicists and managers adopt the Thomistic concept of the common good rather than the prevailing collectivist (consequentialist) alternative when theorizing the firm?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: The "societal good" conflates three historically distinct concepts — Aristotelian *mutual advantage* (τὸ κοινὸν συμφέρον), the Thomistic *common good* (bonum commune), and the modern *collective good* — which posit divergent products, foundations, scales, and anthropologies (Table 1).
- Key constructs: human dignity and personhood; eudaimonia/flourishing; virtue ethics vs. consequentialism; ethics' subordination to (or independence from) politics; charity and justice as the dual foundations of the Thomistic common good.
- Boundary conditions: Aristotelian mutual advantage is realizable only within a polis/nation-state and privileges male citizens, so it lacks safeguards for non-citizens; the collective good, rooted in consequentialism, reduces persons to aggregable utilities and threatens dignity.
- Managerial domains derived: leadership (sympathetic, charitable leadership vs. functional emotional intelligence), strategy (collegial, person-centered deliberation), organization theory (resisting the Weberian "iron cage"), human resources (dignity, inclusion, salary compression), and entrepreneurship (engaged, community-rooted founders).

The paper's central process is conceptual disentanglement: by reading Aristotle and Aquinas in their own terms, the authors show that Aquinas enlarges Aristotle's politically embedded mutual advantage into a charity-grounded common good extending to all of humanity, which subordinates law and politics to the dignity of each person. Because only the common good (bonum commune) — not the polis-bound Aristotelian good or the consequentialist collective good — secures the infinite worth of the human person, the authors argue it offers a superior, dignity-respecting foundation for theorizing the firm and its management.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a clarifying conceptual taxonomy that separates three frequently conflated notions of the societal good and traces each to its intellectual lineage (Aristotle, Aquinas, Enlightenment consequentialism). It argues that the dominant common-good theory of the firm is an "Aristotelian-Thomist amalgamation" and sketches a "purer" Thomistic conception in which ethics is not subservient to politics, thereby positioning the bonum commune as a virtue-ethics foundation for management theory that better protects human dignity.

**Practical Implication**
The authors translate the Thomistic common good into concrete managerial prescriptions: leaders should practice sympathy and charity (e.g., regular "mini-town halls," collegial weekly deliberation); strategy should integrate stakeholder input through humble, person-centered listening; HR should pursue inclusion, regular and prompt pay, and salary compression to express that the common good is shared; and entrepreneurship should be reframed around engaged, community-serving founders rather than the "Great Man." Organizations should resist rule-bound, system-centric governance that "partitions the soul."

**Limitations**
The authors explicitly bound their scope: "Offering a comprehensive Thomist theory of the firm is beyond the scope of this paper," noting that such a theory is being developed in a separate working paper. The managerial implications are sketched rather than empirically tested, and the argument rests on a normative, religiously grounded (Thomistic/Catholic) framework whose premises may not be shared across all readers.

**Future Research**
The paper points toward developing a comprehensive Thomist theory of the firm (referenced as forthcoming work) and toward elaborating the firm-level mechanisms — leadership tied to subsidiarity, discursive/dialogic decision-making, and person-centered HR — through which the common good can be realized within organizations independently of the political community.
