# How Do Entrepreneurs Form Social Business Opportunity Beliefs? An Opportunity Actualization Perspective

**Abstract**
The opportunity actualization perspective has recently gained attention as a key framework for exploring entrepreneurial opportunity. The perspective’s theorizing, however, predominantly focuses on profit-driven opportunities, limiting its applicability across the spectrum of entrepreneurial behavior. To broaden applicability, we examine how entrepreneurs motivated by social and economic considerations form their opportunity beliefs. We use a qualitative research approach that combines think-aloud protocols and semi-structured interviews with 24 impact entrepreneurs. Our findings allow us to develop a holistic model of social business opportunity belief formation, incorporating market and non-economic considerations, emphasizing the role of non-market stakeholders. We derive important implications for both theory and practice.

**Research Question**
"How do entrepreneurs form social business opportunity beliefs?" The paper asks how entrepreneurs motivated by both social and economic considerations form opportunity beliefs that integrate market and non-market (social, non-economic) preconditions, thereby extending the opportunity actualization perspective beyond its for-profit focus.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: social opportunity belief, business opportunity belief, and the integrated "social business opportunity belief"; opportunity "ingredients" (beneficiary needs, feasible solution; consumer desire, technical feasibility, economic viability)
- Triggering condition: individual alertness to social business opportunities (dual acknowledgement that a social issue exists and is best addressed through business-driven solutions)
- Process / phases: social and business opportunity beliefs are formed separately, then iteratively reconciled through assessment and alignment of (mis-)fit; two paths exist (social-first or business-first)
- Boundary conditions: stakeholder breadth (existence of a broad, diverse stakeholder network) and stakeholder depth (expectation of deep stakeholder engagement), each acting as both enabling and constraining forces

Entrepreneurs first become alert to a deteriorating social condition and to the possibility of addressing it commercially, then form a social opportunity belief (identifying beneficiary needs and feasible solutions) and, separately, a business opportunity belief (consumer desire, technical feasibility, economic viability). To arrive at a combined social business opportunity belief, the two beliefs must be imagined as combinable through an iterative balancing act. This reconciliation is governed by perceptions of a broad (breadth) and deeply engageable (depth) stakeholder environment, which the entrepreneur must perceive as leverageable; absent such perceived support, a state of non-opportunity emerges.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the opportunity actualization perspective from its largely for-profit framing to a more holistic, integrative understanding of entrepreneurial opportunities that incorporates non-market and non-economic considerations. It theorizes the perceived breadth and depth of the stakeholder environment as a supportive yet constraining force in opportunity belief formation, and it integrates actualization into the social and hybrid entrepreneurship discourse, arguing that treating social opportunities as embedded and pre-existing (rather than created or discovered) offers a more appropriate and ethical theoretical framework.

**Practical Implication**
Knowledge of the core ingredients and mechanisms of social business opportunity belief formation can support entrepreneurs and managers in more effective opportunity identification. The holistic framework can also be used by startup-ecosystem actors such as investors and accelerators to assess opportunity quality and venture progress, to support responsible opportunity-belief formation, and to gauge the fit between an entrepreneur's and an investor's perception of an opportunity.

**Limitations**
Because of the qualitative nature and the mainly experimental think-aloud design, the findings are contextually bound to the study design. The study does not provide a comparative analysis across institutional, sectoral, national, or cultural contexts, and with no sample restrictions on industry, age, or venture size, it did not fully account for how these factors might shape responses. The focus is on early ideation phases of belief formation that precede actualization.

**Future Research**
Comparative studies of entrepreneurs' decision-making across sectors (including non-profit), industries, and nations could test the integrative belief-formation framework. Researchers could examine other points on the hybridity continuum and different entrepreneurial identities (e.g., Darwinian, Communitarian, Missionary), exploring how initial motivations and cognitive processes shape whether social or business beliefs come first. Comparing expert versus novice entrepreneurs could further illuminate differences in opportunity belief formation.
