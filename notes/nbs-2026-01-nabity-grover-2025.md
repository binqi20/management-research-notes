# Social Media and Political Affiliation: How Expressing Hot-Button Opinions Affects Raters’ Assessments of Job Applicants

**Abstract**
The platform on which the social media posts appeared (i.e., Facebook or Twitter) did not moderate the influence of viewing the posts on hiring decisions, suggesting hedonic platform generalizability. We explore these findings with a qualitative applicability check conducted using insights drawn from 79 hiring professionals. Taken together, our findings suggest that social media posts about hot-button issues impact hiring managers’ assessments of the hireability of applicants regardless of the platform on which the posts appear.

**Research Question**
How, and on which social media platform, does viewing an applicant's posts about a hot-button political issue (immigration) affect hiring managers' (raters') evaluations of the applicant's hireability, and does the platform on which the content appears moderate that influence?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Actual hot-button issue similarity between rater and applicant; rater identification with the applicant's stance; rater disidentification with the applicant's stance; applicant's political stance (anti- vs. pro-immigration).
- DV(s): Judgments of hireability operationalized as expected task performance and expected organizational citizenship behaviors.
- Mediators: Perceived similarity and liking (the sequential similarity → liking chain of the similarity-attraction paradigm / PAM).
- Moderators: Social media platform (Facebook vs. Twitter), hypothesized to strengthen the perceived-similarity → liking link (Hypothesis 4 — unsupported).
- Controls: Two forms of job-relevant individuating information (academic performance; community/volunteer engagement).

The paper contextualizes the political affiliation model (PAM) and the underlying similarity-attraction paradigm to social media screening: viewing an applicant's hot-button posts triggers an early affective response (identification or disidentification), which shapes perceived similarity, which in turn drives liking, which raises hireability ratings. Drawing on CARE theory, the authors frame disclosure as "showing self" and screening as "knowing others." Empirically, the PAM chain holds (H1a–1d, H2, H3 supported), but platform does not moderate it (H4 unsupported), yielding the novel construct of "hedonic platform generalizability." Strikingly, the job-irrelevant hot-button information swamps the effect of job-relevant individuating information on hireability.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances IS and organizational-behavior research by showing that issue-level political stances (rather than party affiliation) bias hiring decisions through perceived similarity and liking as specified by the PAM, and by extending CARE theory's account of how personal digital data shape human dignity in screening. It introduces the novel construct of "hedonic platform generalizability"—the claim that the nature of the disclosed information, not the hedonic platform on which it appears, drives the influence on off-line organizational decisions.

**Practical Implication**
The authors propose three responses to social-media-screening bias: forgoing information-gathering from hedonic social media, integrating any such information into formalized structured assessments that reduce rater bias, and incorporating the findings into training programs that educate hiring managers on the risks of screening. They also alert applicants—especially younger users and cyberactivists—that hot-button posts can cost career opportunities regardless of platform, recommending private or anonymized accounts.

**Limitations**
Because the study used an online experiment, raters may have altered their answers in ways that did not reflect actual behavior, possibly muffling effects. The study was conducted only in the United States, so results may differ in other political cultures. Only Facebook and Twitter were examined, and the platform was operationalized as a binary variable, permitting only a platform-level (not feature-level) discussion.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how hot-button posts combine with other disclosed information (e.g., multiple issue stances, faith, party intensity, or more benign content like sports fandom) to shape decisions, and how broader political moods and shocking events amplify or dampen these effects. Researchers should also study the applicant's perspective on screening and further test and bound the proposed "hedonic platform generalizability" concept across additional platforms and decision contexts.
