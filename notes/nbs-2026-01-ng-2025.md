# Ethical Leadership in Climate Action: Navigating National Government Intervention, Financial Constraints, and Corporate Decarbonization

**Abstract**
This study investigates how national climate policy instruments interact with financial constraints to shape corporate decarbonization, using data on 12,749 domestic and multinational firms across 51 countries from 2005 to 2022. We show that regulatory enforcement and carbon pricing reduce emissions even when firms face capital scarcity, while targeted subsidies have no measurable effect. The impact of regulation depends on institutional context: ESG disclosure requirements and supportive informal norms such as social trust, individualism, and competitiveness amplify effectiveness, whereas strong shareholder rights or uncertainty-averse cultures substitute for regulation and diminish its marginal role. We also uncover ethical risks in global production. Financially constrained multinationals often reallocate emissions to affiliates in weaker jurisdictions rather than invest in abatement, undermining collective climate goals, while firms subject to stringent regulation gain privileged access to global capital, disadvantaging peers in laxer regimes. By integrating institutional theory with business ethics, our findings show that climate accountability is not only a matter of environmental effectiveness but also of justice and fairness. Well-designed and well-enforced policies, aligned with institutional contexts and coordinated internationally, are essential for ensuring that the burdens and benefits of decarbonization are equitably distributed.

**Research Question**
The paper asks two questions: first, do national climate policy instruments (carbon taxes, emissions trading systems, enforcement mechanisms, and subsidies) mitigate the adverse effect of financial constraints on firms' carbon emissions; and second, is the effectiveness of these policy tools shaped by the surrounding formal and informal institutions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm-level financial constraints (FC, a principal component of the Kaplan–Zingales index, Whited–Wu index, firm size, and dividend payout); national policy instruments (RegStringency, RegEnforcement, carbon tax, ETS, government subsidies)
- DV(s): Corporate carbon emissions (log Scope 1 Emissions and log Scope 1–3 TotalEmissions, plus revenue-scaled intensity); financing outcomes (foreign institutional ownership change, international bond issuance)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Formal institutions (legal origin, shareholder/anti-director rights, mandatory ESG disclosure); informal institutions (social trust, religiosity, corruption, individualism, masculinity, uncertainty avoidance); institutional ownership (domestic vs. foreign, common-law origin); MNC vs. domestic status

The paper adopts a substitution–complementarity perspective from institutional theory: formal or informal institutions may substitute for regulation when existing governance structures or societal norms already induce responsible behavior, or complement regulation by amplifying its legitimacy, monitoring, and enforcement. Regulatory enforcement and carbon pricing relax the constraint–emissions link by raising the expected cost of inaction, whereas subsidies do not. Cross-country heterogeneity in effectiveness is interpreted through whether institutions reinforce or compensate for regulation, and financial constraints are shown to tilt MNCs toward shifting emissions to weaker jurisdictions rather than abating.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study makes three contributions to the literatures on business ethics, climate governance, and institutional theory. First, it provides the first global firm-level evidence on how financing frictions shape corporate decarbonization under different policy regimes, extending largely U.S.-focused prior work to 51 countries. Second, it extends institutional theory by formalizing and testing a substitution–complementarity framework for how regulation and the institutional environment (formal rules, informal norms, ownership structures) jointly determine whether regulation substitutes for or complements existing governance. Third, it connects firm-level decarbonization to global markets, showing that constrained MNCs shift emissions rather than abate and that constrained high-emitters face tighter access to equity and bond financing unless they decarbonize under stringent regulation.

**Practical Implication**
Ethical leadership in climate action requires more than voluntary pledges; it requires credible regulation that holds firms accountable and aligns financial incentives with environmental responsibility, since firms demonstrating genuine commitment to decarbonization are rewarded with better access to global capital. For policymakers, addressing emissions arbitrage by multinational firms is essential to prevent unfair advantages and safeguard domestic progress; tools such as carbon border adjustments can reduce loopholes, but broader international coordination is necessary to ensure the burdens and benefits of climate action are distributed fairly.
