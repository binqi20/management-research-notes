# Datafied Selves at Work: Ethical Boundaries of Surveillance in People Analytics

**Abstract**
In the age of algorithmic decision-making, People Analytics has emerged as a powerful tool for enhancing efficiency and strategic alignment in human resource management (HRM). Yet, its growing influence also introduces profound ethical concerns—particularly regarding surveillance, consent, fairness, and employee autonomy. This paper offers a normative framework that critically examines the ethical boundaries of People Analytics by integrating four foundational pillars: data sovereignty, algorithmic fairness, informed consent, and ethical accountability. Drawing from deontological ethics, virtue ethics, and stakeholder theory, the study constructs a conceptual model that positions HR not merely as a technological function, but as a moral actor responsible for upholding dignity, justice, and trust in datafied workplaces. The paper further explores how People Analytics reshapes employees into "datafied selves," enabling new forms of epistemic injustice and organizational control. A visual framework and practical tables support the translation of ethical theory into applied HR governance. Through this lens, the paper identifies four critical boundary zones—consent, purpose limitation, emotional labor, and accountability gaps—where ethical failure is most likely to occur. Finally, the study provides strategic recommendations, including ethics-by-design principles, inclusive governance, and data charters, while proposing a research agenda to empirically validate and refine the normative model. This contribution urges HR leaders, scholars, and policymakers to treat ethics not as an afterthought but as the guiding architecture of algorithmic people management.

**Research Question**
What are the ethical boundaries of algorithmic surveillance in People Analytics, and how can a normative framework reposition HR as a moral and deliberative agent that upholds dignity, justice, and trust in datafied workplaces? The paper asks not "what works" but "what is right" in the design and governance of HR analytics.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: People Analytics is not a neutral instrumental tool but a moral practice; any responsible use must uphold boundary conditions grounded in justice, dignity, and human agency, shifting HR from "what can be measured" to "what ought to be measured."
- Key constructs / pillars: Data Sovereignty (individual control rights over personal data), Algorithmic Fairness (non-discrimination, bias auditing), Informed Consent (renewable, dialogical participation), and Ethical Accountability (shared, traceable governance). The pillars are presented as mutually reinforcing moral boundaries.
- Theoretical anchors: Kantian deontology grounds Data Sovereignty and Informed Consent (humanity as an end, never merely a means); Aristotelian virtue ethics / phronesis grounds Ethical Accountability (HR leaders as moral exemplars); stakeholder theory grounds Algorithmic Fairness (fairness as participatory dialogue among affected parties).
- Boundary zones: consent, purpose limitation / function creep, emotional and relational labor, and accountability gaps / algorithmic opacity—pressure points where ethical failure is most likely, with salience varying by industry and governance setting.
- Emergent harms addressed: "datafied selves," epistemic injustice (testimonial and hermeneutical), power asymmetries, and purpose drift.

The paper develops its normative model by mapping general AI-ethics principles (beneficence, justice, autonomy, accountability) onto context-specific HR pillars, then layering a discursive/deliberative-ethics requirement (García-Marzá & Calvo, 2024) that consent and fairness be sustained through ongoing communicative processes in which employees can question, contest, or withdraw. Operationalization is proposed through ethics-by-design, HR data charters, inclusive governance, ethics boards, and a RACI accountability matrix across HR, IT, Legal, and Vendors.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper synthesizes three non-interchangeable moral-philosophical traditions—Kantian deontology, Aristotelian virtue ethics, and stakeholder theory—into an integrated four-pillar normative framework that fills an identified gap: the absence of a robust ethical theory of surveillance and consent in digital HR environments. It reframes People Analytics as a moral practice and HR as a deliberative moral agent, advancing business ethics scholarship from descriptive utility toward a critical, dignity-centered account of algorithmic people management.

**Practical Implication**
HR leaders, scholars, and policymakers should treat ethics as the guiding architecture (not an afterthought) of People Analytics by implementing the four pillars through concrete mechanisms: ethics-by-design, renewable and dialogical consent, purpose-specific protocols against function creep, HR Data Charters, inclusive/participatory governance, People Analytics ethics boards, and a RACI accountability matrix clarifying HR–IT–Legal–Vendor responsibilities.

**Limitations**
The authors explicitly state the work is conceptual and does not involve empirical data collection or analysis, so the normative model is proposed but not yet empirically validated or refined. The paper itself frames empirical validation across contexts as outstanding and notes that ethical frameworks risk remaining aspirational and detached from operational realities absent coordinated implementation.

**Future Research**
The paper calls for empirical work examining how the four pillars manifest across organizational contexts, industries, and cultural settings (via qualitative case studies, comparative policy analyses, and new measurement instruments for ethical maturity); longitudinal study of whether ethics-by-design and participatory governance yield outcomes such as increased trust and perceived justice; interrogation of the unintended consequences of ethical frameworks; and critical scholarship engaging deliberative ethics and collective data rights, questioning the epistemological limits of datafication in HRM.
