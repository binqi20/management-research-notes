# Carbon Pricing versus Green Finance

**Abstract**
Green finance—including environmental, social, and governance investing and sustainable finance regulations—is widespread, but can it substitute for carbon pricing in fighting climate change? In a unified model, I show that (i) when carbon prices reflect the social cost of carbon, green finance should not be used; (ii) when carbon prices are too low, green finance can implement the social optimum if each firm's cost of capital can be set to its sustainable discount rate, which increases with the ratio of carbon emissions to firm value. I provide calibrations, analyze stranded assets, and present implementations through subsidies or preferential financing for green firms.

**Research Question**
Can green finance accomplish the same as a carbon tax, and if so, how does a social cost of carbon (e.g., 43$/tCO2) translate into a change in firms' cost of capital, which is measured in different units?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Carbon pricing targets the cost of emissions while green finance targets the cost of capital of emitting firms; a unified general-equilibrium model translates between the two.
- Key construct — sustainable discount rate: the "normal" cost of capital r plus an externality (carbon burden) rate equal to the "missing" carbon tax (St+1 − τit+1) times the firm's emission-to-value ratio.
- Boundary conditions: green finance can implement the social optimum only when the cost of capital can be controlled, firms can commit to their future emissions, and stranded assets are not a severe problem.
- Implementations: emission-based capital taxes, or a voluntary system in which investors internalize emissions as a "negative dividend"; scope 1+2 variants, cash subsidies, and subsidized discount rates for green electricity address stranded assets.

When the carbon price is below the social cost of carbon, the social optimum can still be reached by setting each firm's discount rate to its sustainable discount rate, which makes the firm bear a capital cost equivalent to an implicit carbon tax of St+1 − τit+1; adding the explicit tax τit+1, the firm faces a total carbon tax equal to the social cost St+1. This works because the construction forces market value to equal social value in equilibrium, so a value-maximizing firm chooses the socially optimal investment and pollution policy. The same discount rate also characterizes the return investors require when they internalize untaxed externalities, whether motivated by ESG preferences or emission-based capital taxes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper unifies two previously distinct literatures—the economics literature on carbon pricing and the finance literature on ESG investing and sustainable finance regulation—into a single general-equilibrium framework. Its central new result is the sustainable (externality-adjusted) discount rate, which gives a tractable translation between the cost of pollution and the cost of capital and shows precisely when and how green finance can or cannot reverse-engineer a Pigouvian carbon tax.

**Practical Implication**
A 43$/tCO2 carbon tax corresponds to only a 0.19 pp increase in the value-weighted average firm's cost of capital but a 3.2 pp increase for the average U.S. brown electricity firm, providing a calibrated benchmark for evaluating green-finance policy. Because discount rates are hard to shift voluntarily and face distributional and stranded-asset problems, regions able to impose a carbon tax (e.g., Europe) have a clearer path to a green transition than those relying on burdensome sustainable-finance regulation.

**Limitations**
Implementing the sustainable discount rate requires firms and investors to use it as a forward-looking incentive scheme, which is challenging because the cost of capital depends on the collective actions of many investors with limited regulatory control, and firms must credibly commit to future emissions. The voluntary green-finance channel is undone in part by purely financial investors, cannot sustain the infinite discount rates implied by stranded assets, and is susceptible to greenwashing and corporate restructuring that games sustainability scores.

**Future Research**
The author notes extensions to long-term sustainable discount rates for a green transition, to uncertainty (state-dependent emission commitments), to voluntary carbon-offset and carbon-allowance markets, and to firms' green-versus-brown technology choices, and points to related work (Chittaro et al., 2025; Pedersen, 2026) generalizing the framework with richer input-output structure, short-sale constraints, and multiple externalities.
