# Mapping Heterotopia: Enjoyment as practice in two alternative spaces

**Abstract**
Heterotopias are spaces at the margins that disturb the system from within. They offer a vantage point to explore the affective practices which animate projects existing to challenge the dominant order. In this paper, we are interested in the relationship between heterotopia and enjoyment, specifically the ways in which heterotopias can be sources of enjoyment, while relying on enjoyment as a driving force. Our discussion draws on an immersive ethnography of two alternative projects – the first against urban inequalities and the second against technological dystopia. Data collection involved more than 800 hours of observation, attending dozens of meetings and events, conducting 29 formal interviews and collecting emails and online exchanges. Through our empirical accounts, we identify three types of heterotopic processes that are linked to specific practices of enjoyment: experimenting, articulating and seizing. Placing these in the context of theories of heterotopia, we show how enjoyment acts as a source of strength and potential in the development of alternative imaginaries.

**Research Question**
How do heterotopias give rise to practices of enjoyment through the production of alternative projects? The paper examines the relationship between heterotopia and enjoyment — how heterotopias can be sources of enjoyment while relying on enjoyment as a driving force.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: heterotopia (other spaces that represent, contest and reverse other spaces); enjoyment as a non-calculative practice of othering; the threat of the ephemeral (heterotopias as fleeting, fragile, short-lived).
- Process / phases (three heterotopic processes):
  - Experimenting — enjoyment through illusion: re-appropriating neglected spaces and practices to build an "other than" space within/against the established order.
  - Articulating — enjoyment through otherness: enacting spatial multiplicity so diverse, contrasting identities and practices coexist in one place.
  - Seizing — enjoyment through unsettling/renouncement: framing means as ends; faced with a fragile temporality, actors turn the means they put in place into their own ends.
- Empirical settings: a hackerspace (SouthHackSpace) resisting technological monopolies and an occupied theatre (OccupyTheatre) resisting property speculation and cultural hegemony.

Enjoyment is theorized not as merely symbolic but as a practice that is constitutive of, and fuelled by, heterotopic spaces. Because hackers and squatters organize to occupy and hack spaces and practices, they generate a joyful "illusion" of an alternative order, unify multiplicity without synthesizing it, and incorporate their inherently unsettled nature into how they activate — "seizing fatality as an opportunity." Enjoyment thereby acts as a source of strength and progressive momentum that transcends the pessimism associated with the ephemeral nature of alternative projects.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances understanding of the role of heterotopias in generating alternative projects by examining the coalescence of diverse spaces, practices, temporalities and intentions within one physical space. It foregrounds enjoyment as a driving force that can transcend the pessimism associated with the ephemeral nature of alternative organizations, and consolidates this through a framework of three heterotopic processes (experimenting, articulating, seizing) through which enjoyment, as a practice, is both enacted and generated.

**Practical Implication**
For activists, hackers and organizers of alternative spaces, the study suggests that enjoyment is not a distraction from political goals but a practical resource: positioning enjoyment "as an ethics of life" offers a means of resistance to the politics of despair and sustains involvement even when projects are ephemeral. Such heterotopias carry the potential to instigate change by transforming cultural practices as they permeate public discourse, and their value extends beyond any destabilization of the dominant order their mere existence may cause.

**Limitations**
The authors note that the study of only two alternative spaces does not exhaust the richness and scope of the heterotopology proposed by Foucault (1986), implying limited transferability beyond the two French cases studied.

**Future Research**
The authors encourage further committed, longitudinal and ethnographic study of hackerspaces and occupation movements around the world; research on the relationship between political economy and the formation of heterotopias; investigation of spaces constructed in resistance to established orders (patriarchy, capitalism, imperialism); and deeper examination of the mechanisms of genesis, establishment, decline and dissolution of such spaces ("lifespan" of heterotopias).
