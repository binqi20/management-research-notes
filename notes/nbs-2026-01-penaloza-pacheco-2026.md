# The non-green effects of going green: Local environmental and economic consequences of lithium extraction in Chile

**Abstract**
was concentrated in human settlements as opposed to natural reserves, suggesting a reduction

**Research Question**
What are the local environmental and economic effects of lithium extraction in the Atacama Salt Flat (ASF) in Chile, driven by the global push to adopt electric vehicles? The paper asks whether and how lithium extraction affects water availability, vegetation, economic activity, and population dynamics in surrounding communities.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Exposure to lithium extraction, measured as total EV sales in the U.S., Europe, and China (time variation) divided by the minimum distance from each pixel to SQM's freshwater extraction wells (cross-sectional variation), standardized to mean 0, SD 1; plus an ASF-region dummy interacted with a post-2014 indicator for the groundwater difference-in-differences.
- DV(s): Groundwater static-level index; vegetation (NDVI, in logs); economic activity (nighttime light radiance, in logs); human-settlement population density (in logs).
- Mediators: Reduced groundwater availability → decline in endemic forestry species (Tamarugo) and agricultural crops (Alfalfa) → contraction of agricultural activity and employment → reduced economic opportunity → out-migration.
- Moderators: Human settlements vs. natural reserves (effect concentrated in human settlements); baseline 2013 NDVI (effect larger in greener localities); distance to wells (effect stronger nearer the flat).

The paper argues that the water-intensive nature of lithium brine extraction depletes groundwater around the ASF, lowering the total factor productivity of subsistence agriculture on which local (largely indigenous) communities depend. Within a Roy-Borjas-type consumer-producer household framework, this reduces the utility of staying relative to migrating, so falling agricultural output translates into reduced economic activity and population outflow. A one-SD increase in exposure reduces NDVI by 0.4%, nighttime lights by 2.6%, and human settlements by 1.5%, while groundwater fell up to 15% post-2014.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides, for the first time, causal (rather than purely correlational/observational) evidence on the environmental and socioeconomic effects of lithium extraction, isolating extraction from confounding climate phenomena via an exogenous EV-demand shift and granular fixed effects. It shows that adverse local consequences of resource extraction can arise through water depletion rather than the pollution channel emphasized by prior work (Aragón and Rud, 2016), and contributes to the literature on "North-South displacement" externalities of developed-country environmental regulation onto developing economies.

**Practical Implication**
Cost-benefit and supply-chain sustainability assessments of EV adoption should internalize the local water, vegetation, and livelihood costs borne by rural and indigenous communities around extraction sites. Policymakers in the Lithium Triangle (Chile, Argentina, Bolivia) should anticipate and compensate these communities, recognizing indigenous peoples' special legal status and the ecological value of the salt flat.

**Limitations**
The analysis relies on remote-sensing proxies (NDVI for agricultural activity, nighttime lights for economic activity) rather than direct granular agricultural or income data, and the mechanism evidence on agricultural decline is descriptive and suggestive. The estimates capture net effects, including any company/government transfers and community-engagement efforts, so individual channels cannot be fully separated, and a full life-cycle cost-benefit analysis (incorporating lithium's export revenues and climate benefits) is left outside the paper's scope.

**Future Research**
A life-cycle cost-benefit analysis weighing lithium's economic benefits (export revenue, GDP contribution) and its role in the global energy transition against the local environmental and socioeconomic costs would complement the findings. The implications also warrant extension to the other Lithium Triangle countries, Bolivia and Argentina.
