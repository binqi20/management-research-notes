# Rethinking the Third Mission: Organizing dissonance in transformative universities

**Abstract**
Using this perspective, we assess which organizational forms and governance arrangements enable transformation by sustaining dissonance, identifying heterarchy and an intermediate space between academia and society as central. This delineation sets transformative universities apart from other models and yields far-reaching implications for TM-related policy mixes and impact assessment.

**Research Question**
The paper is guided by two questions: (1) What are the qualities of knowledge processes in the context of TM that contribute to transformation? and (2) How can universities organize a TM that contributes to transformation?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Value collisions ("dissonance") between competing value systems are not byproducts of transformation but its primary drivers; a transformative Third Mission (TM) must therefore deliberately organize and productively mobilize dissonance.
- Key constructs: Drawing on Convention Theory, the paper distinguishes *equivalence-oriented* knowledge processes (shared values, smooth coordination, efficient problem-solving) from *dissonance-oriented* processes (divergent values, problem interpretation, novel solutions), bridged by provisional "settlements."
- Organizational forms: A continuum from problem-solving and matching organizations (TTOs, incubators — minimize dissonance) to transformative organizations (living labs, real-world labs, transdisciplinary initiatives — cultivate dissonance).
- Governance / boundary conditions: Heterarchy (coexisting evaluative principles, situationally shifting authority) and an "intermediate space" between academia and society sustain dissonance; productive use further depends on actors' reflexive capacity, deep dialogue, time, and "multiple insiders."

The framework reframes transformation as the reconfiguration of systems of worth rather than the protection or upscaling of niches. Universities contribute to sustainability transitions when they generate dissonance (a necessary condition) and then mobilize it productively in a "stretch-and-transform" mode (the sufficient condition), challenging and reshaping dominant value systems rather than reducing friction for the sake of efficiency.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper's central contribution is to reposition dissonance — the collision of competing value systems — as the defining analytical core of a transformative Third Mission, distinguishing the transformative university from the entrepreneurial and engaged models not by extension but by its treatment of value conflict. By importing Convention Theory and Stark's concept of dissonance into the literature on universities and sustainability transitions, it shifts attention from institutional rigidities to concrete situations, separates the necessary condition (creating dissonance) from the sufficient condition (using it productively), and introduces heterarchy and an intermediate space between academia and society as the organizational and governance arrangements that sustain dissonance.

**Practical Implication**
Universities and policymakers should design organizational forms and governance (heterarchical, decentralized, intermediate spaces) that deliberately sustain rather than suppress value conflicts, and should resist output-oriented efficiency logics that prematurely force consensus. Because transformative TM cannot be carried by universities alone, support must be embedded in policy mixes, incentive systems, and impact-assessment frameworks that reward value plurality and longer-term outcomes over immediate deliverables.

**Limitations**
The argument is primarily conceptual and therefore subject to limitations, though it is informed by empirical insights from adjacent literatures on the generative potential of dissonance. A further limitation is the paper's implicit focus on universities in the Global North; universities in the Global South often have deeper experience navigating dissonance through traditions of social engagement and regional embeddedness.

**Future Research**
The authors flag four directions: empirically grounding the "intermediate space" (its actors, boundaries, and how universities mobilize dissonance within it); disentangling different types of conflict (institutional, value-based, identity-based) in transformative TM; identifying what organizational conditions enable *productive* dissonance beyond individual actors and methods; and examining how heterarchical coordination and policy mixes / micro-missions can support transformative TM.
