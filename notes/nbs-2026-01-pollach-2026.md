# Sustaining Unsustainability: The Legitimation Paradox of Unsustainable Companies

**Abstract**
Companies with inherently unsustainable business models and high public salience face a paradox, as they need to construct themselves as desirable and appropriate organizations to maintain their license to operate, while at the same time profiting from practices that are fundamentally unsustainable. This paper advances our understanding of legitimation under paradoxical conditions by examining how a highly visible and inherently unsustainable company legitimizes its unsustainability. Based on a micro-level analysis of corporate sustainability reports, press releases, and company quotes in news articles, we identify both traditional legitimation strategies and paradoxical legitimation strategies, the latter of which conflict with other legitimation strategies through tensions related to boundaries, agency, and temporality. Our findings show how paradoxical legitimation strategies enable companies to sustain their unsustainability by engaging in an ongoing oscillation between claiming sustainability and defending unsustainability.

**Research Question**
How does an inherently unsustainable company legitimize its business model in view of the legitimation paradox it faces?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: The legitimation paradox — visible, inherently unsustainable companies must present themselves as sustainable to retain their license to operate while profiting from fundamentally unsustainable practices; nested within a structural and a strategic sustainability paradox.
- Sources of legitimation: Authority, morality, and rationality (after van Leeuwen, 2007), each operationalized through specific discursive strategies.
- Traditional strategies: Endorsing, Re-valencing, Democratizing, and Technologizing.
- Paradoxical strategy pairs: Blurring vs. Forming Boundaries (authority), Delegating vs. Accepting Moral Agency (morality), and Infinitizing vs. Finitizing Processes (rationality).
- Emergent tensions: Boundary, agency, and temporality tensions, in which the firm adopts two interrelated yet contradictory positions on its sustainability.

The paper theorizes that paradoxical legitimation strategies form pairs of interrelated yet contradictory claims that work in tandem to simultaneously defend unsustainable practices and claim sustainability. Individually contestable, together these paired strategies oscillate between contradictory positions in a mutually reinforcing way that renders them uncontestable, allowing the company to sustain its unsustainability through an ongoing oscillation between claiming sustainability and defending unsustainability while deflecting attention from growth and overproduction as the root drivers of its unsustainable business model.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends legitimation research by showing that legitimation strategies do not merely defend organizations against episodic or continuous legitimacy threats but can themselves take paradoxical forms. It develops a model of legitimation under paradoxical conditions that goes beyond an enumeration of strategies to offer a systematic account of how companies construct legitimacy through paradoxical reasoning that embraces contradictory positions in a dual both-and perspective, arguing that considering legitimation strategies in isolation is insufficient to understand responses to the legitimation paradox.

**Practical Implication**
Stakeholders who scrutinize, rely on, assess, or regulate corporate sustainability communication (e.g., NGOs, activists, journalists, ethical investors, ESG rating bodies, and regulators) need to understand how paradoxical legitimation constructs unsustainability as legitimate so they can decode and challenge such narratives rather than amplify them. The findings should alert policymakers to the need for more robust regulation extending beyond product-label claims (e.g., the EU Green Claims Directive) to all forms of direct and indirect corporate sustainability communication.

**Limitations**
The analysis is an extreme single-case study of one highly visible, inherently unsustainable fast fashion company (H&M), conducted at the micro-level of corporate sustainability communication, which constrains generalizability even as the authors argue the paradoxical-legitimation logic applies more broadly. The study does not approach corporate legitimations from a greenwashing perspective and therefore cannot assess whether paradoxical legitimation qualifies as greenwashing.

**Future Research**
Future research could explore the connection between paradoxical legitimation and greenwashing, examining the perceptions of circularity experts, greenwashing activists, and policymakers as to whether such legitimations constitute a potentially new form of greenwashing. This may require expanding traditional conceptualizations of greenwashing beyond false or evidently misleading information to include claims that render the unsustainable status quo as legitimate or attribute sustainability to inherently unsustainable operations.
