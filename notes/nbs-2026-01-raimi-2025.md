# Judgmental Bot: Conversational Agents in Online Mental Health Screening

**Abstract**
Only a fraction of people with mental health issues seek medical care, in part because of fear of judgment, so deploying text-based conversational agents (i.e., chatbots) for mental health screening is often viewed as a way to lower barriers to mental health care. We conducted four experiments and a qualitative study and, contrary to common assumptions, consistently found that participants perceived a text-based chatbot as more judgmental than a human mental health care professional, even though the interactions were identical.

**Research Question**
Why, and with what consequences, do users perceive a text-based chatbot as more or less judgmental than a human mental health care professional during online mental health screening, and what theoretical mechanisms drive that perception?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Agent type (Chatbot = 1 vs. human mental health care professional = 0), holding interaction behavior identical via a common video vignette
- DV(s): Willingness to (a) use the screening service, (b) disclose personal information, (c) follow the agent's recommendations
- Mediators: Perceived judgmentalness; in Study 3, the seven derived mechanisms — real-life experience, surface/emotional/social-meaning understanding feeding deep understanding, and feeling heard feeding feeling validated
- Moderators: Stigma (hypothesized to amplify perceived judgmentalness of humans relative to chatbots; not supported)

Contrary to the "judgment-free chatbot" assumption, participants consistently perceived the chatbot as more judgmental than an identically behaving human, and this perception reduced use, disclosure, and follow-through (partial-to-full mediation). The qualitative study reframes judgmentalness as being "quick to make misinformed judgments," and the experiments show the gap is driven not by behavior but by users believing the chatbot cannot deeply understand the issue (emotionally and socially) or make them feel heard and validated.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper overturns the long-held assumption that chatbots are perceived as nonjudgmental, showing judgmentalness is ascribed to chatbots precisely because they are not human. It reconceptualizes judgmentalness as "quick to form misinformed judgments" rather than moral judgment, and extends the computers-are-social-actors (CASA) paradigm by demonstrating that identical behavior is interpreted differently depending on whether the actor is a human or a chatbot.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should be cautious about deploying chatbots for mental health screening, favoring contexts that do not depend on deep emotional or social understanding. Designers must address users' underlying beliefs about chatbots — not just behavior — and program agents to display deeper understanding, active listening, and validation, while recognizing emotional support from a machine is still valued though less effective than from a human.

**Limitations**
The experiments relied on artificial vignettes lacking real consequences, while the interview study lacked control and risked researcher bias. Participants were exclusively U.S. adults, limiting generalizability to a single sociopolitical, cultural, and healthcare context, and the work examined only screening (triage), not treatment.

**Future Research**
Future work should test the boundary conditions across contexts, cultures, age groups, and countries, and across settings that differ in their need for emotional and social understanding. Researchers should investigate how trust and privacy concerns shape perceived judgmentalness, how to shift users' beliefs about chatbots, and extend the work to chatbot-assisted treatment.
