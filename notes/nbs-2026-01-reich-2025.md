# Homelessness‐based impact hiring and consumers' contagion concerns

**Abstract**
Experiments 4 and 5 demonstrate how social proof may enhance the favorability of consumer response by targeting contagion concerns and therefore reducing disgust. Findings suggest that, despite consumer moral support, marketers cannot rely on halo effects from this emerging practice, while also providing theory-driven guidance on overcoming obstacles to its implementation.

**Research Question**
Why do consumers respond unfavorably to the products of brands that practice homelessness-based impact hiring (IH) even when they morally support the practice, and can a social proof intervention attenuate that unfavorable response by targeting its underlying contagion mechanism?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Homelessness-based impact hiring claim (vs. disabilities-based IH or control / no-IH); social proof cue (present vs. absent); product type (more vs. less contagion-relevant).
- DV(s): Consumer product response — purchase/behavioral intentions and actual choice behavior.
- Mediators: Disgust (self-focused and other-focused), driven by contagion concerns.
- Moderators: Product type (contagion relevance) and social proof.

Although consumers judge homelessness-based IH as at least as morally good as comparison conditions, the unique, pathogen-related stigma of homelessness activates implicit contagion concerns that elicit disgust, which in turn triggers an avoidance response and lowers intentions to buy or consume the brand's products. This disgust pathway can operate independently of moral judgment. The effect is strongest for contagion-relevant products (those involving close, direct, or intimate contact) and attenuated for contagion-irrelevant products. Communicating social proof — signaling that many others safely engage with the product — reassures consumers against contagion risk, reducing disgust and improving intentions, while having little effect in control conditions where contagion is irrelevant.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends the CSR literature by documenting a meaningful exception to the benevolent-halo rule: consumers can respond unfavorably to a brand's products even under otherwise favorable CSR conditions, motivating a more nuanced view that weighs deliberative moral judgment against intrinsic self-protective motives. It advances moral-psychology theory by showing disgust can operate independently of moral reasoning in ethical consumption, departing from social-intuitionist models. It also bridges evolutionary psychology and sociological theories of stigma, demonstrating that a subtle social-proof approach can reduce a contagion-based stigma where overt contact-based approaches may fail.

**Practical Implication**
Practitioners of homelessness-based IH should not take consumer support for granted, since the practice may deter adoption of contagion-relevant products even among morally supportive consumers. By incorporating social proof tactics into marketing communications, brands can communicate the initiative while minimizing its negative impact on consumer response — benefiting society without sacrificing revenue. Social proof may also aid public-policy efforts such as recruiting shelter volunteers or building support for homelessness-friendly policies.

**Limitations**
Experiment 1 observed product choice without examining how consumers physically interact with the product, so disgust was not validated biometrically. The research tested only one intervention (social proof) and does not demonstrate any positive (advantage-conferring) effect of the practice — even contagion-irrelevant products showed no benefit. The authors also acknowledge the COVID-19 pandemic may have heightened disgust sensitivity and thus affected the observed pattern of effects.

**Future Research**
Future work could validate disgust on a biological level through biometric methods (e.g., facial-action coding, salivation) and explore alternative interventions such as opaque versus transparent product or packaging design. Researchers might examine contexts in which employees have no direct contact with the product or consumers, where homelessness-based IH could yield a competitive edge, and further probe potential COVID-related impacts on consumer response.
