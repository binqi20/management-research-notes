# Earnings Pressure and Firms’ ESG Performance: Do Executive Environmental Experience and MBA Experience Matter?

**Abstract**
On the basis of agency theory and upper echelons theory, this study examines the moderating role of executive experience in the relationship between earnings pressure and firms’ environmental, social, and governance (ESG) performance. We argue that earnings pressure negatively impacts ESG performance, as it leads executives to prioritize short-term financial gains over ESG activities with uncertain or delayed financial returns. Moreover, we posit that executives with environmental experience are less likely to reduce ESG efforts under earnings pressure, whereas those with MBA backgrounds are more inclined to do so. Using a fixed effects model, we empirically analyze data from Chinese listed manufacturing firms (2010–2021), finding strong support for these hypotheses. This research enhances our understanding of ESG performance under earnings pressure and offers valuable insights for promoting sustainable corporate development.

**Research Question**
How does earnings pressure (the gap between analysts' forecast EPS and a firm's potential EPS) affect firms' ESG performance, and how do executives' environmental experience and MBA experience moderate that relationship?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Earnings pressure (forecast EPS minus potential EPS, scaled by lagged share price)
- DV(s): ESG performance (Sino-Securities nine-category rating, recoded 1–9), measured at t+1
- Mediators: Managerial myopia (short-term-horizon language in MD&A text), partial mediator
- Moderators: Executive environmental experience (weakens negative effect, H2a); executive MBA experience (strengthens negative effect, H2b)

Drawing on agency theory, the authors argue that earnings pressure threatens executives' compensation and job security, leading these risk-averse, self-interested agents to cut ESG activities whose returns are uncertain or long-delayed in order to protect short-term financial performance (H1). Integrating upper echelons theory, they posit that executives' prior experiences shape how they appraise ESG risk–reward: environmentally experienced executives, attuned to sustainability and identifying with nature, perceive ESG as less risky and resist cutting it under pressure (H2a), whereas MBA-trained executives—socialized into pessimistic, self-interested economic assumptions—perceive greater ESG return uncertainty and cut it more aggressively (H2b). Managerial myopia is shown to be the channel through which earnings pressure depresses ESG.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study identifies earnings pressure (an analyst-driven external force) as a novel antecedent of firms' overall ESG performance, extending a literature that had focused on internal stakeholders (executives, boards, shareholders) and on isolated ESG dimensions rather than its totality. It enriches both agency theory and upper echelons theory by offering a contingent account of how executive experience (environmental vs. MBA) alters the agency problems and opportunistic behavior that earnings pressure provokes.

**Practical Implication**
Boards should reduce or eliminate penalties on executives who miss analysts' short-term forecasts and tie executive compensation to sustainability KPIs to counteract myopia. Firms can improve ESG by selecting executives with environmental backgrounds (and sending managers to Sustainability/Green MBA programs) rather than relying on conventional MBA-trained, finance-focused leaders. Regulators should promote longer-horizon forecasts and stronger ESG disclosure standards.

**Limitations**
The sample is restricted to Chinese listed manufacturing firms, limiting generalizability to services, other emerging economies, or developed-economy governance contexts. Executive experience—especially environmental experience—is measured from publicly available resumes, which may oversimplify the construct, and data limits prevented distinguishing among MBA program types (e.g., Green/Sustainability MBAs).

**Future Research**
Test the hypothesized relationships across other industries (e.g., services) and in developed-economy or multi-country datasets; triangulate resume-based experience measures with qualitative methods such as in-depth interviews; differentiate specialized (Green/Sustainability) MBA programs from conventional ones; and examine whether star versus non-star analysts generate differential earnings-pressure effects on ESG.
