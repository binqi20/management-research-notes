# My Oppressive Past- and Present-Day Vulnerability: Understanding How and When Authoritarian Leadership Adversely Impacts Black Employees at Work

**Abstract**
Our research contributes to the literature centered on increasing awareness, and understanding the impact, of supervisory leadership on vulnerable employees. Whereas recent scholars have explained the bright side of leadership that is helpful and socio-culturally relevant to vulnerable employees, we complement these prior studies by explaining why a dark side of leadership is harmful and socio-culturally relevant to vulnerable employees. We draw on insights from Black employees' oppressive past to shed light on their present-day vulnerability in the workplace. Subsequently, we rely on social information processing theory to explain how and when authoritarian leadership impedes Black employees' ability to thrive at work. We argue that authoritarian leadership is a salient workplace cue that conveys to Black employees that there is significant opposition to them being treated inclusively and fairly. Accordingly, we propose that authoritarian leadership should adversely impact Black employees' evaluations of psychological diversity climate. In turn, as Black employees believe that they are not welcomed (i.e., evidenced by their evaluations of psychological diversity climate), their ability to thrive at work becomes hindered. We also explain why this is particularly the case for Black employees with relatively low levels of psychological attachment to the organization. We find support for our model across a combination of field and experimental vignette studies. Theoretical and practical implications are discussed.

**Research Question**
How and when does authoritarian leadership adversely impact Black employees' ability to thrive at work? The paper investigates which dark side of leadership is harmful and socio-culturally relevant to vulnerable employees, focusing on psychological diversity climate as the linking mechanism and psychological attachment as the boundary condition.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Authoritarian leadership
- DV(s): Workplace thriving (vitality and learning)
- Mediators: Black employees' evaluations of psychological diversity climate
- Moderators: Psychological attachment (first-stage moderator)

Drawing on social information processing theory, the authors argue that leadership style is a salient social cue that shapes how employees interpret the work environment. Authoritarian leadership signals to Black employees that there is opposition to their fair and inclusive treatment, a cue made culturally salient by Black employees' history of slavery and systemic oppression in the United States. This cue lowers their evaluations of psychological diversity climate, which in turn hinders their ability to thrive at work. The negative authoritarian-leadership-to-climate relationship (and the downstream indirect effect on thriving) is stronger when Black employees' psychological attachment to the organization is relatively low, yielding a first-stage moderated-mediation model.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions: it extends the supervisor-leadership-and-vulnerable-employees literature by adding authoritarian leadership as a harmful, socio-culturally relevant style (complementing prior work on the "bright side"); it extends research on factors shaping vulnerable employees' thriving by foregrounding psychological diversity climate; and it shows when psychological attachment moderates a leadership style's effect on employee attitudes. It also affirms social information processing theory as a useful lens for explaining how authoritarian leadership operates and contributes to Black scholarship and business-ethics research on oppressive leadership.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations risk superficial allyship if they issue diversity statements while tolerating authoritarian supervisors without oversight. Because Black employees' thriving is directly linked to psychological diversity climate, organizations should attend to factors that raise or lower that climate and build training and selection/promotion processes that screen out dark-triad-linked authoritarian tendencies while cultivating an ethical-leadership norm celebrated through reward and recognition.

**Limitations**
Study 1 is single-source and cross-sectional, raising common-method-variance and causality concerns, and controls only for organizational tenure, supervisor tenure, sex, and industry. Studies 2 and 3 have small samples (N = 33 and N = 54), rely on experimental vignettes (external-validity concern), and use single-item dependent-variable measures. Convenience sampling of Black working adults may limit generalizability to broader populations.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine how cultural variations within Black communities (region, social class, ethnicity) shape responses to authoritarian leadership, and whether authoritarian leadership similarly undermines other vulnerable groups such as women or refugee employees. Researchers could study additional harmful outcomes (mental health, withdrawal, counterproductive behaviors), test other moderators (e.g., Black employee resilience) and intersectional perspectives, and investigate how colonial legacies and intergenerational transmission of systemic racism operate in the workplace.
