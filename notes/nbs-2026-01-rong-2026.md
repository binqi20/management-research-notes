# The data-based power of big-tech multinational enterprises

**Abstract**
Despite the increasing recognition of digital globalization in international business (IB) literature, there is a more pressing need for a comprehensive understanding of the unprecedented power generated by big-tech multinational enterprises (MNEs). This paper suggests that harnessing data ubiquity globally, instantaneously, and continuously represents a defining feature and a revolutionary power source for big-tech MNEs. By leveraging global data value chains through a mutually reinforcing data-AI feedback loop of data access, analysis, and application, big-tech MNEs can reshape IB practices and challenge global regulatory frameworks. Drawing on power-dependence theory, our research framework highlights a logic of power mutuality characterized by the interplay between power generation and power counterbalance. These two forces provide a holistic lens for examining how big-tech MNEs create data-based power, but are simultaneously countered by the economic and sociopolitical stakeholders of big-tech MNEs. We propose promising avenues for future research on MNEs' data strategies, global data value chains, foreignness and liabilities of data, and global data governance.

**Research Question**
How is power generated from the ubiquity of global data for big-tech MNEs, and how do various stakeholders—such as foreign users and governments—respond to and counterbalance this power across global data value chains (GDVCs)?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Big-tech MNEs derive a new source of power from harnessing data ubiquity across global data value chains (GDVCs), comprising the interplay of three interconnected processes—data access, data analysis, and data application.
- Key constructs: power mutuality (power generation vs. power counterbalance); the three attributes of data (virtuality, non-rivalry, externalities); the data-AI feedback loop / data network effects.
- Power generation: data-harnessing capabilities create dependency of users, businesses, and governments on big-tech MNEs, which—per the power-dependence logic—grants the MNEs market, information, and sociopolitical power.
- Power counterbalance: this data-based power is reciprocally dependent on every process and participant in the GDVC, so users (societal resistance), firms (supply limitation, alternative platforms), and governments (regulation, data localization, antitrust) counter it.

Drawing on power-dependence theory (Emerson, 1962) and resource dependence theory (Pfeffer & Salancik, 1978), the paper develops a mutuality logic in which power is a relational property: A's power over B equals B's dependence on A. Applied to GDVCs, big-tech MNEs generate power by creating dependence through data access, analysis, and application, yet remain simultaneously dependent on the consent, cooperation, and regulatory tolerance of the very stakeholders they dominate—producing a dynamic interplay of power generation and counterbalance rather than unilateral dominance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends power-dependence theory into the digital, data-driven IB context by articulating a "power mutuality" logic that explains how big-tech MNEs both generate and are counterbalanced in their data-based power across GDVCs. It defines big-tech MNEs as enterprises whose value propositions are fundamentally based on data-based power on a global scale, and reframes the liability of foreignness, internationalization, and global governance around the unique features of data (virtuality, non-rivalry, externalities).

**Practical Implication**
Big-tech MNEs must adapt their data strategies to divergent and conflicting national data laws (e.g., data localization, opt-in vs. opt-out consent), balancing analytical efficiency against regulatory and national-security constraints. Conventional ("brick-and-mortar") MNEs are increasingly subject to the same data-based power dynamics and should reevaluate data as a strategic resource in cross-border strategy.

**Limitations**
The authors deliberately focus on big-tech MNEs as the most novel and powerful form of MNE and do not intend to provide an entirely comprehensive perspective covering all related issues; the boundary between big-tech and brick-and-mortar MNEs is acknowledged to be blurring. The paper is conceptual and analyzes no data, leaving the proposed agenda open for empirical development.

**Future Research**
The paper proposes a multi-theme agenda: MNEs' data strategies (optimal data acquisition, internalization, data-center location, security-seeking FDI, global virtual work); foreignness and liabilities of data (data nationality, "state-MNE-data compounds," pendulum-like internationalization); orchestration and power asymmetries in GDVCs (data monopolies, core-periphery and "data divide" dynamics); and data fragmentation and global data governance (comparative regulation, multilateral standard-setting, grand challenges).
