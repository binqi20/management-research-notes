# Reimagining ESG: A Tetralemma Approach to Multifunctional Sustainability Reporting

**Abstract**
ESG frameworks have become a central tool for aligning corporate strategies with sustainability imperatives. Yet, by reducing environmental, social, and governance dimensions to standardised metrics, conventional ESG framework risk obscuring the complex ethical commitments that arise from incommensurable societal subsystems. Drawing on Niklas Luhmann’s social systems theory, this paper critiques ESG’s tendency to privilege specific function systems – science, politics, and economics – while marginalising others such as religion, art, and education. We further introduce the tetralemma, a concept from Indian logic, to illuminate novel ways of reconciling or transcending the paradoxes inherent in integrating these domains. By combining a multifunctional approach with tetralemma thinking, we demonstrate how organisations can expand their reporting scope beyond narrow ESG categories, foster inclusive stakeholder engagement, and respond more effectively to morally fraught sustainability dilemmas. This integration ultimately reframes ESG as a flexible, context-sensitive practice that embraces societal complexity and offers a holistic route to accountability.

**Research Question**
How can sustainability reporting better account for the coexistence and incommensurability of multiple moral and functional logics in society? The paper asks the deeper diagnostic question of which dimensions of social life get counted and which remain invisible in existing ESG frameworks.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: By collapsing functionally differentiated societal subsystems (science, politics, economy, law, religion, art, education, etc.) into three macro-categories (E, S, G), conventional ESG frameworks commensurate incommensurable logics and standardise moral imagination, obscuring ethical concerns that do not fit the predefined boxes.
- Key constructs: functional differentiation (each function system observes its environment through its own incommensurable code); commensuration as complexity reduction; the focus-shaping power of metrics; quantification as a technology of governance and power.
- Proposed remedy (two-fold): (1) a multifunctional approach that explicitly maps how each subsystem evaluates a corporate action rather than merging logics into a single score; (2) the tetralemma — "this", "not this", "both this and not this", "neither this nor not this" (and a fifth transcending position) — as a structured grammar for navigating paradoxes without prematurely collapsing them.
- Boundary conditions: critique is directed primarily at rating and integration frameworks, where pressures of quantification and commensuration are most pronounced; the tetralemma is not a panacea and may be exploited to evade accountability.

Drawing on Luhmann's functional differentiation, the authors argue that modern society comprises multiple subsystems with logically dissimilar codes (e.g., payment/non-payment, true/false, power/opposition) that resist seamless translation; any attempt to weight them under one ESG metric inevitably reflects observer biases that privilege scientific, political, and economic codes while marginalising religion, art, and education. The tetralemma extends paradox theory's "both–and" framing by legitimising "neither–nor" and "none of the above" positions, opening discursive space for reframing purportedly insoluble sustainability dilemmas rather than forcing binary trade-offs. Together, the multifunctional approach plus tetralemma logic reframe ESG as a flexible, context-sensitive practice that surfaces cross-system conflicts instead of compressing them into headline scores.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The article contributes to the sustainability-reporting and commensuration literatures by showing that functional differentiation fundamentally complicates commensuration: because Luhmann's theory treats social, economic, and political domains as dissimilar systems, attempts to unify them under a single metric inevitably suffer from biases. By combining paradox theory with the tetralemma concept, it demonstrates how an expanded logic of decision-making lets organisations face moral conflicts without artificially reducing them, and it extends established social and environmental accounting critiques by offering a systems-theoretical explanation for why these problems persist.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can diversify their metrics — incorporating cultural or local-level concerns that escape standard E–S–G classification — to reduce legitimacy gaps and social frictions, while policymakers might revisit disclosure mandates to encourage a more pluralist set of recognised domains. Financial intermediaries and rating agencies could normalise multifunctional metrics by permitting "qualitative expansions" and context-specific data (e.g., art-based or religiously oriented initiatives) as complementary evidence of sustainable operations.

**Limitations**
The arguments advanced here remain primarily conceptual, underscoring the need for empirical research that tests the viability and outcomes of a multifunctional, tetralemma-based approach to ESG. The authors also caution that the openness of the "neither–nor" stance could be exploited as a rhetorical device for stalling or diluting meaningful ESG commitments.

**Future Research**
Scholars might investigate how specific companies (large multinationals or local social enterprises) apply or reject a broader set of indicators reflecting religion, health, or art, using comparative case studies and quantitative projects linking multifunctional reporting to market reactions and investor confidence. Further work could examine how technological advances in data analytics (machine learning, big data) might aid or complicate efforts to track multiple function systems, and what new governance structures algorithmic rating would require.
