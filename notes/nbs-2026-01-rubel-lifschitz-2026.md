# Ethical Decision-Making in Organizations in Times of Conflict: The Role of Personal Values and Collective Victimhood

**Abstract**
This study examined the links between values and ethical decision-making in organizational contexts during a time of escalatory intergroup conflict, when social tensions can spill over into organizational life. Specifically, we compared how personal values are associated with general ethical decision-making and intergroup-related ethical decision-making and tested whether a subjective sense of collective victimhood mediates these associations. Using vignette-based measures of general and intergroup ethical decision-making, we surveyed 272 employed adults in Israel during a major escalation of the Israeli–Palestinian conflict. While general ethical decision-making was positively related to social values (self-transcendence and conservation) and negatively related to individual values (self-enhancement and openness to change), intergroup ethical decision-making was positively related to growth values (self-transcendence and openness to change) and negatively related to self-protection values (self-enhancement and conservation). Collective victimhood mediated associations between personal values and intergroup ethical decisions, but did not mediate associations between personal values and other ethical decisions. Overall, findings suggest that in the context of intergroup conflict, distinct patterns of association between values, collective victimhood, and ethical choices appear across decision types. The paper concludes with implications for organizations operating under intergroup tension and notes the need for strategies that temper collective-victimhood dynamics while fostering ethical decision-making that transcends group boundaries.

**Research Question**
How are personal values associated with general ethical decision-making (EDM) versus intergroup-related ethical decision-making (IG-EDM) in organizations during escalating intergroup conflict, and does a subjective sense of collective victimhood mediate these associations?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Four Schwartz high-order personal values — self-transcendence, self-enhancement, openness to change, conservation
- DV(s): General ethical decision-making (EDM) and intergroup ethical decision-making (IG-EDM), measured via reverse-coded vignettes
- Mediators: Subjective sense of collective victimhood (competitive victimhood)
- Moderators: None (the value type effectively differentiates EDM vs. IG-EDM rather than being moderated); actual harm and social desirability included as controls

The paper argues that the value structure organizes EDM along two distinct motivational principles. General EDM tracks the social-versus-individual focus axis (self-transcendence and conservation positive; self-enhancement and openness to change negative). Under intergroup conflict, however, IG-EDM tracks the growth-versus-self-protection (anxiety-regulation) axis: self-protection values (self-enhancement, conservation) elevate a sense of collective victimhood, which in turn rationalizes prioritizing ingroup security over outgroup fairness and lowers ethical choices; growth values (self-transcendence, openness to change) reduce collective victimhood and sustain ethical conduct across group lines. Collective victimhood mediated all four values' effects on IG-EDM (indirect effects accounting for 38–61% of total effects) but had no role for general EDM, and objective harm showed no significant association with either outcome.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study demonstrates that the well-established values–EDM associations are not invariant: in conditions of active intergroup conflict, the motivational tension underlying ethical decisions shifts from a social–personal axis (for general EDM) to a growth–self-protection axis (for IG-EDM), reversing the roles of conservation and openness to change. It introduces and validates a novel measure of intergroup ethical decision-making (IG-EDM) grounded in real organizational decisions, and establishes collective victimhood as a subjective psychological mechanism that mediates values–IG-EDM links independently of objective harm.

**Practical Implication**
For organizations operating in diverse and conflict-prone environments, fostering ethical decision-making toward outgroup members requires addressing not only individual values but also the broader social and psychological forces—particularly a collective-victimhood mindset—that shape ethical perceptions. The findings suggest cultivating values (self-transcendence, openness to change) that discourage a collective-victim mindset to help sustain fair, inclusive decisions during crises.

**Limitations**
The correlational, self-report design limits causal inference; although a two-wave structure reduced common method bias, it did not establish temporal precedence. Evidence derives from a single field survey within one intergroup conflict, limiting generalizability across cultures and conflict types. The study focused on the majority group within Israeli society, so it does not capture the experiences or ethical challenges of minority or outgroup members.

**Future Research**
Future studies should employ experimental or longitudinal designs to better examine directionality and mechanisms, and should test the findings across diverse intergroup-conflict settings and cultural contexts (e.g., Ukraine–Russia, India–Pakistan). Researchers could also develop a broadly applicable, rigorously validated IG-EDM scale and compare perspectives across conflict groups, including minority and outgroup members, to build a more balanced understanding.
