# Certified Sustainability: Third-Party Certification of Sustainable Ventures and its Relationship to Venture Funding

**Abstract**
This study explores how sustainability certifications help attract investor funding to sustainable ventures, depending on the venture's stage at the time of certification. Sustainable ventures secure financing at every stage to build viable businesses and achieve ethical missions, such as addressing climate change and social inequalities. To signal their genuine commitment to sustainability efforts, these ventures seek third-party certifications. Research has investigated third-party certification as a means to reduce information asymmetries between ventures and investors. However, it has yielded ambiguous results regarding the financial implications of such certifications for sustainable ventures. In our study, we use propensity score matching on a sample of 748 certified and comparable non-certified sustainable ventures. We examine the role of sustainability certifications in attracting investor funding and the moderating role of venture stage at certification. Drawing on signaling theory, we show that the positive relationship between sustainability certification and funding weakens at later venture stages. Our findings highlight third-party sustainability certification as a key signal in venture financing, enabling sustainable ventures to prove the materiality of their sustainability efforts and increase funding. We thus advance the literature on business ethics and sustainable finance.

**Research Question**
Do third-party sustainability certifications enhance the funding outcome of certified sustainable ventures compared to non-certified sustainable ventures, and how does the venture stage at the time of certification moderate this relationship?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Sustainability certification (binary; 1 = B Corp certified, 0 = non-certified)
- DV(s): Venture funding (USD millions acquired within a three-year observation period)
- Mediators: None (mechanism theorized but not empirically tested as a mediation)
- Moderators: Venture stage (continuous, 1 = earliest "early stage" to 5 = "post-IPO stage")

Drawing on signaling theory, the authors argue that a B Corp sustainability certification is a costly, credible, and observable third-party signal that reduces the severe information asymmetry between sustainable ventures and investors, signaling the materiality of a venture's sustainability commitment beyond mere discourse or greenwashing. Because the certification demonstrates genuine, hard-to-imitate commitment, investors allocate more funding to certified than to comparable non-certified ventures (H1). As ventures mature, however, the certification signal becomes less costly, less credible, and less observable—more financial slack lowers its cost, redundant performance information lowers its credibility, and signal noise lowers its observability—so the positive certification-funding relationship weakens at later stages (H2).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the instrumental societal logic of business ethics by showing that third-party-certified ethical standards positively influence venture funding, helping alleviate the founder's tension between investing scarce resources in certification versus building the venture. It advances the sustainable-finance literature by isolating the funding effect of certification within a sample restricted exclusively to sustainable ventures, and by introducing signal timing—the venture stage at certification—as a boundary condition that conditions when sustainability signals pay off across the venture life cycle.

**Practical Implication**
Sustainable ventures should obtain third-party certification, ideally at early stages, to secure funding that finances their ethical mission, and can strategically combine internal (self-proclaimed) and external (certified) sustainability signals. Investors are advised to incentivize early-stage certification to attract syndicate co-investors, and policymakers can encourage certification through tax benefits, government procurement, or support networks and workshops that ease the certification process.

**Limitations**
Although PSM and the Heckman/2SLS corrections address observed selection and endogeneity, unobserved variables (e.g., financial slack, founder networks, other certifications) may still bias the likelihood of certification and funding. The authors theorize but do not empirically test why certification signals materiality—they cannot explain the underlying mechanism—and they study only the B Corp certification, which holistically assesses ventures and may differ from product-based or regional certifications.

**Future Research**
Future studies should use (quasi-)experimental designs with real investors and mixed-method/interview approaches to uncover the mechanisms by which certification increases funding. Scholars could examine other certifications (e.g., Green Seal, ISO 26000, Climate Neutral, EU Ecolabel), split sustainability into distinct impact areas, analyze venture scores below 80, and conduct more granular analyses of diverse funding sources and investor types.
