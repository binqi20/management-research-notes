# Degrowth and Organization Studies

**Abstract**
This essay explores the relevance of degrowth for organization studies, questioning the institutionalization of growth as a taken-for-granted organizational and societal goal. Drawing on historical and contemporary examples, we induce four organizing principles—resizing, decelerating tempo, regenerating sufficiency, and governing trade-offs. These principles reveal how organizations can prioritize ecological sustainability and social well-being over perpetual expansion. We emphasize that degrowth is not about shrinking for its own sake, but about rethinking growth as a contingent, context-sensitive outcome rather than a normative imperative. By highlighting practices that operate within, alongside, or against dominant institutions, we show how degrowth organizing provides empirical grounding for post-growth imaginaries. Our aim is to catalyze a broader research program that considers how organizing can unfold differently within the space between a social foundation and an ecological ceiling.

**Research Question**
How can organization studies theorize and imagine degrowth—an intentional reduction in material and energy throughput—when growth is so deeply institutionalized, and what organizing principles can be induced from small-scale instantiations to envision what degrowth organizing might look like?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Growth is an institutionalized, taken-for-granted norm (measured via GDP and shareholder value) rather than an inherent or inevitable feature of organizing; degrowth scrutinizes this status rather than rejecting growth outright.
- Key constructs: degrowth micro-instantiations ("small wins"); the space between a social foundation and an ecological ceiling (doughnut economics); growth as a strategic decision rather than a given.
- Organizing principles (induced from past/present examples): resizing organizations and organizing; decelerating tempo; regenerating sufficiency; governing trade-offs.
- Boundary conditions: ventures often remain dependent on growth-rooted systems (funding models, supply chains, legal structures), raising the question of how degrowth organizing sustains itself within, alongside, or against dominant institutions.

The authors theorize inductively: rather than testing variables, they assemble historical and contemporary micro-instantiations of constrained-growth organizing (agrarian commons, Ostrom's managed commons, eco-communities such as Kan Pasqual and Can Masdeu, and firms such as the Keiunkan hotel, Fairphone, and Radically Open Security) and derive four organizing principles from their shared features. The central claim is that organizational designs enabling resizing, decelerating, regenerating sufficiency, and governing trade-offs are more likely to de-emphasize "growthism" and center well-being, sufficiency, and ecological fit, with isomorphism (not only disruptive institutional work) potentially rendering such experiments visible and legitimate over time.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The essay shifts the degrowth conversation from the "why" to the "how" of degrowth organizing, offering four inductively derived organizing principles as a research agenda for organization studies. It reframes growth as a contingent, context-sensitive outcome rather than a normative imperative, and provokes reconsideration of institutional theory (e.g., whether isomorphism developed for growth-driven organizations applies under degrowth), practice theory and measurement, and resource dependence as a theory of power—proposing "a-growth" as an absence of belief that growth is necessarily good.

**Practical Implication**
Managers and organizations can treat growth as a strategic decision (how big should we grow) rather than a default imperative, designing governance "guardrails" that protect both an ecological ceiling and a social foundation. Practices include scaling deep rather than up, slowing product turnover (e.g., durable, repairable design), reclaiming and sharing commons to create sufficiency, and substituting judgment for metric-driven measures of success.

**Limitations**
The paper is a conceptual essay drawing on a non-comprehensive set of illustrative, small-scale micro-instantiations, which the authors acknowledge may "independently seem unrealistic, unscalable or even undesirable" and are offered as conceptual fodder rather than templates to be followed precisely. It does not empirically test the proposed principles or their scalability within growth-oriented institutional environments.

**Future Research**
The authors call for a broad program studying the antecedents, mechanisms, and outcomes of "small wins" rather than grand challenges; how local experiments coalesce into institutional change via isomorphism; how organizations might measure success beyond growth (or whether to measure at all); resource-dependence questions of when sharing reduces individual power but increases collective power; and what governance "guardrails," guardians, and means degrowth organizing requires.
