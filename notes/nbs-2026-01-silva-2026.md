# Managing multi-stakeholder co-creation to address grand challenges: The role of paradox management capabilities

**Abstract**
Our findings advance paradox theory by articulating six higher-order paradox-management capabilities needed to manage paradoxes relating to two levels of transformative innovation, project co-creation and programme co-creation. For each of these higher-order capabilities, we identify more specific routine capabilities that allow partners to manage these paradoxes to achieve swift and successful co-creation for disruptive sustainability. We provide implications for policy and practice.

**Research Question**
What are the paradox management capabilities that enable multi-stakeholder co-creation networks — set up in the context of initiatives directed at addressing Grand Challenges — to swiftly and successfully achieve their objectives?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Organizational paradoxes (performing, organizing, belonging, learning) arising in multi-stakeholder co-creation networks, mapped onto the defining features of Grand Challenges (complexity, uncertainty, large scale, urgency).
- Levels of analysis: Two distinct levels of transformative innovation — programme co-creation (orchestrating a portfolio of interrelated projects) and project co-creation (time-bound collaboration on a defined deliverable).
- Higher-order capabilities (programme level): macro- and meso-level; top-down and bottom-up; cross-departmental paradox management capabilities.
- Higher-order capabilities (project level): social and business; effectuation and causation; short-term and long-term paradox management capabilities.
- Routine capabilities: Each higher-order capability decomposes into three operational routine capabilities (e.g., integrating a portfolio of projects, horizontal coordination, repurposing assets, flexible fast-tracked decision-making, swift digital trust building).
- Boundary conditions: Networks established under pressing conditions with little room to design optimal structures ex ante; capabilities partners bring matter more than network design.

The paper develops a capability-based account of how partners manage the contradictory-yet-interdependent demands of Grand Challenge co-creation. Rather than relying on optimal incentives or partnership structures designed in advance, successful networks deploy higher-order paradox-management capabilities — and their underlying routine capabilities — to reconcile competing logics (social vs. business, short- vs. long-term, exploration vs. exploitation, control vs. autonomy). The analysis links the empirically derived capabilities back to Smith and Lewis's (2011) paradox taxonomy, showing that belonging and organizing paradoxes are more salient at the programme level while performing and learning paradoxes dominate at the project level.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances paradox theory by identifying the specific capabilities that underpin the management of paradoxes across organizational boundaries, extending Jarzabkowski et al.'s (2019) work with a granular, capability-based and multi-level framework. It contributes to the transformative innovation policy literature by specifying the concrete paradox-management capabilities (higher-order and routine) that public-sector actors and network participants need, addressing the noted gap that this literature does not engage with what such capabilities entail. It also enriches the inter-organizational and interdisciplinary collaboration literature by showing that the success of co-creation networks hinges on the capabilities partners bring rather than on ex ante network design.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers should integrate paradox-management skills into funding criteria and evaluation frameworks, design support structures (training, facilitation, peer learning) to build these capabilities, and encourage iterative learning and adaptation across both project and programme levels. Participants in co-creation networks should anticipate the paradoxical tensions most likely to emerge at their level and develop the corresponding higher-order and routine capabilities to manage them.

**Limitations**
The study relies on 30 cases of multi-stakeholder co-creation networks addressing COVID-19, and as a qualitative study its aim was to identify (not statistically generalize) paradox-management capabilities. COVID-19 may have specificities (e.g., its scale and urgency) not shared by all Grand Challenges. Although cases varied in scale, the capabilities were consistent across them, so a more nuanced understanding of how scale shapes paradox management was beyond the study's scope.

**Future Research**
Similar analyses of other cases — and quantitative studies — could test the robustness and generalizability of the findings. The framework should be extended to multi-stakeholder co-creation networks developed in the context of other Grand Challenges beyond COVID-19. A dedicated study focused specifically on how scale influences the management of paradoxes would also be a valuable avenue.
