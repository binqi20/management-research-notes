# Climate-Triggered Institutional Price Pressure: Does It Affect Firms’ Cost of Equity?

**Abstract**
We document that climate-triggered institutional portfolio rebalancing affects S&P 500 firms’ cost of equity through climate change price pressure (CCPP). Using a demand-based asset pricing framework, we estimate firm-level CCPP from physical and transition exposures over 2005–2021. A one-standard-deviation intensification of CCPP raises the cost of equity by up to 6% of its average, with banks and insurers as the main drivers. Yet firms do not subsequently improve environmental performance, indicating that the statistically significant effect of CCPP on cost of equity is ineffective to alter corporate behavior. Our CCPP metrics can help policymakers and investors design targeted environmental strategies.

**Research Question**
Does institutional portfolio rebalancing triggered by firm-level climate change exposures affect firms’ cost of equity; if so, does it affect their future environmental profiles; are physical or transition exposures more important; and which types of institutional investors drive these effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Stock-level climate change price pressure (CCPP) derived separately from total, opportunity, regulatory, and physical climate change exposures (textual measures from earnings-call transcripts), constructed as a closed-form equilibrium sensitivity of the stock price to a one-standard-deviation change in exposures.
- DV(s): Option-implied firm-level cost of equity (Martin-Wagner and Chabi-Yo et al. measures) across horizons; and future environmental profiles (green patents, environmental expenditures, provisions, fines, carbon-emission intensities).
- Mediators: Investor portfolio-weight sensitivities (β1) and demand elasticities (β0) aggregated across heterogeneous 13F investors, which translate exposure changes into equilibrium price pressure.
- Moderators: Investor type (banks, insurers, pension funds drive CCPP); PRI vs. non-PRI signatory status; physical vs. transition exposure type; brown vs. green firms (for the environmental-profile tests).

Within the Koijen-Yogo (2019) demand system, each investor's optimal portfolio weight depends on stock characteristics; adding firm-level climate exposures as a characteristic yields a closed-form CCPP equal to the ownership-weighted sum of demand responses divided by aggregate demand elasticity. Because the market is inelastic, climate-triggered underweighting produces negative CCPP, making such stocks riskier and raising their required returns (cost of equity). Yet because environmental reforms carry large tangible and intangible costs, the roughly 6% cost-of-equity effect is too small to change firms' cost-benefit calculus, so environmental behavior is unaffected.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides a microfounded, closed-form CCPP channel that links heterogeneous institutional portfolio rebalancing in response to climate exposures to firms' cost of equity, departing from CAPM/homogeneous-investor settings (e.g., Berk & van Binsbergen). By analyzing cost-of-equity and environmental-profile effects within a single unified, demand-based framework using forward-looking option-implied returns and "soft" textual exposure measures, it supplies an economic metric for assessing whether divestment-driven price pressure can promote climate adaptation.

**Practical Implication**
At current divestment levels, climate-triggered price pressure raises firms' equity-financing costs without altering their climate behavior, suggesting investor engagement (voice) may be more effective than divestment for driving corporate reform. Policymakers and investors can use the CCPP metrics and portfolio-sensitivity estimates to target sectors facing more negative CCPP and to check whether investors rebalance in line with their sustainability commitments.

**Limitations**
Identifying how large CCPP must become to influence environmental outcomes is nontrivial because it requires weighing mitigation benefits against hard-to-quantify intangible reform costs (corporate culture, organizational structure, labor relations). The analysis is confined to large, optionable S&P 500 U.S. firms over 2005–2021, and exposure measures derive from earnings-call text, which may not capture all dimensions of climate risk.

**Future Research**
The authors suggest that results could differ if more investors were to divest, implying value in studying counterfactual higher-divestment regimes and the relative efficacy of engagement versus price pressure. Further work could assess how large CCPP must be to shift environmental outcomes and extend the framework beyond large-cap U.S. equities.
