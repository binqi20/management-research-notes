# Corporate ESG Profiles and Investor Horizons

**Abstract**
We find that long-term institutional investors tilt their portfolios toward firms with better Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) profiles, in the cross sections of both institutional investor portfolios and the ownership of firms. We test whether several theoretically motivated mechanisms can explain this relationship. Our results that long-term investors exhibit patience with firms around poor earnings announcements, but quickly sell portfolio firms after negative ES incidents, support the view that longand short-term investors evaluate information differently. Our evidence shows that limits-to-arbitrage play a role, as we find that investors' ESG tilt weakens following regulatory shocks that shorten their horizon.

**Research Question**
Do longer-term institutional investors have stronger revealed preferences for higher-ESG stocks than shorter-term investors, and if so, which mechanism—differential information processing, limits-to-arbitrage, or clientele-catering—explains this association between investor horizon and ESG portfolio composition?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Investor horizon, proxied by mutual fund reported portfolio turnover ratio, churn ratio, and adjusted churn ratio (and, for 13f institutions, churn-based measures and the Bushee classification); instrumented via the May 2004 SEC quarterly-disclosure mandate.
- DV(s): Weighted-average ESG score (MSCI, with Sustainalytics and Refinitiv robustness) of an investor's portfolio holdings, and firm-level aggregate shareholder horizon; trading responses to negative earnings surprises versus severe ES incidents; fund flows around Morningstar globe ratings.
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Climate Change News Index (CCNI) intensity and Morningstar globe ratings, used to probe the catering channel.

Long-horizon investors systematically overweight high-ESG stocks. The information channel is supported: long-term funds are patient through poor earnings announcements but disproportionately sell stocks hit by severe ES incidents, consistent with longand short-term investors specializing in different information. The limits-to-arbitrage channel is supported: low flow-performance-sensitivity (more arbitrage-resilient) funds tilt more toward ESG, and a 2004 SEC disclosure shock that shortened horizons causally weakened the ESG tilt. The clientele-catering channel finds no support—flow responses to Morningstar globes and the ESG tilt's variation with climate news do not differ by horizon.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper moves beyond aggregate institutional-ownership studies by exploiting the cross section of investors along the horizon dimension, showing that investor horizon is a meaningful determinant of revealed ESG preferences. It adjudicates among three competing theoretical accounts of why long-term investors prefer high-ESG firms, providing evidence consistent with information-based and limits-to-arbitrage explanations rather than clientele-catering, and links these revealed preferences to long-standing debates on long-termism versus short-termism in financial markets.

**Practical Implication**
For asset owners and regulators, the findings suggest that policies shortening institutional investors' horizons (e.g., more frequent mandatory disclosure) can blunt the flow of capital toward high-ESG firms, since horizon itself shapes ESG demand. The results also indicate that long-term investors' attention to severe ES incidents makes ESG profiles materially relevant to a firm's shareholder base and trading dynamics.

**Limitations**
Investor horizon is measured indirectly through turnover and churn ratios rather than observed intentions, and the ESG preferences of fund end-investors cannot be directly observed. The authors note that, given the difficulty of measuring clientele catering, they cannot definitively rule out that the catering channel matters; they simply find no evidence consistent with it.

**Future Research**
The paper points to a potentially interesting feedback loop between long-term investors' portfolio decisions and their voting behavior as ES proposals gain traction over time, inviting further study of how horizon shapes engagement and voice alongside portfolio tilting. Further work could refine horizon and clientele-preference measures to better separate the information and catering channels.
