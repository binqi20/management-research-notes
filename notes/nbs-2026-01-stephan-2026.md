# Institutions and social entrepreneurship: a hierarchy of institutions to revisit institutional voids, support, and configurations

**Abstract**
In this retrospective, we expand on theorizing about institutions and social entrepreneurship (SE) as presented in our Decade Award-winning 2015 article. Our 2015 paper developed the institutional configuration perspective, which considers joint and interaction effects of formal and informal institutions. It organized disagreement about how formal institutions shape SE, contrasting the institutional voids and institutional support perspectives. In this retrospective, we introduce a revised hierarchy of institutions as a 'middle-ground' approach to advance research on institutions in international business and entrepreneurship. This hierarchy integrates insights from different disciplines (institutional economics, sociology, political science, and cultural theory). It explicates the macro-level economic and microfoundational psychological effects of four layers of formal (government activism and constitutional institutions/rule of law) and informal institutions (cultural values and norms). The hierarchy progresses research on institutional voids by distinguishing four types of formal and informal institutional voids. It advances research on institutional configurations by highlighting new synergistic and compensatory configurations. It also offers new insights into institutional change. Finally, we advance research on institutions and SE by theorizing a new institutional paradox for SE that reconciles the institutional void versus support debate and by charting opportunities for how institutions shape the SE process from entry to exit.

**Research Question**
How can a revised "hierarchy of institutions" — a middle-ground approach that jointly considers four layers of formal and informal institutions and their macro-economic and microfoundational effects — advance theorizing on institutional voids, support, and configurations, and reconcile the long-standing debate over whether social entrepreneurship is driven by institutional voids or institutional support?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: institutions should be theorized at a "middle-ground" scope — four layers comprising two formal (government activism; constitutional institutions / rule of law) and two informal (cognitive = cultural values; normative = cultural norms) — rather than as single aggregate "institutional quality" or hyper-specific typologies.
- Key constructs: macro-level economic effects (resources, transaction costs, productivity) paired with often-overlooked microfoundational psychological effects (motivation, moral legitimacy, agency, self-efficacy) for each layer.
- Microfoundational logic: Coleman's bathtub links macro-level institutions to individual thoughts, feelings, and actions, which aggregate back to macro-level outcomes such as the country-level rate of SE.
- Institutional configurations: synergistic and compensatory combinations of formal and informal institutions; cultural values (motivation) can compensate for low government activism (resources), but operating a venture still requires resource support.
- Institutional paradox of SE: SE is needed most where institutional voids are deepest, yet these contexts are least conducive to creating and sustaining social enterprises.

This conceptual retrospective synthesizes institutional economics, sociology, political science, and cultural theory into a multilevel hierarchy that explains how four layers of institutions shape social entrepreneurship through both economic provisioning and psychological mechanisms. By distinguishing four types of institutional voids and articulating synergistic and compensatory configurations, the framework reconciles the institutional voids versus support perspectives via an "institutional paradox of SE" and charts how institutions shape the SE process from entry to exit.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances institutional theorizing in international business and comparative entrepreneurship by introducing a revised hierarchy of institutions that integrates previously siloed disciplinary traditions and explicates the microfoundational psychological mechanisms (alongside macro-economic effects) of four institutional layers. It introduces precision to the institutional-voids literature by differentiating four void types, extends the configuration perspective with new synergistic and compensatory forms, offers new insight into institutional change and ruptures, and theorizes an "institutional paradox of SE" that reconciles the voids-versus-support debate.

**Practical Implication**
For empirical researchers, the authors recommend specific databases and indicators (in the Online Supplement) to capture each institutional layer and caution against aggregate indices such as "economic freedom," which automatically penalize government activism. The framework also implies that policymakers and multinational enterprises should recognize that active governments and supportive informal institutions create foundational conditions enabling entrepreneurs and firms to flourish and contribute to social impact and sustainability.

**Limitations**
As a conceptual retrospective, the framework's empirical validation rests on the original 2015 study and subsequent replications rather than new data. The authors note persistent methodological challenges in studying institutional configurations — multicollinearity among institutional indicators and endogeneity (path dependencies between formal and informal institutions) — which are especially acute at the country level where sample sizes are limited.

**Future Research**
The authors chart opportunities along the entire SE process: theorizing government activism more deeply, attending to within-country regional variation, examining the inclusiveness of SE entry, studying organizational forms and internationalization, and developing comparable metrics (e.g., psychological well-being) for social impact and economic performance, including exit and failure. They also call for research on synergistic and compensatory configurations across scale levels and on institutional change and ruptures.
