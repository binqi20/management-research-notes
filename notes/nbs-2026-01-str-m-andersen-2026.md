# Navigating sustainability paradoxes: Unveiling the dynamics of transformative change in the food industry

**Abstract**
Primarily, the conclusions signal a need for governance and incentive structures at the system level beyond the action space of individual firms, and secondarily, illustrate how such governance approaches to sustainability transitions are sector-specific and geographically embedded.

**Research Question**
The study is guided by two questions: (1) What do sustainability paradoxes imply in the context of food waste collaboration? and (2) How does the food industry navigate them? It asks how firms balance economic and broader societal concerns in transitioning from business thinking to sustainability thinking.

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / frames: business case frame (eliminate tensions, treat sustainability instrumentally) vs. paradoxical frame (accept and accommodate conflicting economic, environmental, and social concerns) (Hahn et al., 2014).
- Paradox types (Smith and Lewis, 2011): learning (incremental "low-hanging fruit" innovations vs. existing marketing/agricultural policies), belonging (firm vs. industry identities; uneven commitment to the voluntary agreement), organizing (need for data-sharing transparency vs. fear of competition/coopetition), performing (competing organizational goals; purchasing agreements and free-return schemes generating waste).
- Collaboration factors (Dania et al., 2018): ten benchmark factors (f1-f10: joint efforts, sharing activities, collaboration value, adaptation, trust, commitment, power, continuous improvement, coordination, stability) used to interpret how paradoxes can be navigated.
- Triggering/boundary conditions: Norwegian 2017 industry agreement (50% food-waste reduction by 2030, SDG 12.3); concentrated market power among a few large retailers/producers; macro-level agricultural policies incentivizing overproduction.

The central process is the industry's attempted shift from a business-case mindset to a paradoxical mindset. Firms' individual food-waste innovations reach value thresholds and collide with entrenched industry agreements, standards, regulations, and agricultural policies, so the four paradoxes intensify and span categories rather than resolving in isolation. Navigation depends on collective governance—strengthening joint efforts, trust, sharing activities, coordination, and stability—at a system level beyond any single firm's action space.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends paradox theory beyond the firm level to the industry level, encompassing both individual firms and a broader systemic perspective, and refines Smith and Lewis's (2011) four-paradox typology by showing how the paradoxes unfold in practice and interrelate across categories. It contributes to the sustainability transitions literature by demonstrating that a firm-level approach is insufficient to make sense of the paradoxes conditioning collaboration, and makes a methodological contribution by foregrounding an industry-level, qualitative approach to food-system transitions.

**Practical Implication**
The industry needs system-level governance and incentive structures beyond individual firms: a common platform for openly sharing data and insights, stronger regulatory support, reconsidered agricultural policies, and consumer engagement that goes beyond one-way CSR communication toward active involvement in innovation. Framing food-waste solutions as long-term investments and clearly communicating shared responsibility across the value chain are emphasized.

**Limitations**
The authors acknowledge limitations inherent in conducting an empirical single-country case study, and note that the inherent complexity of food systems poses substantial methodological and conceptual challenges for assessing food-sector transitions. Data are confidential, and findings are geographically embedded in the Norwegian context.

**Future Research**
The paper positions its findings as a foundation for further empirical and conceptual exploration of the complex dynamics between business-oriented and paradoxical perspectives in sustainability transitions. It points to closer attention to tensions embedded within existing regimes of food production and distribution, and to industry-level analysis across both firm and system levels.
