# Integrating Drama Performance Methods with Business Ethics Education: A Mixed-Methods Approach

**Abstract**
This study investigates the integration of drama performance methods into business ethics education through a mixed-methods approach. A quasiexperimental design was employed to compare the effects of drama-based pedagogy and traditional lecture-based methods on undergraduate business students' ethical decision-making, critical thinking, and empathy. The quasiexperiment aimed to test whether drama-based pedagogy enhances these competencies more effectively than traditional approaches do. Complementing this, a qualitative study was conducted to explore the mechanisms through which emotional engagement in drama-based activities influences these learning outcomes. The findings from both studies provide comprehensive insights into the efficacy and underlying processes of drama-based pedagogy in fostering essential ethical skills.

**Research Question**
The study addresses two questions: (1) Compared with traditional methods, does drama-based pedagogy significantly enhance ethical decision-making, critical thinking, and empathy? and (2) What mechanisms of emotional engagement influence these outcomes?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Pedagogical approach (drama-based pedagogy vs. traditional lecture-based methods)
- DV(s): Ethical decision-making (DIT-2 P score), critical thinking (CTDS), and empathy (IRI)
- Mediators: Six emotional-engagement mechanisms surfaced qualitatively — cognitive elaboration, moral imagination, motivational relevance, narrative immersion, emotional resonance, and social bonding
- Moderators: None tested (control variables: age, gender, prior coursework, year of study, GPA)

Grounded in Kolb's (1984) experiential learning cycle and performance studies, drama-based pedagogy immerses students in role-plays, simulations, and improvisational exercises that enact ethical dilemmas. The argument is that embodied, emotionally charged participation activates emotional engagement as a central driver, which in turn triggers cognitive, affective, and social mechanisms (framed through Pekrun's control-value theory, Green & Brock's transportation theory, and Vygotsky's sociocultural theory) that deepen moral reasoning, analytical thinking, and perspective-taking beyond what intellectually focused lecture methods achieve. ANOVA results show the performance-based group scored significantly higher on all three outcomes (ethical decision-making F = 170.94, critical thinking F = 150.56, empathy F = 250.87; all p < 0.001).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends experiential learning theory by arguing that the performative immersion of drama intensifies Kolb's embodied cycle and produces stronger gains than conventional hands-on activities, while linking Mead's role-taking theory to an embodied pedagogical context to explain how performance-based activities elicit affective and cognitive shifts. It contributes a mechanism-level account — six emotional-engagement processes grouped by emotional, narrative, and social theoretical perspectives — that integrates cognitive and affective dimensions of moral development and empathy in business ethics education.

**Practical Implication**
Educators and curriculum designers can integrate drama activities such as role-play and simulations alongside lectures and case studies to build a balanced curriculum that fosters critical thinking, empathy, and ethical decision-making. The authors suggest the approach is transferable across disciplines (law, health care, STEM), though it requires instructor training and institutional resources, motivating cost-effective or technology-assisted delivery models.

**Limitations**
The use of preexisting class sections introduced potential confounds (class dynamics, scheduling, instructor-student interactions) despite randomization of individual students. The quantitative study omitted identical pretest measures of the dependent variables to avoid pretest sensitization, relying on proxy baselines and thereby constraining direct change assessment and causal attribution. Reliance on self-reported, validated instruments leaves outcomes susceptible to social desirability bias, and the single-university Chinese undergraduate sample limits generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future studies could add controls (e.g., class cohesion, student motivation) or more controlled settings, and use different-but-equivalent pre/posttest measures to balance baseline needs against sensitization risk. Cross-cultural comparisons, longitudinal designs tracking whether gains endure into professional contexts, objective or behavioral outcome measures, and scalable, technology-assisted or modularized drama formats with instructor-training programs are recommended.
