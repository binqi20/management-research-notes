# The Power of Retail Investors: The Effect of Online Interactions Over Environmental Issues on Corporate Greenwashing

**Abstract**
Greenwashing is a growing concern that demands immediate attention. Based on legitimacy theory and the attention-based view, this paper investigates whether retail investors’ environmental concerns expressed through digital platforms promote or inhibit corporate greenwashing. This paper utilizes the ERNIE large language model to construct an “environmental concern” index and examines the relationship between retail investors’ environmental concerns and corporate greenwashing, employing a sample of Chinese firms. The findings indicate that retail investors’ environmental concerns can effectively inhibit corporate greenwashing by increasing environmental comment letters and strengthening executive environmental awareness. We also find that regulatory enforcement, female executives, and executives’ climate change experience strengthen this effect, but state ownership weakens it. Furthermore, when retail investors raise more detailed questions and firms provide more specific, prompt, and detailed responses, this effect becomes stronger. This paper offers a novel perspective on the effect of retail investors’ environmental activism on greenwashing governance in the digital era.

**Research Question**
Do retail investors’ environmental concerns, expressed through online investor-interaction platforms, promote or inhibit corporate greenwashing?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Retail investors’ environmental concerns (Concern) — the number of negative environment-related questions raised by retail investors on investor interactive platforms (IIPs), identified via the ERNIE LLM.
- DV(s): Corporate greenwashing (GW) — the ratio of symbolic to total (symbolic + substantive) environment-related disclosure sentences in annual reports, classified by the fine-tuned ERNIE model and scaled by 100.
- Mediators: Environmental comment letters (external supervision channel) and executive environmental awareness (internal attention channel).
- Moderators: Regulatory enforcement (+), female executives (+), executives’ climate change experience (+), state ownership (−).

Integrating legitimacy theory with the attention-based view, the paper argues retail investors’ online environmental concerns act as an informal environmental regulation. Externally, they raise the likelihood that firms receive environmental comment letters from stock exchanges, threatening environmental legitimacy and constraining greenwashing opportunities. Internally, they elevate executives’ situated attention to environmental issues, increasing executive environmental awareness and motivating resource allocation to substantive environmental improvement rather than symbolic communication.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces retail investors’ topic-specific (environmental) online activism as a novel antecedent of greenwashing, distinct from conventional finance-focused activism. It extends the institutional perspective of legitimacy theory by showing environmental comment letters inhibit greenwashing (contrasting prior work on financial-report comment letters), and extends the attention-based view by identifying executive environmental awareness as an internal mediating mechanism, while advancing greenwashing measurement through a fine-tuned ERNIE LLM index.

**Practical Implication**
Governments and stock exchanges could create dedicated platform sections where retail investors access corporate environmental information and voice concerns, and could deploy environmental comment letters as a governance tool. Firms can increase the representation of women in senior management, since female executives are associated with reduced greenwashing.

**Limitations**
The environmental vocabulary used may not fully capture all environment-related questions. Because text analysis cannot fully capture contextual nuance, the approach may misclassify question topics when identifying environmental questions.

**Future Research**
Future research could adopt more advanced methods to expand and refine the environmental vocabulary for broader identification. Future studies could apply machine learning and other advanced techniques to improve the accuracy of environmental-question identification.
