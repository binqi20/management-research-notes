# ESG Controversies and Non-Audit Service Fees: European Evidence

**Abstract**
Lapses in environmental, social, and governance (ESG) conduct often manifest through controversies like environmental violations, social misconduct, or governance failures, creating reputational and informational risks for firms and their auditors. While many studies focus on how ESG performance influences audit and assurance outcomes, few examine how adverse ESG events, particularly controversies, shape auditor behavior and market pricing. This study examines whether such controversies affect non-audit services (NAS) pricing and explores how auditors incorporate ESG-related reputational risks into advisory engagements. Drawing on a panel of 4,552 firm-year observations from European listed firms between 2014 and 2023, ESG controversy data from Refinitiv Eikon are analyzed with two-way fixed-effects and instrumental variable (IV) estimations. The findings suggest that ESG controversies and NAS fees are positive and significantly correlated only for severe controversies, indicating that auditors selectively respond to material ethical risks. Supplementary analyses indicate that, in Europe, this relationship is not systematically conditioned by cross-country institutional strength or firm-level governance characteristics, suggesting their limited moderating role. The IV analysis reveals a weak instrumental relationship, implying the observed associations may partially reflect correlated omitted factors rather than definitive causal effects. Overall, the findings suggest ESG controversies have economic repercussions for firms through advisory cost adjustments, particularly when reputational risk is salient. The study enhances understanding of how auditors price ethical and reputational risks, contributing to ongoing debates regarding auditor independence, ESG accountability, and corporate ethics in assurance markets, while offering insights for regulators, managers, and auditors regarding pricing of ESG-related advisory risk.

**Research Question**
Does a firm's entanglement in ESG controversies lead to higher non-audit service (NAS) fees, and does this association vary across the environmental, social, and governance domains and with the severity and nature of the controversy?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): ESG controversy exposure — aggregate score (ESGC) and its disaggregated environmental (ENV_C), social (SOC_C), and governance (GOV_C) components, each multiplied by (-1) so higher values reflect more severe controversies.
- DV(s): Non-audit service (NAS) fees, measured as the natural logarithm of a firm's non-audit fees.
- Mediators: None modeled.
- Moderators: Controversy severity (high vs. low) and firm size (sample splits); institutional environment (rule of law, regulatory quality) and firm-level governance (board size, Big 4 auditor status) tested as supplementary moderators (interactions statistically insignificant).
- Controls: Firm size, Big 4 status, leverage, profitability (ROA), market-to-book, receivables/inventory intensity, board size, board independence, earnings restatement.

The paper argues that ESG controversies raise perceived client risk, engagement complexity, and reputational urgency, increasing auditor effort and firms' demand for auditors' advisory expertise, which lifts NAS fees (consistent with Simunic's 1980 risk-based audit pricing framework). Agency theory frames controversies as revealing governance weaknesses and information asymmetry that heighten monitoring demand; the resource-based view casts auditors' assurance and advisory skills as scarce resources for legitimacy repair; and contingency theory explains heterogeneity across domains, industries, and governance contexts. Empirically the positive ESGC-NAS association holds only for high-severity incidents and is driven primarily by environmental controversies, with social and governance effects weaker and less robust.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends ethics and auditing research by treating ESG controversies as adverse, event-driven ethical phenomena rather than the inverse of ESG engagement, and introduces controversy severity as a determinant of auditor risk perception, showing that only material reputational failures elicit pricing responses. By situating ESG controversies within the audit pricing framework, it advances understanding of how professional service providers price reputational and ethical risk under stakeholder-driven legitimacy pressures, while surfacing the ethical tension between NAS as a legitimacy-restoration tool and a potential threat to auditor independence.

**Practical Implication**
For auditors, the results underscore the role of ethical and reputational risk in advisory pricing decisions, and audit committees can better anticipate how ESG failures affect the cost and scope of external engagements. For corporate managers, the evidence indicates that ethical lapses carry measurable financial consequences through increased advisory costs. Regulators and standard setters may draw on the findings when designing sustainability assurance frameworks that preserve auditor independence while firms address ESG-related risks.

**Limitations**
The available NAS data do not distinguish between specific advisory service types, limiting interpretive granularity. The focus on European firms, where ESG disclosure and governance standards are relatively mature, may constrain generalizability to other institutional settings, and Refinitiv Eikon controversy measures may differ from alternative rating frameworks. The identification strategy relies on industry-level variation with limited first-stage explanatory power (first-stage F = 3.148, below the threshold of 10), restricting causal inference, so results should be read as correlational rather than definitively causal.

**Future Research**
Future research could exploit service-level NAS disclosures to gain interpretive granularity, extend analyses to less harmonized regulatory environments beyond mature European markets, and leverage natural experiments linked to ESG regulation and assurance mandates to strengthen causal identification.
