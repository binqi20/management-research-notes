# External search for specialized knowledge and green new product development speed: A dual mediation mechanism and moderating role of absorptive capacity

**Abstract**
The results revealed that external search from the two sources of knowledge, namely market sources and institutional sources, exhibits a significant positive association with green NPD speed. Further, the two mediators, including green process innovation and green product innovation, mediate the positive relationship between external search and green NPD speed. The findings also proved the effects of external search from market sources on green process innovation and green product innovation are positively moderated by absorptive capacity (AC). However, AC does not demonstrate a moderating effect when it comes to knowledge obtained from institutional sources in relation to green process innovation and green product innovation. The study contributes to the existing literature on open innovation, green innovation, AC and suggests ideas for future research.

**Research Question**
How does external knowledge search from distinct sources (market versus institutional) exert varying effects on green NPD speed; how do green process and product innovation mediate these effects; and what moderating role does a firm's absorptive capacity play in these relationships?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): External knowledge search from market sources; external knowledge search from institutional sources.
- DV(s): Green new product development (NPD) speed.
- Mediators: Green process innovation; green product innovation (dual mediation).
- Moderators: Absorptive capacity (AC).

Grounded in the knowledge-based view, open innovation, and absorptive capacity theories, the paper argues that knowledge acquired externally from market sources (suppliers, customers, competitors, R&D enterprises) and institutional sources (universities, research institutes) functions as a valuable resource that firms translate into green process and product innovation, which in turn accelerates green NPD speed. AC—a firm's ability to recognize, assimilate, and apply external knowledge—conditions this translation, but only for market-sourced knowledge: AC strengthens the link between market search and both green innovations, whereas it does not significantly moderate the institutional-source paths, plausibly because scientific institutional knowledge offers a theoretical base that is not readily commercialized.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study sharpens open innovation literature and advances the knowledge-based view by disaggregating external search into market versus institutional channels and showing they contribute differently to green NPD, rather than treating openness in aggregate. It marks a novel intersection of open innovation and sustainability research and positions absorptive capacity as a boundary condition whose effectiveness is activated for market-based but not science-based knowledge.

**Practical Implication**
Firms and policymakers in developing economies should adopt boundaryless strategies that encourage seeking knowledge beyond organizational borders—strengthening ties with suppliers, customers, consultants, R&D enterprises, universities, and research institutes—and embed external search into performance evaluation (e.g., KPIs). They should phase out obsolete, carbon-intensive technologies and invest in organizational absorptive capacity through trust, knowledge sharing, and targeted training.

**Limitations**
The cross-sectional design limits causal inference, and cross-lagged longitudinal studies could better capture long-term effects. The bird's-eye survey across multiple industrial sectors overlooks industry-specific dynamics, suggesting future focus on specific high-polluting industries. Additional mechanisms linking external search to green NPD speed remain to be unraveled.

**Future Research**
Future studies could examine specific (especially high-polluting) industries such as fossil fuels, agriculture, and fashion; unpack other external contingency factors such as green subsidies and supply chain risks; and explore alternative mediating mechanisms such as digital transformation.
