# Heterogeneous responses to carbon pricing: Firm-level evidence from Beijing emissions trading scheme

**Abstract**

**Research Question**
Does the Beijing ETS pilot significantly reduce covered firms’ carbon emissions, and—if responses are heterogeneous across firm types—through what abatement mechanisms and under what overlapping regulatory pressures do those reductions occur?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Coverage by the Beijing ETS (treatment status instrumented by whether a firm’s 2012 CO2 emissions exceeded the 10,000-ton cutoff)
- DV(s): Firm-level CO2 emissions in 2015 (log); secondary outcomes for industrial firms: total energy consumption, coal consumption, output, energy intensity, and fuel switching (emissions per unit of energy)
- Mediators: Reduction in coal consumption (the primary channel through which industrial-firm emissions fell)
- Moderators: Sector (industry vs. service); pre-treatment reliance on coal and oil (heavy vs. non-heavy users); exposure to overlapping command-and-control regulations (the Plan and coal phase-out mandates)

Because firms just above and below the emissions cutoff are otherwise comparable, the fuzzy RD identifies the local causal effect of ETS coverage. The ETS cut average emissions by about 39%, concentrated in industrial firms (~45%) and heavy coal/oil users (~54%), with negligible effects for service firms. The reduction operated chiefly by curtailing coal consumption rather than improving energy efficiency or cutting output, and was partly amplified by firms simultaneously subject to overlapping pollution and coal phase-out regulations, which receive disproportionate weight in the RD estimate.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides rare firm-level causal evidence on an emissions-trading scheme using verified emissions data spanning both industrial and service sectors, addressing prior reliance on sector-level or power-generation-only samples. It documents that the response to carbon pricing is highly heterogeneous and that headline ETS effects can be partly driven by interaction with overlapping command-and-control regulations, refining how the effectiveness of carbon pricing is interpreted.

**Practical Implication**
For the expansion of China’s national ETS, the authors caution that when an ETS launches with a relatively low carbon price, policymakers should be wary of including service firms or firms with limited low-cost abatement options, since their compliance and administrative costs may exceed the modest emissions reductions achievable; broader coverage of such firms is better deferred until a tighter cap raises the carbon price.

**Limitations**
The estimates are local to firms near the 10,000-ton cutoff and may not generalize to the full population; the auditing/verification process accompanying ETS inclusion could itself constitute part of the treatment and cannot be fully separated. Output data were self-reported and less reliable, and the effect of the ETS cannot be cleanly disentangled from overlapping environmental policies whose targeted firms cluster near the cutoff.

**Future Research**
The authors suggest that as China’s carbon market matures and prices rise toward the social cost of carbon, service firms could be gradually integrated, implying scope for future evaluation of ETS effects under higher and more stringent carbon prices and broader sectoral coverage.
