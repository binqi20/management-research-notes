# Muddling Through During the Pre-Liminal Period: Women’s Transition to Entrepreneurship in the Midst of a Compound National Crisis

**Abstract**
Integrating theories of liminality and intersectionality, this study examines the experiences of socially disadvantaged individuals during the pre-liminal period of their entrepreneurial transition driven by a compound national crisis. Applying a qualitative, interpretivist approach, we trace the trajectories of Lebanese women in their fifties from low socioeconomic backgrounds as they navigate the pre-liminal period, from its inception through its unfolding to its culmination. Our findings demonstrate how financial hardships and a sense of ethical responsibility for their families’ survival push the women toward creating informal ventures, while intersectional structures of oppression, patriarchy, and ingrained gendered expectations pose internal and external barriers for venture creation. We show how women entrepreneurs exercise their agency by challenging male dominance, reconfiguring their social disadvantages as resources, and strategically employing verbal accounts to overcome their families’ objections to their decision to start a business.

**Research Question**
The study asks what circumstances lead older, socioeconomically underserved women to transition to entrepreneurship; what internal and external barriers they face during the pre-liminal period of that transition; and how they navigate those barriers within the patriarchal, crisis context of Lebanon.

**Mechanism Process**
- Triggering conditions: A compound national crisis (economic collapse, hyperinflation, capital controls, COVID-19, the Beirut port explosion) that separates women from a more secure past and makes formal employment impossible.
- Key constructs / tensions: The pre-liminal period (separation, decision, justification); intersecting structures of oppression (gender × age × socioeconomic status); ethical responsibility for family survival; the performativity of gender (doing/undoing gender).
- Process / phases: Beginning of the pre-liminal period (compound crisis and its indicators) → unfolding (the decision to start a venture, driven by ethical responsibility) → culmination (internal barriers of self-doubt and external barriers of family objection).
- Practices: Reconfiguring social disadvantages (age, gender, modest background) as resources; deploying excusatory verbal accounts with husbands and accusatory accounts with children to win consent.
- Emergent outcomes: Surreptitious rebellion against patriarchy, undoing of gender norms, and transition toward liminal venture creation.

The central process is interpretive rather than causal: financial hardship plus an intensifying sense of moral responsibility propels women to act when male relatives remain complacent, and intersectional oppression magnifies internal self-doubt and external opposition. Women exercise ethically-imbued agency, recasting their perceived deficiencies as assets and strategically blending verbal accounts to overcome objections, thereby muddling through a protracted and contested pre-liminal passage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions. First, it advances liminality research by foregrounding the pre-liminal period as a complex, multi-phase stage of preparation and internal polemic, expanding the temporal orientation of liminality studies. Second, it integrates the intersectionality feminist lens with liminality theory, extending intersectionality beyond gender and race to the interplay of gender, age, and socioeconomic status and showing how disadvantages can be reconfigured as resources. Third, it contributes to business ethics and entrepreneurship studies by demonstrating that crisis-driven necessity entrepreneurship can be ethically and emancipatorily motivated rather than driven solely by economic calculation.

**Practical Implication**
NGOs and organizations supporting women's entrepreneurship in crisis contexts should recognize that moral responsibility and agency—not only financial need—drive women's venture creation, and treat women as agentic rather than passive aid recipients. Policymakers and NGOs should design programs that address women's specific internal barriers (self-doubt, lack of confidence) and external barriers (family opposition, patriarchy), tailoring initiatives to women's family-oriented ethics.

**Limitations**
The study is cross-sectional and relies on a relatively homogeneous sample of older, socioeconomically disadvantaged Lebanese women, limiting generalizability to other groups (younger, unmarried, or childless women). Reliance on retrospective reflections on pre-liminal experiences may introduce recall bias or selective memory. The study also did not examine the liminal and post-liminal periods of the women's transition.

**Future Research**
Future work could select more diverse samples and conduct comparative analyses across business types and disadvantaged groups, and use chronological reconstruction and cross-case analysis to reduce recall bias. Scholars are encouraged to explore the influence of religious affiliation and religiosity on socially disadvantaged individuals' pre-liminal experiences, and to study the liminal and post-liminal phases for a fuller account of the transition.
