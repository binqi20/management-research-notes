# Can Compassion Appeals Change the World? A Critical Review and Reconceptualization

**Abstract**
Compassion appeals are employed to mobilize prosocial actions that address global grand challenges. However, the evidence of the effectiveness of such appeals is still mixed, with studies using a multitude of persuasion appeals and examining diverse outcomes. To explain the mixed findings, we conduct an integrative, critical review of 77 articles (altogether reporting 116 empirical studies) on the role of compassion appeals in motivating prosocial behavior. This review explains the conditions that determine the relative effectiveness of compassion appeals. First, we identify the appeal components and contextual factors that elicit compassion. Successful elicitation is a prerequisite for the effectiveness of compassion appeals. Second, we reveal that compassion often is elicited with distress, an emotion actively regulated by audiences. Consequently, persuasive compassion appeals are most effective when elicited compassion is maintained within an area of acceptability, demarcated by elicitation and backfiring thresholds. Third, we show that mixed findings are often due to a mismatch between the motivation elicited by compassion and the prosocial action encouraged by the appeal. Compassion motivates the relief of immediate suffering but is not suited to drive sustained commitment toward promoting structural social change. To address this limitation of compassion, we conceptualize the need for compassion blends, mixed emotions where compassion is elicited along with guilt and/or moral anger. We explain why and how compassion blends can motivate transformative prosocial behavior. The results from our critical review inform an agenda for future research and offer actionable insights for the design of effective compassion appeals.

**Research Question**
The review poses three questions about appeal-induced compassion and prosocial behavior: RQ1 — how and under what conditions is compassion elicited by persuasive appeals; RQ2 — why and when are appeals to compassion resisted; and RQ3 — how and why does appeal-induced compassion motivate prosocial helping or transformative behavior.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Compassion appeals "moralize suffering" via an ethic of care, but their effectiveness depends on three conditions — successful elicitation, the regulation of co-activated distress, and a match between the emotion's action tendency and the prosocial action sought.
- Key constructs: severity and legitimacy of the plight (primary elicitors); identification, affective traits, moral-political beliefs, and contextual cues (four groups of boundary conditions on elicitation); distress as a co-activated, actively regulated emotion.
- Boundary conditions / thresholds: an "area of acceptability" bounded by an elicitation threshold (appeal moving enough to trigger compassion) and a backfiring threshold (distress overcomes compassion), theorized as a nonlinear relationship.
- Prosocial-behavior distinction: prosocial helping (occasional relief, grounded in an ethic of care) versus prosocial transformative behavior (sustained, structural change, grounded in an ethic of responsibility); compassion alone drives helping but is psychologically ill-suited to transformative action.
- Reconceptualization — compassion blends: compassion combined with moral anger ("commitment," desire to restore justice through other-directed redress/punishment) or with guilt ("atonement," desire to restore justice through self-directed self-improvement), drawing on mixed-emotion specificity (Oh & Tong, 2022) and additive appraisal effects.

Synthesizing 116 studies, the authors argue that compassion is an "erratic force" stabilized or undermined by identifiable situational factors, and that its limited power over transformative action arises because the care-based motivation it produces does not supply the appraisals of responsibility, accountability, and moral agency that structural change requires. They theorize that pairing compassion with moral anger or guilt produces complementary appraisals and additive effects (mechanisms M1–M3 in Fig. 3) that can channel motivation from immediate helping toward transformative prosocial behavior.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The review reconciles mixed evidence by (a) mapping the antecedents and moderators of compassion elicitation, (b) introducing the threshold-bounded "area of acceptability" governing when appeals persuade versus backfire, and (c) reconceptualizing compassion appeals around "compassion blends" (atonement and commitment), revealing for the first time the distinct psychological mechanisms linking compassion to helping versus transformative prosocial behavior and bridging an ethic of care with an ethic of responsibility.

**Practical Implication**
Severity and legitimacy of the plight, and receiver characteristics conducive to compassion, guide segmentation, targeting, and campaign design (Table 1). Practitioners should pretest campaigns with discrete-emotion scales to minimize distress and backfiring risk (Fig. 2), and deliberately design appeals that blend compassion with moral anger or guilt when transformative prosocial action is the goal (Figs. 3 and 5 provide a hands-on tool).

**Limitations**
As a critical (rather than systematic-descriptive) review, the goal is theoretical integration and reinterpretation rather than detailed description of past studies, so the proposed model of compassion blends is conceptual and not yet empirically tested. The synthesis is bounded by the heterogeneity and gaps of the reviewed evidence (e.g., few studies measure emotions beyond compassion or distinguish helping from transformative outcomes).

**Future Research**
The authors propose three directions (Table 3): empirically test the conceptual model of how appeal components activate each compassion blend and the conditions moderating helping versus transformative outcomes; extend the model to other compassion blends (e.g., disgust, hope) and to mechanisms beyond additive integration (ambivalence, coexistence); and examine the role of emotion regulation and blend composition on elicitation and backfiring thresholds, drawing on mixed-emotions, emotion-regulation, and coping scholarship.
