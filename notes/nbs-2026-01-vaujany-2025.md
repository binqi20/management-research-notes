# Rethinking Responsibility in the Digital Age: A Narrative Approach

**Abstract**
Researchers, policymakers, and industry are increasingly aware of the urgent risks and threats arising in the digital age. Their awareness of this urgency has led to a rise of interest in responsibility. While this “turn to responsibility” is well-intentioned, an underappreciated problem is that the dominant, centuries-old view of responsibility is not up to this task—it is unable to make sense of the increasingly extended scope of responsibility in the digital age because it is mired in outdated assumptions about causality, agency, and human action. Inspired by Paul Ricoeur’s philosophy, we show the benefits of rethinking responsibility as an ongoing process of becoming responsible—that is, becoming responsible by being imputed through the narrative emplotment of extended sociomaterial events. We illustrate the benefits of this conception for the digital age using vignettes from plagiarism detection, social media, and AI. The paper concludes by proposing a Ricoeur-inspired narrative topology of the multidimensional time-space of responsibility emplotment. This paper calls on the MIS community, and society more broadly, to draw on this topology to reflect on their imputations and take up responsibility, individually and collectively.

**Research Question**
How can responsibility be reconceptualized so that it makes sense of the extended, connective, and posthuman conditions of the digital age—specifically, how do sociomaterial assemblages narratively impute actors as already responsible, and how can actors reflexively take responsibility through narrative emplotment? The traditional, individualistic, human-centered conception "is unable to make sense of the increasingly extended scope of responsibility in the digital age."

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: responsibility is not a fixed attribution but an ongoing "becoming responsible" through imputation enacted in the narrative emplotment of extended sociomaterial events (Ricoeur).
- Key constructs: imputation ("attributing an action to its true author"); sociomateriality / performativity; narrative emplotment via the threefold mimesis of prefiguration, configuration, and refiguration.
- Three narrative emplotments of responsibility: (1) causal responsibility as responsibility *attribution* (past actions, third-party judgment); (2) relational responsibility as responsibility *making* (sociomaterial performativity enacts actors as already responsible); (3) narrative responsibility as responsibility *taking* (individual/communal self-imputation, future-oriented).
- Boundary conditions / target domain: digital technologies characterized by radical connectivity, nonlinear consequences, extended/emergent actions, and posthuman performative agencies.

The paper argues that the traditional model rests on three untenable assumptions—linearity of the world, a single space-time for agency, and the centrality of intentional human action—that digital connectivity subverts. Drawing on Ricoeur's hermeneutic phenomenology, it reframes responsibility as a temporal process in which sociomaterial assemblages "in their performativity... not only actively produce the world... but also actively impute actors as being already responsible." Through prefiguration, configuration, and refiguration, narratives both relationally assign responsibilities to actors/characters and allow actors to reflexively re-narrate their emplotments to "take responsibility, differently," shifting the emphasis from backward-looking attribution toward forward-looking, future-oriented responsibility-taking.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends responsibility theory in information systems by introducing the *imputative* (not merely performative) function of digital sociomaterial assemblages and a Ricoeur-inspired narrative theory of the "becoming" of responsibility. It distinguishes three narrative emplotments—attribution, making, and taking—and proposes a narrative topology of the multidimensional time-space of responsibility, departing from Coeckelbergh's (2023) hermeneutic-responsibility terminology to foreground temporality and sociomaterial performativity.

**Practical Implication**
IS professionals, designers, managers, and policymakers should attend not only to what their systems do but to "what sort of responsibilities, or responsible actors, they are continuously producing." The paper urges constructing counternarratives, value-sensitive and transparent design, and collective, forward-looking responsibility-taking that "speculatively engages with the future on its own terms" rather than treating technological trajectories as inevitable.

**Future Research**
The authors invite the MIS community and society to draw on the proposed narrative topology to reflect on their imputations and to develop "refiguring alternative narratives" of technological futures—elaborating alternative narrative trajectories from past to future to keep open new spaces for responsibility-taking, individually and collectively.
