# Indigenous employees’ experiences of work: An interdisciplinary review

**Abstract**

We canvas four factors that existing literature suggests are especially relevant for understanding Indigenous work experiences: colonialism and Indigenous employment; the work-culture interface; relationships; and perceptions. We highlight how different constellations of these factors can lead Indigenous workers to experience the relationship between their Indigenous identity and their work as broadly synergistic, strained, or ambivalent. To conclude, we present a constructive critique of the literature on Indigenous employees, and in doing so, propose three key priorities for future research: engaging with the complexities of Indigenous identities, exploring the varied contexts in which Indigenous employees experience work, and developing solutions to common challenges Indigenous employees face at work.

**Research Question**
How does being Indigenous shape life at work? The review critically integrates research across geographic and disciplinary boundaries to understand the role of work in both enabling and disabling Indigenous employees and communities.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: The interplay of colonialism with features of Indigenous cultures and communities produces distinct work experiences that mainstream scholarship has overlooked.
- Key factors (organizing structure of the review): (1) colonialism and Indigenous employment; (2) the work-culture interface (work-culture alignment and cultural double-shifting); (3) relationships (with managers, Indigenous co-workers, and family/community — including work-community integration); (4) perceptions (workplace stereotypes — "lazy worker," "authentic" Indigenous person, troublemaker — and work-related stereotypes).
- Integrating heuristic: the "Indigeneity-work relationship," whose constellations of the four factors yield three varieties — synergistic, strained, and ambivalent.
- Boundary conditions: outcomes depend on context (mainstream vs. Indigenous work settings), how cultural double-shifting is channeled and rewarded, managerial autonomy-brokering, and whether a critical mass of Indigenous co-workers exists.

The review synthesizes how the enduring structural effects of colonialism create barriers to quality employment and normalize foreign ways of organizing, such that Indigenous employees frequently experience misfit between their culture and mainstream work contexts. Where work draws on Indigenous cultural knowledge without recognition (cultural double-shifting), or where adverse stereotypes operate, the Indigeneity-work relationship tends toward strain; where work advances Indigenous causes and aligns with cultural values, it becomes synergistic, reinforcing identity and wellbeing. Because both work and Indigenous identity are multifaceted, the most common lived experience is ambivalence — a mix of synergy and strain across different facets.

**Theoretical Contribution**
Because dedicated theories of Indigenous work experience have yet to emerge, the review's primary contribution is to synthesize empirically grounded conceptual "building blocks" — including the Indigeneity-work relationship, work-culture alignment, and work-community integration — that provide a conceptual language for theorizing the distinct patterns and processes of Indigenous work lives. In doing so it answers calls to make management and organizational scholarship more reflective of diverse workforces and to centre Indigenous and racialized experiences in the field.

**Practical Implication**
Enhancing Indigenous work experiences requires coordinated action across domains: better jobs (equitable, secure, meaningful roles), better management (culturally competent and policy-aware managers who act as autonomy and flexibility brokers), better work environments (organizing systems that value relationality as well as results), and better personal strategies (equipping Indigenous workers to navigate requests for cultural labor). Cultural double-shifting should be formally accounted for and rewarded, and non-Indigenous managers trained in Indigenous ways of knowing and being.

**Limitations**
As an interpretive rather than highly structured systematic review, the authors deliberately adopted a fluid approach to article collection, which risks omitting relevant studies and limits replicability. Evidence is uneven — concentrated on certain Indigenous groups (Māori, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders, First Nations) and certain mainstream sectors (public sector, mining) — and the proposed mechanisms for why Indigenous work contexts improve experiences are not yet tested empirically.

**Future Research**
The authors propose three priorities: (1) engaging with the complexities and pluralism of Indigenous identities, including weaker or intersecting identifications and multicultural/multiracial Indigenous employees; (2) exploring the varied contexts in which Indigenous employees work, especially under-studied Indigenous work contexts versus mainstream settings; and (3) developing and testing solution-focused interventions to common challenges, using longitudinal, action-research, comparative, and intervention/field-experiment designs attentive to moderators and boundary conditions.
