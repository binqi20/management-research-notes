# Does central supervision mitigate border pollution? Evidence from the national specially monitored firms program in China

**Abstract**
This paper investigates the effects of central supervision on border pollution under environmental decentralization. We use the NSMF program implementation to capture the exogenous variation at the central environmental supervision level and construct a DDD model to investigate the spatial heterogeneity effects of the NSMF program. We find that the NSMF program implementation significantly reduces SO2 emission intensity for polluting firms located closer to provincial borders compared with those farther away.

**Research Question**
Does strengthened top-down central supervision mitigate transboundary (border) air pollution that arises when local governments strategically relax environmental enforcement for firms near provincial borders under a decentralized regulatory framework? The paper investigates this using China's NSMF program and asks through which mechanism any such effect operates.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Treatment by the 2007 NSMF-air program interacted with a firm's proximity to its provincial border (`Border × did_air`; continuous `dis × did_air`), proxying an exogenous increase in central supervision intensity.
- DV(s): SO2 emission intensity (log of SO2 emissions per unit of output); also waste-gas, soot/dust, COD, and NH3-N emission intensities; city-level environmental penalties, industrial SO2 emissions, and PM2.5 concentrations.
- Mediators: Strengthened local environmental enforcement near borders (increased environmental penalties; concentrated in firms with low pre-existing emission charges); firm-level end-of-pipe abatement (waste-gas / SO2 treatment capacity, SO2 removal rate).
- Moderators: Wind direction (effect concentrated downwind of cities); administrative level (stronger at city/county borders than provincial borders); pre-existing local enforcement stringency.

In a principal-agent framework, decentralization lets local-government agents apply lenient emission standards to border firms because those firms export pollution to neighboring jurisdictions, lowering locally borne environmental costs. By forcing real-time automatic monitoring of designated firms, the NSMF program raises the probability that the central principal detects selective enforcement, increasing the expected cost of forbearance and shrinking local discretion. The model predicts permitted emissions converge to the central standard, with the sharpest reduction at border firms; empirically the program cuts SO2 emission intensity by up to 20.4% at provincial borders, an effect realized through tighter enforcement and firms' end-of-pipe abatement rather than through changed energy efficiency or curtailed output.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper complements the border-pollution and environmental-decentralization literatures by providing rigorous evidence on the second remedy for the principal-agent dilemma—strengthening top-down supervision—rather than the more-studied path of realigning intergovernmental incentives. It documents the spatial heterogeneity of within-jurisdiction enforcement, showing that local governments systematically relax enforcement near provincial borders and that direct central supervision counteracts this, and it extends prior NSMF evaluations (Zhang et al., 2018) by identifying border-specific effects, directly testing the enforcement and abatement mechanisms, and shifting focus from water to air pollution.

**Practical Implication**
Automatic real-time pollution-monitoring programs that give central authorities direct firm-level oversight can curb selective local enforcement and reduce interregional environmental inequality, and are worth promoting—especially in border areas where enforcement is weak. Because city- and county-level governments mitigate border air pollution more effectively than provincial governments, supervisory resources should be targeted at lower-level enforcement. The authors' cost-benefit calculation suggests the NSMF-air program's annual health benefits are roughly 2.68 times its implementation cost.

**Limitations**
The empirical sample ends in 2012 and relies on matching the China Industrial Enterprise Database with the China Environmental Statistics Database, yielding modest matching rates and requiring exclusions (e.g., relocated firms, municipalities, islands, the controversial 2010 CIED data). County-border effects, though large in point estimate, are not always statistically significant given limited sample sizes, and the cost-benefit figures rest on back-of-the-envelope assumptions drawn from prior valuations.

**Future Research**
Because air pollutants and greenhouse gases strongly co-occur, the findings invite work on designing synergistic pollution- and carbon-reduction policies. The paper also points toward further study of how supervisory authority should be allocated across administrative tiers to address transboundary spillovers, and toward additional channels of air-quality benefit (e.g., labor productivity, human capital, innovation, land prices) not captured in the cost-benefit analysis.
