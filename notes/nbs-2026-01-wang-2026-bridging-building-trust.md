# Bridging the Gap, Building Trust: Structural Holes as Dual Forces in Corporate Philanthropy

**Abstract**
Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is widely recognized as a vital tool for reputational repair, yet its efficacy in addressing ethical challenges arising from social networks is unclear. Specifically, while firms leverage structural holes for their informational benefits, these network positions also create reputational risks that likely require tailored CSR strategies. This study addresses this gap by examining how firms' structural holes—and the moderating role of industrial clusters—influence their engagement in different forms of corporate philanthropy. Based on a multi-industry sample of 667 Chinese firms and a supplementary sample of 212 distributors from Chinese paper-making industry, our findings indicate that structural holes promote financial corporate philanthropy but discourage non-financial corporate philanthropy. Industrial clusters positively moderate both relationships. By shifting the scholarly focus from the informational benefits to the reputational implications of structural holes, this study contributes to disentangling financial and non-financial philanthropy as distinct strategic responses to structural holes.

**Research Question**
How do structural holes shape financial and non-financial corporate philanthropy, and how are these effects moderated by industrial clusters?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Structural holes (Burt efficiency measure of a firm's access to network brokerage)
- DV(s): Financial corporate philanthropy (monetary donations scaled by total assets); Non-financial corporate philanthropy (employee volunteer service hours/participation scaled by number of employees)
- Mediators: None modeled (a post hoc analysis uses media reputation as a downstream mechanism check)
- Moderators: Industrial clusters (dummy = 1 if firm located within an industrial cluster)
- Controls: Firm age, firm size, ISO9000, ISO14000, political ties, state ownership (SOE), and eight industry dummies

Occupying a structural hole grants informational and control advantages but simultaneously casts the firm as an opportunistic broker with information asymmetries, generating ethical deficits and reputational risk. To repair reputation, firms favor highly visible, quickly signaled financial philanthropy (supporting H1) while deprioritizing low-visibility, embeddedness-dependent non-financial philanthropy that employees are less motivated to supply when the firm is seen as ethically compromised (supporting H2). Industrial clusters—through dense information diffusion, normative pressure, relational embeddedness, and shared logistical resources—amplify the reputational payoff of financial philanthropy (H3) and attenuate the barriers to non-financial philanthropy (H4).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study disentangles financial versus non-financial corporate philanthropy as distinct strategic responses rather than a monolithic construct, and reframes structural holes by shifting attention from their well-documented informational benefits to their reputational and ethical implications—positioning the authors as the first to show how brokerage positions shape philanthropic behavior. By theorizing industrial clusters (a macro phenomenon) as a moderator of firm-level (micro) philanthropic responses, it bridges social network theory and CSR across levels of analysis.

**Practical Implication**
Executives in firms that bridge structural holes should treat highly visible financial contributions as the primary component of reputational risk management, while approaching less-visible non-financial philanthropy cautiously given its weaker immediate signaling. Within industrial clusters, managers can lower barriers through collective philanthropy—forming alliances with cluster members for joint community projects that share cost and effort while embedding the firm in relational capital.

**Limitations**
The samples focus primarily on firms in China, limiting generalizability to other countries and cultures. The binary financial/non-financial split overlooks the multifaceted nature of corporate giving (beneficiary types, motivations, specific strategies). Cross-sectional design and reliance on industrial clusters as the single studied contextual moderator leave other contextual factors (government policy, societal norms, cultural values) unexamined.

**Future Research**
Future work could broaden samples to diverse geographic and cultural settings, examine finer-grained dimensions of philanthropy (beneficiaries targeted, motivations, strategies), and investigate the differential long-term reputational-repair effects of financial versus non-financial philanthropy. Additional contextual moderators—such as government policies, societal norms, and cultural values—and their interaction with firms' network positions warrant study.
