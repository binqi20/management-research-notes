# Girls Help Girls: The Gender Preference of FOF Managers

**Abstract**
The Fund of Funds (FOF) is a specialized form of fund that invests in other mutual funds rather than directly in stocks or bonds. Using disclosures of FOF holdings, we find significant gender-based preferences: female FOF managers prefer to invest in funds managed by females, whereas their male counterparts prefer those managed by males. To mitigate potential endogeneity concerns, we take advantage of management team turnover events in FOFs that induce shifts in gender composition, and apply the generalized difference-in-differences (DID) approach. Results indicate that transitions from all-male to all-female management teams result in an increase in the proportion of investments in female-managed funds, and vice versa. Our mechanism tests reveal that neither social networks overlap between FOF and fund managers nor similarity in risk preferences within the same gender can fully explain the observed effects. Crucially, we find that male FOF managers' gender preferences lack financial justification, raising concerns about potential gender bias (taste-based discrimination), whereas their female counterparts' behavior is consistent with rationally using gender as a proxy for unobserved fund manager characteristics (statistical discrimination).

**Research Question**
Do male and female Fund of Funds (FOF) managers exhibit systematic same-gender preferences in their allocation decisions, and—critically, within the context of gender inequality—do women receive support from other women? What mechanisms (statistical vs. taste-based discrimination) underlie any observed gender-based preferences, and do men and women differ in their role in perpetuating gender inequality in asset management?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Gender composition of the FOF management team (FemaleFOF / MaleFOF dummies; all-female vs. all-male teams); management-team turnover events that shift gender composition (DID treatment).
- DV(s): Gender composition of underlying funds held (FemaleFund / MaleFund); value share of portfolio allocated to female-managed funds (FVS) and male-managed funds (MVS); risk-adjusted performance of same- vs. opposite-gender pairs (benchmark-adjusted return, Sharpe ratio, multi-factor alpha).
- Mediators / channels tested and ruled out: social-network overlap (shared college / fund family) and similarity in risk preferences within gender (no systematic risk difference between funds chosen by female vs. male FOF managers).
- Moderators / conditioning: fund category (equity / balanced / bond) subsamples; manager gender (asymmetry between male and female FOF managers).

FOF managers display homophily—an in-group preference for funds run by managers of their own gender—that survives extensive controls and a DID identification leveraging quasi-exogenous turnover. The authors interpret this through two discrimination logics: statistical discrimination (gender as a rational proxy for unobserved skill) versus taste-based discrimination (prejudice incurring a financial cost). A performance test discriminates between them: female FOF managers' female-managed picks outperform their male-managed picks (consistent with rational, statistical discrimination), whereas male FOF managers' male-managed picks underperform the female-managed alternatives they hold (consistent with costly taste-based discrimination), revealing similar surface behavior driven by fundamentally different motives.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances research on gender inequality in financial markets by conceptualizing the FOF-underlying-fund relationship as an investor-fundraiser dynamic and showing that men and women play asymmetric roles in perpetuating gender disparities—same-gender preference is taste-based bias for male managers but performance-driven (statistical) for female managers. It contributes to the emerging literature on behavioral biases among FOF managers by identifying gender bias as a new form of bias, and it addresses pervasive endogeneity concerns by exploiting FOF manager-turnover events within a generalized DID design rather than relying on OLS cross-sectional comparisons.

**Practical Implication**
The finding that male FOF managers sacrifice returns to favor male-managed funds shows that taste-based discrimination systematically constrains qualified female fund managers' access to capital and career advancement, raising equity concerns in asset management. By documenting that women's support for women is financially justified rather than blind solidarity, the authors aim to raise awareness of an often-implicit same-gender preference and encourage more conscious efforts toward a more gender-equal financial market.

**Limitations**
Most notably, the analysis excludes FOFs and funds managed by mixed-gender teams because investment decisions cannot be cleanly attributed to a specific gender, leaving the behavior of mixed-gender teams unexamined. The setting is confined to the Chinese FOF market over 2018-2024, and the baseline sample relies substantially on quarterly top-10 holdings disclosures (validated against semiannual/annual complete holdings).

**Future Research**
Future work could explore behavioral differences between mixed-gender and single-gender teams—for example, whether male-female collaboration mitigates in-group favoritism and yields more gender-neutral, returns-driven portfolio choices. Subsequent studies could also use manager characteristics such as age or work experience to infer dominance within mixed-gender teams, enabling comparisons between male- or female-dominated mixed-gender teams and single-gender teams to illuminate internal gender-interaction dynamics.
