# Harvesting or nurturing? Corporate venture capital and startup green innovation

**Abstract**
We find that CVC investors preferentially select ventures exhibiting higher pre-investment green innovation, an effect driven by those whose parent corporations actively engage in green innovation themselves, indicating substantive rather than symbolic motives behind these investments. Critically, post-investment nurturing of green innovation depends on investor heterogeneity: only CVC investors whose parent corporations possess complementary green innovation resources significantly increase their portfolio ventures' subsequent green innovation.

**Research Question**
Do corporate venture capital (CVC) investors merely select ("harvest") startups that already exhibit green innovation, or do they also "nurture" their portfolio ventures' subsequent green innovation post-investment — and how does heterogeneity among CVCs (whether the corporate parent itself engages in green innovation) shape both the selection and nurturing effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Venture green innovation before investment (count of green patents in the five pre-investment years); CVC backing (binary); Green CVC vs. Nongreen CVC (corporate parent filed green patents in prior five years).
- DV(s): Likelihood of receiving CVC / green CVC backing (selection); yearly count of venture green patents post-investment (nurturing).
- Mediators: Not reported in paper (no formal mediation tested).
- Moderators: Corporate parent's complementary green innovation resources (green vs. nongreen CVC), which conditions the CVC × Post-investment treatment effect.

Drawing on institutional logics, the authors argue that CVCs' corporate logics — strategic motives, patient capital, attention bases, and norms — make them more likely than independent VCs (IVCs) to select and support ventures with green innovation. Resource complementarity arguments then explain within-category heterogeneity: only when the corporate parent itself possesses green innovation resources (complementary assets in the sense of Teece, 1986) can the CVC transfer relevant knowledge, connections, and patient capital that materially increase the portfolio venture's green innovation after investment. The empirical pattern is "necessary-but-not-sufficient": CVC status alone does not raise venture green innovation, but green CVCs do.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study makes three contributions to CVC research: (1) it shows that resource heterogeneity among corporate parents shapes both CVC selection and value-added, refining the boundary conditions of treating CVCs as a uniform group; (2) it integrates institutional logics with resource complementarity arguments to reconcile prior conflicting findings about whether CVCs add value, showing both patterns hold once parent-level green resource heterogeneity is modeled; and (3) it extends work on CVC innovation effects to green innovation as a distinct, technologically complex form of innovation output measured via green patents.

**Practical Implication**
Startups developing green technologies should not treat all CVCs as equivalent: only "green CVCs" whose corporate parents possess green innovation resources actually nurture subsequent green innovation, so ventures should weigh investors' ability and willingness to support green development. VC investors lacking green resources but seeking environmental impact may benefit from syndicating with green CVCs. Policymakers should recognize that CVC investment in green startups does not automatically yield continued green development and should support post-investment capabilities rather than only increasing investment volume.

**Limitations**
Green-patent measures may not capture all sustainability-oriented activity, particularly non-technological innovations such as sustainable business models or processes, and some ventures protect innovations through means other than patenting. The sample is restricted to EU-27 ventures receiving equity funding, with the analysis focused on initial CVC investments, so subsequent CVC–startup relationships (collaborations, partnerships, acquisitions) are not modeled.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine alternative, broader dimensions of sustainability-related innovation as more longitudinal and objective measures become available for early-stage private ventures, and test whether the effects hold in regions only recently advancing their green innovation efforts or with less-developed VC markets. Industry-specific dynamics and the broader consequences of CVC investment for venture performance, survival, and exit also warrant investigation, as does how CVC investor motives intertwine with investment in sustainable startups.
