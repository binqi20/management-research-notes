# Political Tension and MNEs’ Disclosure of Host-Country ESG Activities

**Abstract**
Scholars have explored the ways in which multinational enterprises (MNEs) react to host-country stakeholders amid the political tension between the host country and home country. However, scant attention has been paid to understanding how MNEs respond to home-country stakeholders in the context of political tension. Our study formulates a theoretical framework to investigate the impact of political tension on MNEs’ decisions about disclosing their host-country ESG activities. Our analysis reveals that an MNE is less likely to disclose host-country ESG activities in its home-country ESG report when political tension between its home country and host country is stronger. We further find that this negative relationship is more pronounced for MNEs with higher domestic political connections, larger domestic marketing dependence, and located in cities with a colonial legacy. These findings raise important ethical considerations about transparency in multinational contexts and offer a new perspective to interpret MNEs’ ESG disclosure decisions.

**Research Question**
When and why do MNEs choose to selectively disclose — or strategically withhold — their host-country ESG activities from home-country stakeholders under political tension between the home and host countries, and which firm-level conditions amplify this effect?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Political tension (absolute annual decline of at least one point in the bilateral political-relations index between China and the host country)
- DV(s): MNEs’ disclosure of host-country ESG activities in the home-country ESG report (binary, coded via content analysis)
- Mediators: None directly measured; the proposed (unmeasured) mechanism is heightened negative national sentiment among home-country stakeholders
- Moderators: Domestic political connections (H2); domestic marketing dependence (H3); colonial legacy of the headquarters city (H4)

Under political tension, deteriorating bilateral relations fuel negative national sentiment toward the hostile host country, so home-country stakeholders may reframe favorable treatment of host-country stakeholders — including ESG activities — as benefiting an enemy and diverting domestic resources, threatening home-country legitimacy. To avoid this legitimacy loss, MNEs depart from a deontological duty of transparency and adopt a consequentialist logic of selective disclosure, withholding host-country ESG activities from domestic reports. This withholding is stronger when firms face greater home-country scrutiny (political connections), greater consumer-animosity exposure (marketing dependence), or deeper institutionalized skepticism toward foreigners (colonial legacy). All four hypotheses are supported (e.g., H1 β = -0.62, p < 0.001; interaction terms negative and significant in Table 9).

**Theoretical Contribution**
First, the paper extends ESG-disclosure research by theorizing the conditions under which firms deviate from a deontological duty of transparency and instead adopt a consequentialist logic of selective disclosure, foregrounding how home-country stakeholder pressures (not just host-country audiences) shape what MNEs report. Second, it contributes to the political-tension literature by identifying a home-country pathway — negative national sentiment — through which political tension shapes ESG disclosure, reframing ESG from a purely legitimacy-enhancing practice abroad to one that can simultaneously entail legitimacy loss at home.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should exercise heightened sensitivity to political tension and adopt a contextually nuanced ESG-disclosure strategy, taking particular care when the firm is heavily reliant on home-country stakeholders or located in a city with a colonial legacy, to mitigate political and reputational costs while ethically aligning with both home- and host-country expectations. Policymakers can help by fostering a standardized, internationally recognized ESG reporting framework that lets MNEs disclose with greater transparency and integrity without incurring undue political backlash.

**Limitations**
The sample captures only political relations between China and a set of major countries, so generalizability to other dyads may be limited. Despite the authors' efforts, the media data used to validate host-country ESG activity may not be comprehensive, and the proposed negative-national-sentiment mechanism could not be directly measured because of data-availability constraints. The study also treats legitimacy as a binary construct rather than capturing its pragmatic, moral, and cognitive dimensions.

**Future Research**
Future work could employ alternative or qualitative data to capture firms' actual host-country ESG performance, and use experimental or other methods to directly test the underlying sentiment mechanism. Researchers could also investigate non-sentiment pathways (e.g., political risk and operational threats) through which political dynamics shape MNE strategy, and adopt a more nuanced, multidimensional operationalization of legitimacy (pragmatic, moral, cognitive) under varying forms and intensities of political tension.
