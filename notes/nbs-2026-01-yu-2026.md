# Water stress and industrial firm productivity

**Abstract**
We combine data on over half a million industrial firms with fine-scale hydrological data to estimate the causal effect of water stress on firm productivity in China. We find that runoff shocks exert asymmetric effects on firm-level TFP.

**Research Question**
What is the causal effect of water stress — measured by local water runoff rather than precipitation — on the total factor productivity of industrial firms in China, and through which channels does it operate?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): County-level water runoff shocks (binned relative to the county-specific long-run mean and standard deviation: large/moderate negative shocks ≤−2σ and (−2σ,−1σ]; moderate/large positive shocks)
- DV(s): Firm-level total factor productivity (TFP), estimated via Olley-Pakes with ACF correction
- Mediators (transmission channels): reduced water inputs (freshwater withdrawals), disruptions to power generation (thermal and hydropower), and tighter financial constraints (lower cash flow)
- Moderators: groundwater reserves, river network density, irrigated-land share, coastal location, and state ownership (SOE); dams and urban drainage density as adaptation moderators

Runoff shocks affect productivity asymmetrically because water often serves as a binding input under Leontief-type production technology: when water availability falls below a critical threshold, production cannot be sustained, whereas additional water above the threshold yields diminishing marginal returns. A moderate negative shock lowers TFP by 1.93% and a large negative shock by 5.40%, while positive shocks are insignificant. The losses are transmitted primarily through constrained water inputs, reduced (especially thermal and hydro) power generation, and tighter cash flow, with SOEs and groundwater-rich or coastal counties more insulated.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper argues that local water runoff is a more appropriate proxy of water availability for industrial firms than the precipitation measure dominant in prior work, reconciling the literature's mixed findings on water stress. It documents an asymmetric, threshold-type production response to water availability and shows that water stress can have an adverse effect on industrial productivity that is equal to or greater than heat stress, linking climate-driven hydrological variability to economic development.

**Practical Implication**
Water stress imposes meaningful, threshold-driven productivity penalties on industrial firms, implying aggregate GDP-growth costs that intensify as climate change amplifies hydrological variability. Government support and water infrastructure matter: SOEs' preferential resource access mitigates losses, and water-supply/flood-control dams and denser urban drainage networks attenuate the adverse effects of negative runoff shocks, suggesting targets for adaptation policy.

**Limitations**
Several channel and adaptation analyses rely on substantially smaller matched samples (e.g., shrinking from 1.81 million to as few as 0.34–0.64 million observations) and on model-predicted or machine-learning-derived water-quality measures rather than direct observations, making some mechanism results suggestive rather than causal. The matching and instrumental-variable strategies for dams and drainage cannot fully rule out unobserved confounders, and the political pecking-order (SOE) result is specific to China.

**Future Research**
The authors note that water-quality channels concentrated in ultrapure-water industries warrant further study using direct in-situ measurements with broader geographic coverage, and that the suggestive evidence on adaptation through dams and drainage networks could be strengthened. Extending the analysis beyond China and beyond the 1998-2007 window would help assess external validity of how water stress impedes economic development.
