# Artificial Intelligence and Disability Entrepreneurship: Moving the Field Forward

**Abstract**
AI has already generated transformative effects across domains of business and everyday life. However, the integration of AI into the scholarship of disability entrepreneurship remains nascent. One critical challenge is the difficulty to identify what constitutes a theoretically distinct effect of AI on disabled entrepreneurs and corresponding entrepreneurial processes, as opposed to merely exploring its use among a marginalized group. To address this issue, the present study systematically analyzes how AI uniquely affects disability entrepreneurs. On this basis, the paper proposes a preliminary theoretical model of disability entrepreneurship in the AI era that examines both the positive and negative impacts of AI on disabled entrepreneurs, as well as the ethical dilemmas that arise from these dynamics. It then outlines a set of theoretically meaningful and practically relevant research opportunities that bridge the literatures on AI and disability entrepreneurship. By doing so, this paper positions disability entrepreneurship as a theoretically significant research domain in the AI era, advances discussions of ethical dilemmas related to AI, and offers a systematic framework for guiding future theoretical and empirical research.

**Research Question**
The paper addresses three questions: (1) How does AI uniquely influence disabled entrepreneurs? (2) What opportunities and risks does this bring to disabled entrepreneurs? and (3) How can future research build on these transformations to advance new lines of inquiry?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: AI is theorized as distinctly reshaping disability entrepreneurship along four dimensions — effects on the body and bodily functions (substitution and activation), psychological experiences (empowerment and vulnerability), AI as an external extension of the body (cognitive/operational augmentation), and transformation of the external environment (algorithmic evaluation, shifting legitimacy and expectations).
- Key constructs: entrepreneurial agency, self-efficacy, entrepreneurial identity, legitimacy, the ideology of ableism, external attribution, AI inclusivity.
- Feedback loops: a virtuous (positive) loop in which functional empowerment strengthens self-efficacy and identity, normalizes AI as a capability resource, and elicits favorable stakeholder attribution; a vicious (negative) loop in which dependence, uneven empowerment across disability types, low AI inclusivity (opacity), and misattribution of outcomes to the machine intensify ableist bias.
- Tipping-point conditions: AI technology, individual traits, and external attribution determine which loop dominates (Propositions 1-9; Fig. 1 balance-scale model).

The model reframes ethical dilemmas not as simple benefit-versus-exclusion trade-offs but as inherent outcomes of the entanglement among disabled entrepreneurs, AI technologies, and external environments: the same technologies that broaden functional capacity and reduce visible disadvantage can simultaneously produce psychological dependency, exclusionary stratification within and across disability categories, and new forms of "algorithmic ableism." Relative dominance of the positive versus negative cycle is primarily shaped by whether entrepreneurial outcomes are attributed to disabled entrepreneurs or to the machine, as conditioned by individual traits and AI design/inclusivity.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions: it establishes disability entrepreneurship as a theoretically significant domain in the AI era by identifying how AI distinctly reshapes disabled entrepreneurs and entrepreneurial processes (repositioning the field from a study of demographic variance to a lens on human-technology interaction, embodiment, and inclusion); it advances ethics scholarship by theorizing how AI generates ethical paradoxes (not simple trade-offs) for a marginalized group through coexisting positive and negative feedback loops; and it offers a systematic framework, organized around the ideology of ableism, for structuring future theoretical and empirical research at the intersection of AI and disability entrepreneurship.

**Practical Implication**
The framework is intended to guide inclusive technology design and evidence-based policymaking by clarifying when AI empowers rather than excludes disabled entrepreneurs. It highlights the need to attend to AI inclusivity and transparency, to how stakeholders (investors, partners, regulators) attribute success between entrepreneur and machine, and to governance mechanisms that protect sensitive disability-related data while preserving the empowering potential of AI.

**Limitations**
The paper is explicitly conceptual and preliminary: it proposes a theoretical model and propositions rather than testing them empirically. It deliberately narrows scope to how AI influences entrepreneurs with disabilities and their interactions with the external environment, so broader ethical issues that arise when enterprises rely on AI fall outside its scope and the propositions await empirical validation.

**Future Research**
The authors propose a structured research agenda mirroring the model's dimensions: using AI as an exogenous shock to study how lifting bodily/sensory constraints affects opportunity recognition and venture creation; examining the psychological effects of AI (which may operate even without adoption) on cognition, motivation, and self-efficacy; investigating the inclusiveness of AI as an extension of the body across heterogeneous impairment types; and studying how AI-mediated external evaluation (e.g., algorithmic screening, raised performance baselines, homogenized creativity, privacy of intimate data) reshapes legitimacy and resource access for disabled entrepreneurs.
