# Climate Beliefs and Attitudes and Corporate Tax Savings

**Abstract**
This study examines the relationship between climate beliefs and attitudes (CBA) and corporate tax savings. We document that firms headquartered in counties with high CBA are more likely to utilize climate change-related tax incentives to reduce their tax payments. Our evidence suggests that CBA’s influence manifests as increased climate-related R&D investments and corporate environmental commitments, which give rise to various investment-related tax credits, resulting in lower tax payments. Further analyses indicate that the positive relationship between CBA and corporate tax savings is more pronounced for firms in climate-vulnerable industries and those in states with climate action plans in place. Using attention-grabbing disaster events, the implementation of climate adaptation plans, and corporate headquarters relocation events, we document that heightened CBA incentivizes firms to realize greater tax benefits. Our findings present an important business ethics paradox: while firms in high-CBA counties genuinely invest in sustainability, they simultaneously engage in tax planning that reduces public revenue available for broader climate initiatives. This creates a tension between shareholder value maximization and responsible corporate citizenship, raising critical concerns about distributive justice and the alignment of private financial incentives with public environmental goals.

**Research Question**
Whether and to what extent do county-level climate beliefs and attitudes (CBA) surrounding a firm's headquarters influence corporate tax savings, and through what channels does CBA operate?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): County-level climate beliefs and attitudes (CBA), a PCA index of Yale Climate Opinion Maps survey responses (global warming "happening," "worried," "harm U.S."); shock-based variants for hurricane exposure, climate adaptation plans, and CBA-increasing headquarters relocation.
- DV(s): Corporate tax savings, proxied by the industry-size-adjusted GAAP effective tax rate (AdjETR; lower = greater savings), with AdjCETR, ETR5, CETR5, and UTB as alternatives.
- Mediators / channels: Climate-related green R&D investment (green patents) and CSR environmental commitment (Sustainalytics Escore); future environmental performance as the downstream outcome in the path analysis.
- Moderators: Industry climate vulnerability (CBA*Vul) and state climate adaptation plans (CBA*CAP).

Drawing on social norm theory, the paper argues that managers of firms in high-CBA counties conform to a nascent, increasingly prevalent climate social norm even absent personal belief, because stakeholder pressure and reputational risk push them toward sustainable practices. Firms respond by investing in climate-related R&D and pro-environmental initiatives, which generate investment-related tax credits and thereby lower effective tax rates. The DiD, relocation, conditional-mean, and placebo tests are used to argue that it is CBA, rather than concurrent climate risk, that drives the lower tax payments.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study documents a counterintuitive positive link between CBA (and, by extension, CSR) and corporate tax avoidance, contrasting with the dominant negative CSR–tax-avoidance finding and with the "organized hypocrisy" view of CSR-as-tax-camouflage. It surfaces a "business ethics paradox" in which high-CBA firms simultaneously advance and undermine climate goals, contributing to the social-norms, climate-awareness, and taxes-and-sustainability literatures by identifying a previously undocumented nontax benefit of tax planning and raising distributive-justice and intergenerational-equity concerns.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can align tax strategy with sustainability goals by channeling climate-related tax savings into verifiable green investments, demonstrating responsible corporate citizenship rather than financial arbitrage. Policymakers could institutionalize this alignment through regulatory safeguards—e.g., mandating that a defined portion of tax savings be allocated to carbon offsets or green R&D—so that private financial incentives serve collective decarbonization goals.

**Limitations**
Data limitations prevent the authors from distinguishing conventional "dirty" R&D from climate-related R&D, and reported R&D expenses may not accurately reflect actual R&D expenditures. The CBA measure is a county-level proxy for managers' own beliefs rather than a direct measure, and headquarters relocation decisions are potentially endogenous to state tax policy (partially addressed with controls and an instrumental-variable approach).

**Future Research**
The authors call for deeper investigation of how managers incorporate tax considerations into broader ESG initiatives, given the absence of an established theory or framework linking tax planning to ESG strategy. Further work is invited on the unintended consequences and ethical considerations of using climate-related tax incentives, particularly amid the growing popularity of policies such as the Inflation Reduction Act.
