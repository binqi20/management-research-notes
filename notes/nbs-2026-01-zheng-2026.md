# Short-term fit, long-term trap: The career development lock of low-skilled gig workers

**Abstract**
Why do low-skilled gig workers remain stuck in work they originally intended as temporary? Although gig work is widely portrayed as flexible and temporary, our study shows that platforms can gradually trap workers in place. Drawing on grounded theory and fieldwork—including 70 interviews with 42 food delivery riders and 14 ride-hailing drivers, 30 hours of firsthand riding experience, and observations of online communities totalling 820 riders—this study develops a conceptual framework of career development lock, identifying four interrelated forms: lock-out, lock-in, lock-up, and lock-down. We find that career development lock stems from a structural conflict

**Research Question**
(1) What does the career development lock look like for low-skilled gig workers? (2) What is the role of algorithmic management in low-skilled gig workers' career development lock?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Career development lock (four forms — lock-out, lock-in, lock-up, lock-down); short-term person–career fit vs. anticipated long-term person–career misfit; continuous temporal narrative (workers) vs. fragmented temporal logic (platform algorithms).
- Triggering conditions: Algorithmic management operating through agency suppression, resource deprivation, and normative framing (entrepreneurial/"freedom" discourse).
- Process / phases: Workers enter gig work as a strategic, temporary transition; algorithmic governance commodifies labour and dynamically intensifies discipline (declining pay, tightening control); this produces superficial short-term fit but growing long-term misfit.
- Practices: Adaptive self-exploitation — cycles of compensatory effort (longer hours, faster pace, greater risk) and "deferred futurity" that prioritize immediate survival over long-term development.
- Emergent outcomes: Voluntary retention and deepening entrapment; job-level constraints (lock-out, lock-in, lock-up) drive role-level lock-down, which feeds back to reinforce lock-out.

Career development lock arises from an irreconcilable conflict between workers' long-term aspirations (grounded in a continuous temporal narrative) and platform algorithms' need for flexible, on-demand labour (a fragmented temporal logic). Under algorithmic constraints, workers engage in adaptive self-exploitation that magnifies short-term fit while obscuring long-term misfit, reframing the erosion of labour protections as voluntary freedom and ultimately locking workers into the gig economy.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study introduces career development lock as a multidimensional, multidirectional, and dynamic form of entrapment, extending non-linear career concepts such as workplace "locked-in" (Stengård et al., 2016) and career inaction (Verbruggen & De Vos, 2020) by shifting the explanation from individual decision failure to exogenous structural forces. It recasts algorithmic management as a long-term career-structuring force—not merely a day-to-day control mechanism—that induces hidden adaptive self-exploitation, and it advances person–environment fit research by showing that fit and misfit coexist under different subjective time frames.

**Practical Implication**
Platforms should adopt moderated algorithmic designs (per-worker assignment caps, dynamic time buffers, limits on surge allocation) and mandatory algorithmic transparency, complemented by portable benefits to buffer income volatility. Governments, unions, and non-profits should provide embedded vocational training, skills-upgrade subsidies, and community support centres, while platforms implement skill-certification and promotion systems to dismantle the mobility ceiling of flat job designs.

**Limitations**
Findings are based on delivery riders and ride-hailing drivers, whose algorithmic oversight is more intensive than in other crowdwork, so generalizability to other low-skilled gig workers is uncertain. The study also relies on workers' retrospective accounts rather than longitudinal data tracking how lock unfolds over time.

**Future Research**
Future work should compare career development lock across platform governance models (e.g., centralized vs. decentralized) and adopt longitudinal approaches such as event-history analysis to capture how workers move across the four lock dimensions over time. It could also examine worker agency through algorithmic activism—how gig workers individually or collectively resist career development lock.
