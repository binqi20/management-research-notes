# Rewards-based crowdfunding as an institutional carrier: The cross-border backing of environmental projects

**Abstract**

Using Kickstarter data spanning campaigns in 153 countries and backers from 226 countries and territories, we find a positive relationship between projects' environ­ mental orientation and their likelihood of attracting cross-border funding. Moreover, this crossborder support originates from countries with stronger environmental institutions and is espe­ cially directed toward projects in weaker institutional contexts. Our findings differ from pre­ vailing expectations from international entrepreneurial finance research focused on venture capitalists and angels, which emphasize cross-border disadvantages for risky, environmental projects in weak-institutional contexts, while we report cross-border fundraising advantages for these projects. Our study contributes to research on the nexus of institutional theory, interna­ tional entrepreneurship, and crowdfunding research by showing how international crowdfunding platforms can enable the bottom-up diffusion of pro-environmental values and resources across country borders.

**Research Question**
Do environmental projects have an advantage in attracting cross-border funds on rewards-based crowdfunding platforms, and in which direction do funds flow across borders — specifically, do pro-environmental community logics channel cross-border backing from countries with stronger environmental institutions toward environmental projects located in countries with weaker environmental support systems?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Project's environmental orientation (dictionary-based text measure from Mansouri and Momtaz, 2022); project's country-level environmental institutions (Environment Performance Index, EPI)
- DV(s): Has International Community (binary indicator of any cross-border backing); Environmental Distance (relative strength of backers' environmental support systems compared to the project country)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper (community/institutional logic posited theoretically but not directly measured)
- Moderators: Local EPI (project country's environmental institutional strength) moderates the relationship between environmental orientation and environmental distance

Drawing on institutional theory, the authors theorize that global rewards-based crowdfunding platforms act as cross-border institutional carriers infused with a pro-environmental community logic. Because backers on such platforms are motivated by non-pecuniary, prosocial and environmental values rather than market-based financial returns, projects with stronger environmental orientations become salient to backers in countries with stronger environmental protection institutions, who then transmit resources and pro-environmental values across borders. This mechanism is amplified when recipient projects sit in weak-institution contexts, because backers in strong-institution countries recognize the acute resource constraints and global interconnectedness of environmental problems and direct support accordingly. The result is a bottom-up diffusion of pro-environmental logics and resources that overturns the cross-border disadvantages predicted by market-logic-based international entrepreneurial finance research.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to institutional theory by conceptualizing global crowdfunding platforms as cross-border institutional carriers that, unlike prior top-down accounts centered on multinationals, returnee elites, or state-linked intermediaries, enable dispersed crowds to diffuse pro-environmental values and resources bottom-up across national borders. It contributes to international entrepreneurship and entrepreneurial finance by showing that pro-environmental community logics in rewards-based crowdfunding reverse the cross-border disadvantages documented for venture capital and angel investing, producing fundraising advantages for risky environmental ventures in weak-institutional contexts. It further extends crowdfunding research by highlighting how community logics — distinct from the market logics dominating traditional finance — reshape global resource flows toward entrepreneurs addressing sustainability challenges.

**Practical Implication**
Rewards-based crowdfunding is a meaningful channel for raising cross-border funding for environmental sustainability projects, with environmentally oriented campaigns attracting a larger share of non-local backers (25.13% vs. 21.45%) than less environmental ones. Environmental entrepreneurs operating in countries with weak environmental support systems should strategically target and cultivate backers in countries with stronger environmental institutions, foregrounding the environmental impact and resource constraints of their ventures to activate prosocial motivations. Entrepreneurs should also invest in well-designed campaign content (video pitches, images, narratives, reward structures) because campaign quality strongly shapes cross-border fundraising success.

**Limitations**
The authors do not directly observe individual-level motivations of backers, so results document cross-border funding patterns consistent with pro-environmental community logics rather than directly testing the mechanism. The study uses a single platform (Kickstarter) and a composite country-level Environment Performance Index, leaving open whether findings generalize to other crowdfunding types/platforms and to specific components of environmental institutions (e.g., regulations, subsidies, enforcement). The sample is restricted to 2009-2016 (pre-Paris Agreement), so post-2016 institutional shifts lie outside the analysis.

**Future Research**
Future research should directly test the underlying community-logic mechanism using surveys, experiments, or qualitative analyses of backer motivations and project communications, and should examine backer heterogeneity and specific environmental policy dimensions rather than composite indices. Longitudinal studies are needed to document how crowdfunding platforms' role as cross-border institutional carriers evolves with technological, political, and social change, and to identify project- and country-level factors (including culture and language) that amplify or dampen this carrier role. Researchers should also investigate reverse flows — how innovations from weak-institution contexts diffuse to developed settings — and test whether the institutional-logic framework extends to funding of other social grand challenges beyond environmental issues.
