# Sharing is ethicizing: Examining the impact of sharing observed unethical behavior with family members on ethical voice at work.

**Abstract**
prior research has primarily focused on personal and intraorganizational factors shaping observer responses to workplace unethical behavior, the potential inﬂuence of extraorganizational factors has been largely overlooked. Recognizing that employees often share workplace experiences with close others, such as their family members, we explore how the extent of sharing with family members inﬂuences employee responses to observed unethical behavior. Integrating the functional perspective of rumination and social sharing research, we theorize that the extent of sharing with family members increases observer reﬂective rumination about the unethical behavior and their tendency to engage in ethical voice at work. We found empirical evidence in two critical incident surveys (Studies 1 and 4), an experiment (Study 2), and a crosslagged panel survey (Study 3). Finally, we discuss the theoretical and practical implications of this research.

**Research Question**
How does the extent to which employees share observed workplace unethical behavior with family members — an extraorganizational factor — influence their subsequent ethical voice at work, and through what cognitive mechanism does this effect operate?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Extent of sharing observed unethical behavior with a family member
- DV(s): Ethical voice at work
- Mediators: Reflective rumination about the unethical behavior
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

Integrating social sharing research (Rimé, 2009) and the functional perspective of rumination (Martin & Tesser, 1996), the authors argue that greater extent of sharing observed unethical behavior with a family member preserves unfiltered factual details and keeps the behavior salient in memory, thereby increasing reflective rumination — recurrent, problem-solving thoughts about the issue, its causes, and potential solutions. Because family members are uninvolved and trusted, conversations foreground factual details and broader implications rather than self-focused distress, fostering the adaptive reflective (rather than affective or risk-focused) form of rumination. Reflective rumination, in turn, mobilizes cognitive and motivational resources that propel the observer to engage in ethical voice — communicating ethical concerns or suggestions to those who can effect change. Study 4 shows that the indirect effect is uniquely driven by reflective rumination, not by risk, ethical, or affect-based rumination.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances behavioral ethics by offering a novel extraorganizational predictor (sharing with family members) of observer responses to unethical behavior, demonstrating unique variance beyond established personal and intraorganizational predictors and beyond sharing with coworkers. It extends the social sharing literature by differentiating sharing targets (family vs. coworkers) and by using a granular extent-based operationalization of sharing to show issue-specific downstream behavior rather than general well-being outcomes. It also contributes to the nascent literature on the beneficial effects of rumination by showing that reflective rumination — specifically, its problem-solving content — uniquely enables proactive ethical action, underscoring the importance of specifying the content of rumination rather than treating it as a unitary construct.

**Practical Implication**
Employees who suspect wrongdoing at work can benefit from openly and extensively discussing the issue with uninvolved, trusted family members, as this fosters reflective rumination and motivates them to raise ethical concerns at work, potentially saving organizations from future reputational damage. Organizations and policymakers should carefully review the scope of nondisclosure and confidentiality agreements to ensure they do not impede external reflections on observed unethical behavior and inadvertently suppress ethical voice. More broadly, open and candid work-family communication emerges as a potential tool for bolstering ethical behavior within organizations.

**Limitations**
In Studies 1 and 4, sharing extent was operationalized as the amount of time spent discussing the issue with a family member, which may not fully align with observers' subjective perceptions of how extensive the discussion was. The laboratory experiment (Study 2) used a constrained time frame, moderately intense manipulated unethical behavior, and an anonymous, lower-risk voice context, which may not elicit the highly persistent rumination or real-stakes voice behavior typical of field work settings. Only one study (Study 4) was conducted to rule out alternative forms of rumination as explanations, leaving the unique explanatory power of reflective rumination in need of further replication.

**Future Research**
Future research should use measures that capture observers' subjective perceptions of sharing extensiveness, present more intense unethical behavior with longer intervals between sharing and rumination measurement, and replicate Study 4's findings using different methods to strengthen the unique explanatory power of reflective rumination. Scholars should examine the temporal dynamics and bidirectional relationships between sharing and rumination over time and across sharing targets, investigate how conversation partners' reactions shape rumination content, and compare observer versus victim sharing in eliciting different rumination forms and ethical voice outcomes. Additional avenues include extending the framework to nonmoral workplace issues and examining when sharing unethical behavior helps or hinders family relationships and work-family conflict.
