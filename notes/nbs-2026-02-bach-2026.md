# Fair Market Value of Used Capacity Assets: Forecasts for Repurposed Electric Vehicle Batteries

**Abstract**
In response to growing economic and environmental concerns, companies in a range of industries seek to repurpose products (assets) that retain functional capacity beyond their initial first life. This paper examines a generic valuation model for used capacity assets that can either be recycled immediately or repurposed for a second life application. We apply our model framework to lithium-ion batteries retired from electric vehicles, as these assets typically retain substantial energy storage capacity at the end of their first life. Our analysis focuses on two battery chemistries: lithium-iron-phosphate (LFP) and nickel-cobalt-X (NCX) based. We project their future fair market values in the United States and China. Our findings indicate that repurposing LFP batteries will be economically viable in both countries for the coming decade. In contrast, for most NCX batteries immediate recycling will soon be preferable due to their more valuable raw material content and shorter usable lives.

**Research Question**
How should the fair market value of used capacity assets be determined when owners face a choice between immediate recycling and repurposing for a second-life application, and what does this imply for the economic viability of repurposing retired lithium-ion EV batteries (LFP vs. NCX) in the U.S. and China over the coming decade?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Battery chemistry (LFP vs. NCX); remaining useful capacity (state of health); expected rate of decline in new-asset prices due to technological progress; repurposing cost; recycling (terminal) value; market geography (U.S. vs. China)
- DV(s): Fair market value of used battery packs; value retention share (VRS); repurpose-vs.-recycle decision
- Mediators: User cost of capital (equilibrium price of one unit of capacity per period), determined by the more cost-competitive chemistry
- Moderators: New-battery price differential between markets; degradation rate; raw-material recycling value; competitive pressure from alternative chemistries

The authors extend replacement-value and user-cost-of-capital models to assets that can be recycled or repurposed. Under no-arbitrage pricing for energy storage services, the fair market value of a used asset equals the discounted cash flows from its remaining capacity valued at the user cost of capital, with two new elements: a terminal recycling payoff and a repurposing cost. The effective FMV at the end of first life is the better of immediate recycling versus costly repurposing followed by second-life deployment and delayed recycling. Because LFP and NCX chemistries compete in the same capacity market, NCX values are bounded by the lower user cost of capital set by LFP, so NCX batteries with high raw-material recycling value are pushed toward immediate recycling while LFP batteries remain attractive for repurposing.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends prior accounting and economic studies on the replacement value of operating assets (Arrow 1964; Rogerson 2008; Rajan and Reichelstein 2009) by incorporating two new elements into the user-cost-of-capital framework: a terminal recycling cash flow and an explicit repurposing cost that gates entry into a second-life application. The authors develop a no-arbitrage valuation framework in which multiple competing asset technologies determine the user cost of capital for capacity services, yielding a general model of how technological progress, repurposing costs, and raw-material values jointly shape repurpose-versus-recycle decisions. The framework generalizes beyond EV batteries to other second-life assets such as consumer electronics, data-center hardware, industrial equipment, and renewable-power components.

**Practical Implication**
For LFP batteries, repurposing into stationary energy storage is economically viable in both the U.S. and China over the coming decade, supporting business cases and investment in second-life deployment. For NCX batteries, owners should generally favor immediate recycling, especially in China and for batteries below roughly 80 percent state of health, with recycling becoming preferred in the U.S. even at 90 percent SOH by around 2031. Managers and investors can use the framework to guide corporate investment toward recycling versus repurposing infrastructure, and automakers may redesign vehicles (e.g., cell-to-body structural batteries for NCX, repurposing-friendly designs for LFP) and contractual arrangements (e.g., retained battery ownership rights) around these asset economics.

**Limitations**
The model assumes competitive and frictionless markets for new and used assets and treats key parameters (such as degradation pathways and raw-material values) as known; introducing uncertainty, volatility, and real-option effects is acknowledged as a complication. The empirical application is restricted to two battery chemistries (LFP and NCX) and two markets (U.S. and China), and does not incorporate temporary recycling or repurposing capacity constraints or risk aversion on the part of decision-makers.

**Future Research**
Future work could examine the sensitivity of fair market value estimates to market frictions such as limited recycling or repurposing capacity, and extend the framework to emerging battery chemistries such as sodium-ion and solid-state batteries, whose declining costs would further reduce the user cost of capital and the fair market value of incumbent chemistries. Researchers could also explore how vehicle owners will capture the residual value of used battery packs, including contractual models in which manufacturers retain ownership rights over the battery in exchange for lower upfront vehicle prices.
