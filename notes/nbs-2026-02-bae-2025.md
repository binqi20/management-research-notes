# Memento Mori: mortality salience, legacy and time perspective in sustainable consumption

**Abstract**
Purpose – The purpose of this study is to advance the understanding of the interplay between mortality salience, mortality legacy and sustainable consumption behavior. Design/methodology/approach – Data were collected through an online questionnaire from 1,000 consumers aged over 25 in the USA. Findings – The results show that mortality salience induces sustainable behavior and that mortality legacy mediates this relationship. Moreover, a limited future time perspective is found to moderate the mediation process in the path from mortality salience to mortality legacy. Furthermore, age did not emerge as a significant moderator of the indirect effect of mortality salience on sustainable consumption behavior through mortality legacy. Originality/value – The findings connect the terror management theory with the socioemotional selectivity theory. They also reveal that marketers can nudge consumers’ sustainable consumption behavior by incorporating mortality salience and legacy appeals into their marketing communications.

**Research Question**
How does mortality salience influence sustainable consumption behavior, and do mortality legacy (as mediator) and limited future time perspective and chronological age (as moderators) shape this relationship, drawing on terror management theory and socioemotional selectivity theory?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Mortality salience
- DV(s): Sustainable consumption behavior
- Mediators: Mortality legacy
- Moderators: Limited future time perspective; chronological age

Drawing on terror management theory, the authors argue that awareness of death prompts individuals to manage existential anxiety through distal defenses such as upholding cultural worldviews and boosting self-esteem. In contemporary society, sustainability functions as a dominant moral worldview, so mortality salience heightens consumers' desire to leave a mortality legacy — a lasting positive contribution that transcends physical death — which in turn channels behavior into sustainable consumption as a form of symbolic immortality. Integrating socioemotional selectivity theory, the authors further theorize that consumers with a limited future time perspective perceive time as scarce and therefore become more motivated to engage in generative, legacy-oriented behaviors, strengthening the mediated path from mortality salience to sustainable consumption via mortality legacy. Chronological age was hypothesized to operate similarly but was not supported empirically.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends terror management theory by demonstrating a novel, constructive distal defense: people adopt sustainable consumption behavior, via the desire for a mortality legacy, as a way to manage death-related anxiety and achieve symbolic immortality. It contributes to the literature on antecedents of sustainable consumption by identifying mortality salience and legacy motivation as psychological drivers, and it advances socioemotional selectivity theory by showing that limited future time perspective — but not chronological age — moderates the indirect effect, suggesting that psychological rather than chronological markers of time horizon matter most in mortality-driven sustainable consumption.

**Practical Implication**
Marketers and policymakers can promote sustainable consumption by designing communications that subtly elicit consumers' awareness of mortality and foster a desire to leave a lasting legacy, framing sustainability around "protecting what matters for future generations" or "leaving a meaningful mark" rather than using explicit fear-based appeals. Because age did not moderate the effect, such interventions can be deployed broadly without age-specific tailoring, though they should be implemented ethically and transparently. Managers should also consider consumers' future time perspective when deploying mortality salience appeals, since legacy motivations are strongest among those who perceive their time as limited.

**Limitations**
The survey data were collected only in the USA, which may limit generalizability, especially given that consumer responses to mortality salience, mortality legacy, and sustainable consumption may differ between Asian and Western cultures. The study did not empirically compare the relative strength of opposing psychological mechanisms — helplessness or avoidance versus legacy motives — that mortality salience can elicit. The lack of a significant age moderation effect also invites reconsideration of how chronological age operates within these processes.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine whether mortality salience similarly affects sustainable consumption behavior in Asian cultural contexts (e.g., South Korea, China, Japan) where religious traditions such as Buddhism may shape death-related cognition. Researchers should explore alternative moderating variables such as cultural values, personal environmental attitudes, and socioeconomic status, and investigate psychological markers of age (e.g., subjective, felt, or cognitive age) rather than chronological age. Further work should also examine boundary conditions under which helplessness versus legacy motives dominate as responses to mortality salience.
