# Englishization Beyond the Global North: Dependency, Struggle, and Resistance in Brazilian Management Research

**Abstract**
This paper advances understanding of Englishization in management research by addressing a key limitation in the existing literature: its predominant focus on the Global North, especially Europe. Drawing on dependency theory and postcolonial theory, we examine how management academics experience and navigate Englishization in Brazil. Based on qualitative research, we found that Brazilian academics, like their European counterparts, faced growing pressures to publish in international journals, effectively compelling publication in English, though such expectations were largely concentrated in elite institutions. We also found that Brazilian journals themselves increasingly adopted English in pursuit of international visibility, thereby intensifying pressure to publish in English and extending Englishization beyond the elite. One consequence was the publication of “Frankenstein” papers—the result of limited English proficiency and financial constraints—which paradoxically risked undermining the visibility Brazilian journals sought to enhance. Yet Englishization was not passively accepted: it was actively negotiated through strategic compliance, postcolonial ambivalence, and counter-hegemonic resistance. These findings contribute to a more nuanced, context-sensitive, and theoretically-informed understanding of Englishization by applying postcolonial and dependency perspectives to highlight its uneven trajectory across and within national contexts and to show how it is entangled with enduring Global North–South inequalities.

**Research Question**
How are imperatives to publish in English experienced within Brazilian management academia, and how do dynamics of dependency and postcoloniality shape these experiences?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: academic dependency (Alatas); postcolonial ambivalence and mimicry (Bhabha); Global North–South epistemic hierarchies; intra-national institutional divide between elite management schools and public universities
- Process / phases (the paper's three overarching themes): (1) Navigating Englishization — institutional pressures and strategic appropriation by Brazilian journals and evaluation systems; (2) Struggling with Englishization — linguistic barriers and institutional/material constraints producing "Frankenstein" papers; (3) Contesting Englishization — strategic compliance, postcolonial ambivalence, and counter-hegemonic resistance
- Modes of dependency: pressure from international journal rankings; Qualis and SciELO evaluation systems rewarding English-language publication; elite schools' international-publication expectations; Brazilian journals' own adoption of English in pursuit of visibility
- Practices / acts of resistance: perfunctory mimicry "without mastery" via cheap translations; editors' reluctant compliance while criticizing English as colonization; refusing editorial roles in English-only journals; founding Portuguese-only journals (e.g., RECADM, RCD); requesting exclusion from Qualis; withdrawing from doctoral programs requiring English
- Boundary conditions: elite management schools vs. public universities; availability of translation/editing funds; Brazilian currency devaluation; academic seniority and bilingual capital; journals' ranking status on international lists

Drawing on dependency theory and postcolonial theory, the paper theorizes Englishization as both reflective and constitutive of academic dependency: global Anglophone hierarchies and rankings pressure Global South research systems to align with Northern epistemic norms, and domestic institutions — Qualis, SciELO, and Brazilian journals themselves — internalize these standards in pursuit of international visibility. Within this structural terrain, materially constrained academics and editors engage in hybrid, ambivalent practices that simultaneously reproduce and destabilize the dominance of English. Englishization is thus enacted as a contested, uneven process shaped by the interplay of Global North–South inequalities, domestic institutional logics, and constrained local agency.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study offers a more nuanced, context-sensitive, and theoretically-informed account of Englishization by (1) challenging the assumption that Englishization unfolds uniformly across national contexts and foregrounding intra-national institutional variation (elite management schools vs. public universities); (2) conceptualizing Englishization as both reflective and constitutive of academic dependency, reproduced through domestic institutions' strategic appropriation of Anglo-American norms under structural constraint; and (3) demonstrating that Englishization is actively negotiated through hybrid practices—strategic compliance, postcolonial ambivalence, and counter-hegemonic resistance—rather than being simply adopted or resisted. By bridging dependency and postcolonial theory, the paper advances a politically grounded understanding of Englishization as entangled with enduring Global North–South inequalities.

**Practical Implication**
Business schools, journal editors, and research-evaluation bodies in the Global South should recognize that imposing English-language publication requirements without supporting linguistic and material resources can produce poorly translated "Frankenstein" papers that undermine rather than enhance international visibility. Domestic evaluation systems (e.g., Qualis, SciELO) and local journals should reconsider policies that reward English-language publication as a proxy for quality, and support linguistic diversity and Portuguese-language scholarship as legitimate venues. More broadly, decolonizing management knowledge production requires attending to the intra-national inequalities and material constraints that shape whose scholarship becomes visible in global academic circuits.

**Limitations**
Findings from Brazil may not generalize to other Global South contexts with different colonial histories, economic structures, and academic traditions. The study focused on academics and journal editors but paid limited attention to subfields or subdisciplines within management, and its interpretive qualitative design limits broad generalizability and may reflect self-reporting biases.

**Future Research**
Future studies should investigate Englishization in other Global South countries (e.g., China, former British and French colonies) and use comparative cross-national designs to examine how different colonial legacies and academic traditions shape dependency, struggle, and resistance. Research should also explore disciplinary variation in Englishization within Brazilian and other Global South contexts, use quantitative methods to systematically examine patterns of Englishization and its impact on visibility, and extend analysis to teaching and service domains of academic work.
