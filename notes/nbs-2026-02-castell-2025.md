# Social Media Is a Threat for Democracy! A Political Perspective for Analysing and Diminishing Harm

**Abstract**
Social media platforms, once hailed as potential champions of dialogue, have evolved into commodified spaces in which their business models incentivize hate speech, misinformation, polarization, and the political fragmentation of society, benefiting corporate and political elites while eroding democracy. The existing market and technological conceptualizations of social media platforms offer only partial analysis of and solutions to these issues. This paper advances a political perspective grounded in deliberative democracy, arguing that social media companies are political actors responsible for maintaining a well-functioning public sphere. We argue that transforming social media platforms into true facilitators of democratic discourse requires enhancing their deliberative capacity. Beyond the capacities of the public sphere suggested by the market and technology perspectives (1) transparency and accountability and (2) openness and inclusiveness, we propose that fostering deliberative capacity also requires (3) conduciveness to argumentation and (4) consequentiality. We thus contribute to understanding how management theories can explain and address current threats to democracy.

**Research Question**
Is social media threatening the democratic capacity of our societies, and if so, what should we do about it? The authors ask how management scholarship can re-conceptualize social media platforms — beyond market and technological lenses — as political actors whose governance shapes the deliberative quality of the public sphere.

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Social media platforms are political actors (not merely market intermediaries or technical infrastructures) that produce and regulate a public sphere, and therefore bear responsibility for its deliberative quality.
- Key constructs: Deliberative capacity of the public sphere; four democratic capacities — (1) transparency and accountability, (2) openness and inclusiveness, (3) conduciveness to argumentation, (4) consequentiality.
- Boundary conditions / existing lenses: Market perspective (representative democracy; transparency + accountability) and technology perspective (participatory democracy; openness + inclusion) — each necessary but individually insufficient for a well-functioning public sphere.
- Proposed mechanisms for reform: Platform-level redesign of algorithmic architectures (e.g., diversity-preserving recommendation, sustained engagement with critical issues); regulatory-level frameworks for platform accountability and deliberative governance; individual-level digital literacy and citizenship education.

The political perspective argues that platforms' business models — surveillance-based attention maximization, algorithmic amplification of emotional engagement, and concentrated ownership — systematically erode the conditions for Habermasian rational-critical discourse, producing polarization, hate speech, misinformation, and democratic fragmentation. Market fixes (fact-checking, antitrust, transparency about algorithms) address information quality but not deliberative quality; technological fixes (decentralization, blockchain verification, open-source protocols) address openness and inclusion but not argumentation or democratic will formation. Enhancing deliberative capacity therefore requires adding conduciveness to argumentation (platform affordances that foster reasoned exchange rather than reactive outrage) and consequentiality (mechanisms that translate deliberative outcomes into binding public decisions), jointly enabling social media to support rather than undermine collective will formation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes a political perspective of social media platforms grounded in deliberative democracy and the political view of the firm (Scherer and Palazzo), complementing and integrating the dominant market and technology perspectives in management scholarship on platform governance. It introduces two novel capacities of the public sphere — conduciveness to argumentation and consequentiality — and theorizes how platform design, ownership, and governance jointly shape these capacities. It also connects debates on platform governance with social evaluations, stakeholder engagement, and the "political role of business" literature, advocating a deeper engagement with democratic theory in management research.

**Practical Implication**
The essay proposes concrete reforms at three levels. At the platform level, companies should redesign recommendation and engagement systems to foster diverse, sustained, and reasoned discourse (e.g., connecting neighbouring discourses instead of reinforcing echo chambers; rewarding extended engagement with critical issues rather than quick emotional reactions). At the regulatory level, governments should develop frameworks (e.g., in the spirit of the EU Digital Services Act) that hold platforms accountable as political actors with public-sphere responsibilities, including deliberative capacity obligations. At the individual level, digital literacy and civic education — supported by governments, schools, libraries, and non-profits — should equip citizens to participate competently in algorithmically mediated public debate.

**Future Research**
The authors call for further theoretical and empirical investigation of the tensions underlying conducive argumentation (polarization, fragmentation, hate speech, misinformation, short-termism) across different platform governance models, including varying degrees of centralization and accountability, and the internal power dynamics of platform organizations. They also urge research on consequentiality — how deliberative outcomes on social media translate into legitimacy changes, governance practices, stakeholder engagement, and institutional change — and on the cognitive and emotional barriers that hinder this translation. Finally, they call for interdisciplinary collaboration among technologists, philosophers, political theorists, and management scholars to develop the ethical, political, and regulatory frameworks needed for democratic integration of social media.
