# Are Social Media Platforms a Threat to Democracy? An Ecosystem Governance Perspective

**Abstract**
Castello, Colleoni, Scherer, and Trittin contend that social media platforms threaten democratic processes by facilitating the spread of misinformation and fostering polarized debates – dynamics that ultimately serve to monetize user attention. We acknowledge the problem but challenge their proposed solution of conceptualizing social media as political spaces. Instead, drawing on ecosystem theory, we propose an 'ecosystem failure' analysis as a more robust analytical framework. Within this perspective, we see these information-related problems as negative externalities that weaken the entire platform ecosystem. These externalities are structurally distinct due to algorithmic curation that selectively amplifies content in ways that reinforce user biases, strong network effects with almost zero replication costs, and engagement-based monetization that decouples production from editorial standards. We therefore advance a reflexive ecosystem governance approach that accounts for broader ecosystem value, shifting the focus beyond short-term economic performance to encompass overall ecosystem health. This approach transforms the management of negative externalities into a core strategic interest for the platform and aligns the incentives of individual actors ex ante, by design, prioritizing risk-adjusted reach and demotion of low-integrity content over ex post moderation. We analyse the mechanisms that align private incentives with democratic resilience and highlight the inherent trade-offs involved.

**Research Question**
Are social media platforms inherently threatening democracy, and if so, is the appropriate response to reimagine them as political/deliberative spaces — or is it instead to treat their harms (fake news, misinformation, polarization) as 'ecosystem failures' and redesign platform governance to internalize these negative externalities?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: SMP harms are not defects of commercial nature per se but endogenous 'ecosystem failures' — structural consequences of algorithmic curation, strong network effects with near-zero replication costs, and engagement-based monetization decoupled from editorial standards.
- Key constructs: ecosystem failure (value capture distortion / coordination failure / structural pathology); negative externalities of user attention markets; generativity tension between 'desired' and 'undesired' output; multidimensional value (short-term engagement vs. long-term trust, informational quality, ecosystem health).
- Diagnosis of three failures: (1) fake news as value-capture distortion (virality uncorrelated with truth); (2) misinformation as coordination failure (selective exposure and biased amplification); (3) polarization as structural ecosystem pathology (engagement-driven personalization endogenizing ideological segmentation).
- Proposed mechanisms (reflexive ecosystem governance): participatory governance, adaptive control, integrity safeguards, and intentional value redistribution — operating ex ante by re-pricing visibility and rewards rather than ex post content moderation.
- Boundary conditions / trade-offs: platform heterogeneity (not all SMPs face the same failures); advertiser brand-safety vs. user preferences for controversial content; risk of over- or under-moderation; political capture of moderation authority.

The authors argue that because SMPs are ecosystem architects whose governance design choices shape who sees what and why, misinformation and polarization cannot be corrected by reactive moderation, user education, or by reimagining platforms as public utilities. Instead, changing the structure of the payoff — embedding integrity costs into ranking, replication, and reward rules — realigns private incentives with ecosystem health, so that high-quality engagement dominates low-integrity virality as a design-time rather than police-time outcome.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a 'reflexive ecosystem governance' framework that reframes platform democratic harms as ecosystem failures rather than political-space failures, and operationalizes Jacobides, Cennamo & Gawer's (2024) ecosystem-failure typology for social media through four governance mechanisms adapted from Bacq and Aguilera's (2021) stakeholder governance theory. In doing so, it distinguishes SMP externalities from traditional-media externalities along three structural dimensions (algorithmic selectivity, near-zero replication cost, engagement–monetization decoupling), and reframes platform governance from incentive-control dyads toward multidimensional value management — what the authors call 'platform governance 2.0'.

**Practical Implication**
Platforms should move from ex post content moderation toward ex ante governance design: risk-adjusted ranking, demotion of low-integrity content, bot-detection and political-ad transparency, participatory algorithm-review panels, intentional redistribution of visibility to public-interest journalists and educators, and algorithmic audits with trusted flaggers. Regulators (e.g., under the EU Digital Services Act) should focus on requiring platforms to identify, assess, and mitigate systemic risks rather than directly adjudicating content, and should resist solutions that effectively make platforms arbiters of truth.

**Future Research**
The authors call for further scholarly work on the future of platform governance and on designing more trustworthy digital ecosystems, including how to distinguish structural incentives from actual content effects across heterogeneous SMP models, how to operationalize reflexive governance mechanisms in practice, and how regulation (e.g., DSA-style systemic-risk reporting) can complement rather than substitute for platform-level ecosystem design.
