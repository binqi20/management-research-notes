# Emancipatory entrepreneurship by a stigmatized social group

**Abstract**
In the world, marginalized groups such as women in highly patriarchal societies, displaced war victims, and oppressed ethnicities face structural constraints when attempting careers in established corporations. As a result, they may seek entrepreneurship as a way to break free from various constraints, despite stigma and lower power positions. In this study, we examine how a stigmatized social group can engage in emancipatory entrepreneurship through interactions with different stakeholders. We conducted a qualitative study of how First Nations in Canada collectively opened casinos to emancipate from post-colonial policies. By analyzing documents from 1988 to 2018 and 31 interviews with individuals who played major roles in casino creation or are knowledgeable about the process, we found that a stigmatized group will go through waves of contestations with stakeholders interspersed by interim settlements, once they are internally mobilized for collective entrepreneurship. We identified different types of contestations, including contestation around the stigmatized's collective entrepreneurial identity, autonomy, resource eligibility, venture differentiation and governance. Overall, we found that the stigmatized collective entrepreneur can use a mix of symbolic and material strategies to counter the resistance of stigmatizing stakeholders. In so doing, they can gain access to entrepreneurship and preserve the precarious path of emancipatory entrepreneurship.

**Research Question**
How does emancipatory entrepreneurship by a stigmatized social group unfold over time?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Collective internal mobilization of the stigmatized group; contentious interactions with stigmatizing audiences
- DV(s): Progress of emancipatory entrepreneurship (venture creation and emancipation progress)
- Mediators: Interim settlements; partial acquiescence; managing interdependence with stigmatizers
- Moderators: Nature of contestation domain (identity, autonomy, resource eligibility, venture differentiation, governance)

The authors theorize a three-phase process in which a stigmatized group first mobilizes internally by forging a shared collective entrepreneurial identity and narrative, then enters a first wave of contestations with stigmatizing audiences over collective entrepreneurial identity, autonomy, and resource eligibility, which resolves into an interim settlement enabling venture creation. A second wave of contestations centered on venture differentiation and governance then produces adjusted settlements. Drawing on contentious collective action theory, the authors argue that a mix of symbolic and material strategies, combined with partial acquiescence to powerful stigmatizers, enables the group to recalibrate power, legitimate its ventures, and transition stigmatizers into apparently supportive audiences, sustaining the precarious path of emancipatory entrepreneurship over time.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances the EE literature by developing a process model of collective emancipatory entrepreneurship by stigmatized groups, shifting the locus of EE theorizing from individual entrepreneurs to the dynamic interactions between group entrepreneurs and external audiences. It shows that partial acquiescence to stigmatizing audiences, rather than signaling emancipation failure, can be instrumental in forming interim settlements that sustain ventures and enable long-term progress toward emancipation. It further contributes to the entrepreneurship-stigma intersection by highlighting the importance of managing interdependence with stigmatizing audiences, demonstrating that stigmatizers also depend on the obedience of the stigmatized, which creates leverage that group entrepreneurs can exploit.

**Practical Implication**
Stigmatized groups pursuing collective entrepreneurship should anticipate waves of contention with media, government, and public stakeholders and prepare by mobilizing internal unity through shared collective entrepreneurial identities and narratives. They should combine power-based tactics such as protests with actions designed to gain sympathy and support, and work with alternative resource providers like social procurement programs or inclusive business accelerators when mainstream sources are closed. They should also accept that negotiated partial concessions and interim settlements, though uncomfortable, can be critical stepping stones toward longer-term emancipation.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper.

**Future Research**
The authors call for research in other contexts beyond First Nations casinos to validate and extend the process model of collective emancipatory entrepreneurship, particularly for other stigmatized groups such as women in highly patriarchal societies, undocumented immigrants, and people with histories of mental illness or incarceration. They also invite further study of the dynamics of contestations and settlements in emancipatory entrepreneurship and of how such processes can inform approaches to alleviating oppression via entrepreneurship.
