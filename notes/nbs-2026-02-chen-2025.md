# Do Women Executives Make Workplaces Safer? Evidence from Workplace Safety Violations

**Abstract**
In this study, we develop and test theory on whether, when, and how the prevalence of women in firms' top management influences workplace safety – an important 'do no harm' dimension of corporate social performance. Consistent with our theorizing, we find that there is a negative relationship between the prevalence of women executives in firms' top management and workplace safety violations. We show that the relationship is amplified by board gender diversity and ownership by institutional investors who advocate for greater gender diversity in firms' upper echelons. We also show that the relationship is partially mediated by employee workload. Our findings have important implications for the management literature on the gender profile of firms' top management and for the workplace safety literature.

**Research Question**
Whether, when, and how the prevalence of women executives in firms' top management influences workplace safety — a fundamental 'do no harm' aspect of corporate social performance.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Women Execs (dummy = 1 if the TMT has two or more women executives among the top five highest-paid, else 0)
- DV(s): Safety Violation (binary occurrence); Ln(1 + No. Violations) (frequency); Ln(1 + $ Penalties) (severity) — all from Violation Tracker
- Mediator: Employee Workload (first principal component of abnormal production and ln production per employee)
- Moderators: High Diversity (above-median share of women directors on the board); High Big Three (above-median post-2016 ownership by Vanguard, State Street, and BlackRock, who advocate for gender diversity)
- Controls: board independence and size, CEO-chair duality, firm size, ROA, market-to-book, leverage, turnover, tangibility, capital expenditure, firm fixed effects, year fixed effects

The authors theorize that women executives' communal orientation and greater risk aversion elevate the salience of employee welfare within top management decision-making, discouraging excessive risk-taking and encouraging more deliberative oversight of safety policies, safety climate, and — most directly — employee workload. Because workload decisions cascade from executive priorities, performance targets, and incentive designs, TMTs with critical-mass women's representation are expected to avoid production pressures that push workers beyond safe capacity. The influence of women executives is amplified when structural supports — a gender-diverse board and gender-advocating institutional owners — legitimate and embolden their voice, attenuating the discounting of minority perspectives within the TMT.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reframes research on executive gender by shifting attention from the 'do good' to the 'do no harm' dimension of corporate social performance, showing that demographic composition at the apex is consequential for firms' basic obligation to protect employee welfare. It bridges the largely disconnected literatures on executive gender and board gender diversity by documenting their reinforcing dynamic, and refines theory on institutional ownership by showing that gender-advocating owners can legitimate — rather than substitute for — women executives' influence. By identifying employee workload as a proximal mediating mechanism, it extends the small body of work that opens the 'black box' between women's executive representation and firm outcomes.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations seeking to improve workplace safety have a concrete motivation to recruit, retain, and advance women executives through leadership development, mentorship, and inclusive hiring. Firms with less diverse boards can pursue board-level gender diversity and partnerships with gender-advocating institutional owners to create a reinforcing governance dynamic. Transparent disclosure of workplace safety metrics and diversity initiatives targeting top management can align investor expectations with organizational safety priorities.

**Limitations**
Reliance on US data may limit generalizability to other cultural and institutional contexts with different regulatory enforcement or gender norms. The Violation Tracker proxy captures reported violations and may overlook unreported incidents or latent safety concerns. The workload measure relies on archival proxies (abnormal production, production per employee) rather than employees' self-reported workload, task complexity, or physiological stress indicators.

**Future Research**
Cross-national and cross-cultural studies could uncover additional boundary conditions on how societal, regulatory, and cultural factors shape the gender-safety link. Researchers could examine downstream effects on employee well-being, absenteeism, and long-term firm performance, and explore which specific executive roles (CFO, COO, CHRO) and the alignment between women executives and women middle managers most shape safety outcomes. Employee surveys, qualitative interviews, and third-party safety audits would enrich measurement of both workload and safety.
