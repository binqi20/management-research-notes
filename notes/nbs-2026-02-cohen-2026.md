# The ESG-Innovation Disconnect: Evidence from Green Patenting

**Abstract**
Notes. This table reports the results of OLS regressions where the dependent variable is industry green patenting, which is the mean of total green patents divided by the total patents for all firms in each industry. Energy Sector is defined in Table 1. We identify green patents using OECD’s IPC classification; that is, green patents are the ones that contain one of the following environmental technologies: environmental management; water-related adaptation technologies; biodiversity protection; ecosystem health; climate change mitigation technologies related to energy generation, transmission, or distribution, transportation, buildings, wastewater treatment or waste management, and production or processing of goods; and capture, storage, sequestration, or disposal of greenhouse gases. The unit of observation is industry (two-digit SIC code) and year. The sample covers 1980 to 2020. Reported t-statistics in parentheses are heteroscedasticity-robust and clustered by industry. FE, fixed effect.

**Research Question**
Which firms actually contribute most significantly to green innovation in the United States, and do current ESG rating and capital allocation mechanisms effectively reward the entities best positioned to develop environmental technologies? The authors in particular ask whether traditional energy firms are systematically penalized by ESG ratings and divestment-oriented capital flows despite their role as producers of green patents.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Membership in the traditional energy sector (SIC 10, 12, 13, 14, 29, 49, excluding alternative-energy NAICS); number of green patents granted; interaction of Energy Sector with Green Patents Granted.
- DV(s): Industry and firm green patent ratio (green patents / total patents); Sustainalytics/MSCI environmental score; green patent quality (adjusted citations, blockbuster-patent indicator); first-mover status (USPTO patent number rank, top-10% pioneering indicator); patent-thicket measures; production of low-carbon products; revenue from low-carbon products; green hiring; fund holdings and flows.
- Mediators: Tangible green investment (green R&D, green capital, green labor, low-carbon product revenue) linking patenting to real environmental outcomes.
- Moderators: Energy-sector status (Energy Sector × Green Patents interaction); pre-2004 vs. post-2004 period split relative to the coining of "ESG"; top-3 non-energy sectors vs. energy comparison.

The paper develops a conceptual framework grounded in industrial organization: when incumbent firms rationally anticipate the eventual technological displacement of their existing product lines, profit-maximizing behavior leads them to invest in the very innovations that could cannibalize their current business, aligning private incentives with environmental objectives without external ESG inducements. Empirically, traditional energy firms dedicate roughly 20.91% of their patenting to green innovation (vs. 9.2% for other green-patenting industries), produce patents with about 14% more citations and more top-5% "blockbuster" patents, enter green technology classes over 600,000 patent-sequence slots earlier and are roughly 80% more likely to produce pioneering patents, and accompany this with greater low-carbon product revenue and green hiring. Yet ESG rating providers systematically score them lower and penalize them further for each additional green patent, and environmentally focused funds underweight them, creating a disconnect between green innovation producers and where sustainable capital is directed.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper documents, for the first time at large scale, the landscape of U.S. green patenting and shows that incumbent "brown" firms — specifically traditional energy producers — are first-mover, high-quality, foundational green innovators, contradicting the implicit premise of ESG exclusion frameworks. It offers a competitive-dynamics / cannibalization mechanism from industrial organization under which private, profit-maximizing incentives can drive environmental innovation absent preferential capital allocation, and speaks to broader questions about harnessing market competition to achieve societal objectives. It also provides evidence that ESG ratings fail to capture concrete green innovation, product, and labor inputs, offering a critique of current ESG measurement practice.

**Practical Implication**
Investors, ESG rating providers, and policymakers should incorporate green innovation inputs and outputs (patenting, low-carbon products, green hiring, and the acquisition and citation of foundational green IP) when evaluating firms' environmental contributions, rather than relying on sector-level exclusion or disclosure-centric scoring. A more balanced evaluation framework — one that recognizes both the current environmental footprint of traditional energy firms and their demonstrated role in producing technologies critical for the green transition — could improve capital allocation toward firms actually advancing decarbonization. The authors report that, following circulation of their paper, at least one major ESG rating provider (MSCI) introduced patent-based "Environmental Opportunities" indicators reflecting clean tech, green building, and renewable energy.

**Limitations**
The analysis is confined to U.S. publicly traded firms and USPTO patents, so private and non-U.S. green innovators are outside the sample. ESG score data only begin in 2008, limiting the ESG-specific regressions to 2008-2020. The authors also acknowledge that traditional energy firms remain substantial contributors to fossil-fuel extraction and emissions, so the findings do not imply that ESG investors should refrain from divestment — only that current evaluation frameworks may be incomplete.

**Future Research**
Future work should develop ESG evaluation frameworks that explicitly incorporate green innovation inputs and outputs (patents, low-carbon products, green employment) and test how such richer measures affect capital allocation, prices, and real environmental outcomes. Researchers could extend the incumbent-cannibalization framework to other industries facing technological obsolescence, examine international patenting and non-U.S. energy firms, and directly model the interaction between divestment pressure and incumbent innovation incentives to understand when market competition alone suffices to drive the green transition versus when targeted capital allocation is needed.
