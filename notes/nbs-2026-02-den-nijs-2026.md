# Enhancing regional resilience for energy price shocks: efficient gas use and upstream decarbonization

**Abstract**
Resilience and competitiveness in relation to fossil energy dependencies is of increasing concern to industries and policy makers. We investigate to what extent the competitive position of industries in European regions are sensitive to changes in fossil fuel prices, and whether reductions in gas use along the value chain may increase resilience of regional industry. A new spatial revealed cost competition model based on the input–output price model is used and calibrated to multi-regional world input–output tables on an EU NUTS 2 level. We obtain elasticities of fossil fuel prices on revealed cost competitiveness and analyse how resilience can be enhanced by increasing efficiency and electrification in production. We show that European regions are resilient to global coal price increases, but are vulnerable to gas price shocks. The transition towards using less gas in production, by efficiency improvements or electrification, can reduce these gas price vulnerabilities. However, when competitors become more efficient instead, the vulnerability to such shocks may increase. Decarbonizing upstream sectors like electricity generation in the own region, own country or in Europe, can increase resilience of downstream industrial sectors in most European regions.

**Research Question**
To what extent is the competitive position of industries in European regions sensitive to changes in fossil fuel prices, and whether (and how) reductions in gas use along the value chain — through efficiency improvements, electrification, and upstream decarbonization — may increase the resilience of regional industry to energy price shocks.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Fossil fuel price changes (global and European end-user prices for gas, coal, and oil); marginal changes in technological coefficients capturing efficiency improvements and electrification in own-region, own-country, EU, and competitor locations, including upstream sectors (electricity generation, oil refinery, and cokes production)
- DV(s): Revealed cost competitiveness (RCC) of region-sector industries and its price elasticity, interpreted as regional equilibrium resilience
- Mediators: Input–output cost pass-through along the value chain; overlap of regions on shared sales markets; spatial competitor structure
- Moderators: Sectoral specialization of regions; gas intensity in production; location of competitors (own region, country, EU, non-EU); technological frontier position

Fossil fuel price shocks translate into differential cost increases across region-sectors via an input–output price model that captures full pass-through along value chains. These cost changes are compared to cost changes faced by the region-sector's spatially differentiated competitors in the revealed competition framework of Thissen et al. (2013), yielding a revealed cost competitiveness elasticity. Decarbonization — modeled as marginal changes in technological coefficients reducing gas use or substituting electricity — shifts the baseline equilibrium and thereby alters this elasticity, so that efficiency gains or upstream decarbonization in the own region, country, or Europe reduce vulnerability, whereas equivalent improvements among competitors raise relative dependency and lower resilience.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a new regional revealed cost competition (RCC) model that fully integrates an input–output price model with the revealed competition measure of Thissen et al. (2013), providing a first-order method for analyzing how cost changes, technological changes, or policies affect the cost competitiveness of industries in specific regions without relying on unknown substitution elasticities. It extends the literature on regional energy resilience beyond supply-side shocks to study price-shock resilience of energy-using industrial sectors through competitive position, and offers a framework for linking industrial ecology arguments about efficiency and decarbonization to regional competitive dynamics in global value chains.

**Practical Implication**
Policy makers aiming to enhance regional resilience to gas price shocks should prioritize efficiency improvements and electrification in industrial production and in upstream sectors such as electricity generation, oil refinery, and cokes production, because these measures reduce vulnerability to future gas price spikes. International cooperation within Europe on upstream decarbonization is warranted, since improvements elsewhere — especially outside Europe where competitors benefit — can instead erode the competitive position of European regions. Avoiding lag relative to competitors in efficiency gains is essential to limit negative effects of future shocks.

**Limitations**
The analysis is a comparative static input–output exercise and does not capture the dynamics of transforming the production process toward a more decarbonized structure, nor first-mover advantages that a dynamic approach could reveal. There are limitations and constraints on the sectors in which technological adjustments can realistically be made. Consistent regional-level time-series data on energy use by type and interregional trade flows are lacking, which would considerably improve the reliability of the analysis and allow studying how resilience changes over time.

**Future Research**
Future work should extend the framework to a dynamic setting that captures the transformation of production processes and first-mover advantages in decarbonization. When consistent time-series data become available, researchers should analyze how regional resilience to fossil fuel price shocks evolves over time. More consistent regional-level data on energy use by type and on interregional trade flows would enable broader applications of the RCC model to other shocks and policies.
