# A Review of Systems Perspectives in Sustainability: How Systems Properties Convey Systems-Wide Dynamics

**Abstract**
As seven of nine planetary boundaries are breached, management scholars face an urgent challenge: how can organizations address complex social-ecological crises that transcend traditional organizational boundaries and objectives? Responding to this need, researchers have leveraged a plurality of systems perspectives, yet current approaches remain nascent and fragmented. In this paper, we review 25 years (2000–2024) of empirical sustainability management research across 17 leading journals. We identify core systems properties—interrelatedness, nestedness, non-linearity, and emergence—that collectively illuminate four critical systemswide dynamics: equilibrium, disequilibrium, adaptation, and organized systems change. This systematic review offers scholars a unified framework for theorizing and addressing critical sustainability challenges facing organizations, society, and the planet.

**Research Question**
How have sustainability management studies empirically leveraged systems perspectives over the past 25 years, and how can a unified property-based approach help scholars and managers interpret and interfere with systems-wide dynamics that underpin complex social-ecological sustainability problems beyond the organizational level?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Systems properties (interrelatedness, nestedness, non-linearity, emergence)
- DV(s): Systems-wide dynamics (equilibrium, disequilibrium, adaptation, organized systems change) and sustainability outcomes beyond the organizational level
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

The authors articulate a property-based approach in which four core systems properties—interrelatedness, nestedness, non-linearity, and emergence—jointly illuminate four systems-wide dynamics (equilibrium, disequilibrium, adaptation, organized systems change) that underpin sustainable or unsustainable outcomes. Interrelatedness and non-linearity surface feedback loops and thresholds/tipping points; nestedness relates organizational action to embedded social-ecological scales; and emergence captures self-organizing, path-breaking processes. Managers can interfere with these dynamics by supporting or contrasting non-linear feedback loops, cultivating/enabling/leveraging emergence, and anticipating unintended ripple effects, thereby linking organizational processes to broader social-ecological systems change.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The review develops a "property-based approach" that synthesizes a fragmented plurality of systems perspectives (e.g., systems dynamics, panarchy, complex adaptive systems, polycentrism) into a unified framework relating four systems properties to four systems-wide dynamics. This framework establishes a basis for scholarly dialogue and theory advancement across multiple systems perspectives, and offers a complementary lens to mainstream management theories such as institutional theory and paradox theory for interpreting emergent, nested, and non-linear dynamics of social-ecological change. It reframes sustainability management away from single-level, cause–effect inquiry toward multi-level, cross-scale theorizing of systems-wide dynamics.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should invest attentional resources beyond the organizational level to recognize whether to maintain equilibrium or accelerate disequilibrium in the face of reinforcing or balancing feedback loops. They should cultivate, enable, and leverage processes of emergence—by creating spaces for new relationships, dialogue, and experimentation across silos—to either restore equilibrium or trigger path-breaking change. Finally, managers should anticipate plausible unintended ripple effects of their interventions, using adaptive and decentralized (e.g., polycentric) approaches to mitigate the risk of making complex societal challenges worse.

**Limitations**
Seeking to fully resolve semantic fragmentation and bring complete conceptual coherence between systems perspectives remained beyond the scope of this review; instead, the authors aimed to bring the plurality into sufficient dialogue for theory advancement. The review is also bounded by its focus on 17 leading management journals and empirical sustainability management studies between 2000 and 2024, and is therefore limited in the systems perspectives and disciplinary traditions it could cover.

**Future Research**
Future empirical work should go beyond single qualitative case studies to include multi-case, quantitative, and mixed methods (e.g., panel data, systems dynamics, agent-based modeling, experiments, multi-level models, Bayesian approaches) to identify inter-organizational and industry-level drivers of systems change. Researchers should narrow geographical and temporal scales—especially at the underrepresented regional scale—to study how organizations interfere with local and regional systems-wide dynamics. Further research should empirically deepen notions of thresholds and tipping points, the organizational practices that cultivate and leverage emergence, and how organizations anticipate and deal with unintended ripple effects.
