# Firm Growth and Corporate Social Responsibility

**Abstract**
This paper examines how the rate of firm growth influences corporate social responsibility (CSR). Periods of rapid growth can place substantial financial and managerial demands on firms, requiring leaders to prioritize the allocation of limited resources toward managing expansion. We propose that when firms grow quickly, managers may limit CSR investments—not because they undervalue CSR, but because growth itself consumes financial and attentional capacity. Using 19 years of data from 4,305 firms across 46 countries, we find that faster firm growth is associated with lower levels of CSR activity. This relationship is moderated by firms’ financial and attentional resources: firms with greater financial slack and managerial bandwidth maintain stronger CSR engagement even while growing rapidly. Overall, our results suggest that the relationship between growth and CSR reflects a pragmatic balancing of competing demands.

**Research Question**
How does the rate of firm growth affect firms' CSR engagement, and how is this relationship shaped by firms' financial and attentional resources?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Firm growth (measured as 1-year sales growth and 1-year asset growth)
- DV(s): CSR (equally weighted average of environmental and social performance dimensions from LSEG ESG database)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Performance variability (proxy for attentional resources; standard deviation of ROA over rolling 5-year window); Financial slack (undistributed cash flow to total equity, adjusted by industry and country)

Drawing on the attention-based view of the firm, the authors argue that managerial attention and financial capital are finite resources that both firm growth and CSR draw upon. When growth accelerates, managers must allocate scarce cognitive and financial resources toward coordinating expansion, scaling operations, and meeting short-term performance targets, leaving less bandwidth and liquidity for CSR initiatives that yield intangible, long-term, and difficult-to-measure returns. Because CSR outcomes are distant and peripheral to core business activity, managers under pressure to deliver measurable growth-related outcomes reallocate attention and resources away from CSR. Performance variability amplifies this trade-off by further depleting attentional reservoirs, while financial slack attenuates it by providing a capital cushion that preserves managerial latitude to pursue non-growth initiatives during periods of rapid expansion.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances CSR research by identifying firm growth—one of the most pervasive and central features of organizational life—as a previously underexplored driver of CSR engagement, complementing work on organizational architecture, identity, and institutional drivers. It integrates the attention-based view with CSR theory to foreground the role of managerial attention (not only financial slack) as a scarce input that shapes corporate commitments, and it links the attention-based view with stakeholder governance by showing how growth pressures condition the shareholder-stakeholder trade-off. In doing so, the paper reframes the growth-sustainability debate away from a purely ideological contest and toward a pragmatic, trade-off-based account of managerial resource allocation.

**Practical Implication**
For companies committed to CSR, the authors recommend establishing systems and controls that insulate CSR from fluctuations in managerial attention and financial resources—for instance, CSR departments with independent budgets (via corporate foundations or trusts) and strategic autonomy, analogous to governance structures afforded to R&D. Elevating CSR to an executive leadership position can help integrate it into the firm's core rather than treating it as peripheral. Socially responsible shareholders and stakeholders can also counteract growth pressures by putting forth CSR-related proposals during high-growth periods to redirect managerial attention and resources back toward CSR.

**Limitations**
The study does not directly observe the microlevel managerial processes that link growth rates to CSR decisions, since attention and resource-allocation decisions are hard to capture with archival data. The analysis also takes a relatively static snapshot and is agnostic about the long-run and overall effect of growth on CSR and business sustainability, which may differ from the short-term trade-offs documented.

**Future Research**
Future work should directly observe managers in high-growth firms (e.g., through qualitative or field methods) to examine whether growth-CSR trade-offs are made deliberately or emerge unintentionally. Researchers should also study the longer-term, historical relationship between growth and CSR, including whether growth-focused firms eventually produce game-changing CSR innovations that offset earlier trade-offs. Further theorizing is needed on how managers make attention-related trade-offs in pursuing CSR and on which organizational structures and characteristics shape those decisions.
