# Micro-Processes of Constrained Innovation: A Field Study of Constraint-Handling Practices in Base of the Pyramid Innovation Projects

**Abstract**
This study addresses the problem of handling constraints in innovative projects. Using a qualitative research design on base of the pyramid (BOP) innovation projects, we examine how creativity emerges from constraints. By investigating responses to experienced constraints from 60 BOP innovation projects, we identify specific micro-processes used to deal with constraints, which we introduce as constraint-handling practices. Moreover, we investigate the interplay between these constraint-handling practices and the nature of the constraints in the resultant sequence of such micro-processes, distinguishing between goal and task constraints. Drawing from theory on effectuation and causation, we explain how different constraint-handling practices are implemented in innovation projects in response to these constraints. We demonstrate that instead of relying on an either/or mode of effectuation versus causation, actors in constrained BOP innovation projects exhibit repeated cycles of both effectuation-and causation-based constraint-handling practices.

**Research Question**
What specific micro-processes do actors in innovation projects use to effectively address constraints they face, and how does the nature of the constraints (goal vs. task) shape the sequence and type of these constraint-handling practices?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: Goal constraints (market requirements and self-imposed restrictions on the goal state) vs. task constraints (inadequate materials, financial, knowledge, and time constraints); effectuation logic (focus on available means) vs. causation logic (setting specific goals and systematically planning means to achieve them).
- Practices (constraint-handling practices): reducing and reinterpreting (goal-constraint related, causation-based); replacing, tinkering, and networking (task-constraint related, effectuation-based).
- Triggering conditions: Experienced task or goal constraints that limit potential actions in innovation problem-solving, occurring within the resource-scarce, infrastructure-poor BOP context.
- Process / phases: Constraint experience -> identification of constraint nature (goal vs. task) -> selection of corresponding constraint-handling practice -> re-entry into the search space, often recursively combining effectuation- and causation-based practices across project phases rather than adhering to one logic.
- Emergent outcomes: Repeated cycles of effectuation- and causation-based micro-processes that enable continued goal pursuit in innovation projects despite severe constraints.

Actors in BOP innovation projects do not choose effectuation OR causation as a strategic orientation at the project level; instead, the specific nature of the constraint they encounter at a given moment pulls them toward the corresponding micro-process. Goal constraints trigger causation-based practices (reducing elements to a frugal core; reinterpreting the goal toward what BOP users value), while task constraints trigger effectuation-based practices (replacing inputs, tinkering with available means, networking to bridge institutional voids). These practices feed back into the solution search space and often escalate one kind of constraint into another, so project members iterate between effectuation-based and causation-based practices over the course of the project.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study offers three contributions. First, it opens up the micro-processes category of innovation micro-foundations (alongside actors and structures; Felin et al., 2012) by identifying and categorizing five constraint-handling practices that translate constraint-induced needs into creative outcomes. Second, it advances effectuation/causation theory (Sarasvathy, 2001; Fisher, 2012) by moving beyond the dichotomous, macro-level framing of effectuation vs. causation as mutually exclusive orientations and theorizing how actors enact and recombine both logics in situated, day-to-day micro-processes tied to distinct natures of constraints. Third, it contributes to BOP innovation research by specifying how project members in resource-poor settings interplay constraint types and constraint-handling practices, offering theoretical and empirical depth to an under-researched domain.

**Practical Implication**
For innovation project members, the study provides a concrete toolbox of five constraint-handling practices — reducing, reinterpreting, replacing, tinkering, and networking — that can be applied beyond BOP contexts whenever task or goal constraints arise, and encourages reframing constraints as creative triggers rather than as nuisances. For managers, the findings suggest reconsidering the default of avoiding constraints or over-resourcing innovation projects; instead, managers should adapt their leadership to support teams in flexibly combining effectuation- and causation-based responses (e.g., through paradoxical leader behaviour and creative brokering) rather than prescribing one logic.

**Limitations**
The qualitative, inductive design precluded examining whether specific natures of constraints or corresponding constraint-handling practices produce superior creative outcomes. The study did not match project or individual characteristics (e.g., cognitive style) to particular practices, and 97% of interviewees were men, which may have yielded an incomplete set of practices given evidence that pathways to creativity and bricolage differ between men and women. The broad sampling strategy also limited depth on any single case, and the authors did not systematically gather data on institutional or organizational contexts that likely condition the application of constraint-handling practices.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how different constraint natures and corresponding practices affect creative outcomes; study how individual and team attributes (e.g., cognitive style, flexibility) shape switching between effectuation and causation; target female innovators, particularly in BOP contexts, to uncover additional constraint-handling practices; conduct deeper, within-case investigations of fewer projects; integrate the three micro-foundations elements (actors, structures, processes) by analyzing how institutional and organizational contexts shape constraint-handling repertoires; and investigate how effectuation and causation jointly affect outcomes when projects face multiple concurrent constraints, including across innovation-project stages such as prototyping and scaling.
