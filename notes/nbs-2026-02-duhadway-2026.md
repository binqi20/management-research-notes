# Supply Chain Guardianship: Why Some Firms Intervene When Other Firms Commit Fraud

**Abstract**
In supply chains, firms often become aware of illegal actions committed by their partners, prompting the critical question: when and why do those who know become those who act? Drawing on industry examples of supply chain fraud, we introduce the concept of supply chain guardianship to study how firms respond to potential fraud committed by their supply chain partners. We identify key influences on supply chain guardianship and refine these insights into hypotheses, which we test across four behavioral experiments (n = 1000). Study A finds that the tone at the top of an organization can promote supply chain guardianship and that state moral disengagement is negatively correlated with it. Study B manipulates process moral disengagement and finds that it reduces guardianship behavior. Although the network position of the supply chain guardian emerges as important in industry examples, we do not find a significant effect in the experiments. We replicate and validate these findings in Studies C and D. This study offers an initial foundation for a behavioral theory of interfirm fraud responses in supply chains and offers practical insights into how firms can increase supply chain guardianship across macro-, meso-, and microlevels.

**Research Question**
What causes individual decision-makers to have their firm engage in supply chain guardianship, taking actions to stop fraudulent behavior at another firm?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Network position (supplier-side vs. buyer-side fraud); tone at the top (safety vs. cost emphasis); state moral disengagement; process moral disengagement
- DV(s): Supply chain guardianship operationalized as (1) corrective actions toward the fraudulent party and (2) information sharing with potential victims
- Mediators: Not explicitly modeled (state moral disengagement partially functions as a correlated intermediate construct)
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

Drawing on structural fraud theory, tone-at-the-top research, and moral disengagement theory, the authors argue that guardianship is a multilevel phenomenon. At the macro level, a guardian bridging the structural hole between a fraudulent supplier and downstream victims should have greater leverage to stop fraud than one positioned downstream of a fraudulent buyer. At the meso level, leadership emphasis on ethical priorities (e.g., safety) signals acceptable behavior and activates guardianship, while cost-first tones suppress it. At the micro level, moral disengagement — both as a state and as an in-the-moment process invoked through moral justification, displacement of responsibility, and related mechanisms — reduces individuals' willingness to recommend corrective action and information sharing.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces and defines supply chain guardianship as an externally oriented, bystander-firm construct that complements internal-control approaches to fraud. It develops a multilevel theoretical framework linking network position (macro), tone at the top (meso), and moral disengagement (micro) to guardianship behavior, and offers a behavioral-theory foundation for interfirm fraud responses in supply chains that connects leadership and moral cognition with network-based views of interfirm misconduct.

**Practical Implication**
Firms can increase guardianship by setting a tone at the top that makes ethical decision-making an actionable priority rather than a broad appeal, and by designing organizational processes that prevent individuals from disengaging morally through displacement or diffusion of responsibility. Managers should frame guardianship as strategically aligned with reputational and operational interests — "what is bad for our supply chain is bad for our company."

**Limitations**
The two supply chain guardianship measures (corrective actions and information sharing) do not encompass all possible anti-fraud actions. The experiments could not reproduce the archival finding that network position affects guardianship efficacy, limiting causal claims about macro-level effects. Other individual and organizational factors (e.g., guardianship propensity, culture, power, trust, financial position) were not fully ruled out as alternative explanations.

**Future Research**
Future work should use primary field data (firms in fraud, guardians, compliance teams, lawyers, victims) to assess real-world guardianship effectiveness, and investigate additional meso/macro factors such as organizational culture, power, trust, and industry pressures. Researchers should examine decision-making under uncertainty before fraud is confirmed, study temporal dynamics, and explore configurational combinations of causal factors. The guardianship concept could also be extended to monitoring technologies, wildlife trafficking, and preventive (rather than reactive) interventions.
