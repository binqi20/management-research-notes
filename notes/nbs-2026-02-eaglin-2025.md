**Research Question**
What is the impact of relaxing capital constraints on strategic misconduct among small and medium-sized firms in emerging markets whose survival and profitability depend on misconduct, and why do such firms engage in strategic misconduct even when it carries high risks?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Interest rates on vehicle financing (proxy for capital constraints), with quasi-exogenous variation driven by a 2017 industry-wide strike that lowered rates only for vehicles financed afterward
- DV(s): Strategic misconduct measured as (1) average daily speeding alerts over 120 km/hour, (2) kilometers traveled at 100–120, 120–140, and over 140 km/hour, and (3) collision insurance claims
- Mediators: Firms' efforts to avoid loan default (and its economic, social, and physical penalties), evidenced through qualitative interviews and survey data
- Moderators: Industry experience (newer owners less responsive), speeding intensity (the most frequent speeders do not change behavior), association quality, and existing-operator status

High capital costs create high minimum revenue requirements to stay in business; taxi operators speed and drive aggressively to complete as many trips as possible to meet loan obligations and avoid default, whose penalties in this setting extend beyond asset loss to loss of livelihood for extended families, loss of standing in associations, and risk of violence. When interest rates fall, the required monthly revenue drops, reducing the marginal benefit of the most dangerous forms of misconduct; firms therefore cut back on the most extreme speeding and collisions while continuing milder speeding. The mechanism is thus one in which relaxing capital constraints loosens the survival pressure that makes severe strategic misconduct a dominant strategy choice for SMEs operating under intense competition, weak regulation, and institutional voids.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper argues that strategic misconduct is a core choice among strategic options for firms facing severe financial pressure, not merely a product of moral failing or perverse incentives, and shows that relaxing capital constraints can reduce the most severe misconduct with broad public-welfare benefits. It contributes to the literatures on misconduct, entrepreneurship in emerging markets, institutional voids, and prosocial firm behavior by studying survival-oriented SMEs rather than large well-resourced firms or individual financial advisors, and by linking strategic misconduct directly to weak regulatory environments and high capital costs. It also suggests that mainstream management strategy theories may have limited applicability for resource-constrained firms operating under intense competition and weak regulation, and that previous estimates of the social cost of expensive capital understate its true welfare impact once misconduct is accounted for.

**Practical Implication**
For financiers serving SMEs in competitive environments with weak regulation, charging excessively high interest rates harms both the lender and the borrower: lower rates improve loan performance, reduce accidents involving third parties, and protect employees and the broader public by lowering the incidence of the most extreme strategic misconduct. Policymakers and funders should therefore consider how to deliver more affordable capital to such firms, for example through working-capital facilities or interest-rate caps, and should attend to the outsized penalties of default that push owners toward dangerous behavior. Improved measurement of misconduct in SME settings can aid the design of better markets for capital and services.

**Limitations**
Firms are not randomly assigned lower interest rates, so selection could bias estimates upward, though OLS, IV, and matching models all yield consistent results and fieldwork suggests selection operates primarily at the point of joining an association rather than at financing. Generalizability is limited because the sample is drawn from a highly competitive, sparsely regulated, and often violent transport setting; effects may be more muted in settings with stronger enforcement or cheaper capital. The study does not evaluate intrafirm dynamics (e.g., owner–driver principal–agent problems) because of data limitations.

**Future Research**
Future research should examine how differing enforcement norms and regulatory regimes mitigate or magnify the responsiveness of misconduct to capital constraints, and whether the findings extend beyond informal transport markets to other SME-intensive industries in emerging economies. Scholars should investigate intrafirm dynamics underlying strategic misconduct, including monitoring technologies, and explore how policymakers and funders can design financing products (e.g., working-capital facilities, lower interest rates) that reduce the penalties of default and the incentives to engage in misconduct. Further work is needed on improved measurement of misconduct in SME settings and on how such measurement can inform the design of better markets for capital and services.
