# A cultural perspective on informal ride sharing

**Abstract**
Purpose – Although the literature on dynamic ride sharing has grown significantly, there remains a notable gap in understanding informal ride sharing, particularly within the context of developing countries. Informal ride sharing remains prevalent in sociocultural environments where community-level sharing thrives. The purpose of this study is to explore the cultural sharing values that influence consumers’ engagement in informal ride sharing. Design/methodology/approach – This study adopts a qualitative methodology based on semi-structured interviews and uses data-driven thematic analysis approach to analyse 45 semi-structured interviews conducted with participants who reside in Lagos, Nigeria. Findings – This study identifies four sub-dimensions and three key roles of cultural sharing values that influence consumers’ engagement in informal ride sharing. These sub-dimensions − interdependence, functional value, cultural morality and obligation are underpinned by distinct cultural value orientations. Together, they serve as motivating and inhibiting factors that shape consumer engagement. Practical implications – This study has implications for policymakers and mobility planners by emphasising the significance of informal ride sharing within specific sociocultural contexts. The findings provide consumer-focused insights that can inform the design of culturally responsive transport strategies that recognise and leverage the prevalence of informal sharing practices. Originality/value – Adopting a cultural lens, this study provides novel insight into the role of cultural sharing values and how it shapes consumers’ engagement in informal ride sharing.

**Research Question**
Within a collectivist culture, what cultural sharing values influence consumers' engagement in informal ride sharing?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Cultural sharing values (interdependence, functional value, cultural morality, obligation) underpinned by Schwartz's cultural value orientations (autonomy vs. embeddedness; egalitarianism vs. hierarchy)
- DV(s): Consumers' engagement (or disengagement) in informal ride sharing
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Generational differences (older vs. younger consumers); social boundaries of "sharing in" vs. "sharing out"

The authors theorise that culturally transmitted value orientations socialise individuals into shared norms about with whom and why one shares rides. In Nigeria's predominantly collectivist context, embeddedness and interdependence cultivate communal practices, group attachment and altruistic cultural morality that motivate informal ride sharing as a normative, non-transactional practice (Belk, 2010). At the same time, the obligation dimension — rooted in hierarchy and reciprocal expectations — can inhibit engagement, especially among younger consumers who prize autonomy and seek to "escape the gift economy" (Marcoux, 2009). Cultural sharing values therefore operate simultaneously as motivating, inhibiting and conflicting forces, shaping whether consumers enter informal ride sharing on the basis of pure sharing, mutuality or transactional avoidance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends Schwartz's (2006) theory of cultural value dimensions into the sharing-economy literature by identifying four sub-dimensions of cultural sharing values — interdependence, functional value, cultural morality and obligation — that shape consumer engagement in informal ride sharing within a collectivist culture. By integrating Belk's (2010) conceptualisation of sharing, Marcoux's (2009) escaping the gift economy and Arnould and Rose's (2016) mutuality, it uncovers three key roles (motivating, inhibiting and conflicted) played by cultural sharing values, and shows that even within collectivist cultures subgroups may hold divergent values that challenge the dominant cultural narrative.

**Practical Implication**
The findings offer guidance for policymakers and mobility planners in contexts where informal ride sharing coexists with limited formal transport. They should align transport strategies with local cultural norms, integrate informal practices as complementary solutions to gaps in formal infrastructure, and introduce community-based network structures in low-access areas. Mobility planners can scale peer-to-peer informal ride sharing through city-wide schemes targeting underserviced routes and peak periods, delivering both communal and functional value and connecting a broader pool of riders.

**Limitations**
The sample is skewed toward younger consumers because of online recruitment and younger individuals' greater awareness of and activity in shared mobility, leaving older consumers' attitudes underexplored. The study focuses exclusively on informal ride sharing and does not examine the dynamics between informal and dynamic ride sharing within collectivist cultures. As an exploratory qualitative study, the findings provide depth but limit generalisability.

**Future Research**
Further studies should explicitly explore older consumers' attitudes toward informal sharing practices. Future research could expand the inquiry to other sectors and contrasting cultural contexts to offer a more comprehensive understanding of sharing practices across societies. Quantitative methodologies could also be adopted to test relationships between cultural sharing values and consumer motivations for engaging in ride sharing.
