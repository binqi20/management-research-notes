# The Ethics of Global Business, by Denis G. Arnold. Wiley Blackwell, 2023. 161 pp.

**Abstract**
n his book The Ethics of Global Business (2023) Denis Arnold addresses highly important challenges of global business, with a focus on the relations of the powerful transnational corporations (TNCs) to the Base of the Pyramid (BoP)— that is, four billion people below $9.05 per day (107, Figure 7.1)—and the ethical assessment in terms of universal human rights. The author draws on decades of research and his familiarity as a five-year editor of BEQ with a vast part of literature of business ethics. He is also supported by the collaboration of Andrew Valentin and Laura Williams in the last two chapters (7 and 8).

**Practical Implication**
Enderle emphasizes that Arnold's action-oriented analysis is valuable for practice: TNCs should respect the basic human rights of people impacted at the BoP, ensure minimal health and safety conditions in the workplace, inform employees of workplace hazards during hiring, and pay a weekly wage consistent with basic human dignity. Business ventures engaging Moderate and Extreme Poverty populations should pursue empowerment strategies (assessed against human rights, utility, and capabilities) rather than exploitation strategies. The book is recommended for teaching, research, and training in TNCs.

**Limitations**
Enderle raises four critical remarks about Arnold's book: (1) it does not fully apply Gewirth's five-component structure of claim-rights, leaving imprecision about subjects, objects, respondents, binding nature, and justification of human rights; (2) it conflates "duty" and "responsibility," defining TNCs as "Duty Bearers" rather than "Bearers of Responsibility" and retaining the confusing term CSR; (3) its assessment of the Tripartite Framework is less favorable than warranted, and it omits a philosophical justification grounded in human dignity; and (4) the cited literature is almost exclusively pre-2013 and would benefit from more recent references.

**Future Research**
Enderle suggests that the philosophical justification of human rights in pluralistic societies, the precise division of obligations between states and corporations, and the clarification of responsibility as a three-aspect ethical term (who, for what, towards whom) deserve further exploration. He also notes that the long-term impact of the Tripartite Framework—visible, for instance, in the EU's 2025 Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive—merits continued study, and that the intriguing issues raised in Arnold's book deserve to be further explored and discussed.
