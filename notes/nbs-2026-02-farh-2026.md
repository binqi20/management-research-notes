# The gendered costs of voice (un)enacted: Differential effects on belonging in traditionally male-dominated contexts.

**Abstract**
Recent research indicates that performance benefits on complex tasks accrue when members with diverse social identities speak up and their voice is enacted—i.e., incorporated into the team’s approach to the task. In this paper, we argue that the cost of not enacting diverse members’ voice may extend beyond performance to negatively affect their belonging. Integrating social belonging theory with the literature on gender and voice, we examine the case of women in traditionally male-dominated contexts. Archival data from teams of active-duty Marines (Study 1) and student engineering project teams (Study 2) showed that voice enactment was more strongly associated with women’s belonging than men’s, and women’s belonging was disproportionately harmed when voice enactment was low. A pre-registered scenario study set in a male-dominated high-tech organization (Study 3) further showed that these differences were explained by the gender-based voice threat women experience when presented with a voice opportunity. This unique sense of threat amplified the relationship between voice enactment and belonging for women, and by extension, their future voice. A supplemental study showed that the costs in belonging when women’s voice was not enacted could be mitigated when the leader framed voice as having value for the group even if it is not enacted. Still, only when voice enactment was high did women’s sense of belonging reach parity with that of men, showcasing the power of acting on voice as a way of achieving belonging for all in diverse organizations.

**Research Question**
How and why does voice enactment (the extent to which teammates and leaders incorporate a voicer's suggestions into the team's approach) differentially affect the belonging of women versus men in traditionally male-dominated contexts, and what explains and mitigates these gendered effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Voice enactment (extent to which a voicer's expressed ideas are incorporated into the team's approach)
- DV(s): Voicer's sense of belonging (and downstream future voice behavior)
- Mediators: Gender-based voice threat (concerns that one's work-related ideas may be negatively evaluated or devalued on account of one's gender)
- Moderators: Gender (woman vs. man) in a traditionally male-dominated context; leader framing of voice as valuable even when not enacted (mitigating moderator in supplemental study)

Integrating social belonging theory with the gender-and-voice literature, the authors argue that women in male-dominated settings experience a heightened gender-based voice threat when a voice opportunity arises, shaped by prior experience, vicarious observation, and awareness of negative stereotypes about women's voice. This situated threat makes women more attuned to voice enactment as a diagnostic cue for whether they fit in, are accepted, and are valued in the group. When voice is not enacted, this interpretive lens causes disproportionate harm to women's belonging—and, by extension, diminishes their future voice. Men, who experience less gender-based voice threat, are less likely to read voice (non)enactment as a belonging cue, producing the observed gender asymmetry.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances understanding of how voice enactment has differential personal consequences for "diverse members," showing that low voice enactment inflicts particular costs on women's belonging and subsequent voice in male-dominated contexts. It introduces gender-based voice threat as a novel situated social identity threat that explains why women, but not men, interpret voice (non)enactment as a belonging cue—moving beyond group membership to specify a psychological mechanism amenable to intervention. In doing so, it contributes to social belonging theory by pinpointing which everyday interactional cues carry belonging significance and why, and it connects the voice, gender, and belonging literatures in organizational contexts.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should not make voice-enactment decisions lightly, particularly when responding to women in male-dominated contexts, because women are more likely to interpret low voice enactment as a sign that they do not belong, which in turn diminishes their subsequent voice. Leaders can mitigate the belonging cost of low voice enactment by framing voice as valuable to the group regardless of whether it is enacted, and can reduce gender-based voice threat by weakening numerical or normative male dominance of the setting (e.g., greater gender balance, de-emphasizing masculine norms). Because acting on women's voice enables women's belonging to reach parity with men's and can enhance performance on complex tasks, high voice enactment is itself a powerful lever for inclusion.

**Limitations**
Study 1 and Study 2 were externally valid archival studies but Study 1 had very few women in the sample and Study 2 relied on single-sourced data. Study 3 overcame these concerns but lacked the external validity of real teams. The three studies also used different indicators of belonging; although results replicated across measures, the authors acknowledge this measurement heterogeneity as a limitation.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine how patterns, trends, or variability in voice enactment over time shape belonging—for example, whether initial voice-enactment experiences have an imprinting effect or whether voice enactment matters more over time as expectations accrue. Researchers should investigate the "micro context" of voice—frequency, content, quality, and tactics of speaking up—and how these features intensify or attenuate interpretations of voice enactment for belonging. Future research should also explore how other gender identities and demographic minorities experience unique forms of identity-based voice threat and how intersecting social identities interact with voice to shape belonging.
