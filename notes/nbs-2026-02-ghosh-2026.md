# Eliciting Supplier Cooperation for Value Chain Decarbonization: A Field Experiment with Smallholder Farmers in India

**Abstract**

In the first intervention, the firm’s field officers offered the farmers support specific to the crop that the firm sourced from them. The second intervention additionally involved bringing in expert agronomists to also provide the farmers with support on broader agri­ cultural matters of interest to them. Relative to a control condition in which the farmers only received training on the relevant climate-friendly practices, both interventions improved the farmers’ adoption of the recommended practices. The second intervention was more impactful than the first in improving the farmers’ practice adoption as well as their retention with the firm. Exploratory mediation analysis and post-experiment field interviews suggest that these findings are partly driven by the farmers’ positive perception of the firm’s relational investments.

**Research Question**
How can a firm committed to decarbonizing its value chain elicit its suppliers' cooperation for these efforts while also ensuring the economic well-being of its suppliers as well as meeting its own business objectives for the value chain?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Experimental condition — base program (climate-friendly practice training only); Intervention A (base + customized crop-specific advisory services and free soil testing delivered by the firm's field officers); Intervention B (Intervention A + broader customized agricultural advisory services delivered by expert agronomists, including matters beyond Crop X)
- DV(s): Farmers' adoption of recommended climate-friendly practices (reduced land tillage and reduced inorganic fertilizer use) as the primary environmental outcome; farmer retention in the sourcing program as the primary business outcome
- Mediators: Farmers' positive perception of the firm's relational investment / perceived commitment of the firm to farmers' well-being (explored via mediation analysis and post-experiment interviews)
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

Drawing on new stakeholder theory and the literature on relational strategy, the authors argue that supplier cooperation for value chain decarbonization requires addressing gaps not only in suppliers' ability but also in their willingness to adopt unfamiliar climate-friendly practices that they may perceive as risky. Because formal contracts are often infeasible with numerous small suppliers in emerging economies, the firm must rely on relational governance. A costly and visible relational investment — especially one that goes beyond the firm's narrow value-chain interests to address suppliers' broader priorities — serves as a credible signal of the firm's commitment to joint value creation, enhancing farmers' trust and willingness to adopt the practices while also increasing their retention. Intervention B (broader advisory services via agronomists) is expected to send a stronger signal than Intervention A (crop-specific services only) because it demonstrates concern for the farmers' overall welfare rather than only the firm's sourcing interests.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study contributes to the literatures on relational strategy and new stakeholder theory by empirically demonstrating that a "high road" approach — using relational investments that prioritize suppliers' broader interests — can simultaneously advance a firm's environmental, social, and business goals in its value chain. It shows that the design of such relational investments benefits from sensitivity to suppliers' overall perspective of value rather than limiting consideration to value created within the firm's value chain. Empirically, it adds to the nascent body of work using field experiments to examine sustainability-related interventions in the understudied context of small, low-income suppliers in emerging economies.

**Practical Implication**
Firms seeking to decarbonize their value chains should not rely solely on training or formal contracts with small suppliers in emerging economies but should invest in relational mechanisms — such as customized advisory services — that demonstrate tangible value creation for suppliers. Investments that extend beyond the firm's narrow sourcing interests (e.g., providing broader agronomic advice not limited to the sourced crop) generate stronger cooperation and retention than more instrumental, crop-specific support, and can be more cost-effective per unit of impact despite higher absolute cost.

**Limitations**
The mediation evidence on farmers' perception of the firm's relational investment is suggestive rather than conclusive, and post-experiment interviews suggest that knowledge provision may not have been held perfectly constant across conditions because field officers and agronomists sometimes filled ability gaps while delivering advisory services. Data collection relied significantly on surveys because of the difficulty of obtaining objective outcome data from smallholder farmers in rural India, and organizational changes at the partner firm prevented long-term follow-up on actual (rather than stated) retention and practice durability.

**Future Research**
Future research should better identify and disentangle the mechanisms driving relational investments' effects on supplier engagement, explore more objective long-term data collection, and examine how far relational approaches generalize to contexts with lower contract-enforcement costs (e.g., comparing the United States with emerging economies, or large versus small suppliers within the same economy). Further work should also investigate spillovers and diffusion of practices among farmers, the durability of adoption, and alternative engagement strategies suited to settings where the recommended practices are not actually beneficial for suppliers.
