# Constructing Carbon Abatement Cost Curves

**Abstract**
Companies across industries face increasing pressure to assess the costs of decarbonizing their operations. This paper develops a generic model for constructing abatement cost curves in connection with carbon dioxide emissions. The resulting abatement cost curves provide a planning tool for companies seeking to project their decarbonization pathways and to determine optimal abatement levels in response to environmental regulations such as carbon pricing. We calibrate our model in the context of European cement producers that are required to obtain emission permits under the European Emissions Trading System. We find that a price of e85 per ton of carbon dioxide, as observed on average in 2023, incentivizes firms to reduce their annual direct emissions by about one-third relative to the status quo. Yet, this incentive increases sharply when prices rise above the benchmark of e100 per ton of carbon dioxide.

**Research Question**
How can firms construct abatement cost curves that accurately capture the cost of alternative carbon dioxide emission reduction targets when elementary abatement levers exhibit interactions, and what do such curves imply for optimal corporate decarbonization pathways under carbon pricing regimes like the EU Emissions Trading System?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Carbon price level (e.g., EU ETS price per ton of CO2); set of technologically feasible elementary abatement levers and their life-cycle costs, emission effects, and interactions
- DV(s): Total Abatement Cost (TAC) and Marginal Abatement Cost (MAC) curves; firm's optimal combined abatement lever and associated direct CO2 emissions; life-cycle (levelized) product cost
- Mediators: Combinatorial selection of the cost-minimizing combined lever satisfying an emission target; life-cycle discounted expenditures (upfront investment plus fixed and variable operating costs)
- Moderators: Technological interactions among elementary levers (e.g., energy efficiency upgrades reducing the incremental benefit of fuel substitutes); carbon contracts for difference and other policy instruments

The paper articulates a cost-accounting mechanism. A firm choosing among a finite set of binary elementary levers (energy efficiency, process changes, input substitutions, fuel switching, carbon capture and sequestration, etc.) faces a combinatorial optimization problem in which the Total Abatement Cost is the life-cycle expenditure of the cheapest combined lever that meets a given emission target. Because the emission and cost effects of levers interact when deployed together, the resulting MAC curve is a step function that is not necessarily monotonically increasing, and the firm's optimal response to a carbon price is the combined lever whose marginal cost crosses the prevailing carbon price. Carbon prices therefore select among only a subset of cost-efficient combined levers, and shifts in prices generate discrete jumps in optimal abatement, investment, and production cost.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces a generic combinatorial model of abatement cost curves that, in contrast to the classical McKinsey-style MAC curves, explicitly accounts for interactions between elementary levers and yields step-function MAC curves that need not be monotonically increasing. By grounding abatement cost in life-cycle costing concepts familiar from management accounting, it bridges environmental economics and cost accounting and provides a tractable framework for deriving firms' optimal abatement responses, the carbon prices that trigger specific technologies, and the minimum lump-sum payments required under carbon contracts for difference. The paper thereby contributes a formal foundation for assessing the credibility of corporate decarbonization pledges and for quantifying the pass-through of carbon prices into product costs.

**Practical Implication**
The framework gives companies and external analysts a planning tool for projecting decarbonization pathways, identifying cost-efficient combinations of abatement levers, and estimating the carbon prices required to make particular technologies (e.g., carbon capture and sequestration) economically viable. Applied to European cement, the model shows that a €85/ton EU ETS price incentivizes roughly a one-third reduction in direct emissions, while €126/ton triggers a 78 percent reduction and €141/ton delivers near-full decarbonization at an additional levelized product cost of only about €16 per ton of cement (roughly 12 percent of the 2023 market price). The approach also allows governments to gauge the minimum lump-sum payment needed to secure bilateral carbon contracts for difference with heavy-emitting firms.

**Limitations**
The calibration is limited to European cement production and to currently available abatement technologies, so the quantitative estimates may not generalize to other industries or future cost trajectories. The model treats the adoption of elementary levers as a static, deterministic binary choice and abstracts from uncertainty in carbon prices and learning-driven improvements in abatement technologies. Residual emissions projections are contingent on current manufacturing and abatement technologies, and the authors note that such forecasts must be qualified by that reference point.

**Future Research**
A promising extension is to model lever adoption as a stochastic, dynamic decision problem that incorporates uncertain learning effects for abatement technologies and volatile carbon prices, allowing firms to value the option of waiting before investing. The framework can also be extended to quantify the effects of alternative accounting rules for CO2 emissions, including combinations of biomass with carbon capture that could yield net-negative cement production. Finally, the authors encourage applying the model to other hard-to-decarbonize, essential industries such as steel, glass, and chemicals.
