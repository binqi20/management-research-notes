# Joining Decision-Making, Moral Thinking, and Collective Action: Grand Challenges as a Phenomenology of Deliberation

**Abstract**
This conceptual article argues that the mutual relevance of grand challenges and organization and management studies is best approached phenomenologically. Rather than constituting objects to be theorized or denoting special empirical contexts, grand challenges structure researchers' attention and shape their interpretations of the processes and systems of deliberation through which collective action is coordinated. From this perspective, grand challenges require researchers to innovate their understandings of deliberation and to ensure that newly generated knowledge is redirected towards management and policymaking. The article integrates the Carnegie School theory of organization with French pragmatic sociology's theory of justification, or economies of worth, to develop a phenomenological model of situated deliberation that links decision-making with moral reasoning. This model highlights deliberation's articulated, evaluative, contestable, and trans-institutional character, as well as its grounding in the cognitive capacities and sociality of actors and observers – regardless of the scale, scope, or stratification of the underlying coordination problems. Building on this framework, the article advocates that grand challenge researchers adopt the standpoint of entrepreneurial observers: actors anchored by socio-economic and scientific commitments who envision the integration of previously disjointed social systems of deliberation to orient collective action.

**Research Question**
How can deliberation be represented phenomenologically? What distinguishes a grand challenge perspective on deliberation? And what are the theoretical and methodological implications of adopting such a perspective for organization and management studies?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: grand challenges are best approached not as a distinct class of coordination problems or as mere empirical contexts, but as a phenomenological perspective that structures how researchers perceive, interpret, and act on processes and systems of deliberation.
- Key constructs: deliberation (situated, evaluative, contestable, trans-institutional decision-making); social systems of deliberation; decision management; satisficing value; situated judgment; entrepreneurial observer.
- Theoretical integration: Carnegie School (bounded rationality, attention, search, composite organizations) is joined with justification theory / economies of worth (normative pluralism, orders of worth, collective construction and critique of action norms) to yield a unified phenomenological model linking decision-making and moral reasoning.
- Properties of deliberative systems: dynamism, distribution, loose coupling, contestability, extensibility — claimed to apply to ordinary and grand challenge problems alike, on a continuum rather than as a categorical divide.
- Methodological framework: three-phase research logic of detection (identifying uncoordinated representations as 'interesting' problems), diagnosis (theorizing why problems are 'important' and articulating desirable futures), and design (specifying realizable paths via explanatory and predictive middle-range theorizing).
- Boundary conditions: the model depends on observer standpoint, socio-economic and scientific commitments, and is framed as a hybrid of CS and JT that acknowledges but does not fully resolve its borrowings from social systems theory (Luhmann).

The phenomenological model treats deliberation as a situated and socially embedded interlocking of decision-making, moral thinking, and contestation through managerial action. By linking situated judgment with search and connecting local deliberative events into networks, observers construe 'social systems of deliberation' whose scale and scope depend on their epistemic objectives rather than on pre-given ontological categories. Adopting the standpoint of the entrepreneurial observer then allows researchers to anchor their inquiry in socio-economic and scientific commitments simultaneously, reconciling the analyst/actor paradox characteristic of engaged grand challenge research.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The article offers three contributions. First, by integrating the Carnegie School and justification theory it develops a novel model of organizational decision-making co-constituted by actors' natural cognitive capacities and sociality and by the normative pluralism and structural complexity of collective action problems. Second, it advances the debate on the significance of grand challenges by shifting the focus from conceptual or empirical categories to the integration of researchers' analytical capacities, societal commitments, and embedding in scholarly fields. Third, it outlines a methodological framework to support this orientation by coordinating diverse theorizing stances and methodological tools through phases of detection, diagnosis, and design.

**Practical Implication**
Researchers and research-adjacent practitioners (e.g., policymakers, managers, advocacy actors) can adopt the standpoint of entrepreneurial observers: anchoring their work in explicit socio-economic and scientific commitments, identifying 'interesting' problems where actors' representations remain uncoordinated, diagnosing them as instances of broader grand challenge horizons, and designing actionable interventions through middle-range, explanatory theorizing that connects local deliberative contexts to global ones (e.g., treating rural water scarcity as a scale model for national climate deliberation).

**Limitations**
The model is a hybrid construction whose conjunction of CS and JT may require further conceptual refinement to serve as a robust reference point for future research, and some theoretical distinctions (e.g., Simon's logical positivism, Boltanski's critical turn, convention theory's structuralist lineage) were not fully addressed. The article also draws on social systems theory (Luhmann) only at an intuitive level and has not assessed whether these borrowings are theoretically warranted, particularly in relation to pragmatist epistemology and emancipatory or performative theorizing, nor systematically engaged with alternative systems accounts of grand challenges.

**Future Research**
Future work should further develop and refine a social systems approach to deliberation, including empirical studies of deliberative decision-making that join situated judgment and search; study 'decision management' as a techne that links means and ends beyond formal organization; pursue middle-range explanatory and predictive theorizing on the structure and dynamics of social systems of deliberation; and exploit the parsimonious social ontology of the model to enable cross-scale comparisons (e.g., local as proxy for global) and computational social-science designs leveraging digital traces and simulation.
