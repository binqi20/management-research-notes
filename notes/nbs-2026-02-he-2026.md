# Fair Lending in Car Financing: Unintended Racial Consequences of Consumer Financial Protection Bureau Supervision of Dealer Markups

**Abstract**
Over 80% of car buyers in the United States obtain a loan through a dealership. Dealers often mark up lender-provided interest rates (buy rates), but consumers cannot distinguish these markups from total rates. In 2014, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau made an unprecedented public disclosure regarding its supervision of auto lenders, revealing racial disparities in dealer markups disadvantaging minority borrowers. The Bureau strongly recommended—but did not mandate—that lenders providing loans through dealers eliminate dealer discretion in markups by adopting ﬂat dealer compensation per transaction. The authors examine the effectiveness of this intervention in reducing consumer ﬁnancing, car payments, and the racial gap. Using detailed individual transaction-level data and a regression-discontinuity-in-time design, they ﬁnd that dealer markups declined by 5.55 basis points after the intervention, saving $66.60 for a typical loan. However, the intervention also increased buy rates, resulting in no signiﬁcant changes to consumers’ total interest rates and total payments. Dealers experienced a slight increase in ﬁnancing proﬁts but no signiﬁcant change in vehicle proﬁts. Importantly, the racial gap in dealer markups and total interest rates widened; dealer markups decreased for both minorities and nonminorities, but they decreased more for nonminorities. The ﬁndings can illuminate the intended and unintended consequences of such government oversight and provide insights into their underlying mechanisms.

**Research Question**
What was the impact of the CFPB's 2014 supervisory highlights on dealer markups, buy rates, total interest rates, total payments, and racial disparities in indirect auto lending, and through what channel-pricing mechanisms did those effects unfold?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): The CFPB supervisory highlights intervention (September 2014) recommending elimination of dealer discretion via flat compensation
- DV(s): Dealer markups, buy rates, total interest rates (APR), total payments, dealer financing and vehicle profits, and the racial gap in these outcomes
- Mediators: Lender adoption of flat compensation (credit unions more than banks); dealer repricing behavior across lender relationships
- Moderators: Local credit union presence; dealers' preferred versus nonpreferred lending partners; end-of-month loan transactions

Because the Bureau's recommendation was voluntary, credit unions disproportionately adopted flat compensation while banks largely did not. The resulting asymmetry induced a spillover in channel pricing: dealers reduced markups on bank loans most when buyers could plausibly also obtain a flat-compensation offer from a credit union, to retain those transactions. Because nonminorities were more likely than minorities to secure credit-union offers, the spillover concentrated markup reductions among nonminorities, widening the racial gap in dealer markups and, combined with higher buy rates that lenders introduced to keep dealer business viable, in total interest rates.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The article advances research on channel pricing and price disparities in bargaining by empirically evaluating an actual—rather than simulated—government intervention that made removal of dealer discretion voluntary for lenders. It shows that voluntary compensation reform can succeed on its target outcome (dealer markups) yet fail to improve average consumer welfare, and introduces a novel policy-induced spillover mechanism whereby zero-markup contracts offered by one lender type (credit unions) alter strategic intermediary pricing on loans from another type (banks), inadvertently amplifying racial disparities.

**Practical Implication**
For policy makers, the findings caution that voluntary financial-channel interventions can be undone by compensating adjustments from lenders and dealers, leaving consumers' total costs unchanged and potentially worsening racial gaps. Regulators should account for interlender competition and heterogeneous lender incentives when designing fair-lending reforms, and should monitor spillovers across loan channels rather than focusing only on the directly targeted markup.

**Limitations**
The identification relies on a quasi-experimental RDiT strategy using observational data, so readers should weigh causal interpretations alongside the robustness and mechanism checks the authors report. The data cover dealer markups up to 2015, so the long-term effects of the intervention—especially after the 2018 congressional disapproval of the CFPB's heightened scrutiny—cannot be assessed. The authors also observe only accepted loan contracts, not the full set of offers presented to each borrower.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine the long-term effects of the CFPB intervention, including the consequences of the 2018 repeal of the Bureau's heightened scrutiny of indirect auto lenders. More broadly, researchers should investigate how voluntary financial-channel policies interact with lender heterogeneity and intermediary pricing in other credit markets, and how spillover mechanisms across lender types shape disparate impacts by race.
