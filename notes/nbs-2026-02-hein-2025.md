# Magic Will Happen if You Gather Us in One Room: Gender Homophily and Women-Only Networking

**Abstract**
Although networking is critical for career advancement, prior research has primarily examined women’s networking in comparison to men’s, often concluding that it plays a less instrumental role due to gendered organizational structures. Women-only networking events are organized to enable women to leverage gender homophily for mutual support and career advancement. However, the mechanisms of gender homophily in women’s networking are not straightforward. Drawing on observations of women-only networking events, interviews, and secondary data, we identify three meanings women attach to homophilic networking (status-driven, instrumental, and expressive) and show how these meanings shape networking behaviors. In networking contexts characterized by “enforced homophily,” where support is offered under the guise of benevolence, women are more aware of these differences in meanings, which prevent them from leveraging the potential benefits of networking with other women. In contexts characterized by “agentic homophily,” the relevance of these differences is less salient, empowering women to challenge, rather than reproduce, gendered organizational structures.

**Research Question**
How do women perceive and experience networking at women-only events, and how do contextual factors shape the meanings women attach to gender homophily in such settings?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Networking context design (enforced vs. agentic homophily); women's exposure to gendered organizational structures (aligned with seniority level).
- DV(s): Networking behaviors and outcomes (distancing vs. collective networking; challenging vs. reproducing gendered structures).
- Mediators: Meanings attached to homophilic networking (status-driven, instrumental, expressive); salience of differences in meanings among attendees.
- Moderators: Event organizer approach (patronizing/benevolent vs. autonomy-supporting); seniority level; industry context.

Women internalize gendered organizational structures differently depending on their seniority, which shapes the meanings (status-driven for senior-level, instrumental for mid-career, expressive for junior-level) they attach to networking with other women. When events are designed with enforced homophily — a patronizing, benevolent-sexism frame that treats women as a homogeneous disadvantaged group needing help — these differences in meanings become salient, fostering distancing and preventing women from leveraging homophilic ties. When events permit agentic homophily — granting women autonomy to shape the format and pursue professional outcomes — the differences in meanings recede, enabling collective, business-focused networking that can challenge rather than reproduce gendered organizational structures.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study reconceptualizes gender homophily as a multifaceted force shaped by the meanings individuals attach to it and by the context in which networking unfolds, rather than as a singular social mechanism. By distinguishing enforced from agentic homophily and uncovering three interlinked meanings (status-driven, instrumental, expressive), the paper extends research on gender homophily, activist choice homophily, and gendered organizations, showing that well-intentioned corporate women-only events can reproduce benevolent sexism while agentic settings enable women to challenge gendered structures.

**Practical Implication**
Organizers should recognize that women-only networking events designed with a patronizing, "fix-the-women" framing can perpetuate benevolent sexism and structural barriers rather than dismantle them. Companies should grant women agency to shape events from the ground up, focus on collective professional outcomes, de-emphasize seniority hierarchies, and consider mixed-gender formats that recognize women's achievements as alternatives that may better address underlying gendered organizational structures.

**Limitations**
The study focused on corporately sponsored events in highly competitive, male-dominated industries in Germany and the UK, limiting generalizability to other industries, female-dominated fields, and non-Western cultural contexts. The authors' egalitarian cultural backgrounds may have caused them to miss subtle forms of benevolent sexism. Intersectional dynamics (race, disability, LGBT+ status, socioeconomic background) did not feature prominently and were not deeply examined.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine women's networking in female-dominated fields, internal organizational networks tracked longitudinally, and events without corporate sponsorship to capture naturally occurring homophilic ties. Longitudinal studies could explore generational and cultural shifts, while cross-cultural comparative work can surface country-specific dynamics. Research on intersectionality and networking among other disadvantaged groups (by race, disability, or LGBT+ status), as well as mixed-gender networking events, would deepen understanding of context-specific homophily dynamics.
