# Coordination and Commitment in International Climate Action: Evidence From Palm Oil

**Abstract**
Weak environmental regulation has global consequences. When domestic regulation fails, the international community can target emitters with trade policy. I develop a dynamic empirical framework for evaluating trade policy as a substitute for domestic regulation, and I apply the framework to the market for palm oil, a major driver of deforestation and global CO2 emissions. Relative to business as usual, a domestic production tax of 50% reduces CO2 emissions by 7.4 Gt from 1988 to 2016, amounting to 0.26 Gt annually. Coordinated, committed import tariffs of similar magnitude reduce emissions by 5.4 Gt over the same period. The cost of these import tariffs is only $15 per ton of CO2 , even accounting for compensating transfers that recognize welfare losses for producing countries. Without coordination and commitment, import tariffs have more limited effects. Alternative policies include domestic export taxes, which are fiscally appealing independent of emission concerns, and a carbon border adjustment mechanism, which encourages domestic regulation.

**Research Question**
How effective is trade policy as a substitute for direct domestic regulation in reducing carbon emissions, and how do the twin challenges of international coordination (across importers) and commitment (over time) shape the emission reductions and welfare costs achievable with import tariffs, export taxes, and a carbon border adjustment mechanism?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Trade-policy instruments (production taxes, coordinated/uncoordinated and committed/uncommitted import tariffs, export taxes, carbon border adjustment mechanism)
- DV(s): CO2 emissions from palm-driven deforestation (tree biomass and peat); global social welfare including compensating transfers to producing countries
- Mediators: Equilibrium world palm oil prices, plantation development (extensive mill entry and intensive land-clearing margin), and cross-market substitution between palm oil and other vegetable oils
- Moderators: Demand elasticity in unregulated markets (drives leakage → coordination losses); supply elasticity difference between long and short run (drives non-commitment losses); spatial distribution of carbon stocks (peat vs. tree biomass)

Emissions depend on dynamic, forward-looking firm investment in mills and plantations in Indonesia and Malaysia, where deforestation — especially of carbon-rich peatlands — releases CO2. Trade policy changes the world prices firms receive, altering both the extensive margin (mill entry) and the intensive margin (hectares deforested). Demand-side leakage (consumption shifting to unregulated importers) and supply-side leakage (production shifting across regions) erode uncoordinated tariffs, while the government's temptation to drop tariffs once emissions are sunk generates a commitment problem: both coordination and commitment are each necessary to achieve the largest emission reductions.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a new dynamic empirical framework for quantitatively evaluating green trade policy, jointly formalizing and estimating the coordination problem (from leakage to unregulated markets) and the commitment problem (from time-inconsistency once emissions are sunk) in a single industry-equilibrium setting. Methodologically, it combines discrete and continuous Euler conditional-choice-probability techniques to estimate a dynamic discrete-continuous choice model of firm entry and investment without solving it, offering a computationally light strategy that can be applied to other trade-and-environment settings.

**Practical Implication**
For policymakers, EU-led import tariffs on palm oil can reduce emissions by 5.4 Gt at a cost of only $15 per ton of CO2 inclusive of compensating transfers to Indonesia and Malaysia, but only if importers coordinate and commit over the long run; unilateral EU action still yields 1 Gt of abatement at roughly $50 per ton. Export taxes in producing countries achieve the same emission reductions as import tariffs while generating domestic fiscal revenue, and a carbon border adjustment mechanism crediting domestic regulation can encourage producing countries to regulate themselves.

**Limitations**
The author notes that demand estimation relies on annual data, which may attenuate elasticity estimates and thereby understate leakage concerns. The supply model assumes zero exit of plantations and mills (justified by the perennial nature of oil palm and observed low exit rates), and smallholder-mill relationships are treated as vertically integrated because mills extract rents through exclusive contracting. The framework abstracts from substitution between palm oil and fossil fuels in the EU because of binding biofuel blending targets.

**Future Research**
The author closes by asking how international climate action can progress in "an increasingly fragmented and unequal world" and calls for future work grounded in political realities — specifically, analyses that incorporate the political economy of coordination and commitment among heterogeneous, lower-income producing countries facing competing growth imperatives.
