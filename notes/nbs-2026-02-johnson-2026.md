# Sustainable HRM in the Public Sector: A Question of Viability or Legitimacy?

**Abstract**
Recent research has underlined the growing importance of sustainability in HRM policy and practice, taking into account long‐ term multi‐stakeholder goals. However, few studies have specified the drivers and outcomes of sustainable HRM practices, nor the contradictions that arise when managers attempt to satisfy the demands of both internal and external stakeholders. Drawing on in‐depth case studies of the UK public sector, we distinguish between the institutional sustainability logics of (i) viability (concerned with necessary resources and capacities to deliver) and (ii) legitimacy (concerned with satisfying social and moral demands for fair and consistent HR practices) and explore how these two logics interact through positive and negative feedback loops. Our findings show that across an increasingly complex HRM eco‐system, the tensions between viability and legitimacy are increasingly addressed through a focus on building individual human capital and personal wellbeing rather than protecting job security and the collective 'common good'.

**Research Question**
The study asks: What evidence is there of local authority implementation of sustainable HRM bundles and how do these bundles reflect the competing demands of different stakeholders? How are HRM bundles framed by the institutional logics of viability and legitimacy? And to what extent is there organisational feedback and adaptation of sustainable HRM practices over time?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs / tensions: logic of viability (resources/capacities to deliver) vs. logic of legitimacy (satisfying social and moral demands for fair and consistent HR practices)
- Practices: living wage accreditation, flexible working, training and career development, collective negotiation with trade unions, socially responsible restructuring (voluntary severance, internal redeployment), ethical procurement charters
- Triggering conditions: approximately 40% real-terms budget cuts to UK local government since 2010, rising service demand, staff shortages in social care, outsourcing pressures, left-wing (Labour) political leadership in deprived areas
- Feedback loops: positive (virtuous cycle) in which viability-enhancing HRM reinforces external legitimacy and vice versa; negative (vicious cycle) in which cost-driven outsourcing erodes socially legitimate employment and generates worker/union resistance
- Emergent outcomes: heterogeneous, ad hoc HRM bundles; a shift from collective "common good" protections toward individual human capital and wellbeing initiatives; selective insourcing and ethical procurement where trade unions and political leaders align

Across the six cases, managers navigate competing institutional logics by assembling heterogeneous bundles that combine higher wages, flexibility, and training with headcount reductions and functional flexibility. Trade-union engagement and strong political leadership (notably at LA3 and LA4) help break the negative feedback loop from cost-driven outsourcing by re-legitimising in-house delivery and ethical procurement. Elsewhere, pragmatic "business case" logic narrows sustainable HRM to improvements above legal minima rather than best-in-class common good standards.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper moves beyond ideal-type typologies of sustainable HRM (socially responsible, green, triple bottom line, common good) by revealing the tensions and contradictions within heterogeneous HRM bundles as they are assembled in public-sector settings. It elaborates the dynamic interplay between institutional logics of viability and legitimacy, showing how positive and negative feedback loops operate over time and how public-sector managers are—contra some prior claims—highly sensitive to economic viability pressures alongside legitimacy demands.

**Practical Implication**
For policymakers and managers, the findings underscore that coordinated action is needed across the fragmented HR ecosystem, including subcontracted workers. Authorities should rebuild eroded HR capacities, extend decent standards through procurement and supplier charters, and pair individual practices (living wage, flexible working, training) with sector-wide reforms such as the UK Employment Rights Act (2025) fair pay agreement for social care.

**Limitations**
The sample is limited to six English local authorities that had positively engaged with workforce sustainability, which the authors acknowledge is not representative of the sector; cases that disengaged or faced stronger resistance are absent. Data reliance on interviews and documents may be subject to confirmation bias from comparative "selecting-to-similarity" sampling. The study also does not offer a longitudinal test of whether the observed practices sustain worker wellbeing over the long term.

**Future Research**
Future work could investigate public sector organisations that have not engaged with sustainability HRM strategies, or contexts with strong financial barriers or stakeholder resistance. Longitudinal and temporal-dynamics studies are needed to model fit between HRM approaches and viability/legitimacy logics as market conditions change. Matched-pair public/private comparisons would clarify sector-specific reference points, norms and accreditation schemes, especially as EDI debates evolve.
