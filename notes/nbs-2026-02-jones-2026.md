# Contending with Perceived Legitimacy Tensions: Impact Investing in Pluralistic Institutional Environments

**Abstract**
This study advances our understanding of new venture legitimacy and resource acquisition by broadening the epistemological scope of our theory to be inclusive of non-­Western contexts. We conducted a qualitative study of entrepreneurs from India, Kenya, and Mexico seeking impact investments from Western impact investors to better understand how entrepreneurs in non-­Western contexts contend with perceived legitimacy tensions that arise when pursuing financial resources from non-­local investors. In contrast to the prevailing assumption that non-­Western entrepreneurs are resource-­constrained and adhere to existing strategies to seek legitimacy from financial resource providers, we find that such entrepreneurs perceive their local institutional environment to have important non-­financial resources that impact how they seek legitimacy from financial resource providers. This study offers a novel conceptualization of new venture legitimation by acknowledging non-­Western and Western institutional environments as important considerations in how entrepreneurs navigate legitimacy with both financial and non-­financial audiences over time.

**Research Question**
How do entrepreneurs in non-Western countries contend with perceived legitimacy tensions that arise when pursuing financial resources from non-local impact investors?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Entrepreneur's perceived legitimacy tensions between local community (community logic) and Western impact investors (market logic) in a pluralistic institutional environment
- DV(s): Legitimating behaviours and resourcing outcomes (commitment to community, balancing audiences, focusing on investor, withdrawal from resource acquisition)
- Mediators: Initial anchoring to community vs. investor expectations; (mis)alignment between entrepreneur's legitimating efforts and focal audience expectations
- Moderators: Prior interaction with Western/market logics; nature and frequency of investor interactions; entrepreneurs' evolving perceptions of pluralistic environment through recursive exposure

Non-Western entrepreneurs embedded in a local community logic encounter a market-based community logic when seeking financial resources from Western impact investors, generating perceived legitimacy tensions between incompatible audience expectations. Entrepreneurs initially anchor themselves to one audience's expectations and attempt to balance, focus, or withdraw in response. Through recursive interactions with investors, entrepreneurs update their perceptions of what legitimate behaviour looks like, which in turn shapes their subsequent legitimation strategies. In many cases, entrepreneurs ultimately commit to their local community—foregoing financial capital—when they perceive that investor expectations would compromise community legitimacy, illustrating entrepreneurs' agency in the legitimation process.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study advances theory on new venture legitimacy by foregrounding the entrepreneur's (not the investor's) perspective and by showing how non-financial resource providers such as local communities alter the legitimating actions entrepreneurs take toward financial investors. It contributes to the institutional pluralism literature by theorizing how entrepreneurs navigate incompatible market and community logics across non-Western and Western institutional environments, revealing that legitimacy seeking is dynamic, recursive, and may entail opting out of resource acquisition altogether. It further contributes to entrepreneurship research by theorizing non-Western contexts as theoretically distinct rather than a descriptive backdrop, surfacing a community logic as a critical boundary condition on prevailing legitimacy theory.

**Practical Implication**
The findings suggest impact investing has yet to fully realize the value of local communities and may be rendered ineffective if its dominant Western, market-logic framing ignores the community logic central to non-Western contexts. Practically, considering the perspectives of entrepreneurs—and the suggestions they offer—can enable a more mutually beneficial and effective use of impact investing funds in non-Western settings. Powerful actors in the impact investing field (e.g., GIIN members, intermediaries) should adapt benchmarks, due diligence, and engagement practices to the community-logic realities of recipient entrepreneurs.

**Limitations**
The study focuses on entrepreneurs from non-Western contexts, yet non-market logics also exist in Western settings (e.g., artisan communities, maker movements, social enterprises), so findings may generalize only partially beyond the studied contexts. Insights are generalized across three diverse non-Western institutional contexts (Kenya, India, Mexico), which substantiates breadth but may mask country-level differences (e.g., varying legacies of colonization) that could shape entrepreneurs' anchoring decisions. Finally, the sample captures a mix of entrepreneurs with different prior funding outcomes, and these distinct scenarios may differentially shape legitimacy perceptions in ways not fully disentangled in the analyses.

**Future Research**
Researchers could build on the anchoring decisions entrepreneurs make as they seek legitimacy from different audiences, examining Western contexts in which both market and community logics coexist (artisan communities, maker movements, social enterprises). Studies should investigate how structural and cultural legacies of colonization affect entrepreneurs' anchoring decisions at the start of the legitimation process, and how country-level institutional differences shape community-logic strength. Further work can theoretically elaborate on how prior funding experience (successful, unsuccessful, or no prior offers) shapes entrepreneurs' perceived legitimacy tensions and their subsequent legitimating behaviours.
