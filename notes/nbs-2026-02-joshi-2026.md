# Environmental, social and governance impacts of beef production systems: an integrated literature review

**Abstract**
Beef production systems have been evolving for more than a millennium with a primary focus on increasing efficiency. Recently, environmental, social and governance (ESG) concerns have become another key focus due to societal, consumer and regulatory pressures. We identify the temporal evolution of various beef production systems. Using the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines, we assessed the ESG impacts of eight systems across five domains: natural capital; social capital; human capital; produced capital; and governance. This analysis includes 98 articles. Additionally, we conducted a strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats (SWOT) analysis. Regenerative grazing ranks high while landless industrialized system ranks low on all performance metrics. Silvopasture and pasture-based systems excel in community engagement. Biodynamic farming, certified organic and regenerative systems support livelihoods and demonstrate robust governance structures with active stakeholder engagement. Landless industrialized systems demonstrate strong corporate governance. This study provides policymakers with insights on promoting sustainable and ethical beef production practices.

**Research Question**
How have beef production systems evolved over time, and how do eight distinct beef production systems compare in their environmental, social, and governance (ESG) impacts across natural, social, human, produced, and governance capital domains?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Type of beef production system (transhumant, pasture-based, silvopasture, landless industrialized, small-scale landless, biodynamic farming, certified organic, regenerative grazing)
- DV(s): ESG performance scores across 32 indicators grouped into natural, social, human, and produced capital plus governance
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

Drawing on the TEEBAgriFood framework, the authors argue that different beef production systems embody distinct configurations of land use, labor, inputs, animal welfare practices, and governance arrangements, which in turn generate divergent externalities across ESG dimensions. Systematic scoring against 32 indicators derived from the literature, followed by SWOT analysis, reveals how traditional, low-input, and regenerative systems tend to deliver stronger ecological and community outcomes while landless industrialized systems achieve productivity and formal corporate governance at the cost of environmental and welfare performance.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper offers three contributions: it provides a historical overview of how beef production systems have transitioned over time and their ESG assessment; it outlines pathways towards sustainability-enhancing practices in beef production; and it contributes to the development of an integrated ESG framework adapted to the complexities of agri-food production by operationalizing the TEEBAgriFood approach across 32 indicators and eight production systems.

**Practical Implication**
The ESG scoring and SWOT analysis provide a base for actionable, context-specific policy recommendations targeting the strengths and weaknesses of each system. Policymakers should combine system-specific production-side reforms (e.g., supporting regenerative grazing, addressing certification barriers for organic and biodynamic systems, tightening environmental and welfare standards for landless industrialized operations) with demand-side measures such as public awareness campaigns, dietary guidelines, and incentives for protein diversification. Stakeholders are encouraged to promote transparent governance and collaborative, participatory arrangements to steer the beef sector toward ethical and sustainable practices.

**Limitations**
The review may not fully capture within-system variation driven by geography, management, and scale, and it focuses on the production side, excluding processing, transportation, distribution, and food security/nutrition aspects. Integrated dairy-beef systems are not included, and the study is limited to English-language literature. Equal weighting is applied to all ESG indicators, and methodological choices and system boundaries across primary studies produce uncertainties in reported GHG emissions, so the rankings should be interpreted as indicative rather than absolute.

**Future Research**
Future studies could include dairy-origin beef systems as a separate category and explore alternative weighting schemes and sensitivity analyses to test the robustness of rankings under different assumptions. Additional indicators reflecting agricultural value chain variations could be incorporated, and further research is needed on socially conscious beef production, since social and human indicators remain the least discussed in the existing literature. Biodynamic farming in particular warrants more research attention given its limited evidence base.
