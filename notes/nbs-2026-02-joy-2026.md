**Theoretical Contribution**
The editorial reframes knowledge generation in management learning and education as a "pluriversal conversation" rather than a homogenizing, universalist project anchored in Global North research traditions. Drawing on decolonial and pluriversal scholarship (Banerjee, 2022; Jammulamadaka et al., 2021; Mignolo, 2011), the authors argue that journals like AMLE should treat the global academic community as a pluriverse of coexisting worlds with distinct ontologies and epistemologies, and should cultivate ontological and epistemic equality through phenomenon-based research, contextualized theorizing, and ontological/epistemological grounding of methods. The piece positions AMLE's "big table" as a platform for connecting pluriversal conversations across peripheries and centers, thereby challenging epistemic coloniality in elite management journals.

**Practical Implication**
The editors invite scholars from peripheral academic communities — including those within Global North institutions — to submit work that draws on their lifeworlds, focuses on locally meaningful problems, puzzles, and practices, and engages with context-relevant social science and indigenous knowledge traditions (e.g., buen vivir, swaraj, kgotla). They describe concrete AMLE initiatives including community-specific, stage-wise paper development workshops in emerging research ecosystems, a "Talk-to-Editor" feedback initiative, multilingual abstracts and PDWs, and partnerships with local AOM chapters and academic organizations to build reviewer pools and editorial board representation. Authors, reviewers, and editors are urged to practice ontological empathy and to travel to other knowledge worlds as genuine travelers rather than "academic tourists."

**Future Research**
The editorial calls for future AMLE submissions that centre phenomenon-based research on under-studied practices (e.g., rural immersion, land-based learning), contextualized theory-building that draws on disciplines like sociology, anthropology, and political science to fit peripheral settings, and methodological work grounded in diverse ontological and epistemological positions. It encourages studies that investigate dimensions of diversity beyond the dominant gender/race/sexual-identity frame (e.g., urban–rural divide, caste, language), reexamine human–nature relations in light of socio-ecological crises, and generate new forms of evidence beyond corporate, managerial, and curated archival data sources.
