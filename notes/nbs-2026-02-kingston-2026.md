# Social Upgrading in Global Supply Chains: Reframing Corporate Responsibilities

**Abstract**
Much philosophical literature on sweatshop ethics assumes that the individual branded marketers that sell consumer goods either employ sweatshop workers or can strongly influence the conditions under which those workers labor. This oversimplification misidentifies the rationale for and details of the responsibilities of big buyers for the labor standards in their supply chains. Throughout this article, we illustrate how philosophers’ “vertical integration” and “control” assumptions distort our understanding of the internal dynamics within supply chains. Under the more realistic assumption that big buyers have “constrained influence” over labor conditions in their supply chains, we show that big buyers retain the responsibility to work toward social upgrading goals. However, fulfilling such a responsibility requires big buyers to collectively cede power to third parties in supply chains in formalized and accountable ways. Recent developments in transnational industrial agreements, such as the International Accord, are examples of this commitment.

**Research Question**
What moral responsibilities do "big buyers" in high-income countries bear for social upgrading in their global supply chains once we relax the philosophical literature's unrealistic "vertical integration" and "control" assumptions and instead recognize that big buyers exert only a "constrained influence" over working conditions at their suppliers?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Structure of global supply chains (unbundled/outsourced rather than vertically integrated); big buyers' constrained influence over supplier labor practices
- DV(s): Moral responsibility of big buyers for social upgrading (improvement in workers' rights and the quality of their employment)
- Mediators: Collective action among big buyers; binding commitments to third parties (unions, multi-stakeholder bodies); formal accountability mechanisms
- Moderators: Industry codifiability of production (apparel, electronics vs. luxury/high-quality goods); availability of transnational industrial agreements (e.g., International Accord, ACT); state regulatory context

Drawing on structural-injustice theorists (Young, Müller) and empirical work on global value chains, the authors argue that unbundling severs the simple employer-employee link assumed by vertical integrationists and by "big buyer control" theorists. Because no individual buyer can unilaterally guarantee decent working conditions at independent suppliers, standard arguments that assign (or exonerate) buyers on the basis of direct control misidentify the problem. Yet constrained influence does not dissolve responsibility: buyers retain forward-looking duties to contribute to social upgrading, and the most plausible way to discharge those duties is through federated/agonistic CSR arrangements in which buyers collectively cede power to accountable third parties—exemplified by binding transnational industrial agreements such as the International Accord on Fire and Building Safety in Bangladesh and the ACT on Living Wages initiative.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reframes the philosophical debate on sweatshop ethics by replacing the dominant "vertical integration" and "big buyer control" assumptions with an empirically grounded "constrained influence" assumption, and by showing how this reframing changes the rationale and content of big buyers' responsibilities for social upgrading. It bridges structural-injustice theory with emerging work on federated and agonistic corporate social responsibility to argue that responsibility is discharged through collective, formalized commitments among buyers rather than individual due diligence or consumer activism.

**Practical Implication**
Big buyers should not treat social upgrading as a matter of unilateral auditing or codes of conduct, but should instead join binding multi-employer agreements—such as the International Accord and ACT—that commit them to cede authority to accountable third parties (unions, multi-stakeholder bodies) in setting and enforcing labor standards. Such federated commitments can strengthen rather than weaken domestic labor law enforcement and offer a more realistic route to improving worker rights than either purely market-based or state-centric approaches.

**Limitations**
The article focuses exclusively on the duties of big buyers and brackets the responsibilities of sovereign governments, consumers, and other stakeholders, which it acknowledges limits the scope of its conclusions. Its tentative proposals place little emphasis on the state as a mechanism for resolving the epistemic and collective action problems of supply chains, and it does not fully adjudicate critiques that such federated private governance may still enable big buyers to evade political and legal responsibility. The argument is normative and illustrative rather than based on systematic empirical evaluation of the International Accord or ACT's effects.

**Future Research**
Future work should examine the responsibilities of sovereign governments and other actors (consumers, trade policy-makers, domestic regulators) alongside those of big buyers, and assess how state regulation can best interact with federated buyer commitments. Further scholarship is needed on the design and effectiveness of transnational industrial agreements beyond the International Accord and on how collective wage-setting initiatives such as ACT can navigate trade-offs between efficiency and living-wage goals. The authors also invite deeper exploration of how structural theorists' accounts of responsibility can be operationalized across different consumer-goods sectors.
