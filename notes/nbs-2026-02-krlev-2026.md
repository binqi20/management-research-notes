# Social entrepreneurship mechanisms for systems change: A structuration approach

**Abstract**
This article advocates for harnessing Giddens' structuration theory to improve our understanding. I develop a structuration model of systems change through social entrepreneurship, which shows that connected modalities, that is, intricate combinations of (1) interpretative schemes, (2) resources, and (3) norms, act as the key mechanisms that enable systemic effects.

**Research Question**
How do social entrepreneurs seek to advance systems change, and when do they succeed or fail to achieve it? More specifically, which mechanisms enable social entrepreneurial agency to translate into structural, systemic transformations in society?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Social entrepreneurs' mobilization and combination of modalities — (1) interpretative schemes (mindsets, imaginations), (2) resources, and (3) norms — into "connected modalities" versus using separate or single modalities
- DV(s): Systems change (fundamental shifts in social processes, practices, and structures), operationalized via high integrativeness (reach across multiple issues, fields, and societal levels) and high inclusiveness (mobilization of diverse stakeholders)
- Mediators: Integrativeness and inclusiveness as dual pathways linking connected modalities to systems change; dynamic duality of agency and structure
- Moderators: Adverse systemic conditions (resource constraints, rigid norms, mainstream cognitive pressures); rigidity of surrounding institutions; cooptation risks from stakeholder alignment

Drawing on Giddens' structuration theory, the paper argues that agency and structure are recursively constituted through modalities that actors mobilize in practice. Social entrepreneurs drive systems change when they actively combine new interpretative schemes, new resource constellations, and new norms into "connected modalities" rather than relying on a single lever. Such connected modalities simultaneously enable high integrativeness (triggering parallel change across issues and societal levels) and high inclusiveness (activating diverse stakeholders to co-engage), producing a reinforcing interplay between entrepreneurial agency and institutional structure that overcomes the dualism treating structure as a constraint on agency.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces "connected modalities" as the central mechanism through which social entrepreneurship produces systems change, reframing the agency-structure relationship in social entrepreneurship as a productive duality rather than a dualism. By applying Giddens' structuration theory, the model bridges the micro-macro divide in social entrepreneurship research and explains why some social entrepreneurs achieve fundamental transformations while others do not. It further reconceptualizes core but poorly theorized features of social entrepreneurship — its capacity to enhance society's ability to act, its reliance on revolving innovation practices, and its characteristic vulnerability — as consequences of the need to continually generate and align connected modalities with evolving stakeholder constellations.

**Practical Implication**
Support for social entrepreneurship should stop mimicking commercial incubation practices, which focus narrowly on viable business models and financial resource mobilization, because these are insufficient for systems change. Entrepreneurial strategy, incubation, and policy should instead train and support ventures to craft and orchestrate connected modalities — combining new interpretative schemes, resources, and norms — and to engage in political brokerage that aligns diverse stakeholders. Policy makers should favor instruments such as crowdsourcing around broadly defined sustainability challenges over standard pitch competitions and innovation prizes, which select for commercial viability rather than inclusiveness and integrativeness.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
Future work could investigate the relationships between different modalities and their self-perpetuating outcomes, including whether there are optimal combinations, sequences, or numbers of modalities to connect and whether particular "packages" of connected modalities characterize specific industries or issue areas. Researchers could also examine softer discursive and normative forms of bricolage as drivers of innovation, and explore the boundary conditions at which tuning connected modalities to stakeholder agendas, or extending integrativeness to additional societal domains, undermines rather than promotes systems change.
