# Using Photo-Elicitation to Make Marginalised Voices Heard and Seen in Human Resource Management Research

**Abstract**
Photo‐elicitation (PE) is a qualitative research method that utilises images to obtain a deeper understanding of the perspectives, and beliefs of the research participants. The PE approach can be particularly advantageous for marginalised voices (silenced or underrepresented groups with limited power) by exploring different world views, challenging commonly accepted information, and investigating the material work context. Additionally, it has the potential to impact power dynamics between the researcher and the participants, while promoting collaboration. Consequently, we contend that the use of PE can enhance the representation of marginalised perspectives in human resource management (HRM) and the effectiveness of sustainable transformations in the workplace. To support our assertion, we present our PE research endeavours from a participant‐driven and researcher‐driven PE approach. While there are other instances of PE in HRM studies, this observational study identifies and revises the practical translation of applied method and data analysis supporting researchers in their future PE applications.

**Research Question**
How can photo-elicitation be used as a qualitative HRM research method to make marginalised voices heard and seen, and what practical guidance can researchers draw from participant-driven versus researcher-driven PE approaches? Two sub-questions structure the exemplars: "How do women in academic leadership make sense of their experience in a university in Vietnam?" and "How do trans and gender diverse identities influence individual work engagement and personal career development of TGD individuals in relation to the organisational awareness of gender diversity?"

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: Marginalised voices; material and physical practices of work; power dynamics between researcher and participant; embodied, emancipatory knowledge production.
- PE variants: Participant-driven PE (reflexive photography, auto-driving, photovoice — participants generate images) versus researcher-driven PE (researcher preselects or generates images for stimulated response).
- Process / phases: (1) research design aligned with critical theory and humanism; (2) purposive/snowball sampling of marginalised participants; (3) image generation (participant-produced or researcher-generated metaphorical images); (4) photo-elicit interview or open-ended questionnaire; (5) iterative analysis (IPA seven-step for exemplar 1; critical grounded theory with first- and second-cycle coding plus memo-writing and member-checking for exemplar 2); (6) reflexivity throughout.
- Practices: Member-checking; metaphorical image construction; SHOWED-style questioning adapted to questionnaire; method triangulation; positionality disclosure.
- Boundary conditions / challenges: Ethical issues of ownership, authorship, consent, and privacy; risk of tokenism; interpretive subjectivity; emotional labour; resource intensity; limited small samples.
- Emergent outcomes: "Powershift" from disempowered respondent to equitable research partner; surfacing of tacit, embodied, and symbolic knowledge (e.g., "swimming alone" metaphor for women's leadership journey; metaphorical images of dress code, toilet doors, and team inclusion for TGD experiences); authentic inclusion of marginalised voices in HRM research.

The central process is that PE, by introducing images as a "third party" in the research encounter, unlocks embodied and symbolic communication that verbal interviewing alone cannot access, thereby rebalancing power relations and enabling marginalised participants to co-construct meaning about their material and physical work practices. In the Vietnamese exemplar, participant-chosen photos (e.g., motorbikes, rivers, Don Quixote windmills) functioned as metaphorical anchors that prompted deep reflection on gendered leadership constraints; in the Australian exemplar, researcher-generated metaphorical images protected TGD participants' privacy while still eliciting HR/managerial personnel's taken-for-granted assumptions about cisnormativity. Across both, PE operationalises critical HRM/HRD humanism by foregrounding voices that verbal-centric, Global-North methods systematically under-represent.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper offers a conceptual contribution to qualitative research methods in HRM by revising and translating PE for HRM scholarship, clarifying the distinction between participant-driven and researcher-driven PE and their differential implications for power dynamics, ethics, and analytic depth. It re-envisions the role of PE in providing holistic insights into the material and physical practices of work, narrowing Kjellstrand and Vince's (2020) noted gap on how to use photos in the OMS research process. By linking PE to critical HRD's humanistic paradigm, the authors position visual methods as an emancipatory tool that challenges meta-narratives of marginalisation in HRM.

**Practical Implication**
HRM researchers are encouraged to adopt PE to access tacit, embodied, and symbolic dimensions of workplace experience — especially for marginalised populations where verbal interviewing may reproduce suppression. The authors provide a practical research-design toolkit (Tables 1-3) covering participant selection, preparation, ethical considerations (consent, ownership, authorship, privacy), data collection structure, analytic frameworks, and reflexivity prompts. For organisations, the exemplars surface actionable insights: Vietnamese higher education institutions should address systemic underrepresentation of women in leadership (7.6% of rectors vs. 50.1% of academics) and the unpaid gendered labour of event organising; Australian employers should improve TGD inclusion through spatial (e.g., toilets, workstations), symbolic (dress code, documentation), and leadership practices.

**Limitations**
Each exemplar faces interpretive challenges: the participant-driven approach raises complex ethical issues of image ownership, joint interpretation, and consent when others appear in photos (leading Jane to describe rather than publish images); the researcher-driven approach introduces researcher subjectivity in image generation, depends on respondents' willingness to engage with metaphors, and is resource-intensive. COVID-19 restrictions forced the Australian study to pivot from photovoice to researcher-generated images. Small sample sizes (6 and 22) limit generalisability, and the online asynchronous questionnaire format precluded follow-up probing.

**Future Research**
The authors call for HRM scholars to experiment with PE and other alternative, non-verbal research methods beyond the Global North's verbal-linguistic norms, to further diversify research engagement and knowledge production. They encourage further work on addressing power dynamics between researcher and participant, refining ethical protocols for visual data (ownership, authorship, consent), extending PE to other marginalised communities, and developing quality indicators for mixed visual-verbal analysis. They also advocate interdisciplinary collaboration to continue narrowing the gap on how visual methods contribute to critical HRM/HRD scholarship.
