# Democratic Deviations: How Organizations Sustain Decentralization Commitments in the Face of Centralization Pressures

**Abstract**
Organizations that have committed to decentralizing authority can face situations in which centralized decision making is useful or necessary. However, centralization risks undermining an organization’s perceived commitment to decentralization, which can threaten employees’ motivation and commitment, as well as organizational legitimacy. Leveraging a comparative case study of four organizations that had explicitly committed to decentralize authority and that subsequently centralized authority during the COVID-19 crisis, we identify a process by which organizations can deviate from decentralization commitments while keeping those commitments in place. This process, which we call ‘‘democratic deviation,’’ involves leaders and workers collectively authorizing centralization and then leaders enacting that authority with openness and transparency. In the three of our four cases that deviated democratically, decentralization commitments were upheld. Conversely, when centralization occurred implicitly through leader fiat and was then enacted opaquely by leaders, a process we label ‘‘monocratic deviation,’’ decentralization commitments were undermined. Through this study, we develop a theory of the structural antecedents and processes of durable commitments to decentralization, prompting re-evaluation of decentralization’s assumed fragility. More broadly, our theory suggests how organizations can uphold shared commitments even when they must deviate from them in the face of situational pressures.

**Research Question**
How can organizations navigate pulls toward centralization in ways that uphold, rather than undermine, their commitments to decentralization?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Type of centralization process — democratic deviation (collective authorization + open/transparent enactment) vs. monocratic deviation (leader-fiat authorization + opaque enactment)
- DV(s): Durability of workers' perceptions of the organization's commitment to decentralization (upheld vs. undermined)
- Mediators: Authorization phase (explicit collective rules vs. implicit fiat); enactment phase (open/transparent vs. opaque leader behavior); leaders' sense of accountability and legitimacy
- Moderators: Structural antecedents — decentralization at the collective choice level (not just operational level), formal democratic rules governing authority, and distributed (or de facto constrained) ownership/fiat rights

The paper theorizes that structurally similar acts of centralization can produce divergent effects on decentralization commitments depending on the process by which they unfold. Collective authorization by shared democratic rules constrains the scope and duration of centralization, subjects it to oversight, and instills in leaders a sense of accountability that inclines them toward open, transparent enactment; in turn, workers interpret the centralization as a legitimate, bounded, commitment-reinforcing act. By contrast, fiat authorization leaves centralization unbounded and leaders unchecked, weakening legitimacy and producing opaque enactments that workers interpret as a breach of the relational contract. These dynamics are enabled by structural features — multi-level decentralization (operational and collective choice), de facto constraints on owner/leader fiat, and formal democratic rules — that together make decentralization flexible and durable rather than fragile.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a process and structural theory of durable decentralization, showing that preserving commitments to decentralization does not require avoiding centralization but depends on how centralization is authorized and enacted; it identifies two distinct phases (authorization and enactment) and theorizes ‘‘democratic deviation’’ as a process by which organizations can uphold — and even reinforce — decentralization commitments while temporarily centralizing. Structurally, it distinguishes operational-level from collective-choice-level decentralization (drawing on Ostrom), argues that concentrated ownership is compatible with durable decentralization when de facto constraints on leader fiat are in place, and shows that formal democratic rules — rather than informality — make decentralization more flexible and durable. More broadly, it proposes that democratically executed deviations allow organizations to sustain foundational commitments, with implications for corporate purpose, relational contracts, and organizational democracy scholarship.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders of decentralized organizations facing crisis-induced pressures to centralize should not assume they must choose between reclaiming authority and preserving commitments; instead, they can establish and follow formal democratic rules that authorize temporary, bounded centralization and enact that authority openly and transparently. Organizations can strengthen the durability of decentralization by decentralizing collective-choice authority (who decides how authority is distributed), not just operational authority, and by codifying democratic rules for deviation. Such practices help organizations respond flexibly to situational demands without undermining workers' trust, motivation, or legitimacy.

**Limitations**
The study is based on four extreme cases of radically decentralized, knowledge-intensive U.S. firms navigating the singular context of the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have elicited unusual levels of goodwill and deference from workers due to the life-threatening nature of the crisis. The qualitative, inductive design constrains statistical generalizability, and findings may not extend straightforwardly to less-decentralized organizations, other industries, or other types of crises.

**Future Research**
Future scholarship should examine whether the democratic-deviation model extends to less extreme contexts and to situations other than pandemic-induced crises, where workers may be less forgiving of leaders and where the processes of authorization and enactment may be even more consequential. Further work could explore how democratic rules emerge, are revised, and interact with ownership structures, and how democratic deviation applies to sustaining other foundational organizational commitments such as corporate purpose.
