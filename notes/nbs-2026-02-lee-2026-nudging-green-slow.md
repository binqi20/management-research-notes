# Nudging Green-but-Slow Shipping Choices in Online Retail

**Abstract**
We first introduce a conceptual framework for selecting information strategies based on their influence on customer perceptions related to key stakeholders in each context: delivery convenience (self), environmental impact (society), and sustainability intention (retailer). Then, drawing on behavioral research and industry practices, we identify and test the effect of a comprehensive set of information strategies (i.e., Process, Green Label, Green Process, and Green Outcome) through controlled experiments involving 3,800 partici­ pants. Experimental results suggest that different logistical contexts require distinct nudg­ ing strategies, aligning with our conceptual framework. We find that including a green label is particularly effective for the shipping mode context, especially when combined with outcome information. In contrast, process information is more promising in the order consolidation context. Also, adding financial incentives can enhance the effectiveness of information strategies without a clear environmental signal, whereas pairing them with a simple green label may be effective for low eco-conscious individuals.

**Research Question**
Which information strategies (Process, Green Label, Green Process, Green Outcome) most effectively nudge online-retail customers to voluntarily choose green-but-slow shipping, and how does the most effective strategy depend on the logistical context (shipping mode vs. order consolidation) and the presence of financial incentives?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Information strategy (Baseline / Process / Green Label / Green Process / Green Outcome); logistical context (shipping mode vs. order consolidation); presence of a financial incentive.
- DV(s): Customer's binary choice of the green-but-slow shipping option at checkout.
- Mediators: Customer perceptions of delivery convenience (self), environmental impact (society), and sustainability intention (retailer); and, in the order consolidation context, the salience (attribute activation) of environmental impact and delivery convenience during the choice.
- Moderators: Logistical context; financial incentive; individual eco-consciousness.

Drawing on a stakeholder model of customer perceptions (self, society, retailer), the paper argues that information strategies shift slow-shipping choices by raising baseline perceptions on whichever stakeholder dimension is initially low. In the shipping mode context, baseline perceptions of delivery convenience and environmental impact are both low, so an explicit green label is required to signal environmental merit, and adding outcome information (95% CO2 reduction) further amplifies perceived environmental impact. In the order consolidation context, baseline perceptions of self- and society-benefits are already moderate, so process information (fewer boxes, fewer trips) is as persuasive as a green label by activating the saliency of these already-recognized benefits rather than by raising perception levels. Financial incentives substitute for, rather than complement, strategies that already convey a clear green signal.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops a stakeholder-based conceptual framework that explains why the relative effectiveness of informational nudges for sustainable shipping is contingent on the logistical context, bridging behavioral operations, operational transparency, and prosocial-behavior research. It is the first study to test process vs. green-label information in isolation and to examine green-but-slow nudging in an order consolidation context. Using contemporary causal mediation analysis, it identifies two distinct psychological mechanisms — perception enhancement (shipping mode) and saliency activation (order consolidation) — through which information strategies operate on customer choice.

**Practical Implication**
Retailers should tailor nudging strategies to their logistical context: for shipping-mode choices, use a green label, ideally paired with outcome information when carbon savings are substantial; for order-consolidation choices, process information ("fewer boxes and fewer trips") is as effective as a green label and avoids potential greenwashing concerns. Financial incentives add little value once a clear green signal is present, but a small incentive combined with a simple green label can help recruit low-eco-consciousness customers into choosing green-but-slow shipping.

**Limitations**
The controlled experimental design, while enabling causal identification and mechanism testing, may yield effect sizes that differ from real-world e-commerce settings; an auxiliary higher-prize-probability experiment showed that baseline slow-shipping preference is sensitive to purchase stakes. Only one small financial incentive level ($1) was tested, and only gain-framed messages were used. The sample is limited to U.S. MTurk respondents and two product categories (consumer electronics and toothbrushes).

**Future Research**
Future work could run field experiments to estimate effect sizes and explore situational factors such as product price and urgency; test loss-framed messages, social-norm cues, and other nudging tactics beyond message content; investigate larger or differently structured financial incentives; and extend the stakeholder-perception framework to other customer-facing logistical decisions such as store pickup versus home delivery or delivery scheduling.
