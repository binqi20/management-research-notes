# Physical Disability in the Workplace: A Comprehensive Review and Unifying Framework

**Abstract**
In the United States, more than a quarter of the working-age population experiences physical disabilities that shape workplace participation and career outcomes. Drawing on 168 articles, we synthesize the management literature to develop a unifying framework of focal mechanisms, workplace outcomes, and boundary conditions. In doing so, we offer a clear definition of physical disability, long absent from management research. We also highlight the fragmented theoretical and methodological landscape, in which most studies aggregate disability types, overlooking critical heterogeneity. Our review charts a path forward by identifying key gaps, outlining directions for future scholarship, and emphasizing the need for stronger theoretical grounding and methodological rigor. Finally, we discuss implications for policy and practice, underscoring how greater inclusion of persons with physical disabilities can advance various workplace outcomes.

**Research Question**
How can the fragmented management literature on physical disability in the workplace be synthesized into a unifying framework, and what constitutes a precise, shared definition of physical disability that addresses the lack of consensus and absence of an integrative framework across prior reviews?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Focal mechanisms surrounding persons with physical disabilities (PWPDs), including treatment by others, accommodation provision, disclosure/concealment decisions, and attributes of the disability (type, severity, visibility, concealability, onset, progression, unpredictability)
- DV(s): Workplace outcomes at three levels — (1) individual PWPD outcomes (career, well-being, job attitudes), (2) interpersonal outcomes for coworkers without disabilities, and (3) organizational outcomes (performance, reputation, turnover, efficiency)
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Boundary conditions including national culture, legislation and policy context, organizational type, disability type and nature, and diversity climate

Building on Stone and Colella's (1996) seminal framework, the authors integrate focal mechanisms with three categories of workplace outcomes linked to PWPDs, others in the organization, and the organization at large. The framework argues that how PWPDs are treated and how they navigate disclosure and accommodation is shaped by disability attributes and organizational/institutional boundary conditions, which in turn drive heterogeneous individual, interpersonal, and organizational outcomes. The review highlights that aggregation of disability types masks these mechanisms and calls for disaggregated theorization.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The review offers a synthesized and precise definition of physical disability drawing on management, medical, legal, and sociological sources, anchored in the amended ADA (2008), and refines the conceptualization inherited from Stone and Colella (1996). It develops a unifying framework encompassing focal mechanisms, workplace outcomes, and boundary conditions that clarifies the theoretical and methodological fragmentation of PWPD research and identifies underused theoretical lenses (e.g., expectancy violation theory, agency theory) that could advance the field.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should adopt inclusive hiring practices (bias training, transparent disclosure processes), remove structural and procedural barriers (universally designed workspaces, accessible recruitment tools, streamlined accommodation procedures), and visibly include PWPDs across roles to normalize disability and enhance organizational image. Policymakers should expand support for disability entrepreneurship, incentivize private-sector inclusion, strengthen and extend accommodation mandates to non-standard workers, and subsidize assistive technologies to make accommodation feasible for organizations of all sizes.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
Future research should examine interpersonal outcomes (e.g., well-being, organizational commitment of coworkers without disabilities interacting with PWPDs), disaggregate disability types rather than aggregating them, and assess how specific disability attributes (severity, visibility, unpredictability, progression) shape outcomes. Scholars should also explore cross-national and cross-organizational differences — particularly outside Europe and the United States — and apply underused theoretical lenses such as expectancy violation theory and agency theory to questions of perception formation, accommodation monitoring, and performance evaluation for PWPDs.
