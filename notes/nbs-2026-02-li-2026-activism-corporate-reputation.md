# CEO Activism and Corporate Reputation: How Authenticity and CSR Mitigate Stakeholder Misalignment

**Abstract**
Previous research suggests that the effect of CEO activism depends on its alignment with stakeholders' political ideology. We examine when and why CEO activism can enhance corporate reputation across the ideological spectrum. Integrating signalling theory with a cognitive perspective on reputation formation, we argue that the reputational impact of CEO activism depends on the firm's corporate social responsibility (CSR), which fosters perceptions of organizational authenticity. Using a national survey and an experiment, we find that CEO activism improves corporate reputation through perceived moral authenticity. This effect is stronger when firms hold B Corp certification, engage in philanthropic donations, or are perceived as more socially responsible. Although stakeholders rate firms as more reputable when their political ideology aligns with the CEO's activism, this relationship weakens when firms engage in CSR, thereby signalling authenticity. These findings demonstrate how non-­market strategies interact to shape corporate reputation and inform corporate sociopolitical advocacy among heterogeneous audiences.

**Research Question**
Under what conditions and through what perceptual mechanisms does CEO activism improve corporate reputation across stakeholders with heterogeneous political ideologies, and how does CSR engagement shape these reputational effects?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO activism (BLM statements in Study 1; randomized BLM statement in Study 2); CSR engagement (B Corp certification and CSR perception in Study 1; randomized philanthropic donation in Study 2)
- DV(s): Corporate reputation (RepTrak Pulse scale)
- Mediators: Perceived organizational moral authenticity
- Moderators: Stakeholders' political ideology (liberal-conservative); CSR engagement (moderates both CEO activism effect and ideology effect)

CEO activism operates as a visible and politically costly signal that deviates from pure shareholder-value focus and risks alienating ideologically opposed stakeholders; this costliness makes the stance credible evidence of the organization's underlying values, which stakeholders interpret as moral authenticity — consistency between espoused values and actions. CSR engagement functions as a congruent, longer-horizon signal of organizational values that reinforces authenticity perceptions when combined with activism, amplifying reputational gains. Because moral authenticity is valued across the ideological spectrum, congruent activism-plus-CSR signals attenuate the reputational penalty that ideologically misaligned stakeholders would otherwise impose, producing a cognitive-interpretive buffer against partisan discounting.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends signalling theory by showing that multiple non-market signals (CEO activism and CSR) interact synergistically when congruent, and integrates the signalling perspective with a cognitive view of reputation formation by introducing stakeholders' political ideology as a previously underexplored interpretive dimension. It reframes CSR not only as a reputation premium but as a reputation buffer that overrides ideological disagreement through perceived moral authenticity, distinguishing this mechanism from Godfrey's moral-capital insurance view by locating the buffering effect in audience heterogeneity and cross-strategy spillovers rather than within-domain wrongdoing.

**Practical Implication**
Leaders considering sociopolitical advocacy can mitigate the risk of alienating ideologically opposed stakeholders by pairing CEO activism with substantive, sustained CSR engagement (e.g., B Corp certification, philanthropic donations), because such congruent signals broaden reputational gains across the ideological spectrum. CSR therefore acts as a reputation buffer that makes activism more viable in ideologically diverse environments.

**Limitations**
Study 1 is correlational and its mediation analysis does not establish causality or rule out alternative mechanisms; the authors did not control for perceptions of inauthenticity or hypocrisy potentially elicited by the Study 2 control; and the CSR initiative was operationalized in a politically neutral domain distinct from the activism stance, which may explain its null direct effect on authenticity. The authenticity mechanism may not generalize to product markets, and experimental vignettes capture only immediate responses from general-public samples rather than diverse organizational stakeholders.

**Future Research**
Examine more contentious and politically costly forms of CSR (e.g., BLM-aligned CSR) to test whether CSR alone can trigger authenticity perceptions in charged domains. Employ non-intrusive methods such as sentiment analysis to trace longitudinal reputational effects in real-world settings, and investigate the antecedents of CEO activism — in particular, why B Corps were less likely than Fortune 500 firms to issue activist statements despite benefiting more from doing so.
