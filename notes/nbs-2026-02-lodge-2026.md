# Organizations as Stages: A Dramaturgical Theory of the Societal Integration of Stigmatized Groups

**Abstract**
This paper theorizes how organizations enable the societal integration of stigmatized groups. Drawing on Goffman’s relational perspective on stigma and his dramaturgical metaphors, we conceptualize organizations as “stages” on which stigmatized groups are presented to diverse audiences. We identify two fundamental forms of staging—concealing and normalizing— through which organizations can reduce sanctions from stigmatizing audiences and elicit acceptance among more supportive ones, thereby ultimately enabling societal integration. Our model specifies the structural antecedents that shape the likelihood of each form of staging, offering a nuanced understanding of when organizations adopt them as well as the tensions that may arise from doing so. Based on this, we extend research on organizations’ management of stigma by advancing a dramaturgical theory of the societal integration of stigmatized groups. Whereas prior work has largely assumed that managing the stigmatized attributes of groups is central (and often sufficient) for their broader integration, we demonstrate that integration ultimately depends on the management of audiences and must therefore be understood as an inherently audience-focused process.

**Research Question**
How can organizations, conceptualized as "stages," enable the societal integration of stigmatized groups by presenting them to diverse stigmatizing and supportive audiences, and what structural antecedents shape the forms of staging (concealing vs. normalizing) they adopt?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Structural antecedents of the stigma context — salience of stigmatized attributes (visibility, harmfulness) and institutionalization of attributes (legal/regulatory protection, media scripts, professional codes)
- DV(s): Societal integration of stigmatized groups (reduced sanctioning by stigmatizing audiences; acceptance by supportive audiences)
- Mediators: Forms of organizational staging — concealing (cloaking groups to shield from hostile audiences) and normalizing (spotlighting groups to elicit acceptance from supportive audiences)
- Moderators: Audience composition of the organizational environment (stigmatizing vs. supportive); alignment or mismatch between form of staging and audience type

Drawing on Goffman's relational view, the authors argue that stigma is constituted through audience evaluations rather than being inherent to attributes, so integration depends on how organizations manage audiences. Organizations function as stages that present stigmatized groups in ways that either conceal stigmatized attributes from hostile audiences (reducing sanctions) or normalize them before sympathetic audiences (eliciting acceptance). The likelihood of concealing is driven primarily by the salience of stigmatized attributes, while normalizing is driven by their institutionalization. Because environments are fragmented and contain both stigmatizing and supportive audiences, organizations frequently must engage in both forms of staging simultaneously, generating tensions when visibility from normalizing undermines concealment efforts.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions. First, it reconceptualizes the integration of stigmatized groups as an inherently audience-focused process, departing from prior work that treats integration as a consequence of managing group attributes. Second, it develops a structural model in which forms of staging are driven by contextual antecedents (attribute salience and institutionalization) rather than by managerial preferences alone. Third, it theorizes concealing and normalizing as discrete, potentially simultaneous approaches rather than opposite ends of a continuum, and elaborates the dynamics and tensions that arise when organizations engage in both at once.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should recognize that societal integration is not automatically achieved by openly celebrating stigmatized groups; in some contexts, deliberate concealment may be the most effective route to integration, an insight often overlooked in DEI debates that emphasize visibility. Integration outcomes depend on how different audiences evaluate stigmatized groups, so attribute-focused initiatives may fail if audience dynamics are ignored. Stigmatized groups can use the framework to anticipate trade-offs when selecting employers or advocacy partners, and organizations should involve stigmatized groups in co-creating staging approaches tailored to their needs.

**Future Research**
Future research could investigate the "dark side" of staging—how organizational actions may unintentionally exacerbate sanctions and deepen stigmatization when forms of staging and audiences are misaligned. Scholars could examine the relative weight of the structural antecedents, consider how organizations' own stigma interacts with that of the groups they support, and explore how technology and social-media-driven context collapse amplify or complicate concealing and normalizing activities.
