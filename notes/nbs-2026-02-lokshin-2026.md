# How Gender Diversity and Equality in R&D Departments Jointly Affect Firm-Level Innovation Through Innovation Capabilities

**Abstract**
Innovation research has long shown that firms depend on their R&D personnel to strengthen their innovation capabilities, sparking scholarly interest in how to best staff R&D departments for competitive advantage, with a focus on gender diversity. However, left unexamined are the mechanisms linking gender diversity in R&D departments and firm-level innovation. Further, despite growing interest in the conditions under which diversity may enhance innovation, researchers have not adequately addressed how department-level gender rank inequality—the disproportionate concentration of men and women at various hierarchical ranks—interacts with numerical diversity to affect innovation outcomes. We address both limitations by integrating insights from the information/decision-making perspective on diversity and research on inequality with research on innovation capabilities. Specifically, we develop and test a model explaining how R&D department gender diversity and gender rank equality jointly affect three firm-level innovation capabilities: breadth of external knowledge search, use of external knowledge, and quality of the innovation implementation process.

**Research Question**
How and when does gender diversity in R&D departments influence firm-level innovation—specifically, through which innovation capabilities (the "how"), and under what conditions of gender rank equality at different hierarchical levels of the R&D department (the "when") does this relationship hold?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): R&D department gender diversity (Blau index of women/men composition, lagged)
- DV(s): Firm-level innovation (share of firm sales from new-to-the-market products/services)
- Mediators: Three innovation capabilities — (1) breadth of external knowledge search, (2) use of external knowledge, and (3) quality of the innovation implementation process (reverse-coded late-stage project abandonment)
- Moderators: R&D department gender rank equality (entropy-based Mutual Information measure of (in)dependence between gender and hierarchical rank, reverse-scored)

Drawing on the information/decision-making perspective on diversity combined with research on inequality, the authors argue that gender-diverse R&D departments can only convert demographic variety into innovation capabilities when men and women are proportionally represented across hierarchical ranks. Gender rank equality reduces the salience of gender and gender stereotypes, fostering a climate in which complementary knowledge, perspectives, networks, and resources can be voiced and integrated. Under these conditions, gender diversity strengthens external knowledge search, external knowledge use, and implementation-process quality, which in turn raise firm-level innovation. When gender rank inequality is high, by contrast, status-based hierarchies suppress communication and collaboration, attenuating or reversing the diversity-innovation link.

**Theoretical Contribution**
First, the paper identifies firm-level innovation capabilities as the missing mechanism linking R&D department gender composition to firm-level innovation, responding to calls to move beyond upper-echelons explanations and to show how workforce diversity shapes organizational capabilities. Second, it brings inequality-as-disparity back into diversity research by demonstrating that numerical gender diversity cannot be understood without jointly considering the distribution of power and authority across hierarchical ranks, thereby helping to reconcile mixed prior evidence on gender diversity and innovation. Third, it extends theorizing on when gender diversity fosters a climate for constructive collaboration by showing that, in groups with meaningful internal hierarchies, gender rank equality is a necessary enabler of gender-based complementarities—advancing the micro-foundations of knowledge integration and dynamic capabilities.

**Practical Implication**
Managers, HR executives, and diversity professionals should recognize that simply increasing the share of women in R&D departments is insufficient and may even hamper innovation when gender rank inequality persists. To realize the innovation benefits of gender diversity, firms must also ensure proportional representation of men and women at all hierarchical levels (researchers, technicians, and support staff) within R&D. These changes should be paired with holistic, firm-wide practices to translate improved R&D capabilities into new-to-the-market product sales.

**Limitations**
The study measures macro-level innovation capabilities and cannot directly observe the micro-level group processes (e.g., psychological safety, relational conflict) through which gender diversity and rank equality shape capabilities. It captures levels rather than changes in gender rank equality, so it cannot disentangle short-run disruption effects from long-run benefits of structural change toward equality. The sales-based innovation outcome is distal and affected by many factors beyond R&D capabilities, and the secondary TIP data anonymize firms, preventing controls for variables such as TMT gender diversity. Results pertain to Spanish manufacturing R&D departments and may not generalize to other countries with different gender gaps or cultural norms.

**Future Research**
Micro-level research should examine what actually happens inside R&D departments under different combinations of gender diversity and gender rank equality, including psychological safety and additional mediating mechanisms. Longitudinal designs capturing changes in gender rank equality over time could separate short-run adjustment costs from long-run benefits. Scholars should also explore more proximal innovation indicators (e.g., patent applications), test whether the framework generalizes beyond gender to other diffuse-status diversity dimensions (race/ethnicity, age, nationality, disability), and conduct cross-country comparative studies to see how societal gender norms condition the department-level findings.
