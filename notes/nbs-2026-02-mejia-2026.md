# In Defense of an “Ethics Judgment Rule”

**Abstract**
According to the conventional wisdom among business ethicists, the “Business Judgment Rule” gives corporate leaders the discretion needed to abide by the firm’s moral obligations. In the first part of the paper, I challenge this view: managers have compelling reasons to believe that the Business Judgment Rule (and corporate law more generally) allows corporate leaders to pursue ethically motivated decisions only when these decisions are expected to be profit-enhancing. This is problematic because it instrumentalizes ethics, pushes ethically motivated corporate leaders to dissemble, and corrupts the quality of our public discourse. In response, I propose that corporate law should incorporate ethics into the Business Judgment Rule, explicitly giving managers discretion to make ethically motivated decisions that are profitsacrificing. After responding to concerns about implementing such a rule, I contend that such a rule would be an important step to put corporate ethics in its proper place.

**Research Question**
Does the Business Judgment Rule, as commonly understood in Delaware corporate law, actually give corporate leaders the discretion to pursue ethically motivated decisions that are profit-sacrificing, and, if not, how should corporate law be reformed to properly accommodate managers' moral obligations?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Legal standard of review governing managerial discretion (current BJR interpreted under shareholder value maximization, SVM, vs. proposed Ethics Judgment Rule, EJR, interpreted under ethics-constrained shareholder value maximization, E-SVM)
- DV(s): Space for ethically motivated, potentially profit-sacrificing managerial decisions; quality of public moral discourse about corporate conduct
- Mediators: Managerial perceptions of litigation risk; need to dissemble ethical motives as profit-enhancing justifications
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

Mejia argues that although the BJR nominally grants wide deference, its doctrinal tethering to SVM signals to practitioners that courts will only shield ethically motivated decisions when they can be recast as profit-enhancing. This pushes ethically minded leaders either to avoid profit-sacrificing ethical choices or to obfuscate their true motives, which in turn instrumentalizes ethics and corrupts public discourse about the role of morality in business. Incorporating an explicit Ethics Judgment Rule would replace SVM with E-SVM as the standard of review, authorizing good-faith, profit-sacrificing ethical decisions and thereby aligning legal doctrine with the genuine normative structure of corporate moral obligations.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the prevailing business-ethics consensus that the Business Judgment Rule already provides sufficient latitude for ethically motivated corporate conduct, and advances a novel doctrinal proposal—the Ethics Judgment Rule—that reframes the proper standard of managerial review from shareholder value maximization (SVM) to ethics-constrained shareholder value maximization (E-SVM). In doing so, it reconnects normative business ethics with corporate-law doctrine and reconceptualizes corporate success as requiring both profitability and moral integrity.

**Practical Implication**
Adopting an Ethics Judgment Rule would give directors, executives, and managers explicit legal cover to make profit-sacrificing ethical decisions in good faith without fear of liability, freeing them from the pressure to dissemble by recasting moral commitments as profit-enhancing. It would clarify to shareholders, stakeholders, and the broader public that ethics is a non-instrumental constraint on corporate conduct, helping to repair moral discourse in the business community and reorienting conceptions of corporate success toward companies that are both profitable and display moral integrity.

Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The paper points to the need for further doctrinal work on how an Ethics Judgment Rule could be operationalized within Delaware corporate law, including how courts should evaluate good-faith ethical motivations and how E-SVM should be reconciled with existing fiduciary duties. It also invites continued normative inquiry into the substantive content of corporations' moral obligations and how legal institutions can better articulate the mandatory, non-voluntary dimension of business ethics.
