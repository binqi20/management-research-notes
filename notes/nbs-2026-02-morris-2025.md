# How Deadly Is Financial Leverage? Evidence from Care Homes During the COVID-19 Crisis

**Abstract**
private equity firms no longer display higher COVID-19 death rates once their significantly higher leverage is controlled for.

**Research Question**
How does financial leverage — once measured accurately by consolidating corporate group structures and capitalizing operating leases — affect resident death rates in English care homes during the COVID-19 pandemic, and does this relationship explain the previously documented higher mortality in private equity–controlled care homes?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Financial leverage (simple provider leverage; simple brand leverage; simple group leverage; lease-adjusted group leverage)
- DV(s): Care home resident death rates (COVID-19 deaths and all-cause deaths) during the first and second pandemic waves
- Mediators: Cost cutting (especially on non-wage, non-rent operating expenses); reductions in cash reserves / liquidity; reductions in staffing
- Moderators: Pandemic wave (first vs. second, i.e., before vs. after government financial support); nonlinearity in leverage (effect concentrated above ~50-75% leverage); ownership type (private equity vs. family vs. other for-profit vs. charity vs. other nonprofit)

Drawing on Jensen and Meckling (1976) and the broader real-effects-of-leverage literature, the authors argue that highly leveraged operators facing an unanticipated negative shock must cut costs aggressively to preserve debt-service capacity, and in a labor- and care-intensive industry these cost cuts (on PPE, staffing, agency workers) translate directly into higher resident mortality. Because financial flexibility depends on the consolidated group's balance sheet — including off-balance-sheet operating lease obligations — leverage measured at the standalone provider level or without lease capitalization badly mismeasures the true pressure on operators. Once leverage is reconstructed at the corporate-group level with operating leases capitalized (multiplied by 13x, based on REIT comparables), highly leveraged care homes show a first-wave death rate roughly double that of unleveraged care homes, the effect disappears in the second wave (when government support was in place), and the previously observed "PE effect" on mortality is fully absorbed by PE firms' higher leverage.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes two contributions. First, it shows that accurate measurement of financial leverage — requiring consolidation at the corporate group level and capitalization of operating leases — is essential for detecting the real, non-financial externalities of debt, and that conventional provider- or brand-level, non-lease-adjusted leverage measures systematically understate financial pressure. Second, it demonstrates that the documented association between private equity ownership and worse stakeholder outcomes (care home mortality) operates primarily through the leverage channel rather than through ownership type per se, reframing the PE-and-healthcare debate as fundamentally a leverage-and-healthcare debate.

**Practical Implication**
For policy makers and regulators (e.g., the UK Care Quality Commission), the paper argues that quality-of-care regulation should be complemented by systematic monitoring of providers' consolidated, lease-adjusted financial leverage, because high leverage generates negative stakeholder externalities during crises. For prospective residents and families trying to assess a care home's financial stability, currently available public data (Companies House, regulator filings) are insufficient without substantial accounting expertise to reconstruct group-level leverage. Accounting standard setters and disclosure regimes should take seriously the distortive effect of excluding operating leases for smaller or unquoted entities.

**Limitations**
Propensity-score matching is difficult in this setting because high- and low-leverage care homes differ systematically on observables, so matches are loose. The lease-capitalization multiple (13x) is based on two UK care-home REITs and may not generalize. The analysis is confined to English care homes over a single pandemic episode, which, while offering strong identification, limits external generalizability. The leverage measure, although carefully constructed, still relies on self-reported financial accounts of varying quality (especially for smaller private entities).

**Future Research**
The authors' framing implies several directions: replicating lease-adjusted, group-level leverage analyses in other jurisdictions and sectors (especially the United States, where data on private companies and operating leases are harder to obtain) to test whether the leverage-mortality channel generalizes beyond English care homes. The broader trend toward privatization of care services across countries invites cross-national comparative work on how ownership structure and financial leverage jointly shape outcomes in essential services. Further research could also examine whether the leverage channel observed here extends to other non-financial externalities of debt (e.g., quality of care in non-crisis periods, worker safety) and whether alternative accounting treatments of operating leases materially change prior empirical findings on leverage and real outcomes.
