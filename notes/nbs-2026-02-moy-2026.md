# The effects of food insecurity on work outcomes.

**Abstract**
Despite its prevalence and importance, the ﬁeld of applied psychology has ignored this problem with the implicit assumption that food insecurity is not an issue relevant to the workplace. Contrary to this view, we hypothesize and demonstrate that food insecurity is detrimental to the three core work outcomes: task performance, work engagement, and organizational citizenship behaviors. To test our hypotheses, we employed a multimethod design across three empirical studies: (a) an experimental recall study, (b) a within-person diary study, and (c) a ﬁeld experiment. Our ﬁndings consistently demonstrate that food insecurity leads to higher anxiety, which subsequently has a negative effect on task performance, work engagement, and organizational citizenship behavior. In our ﬁeld experiment, we further demonstrated that providing food to those experiencing food insecurity can improve work outcomes. This research highlights that food insecurity is a signiﬁcant issue with important implications for the workplace that requires greater attention from both researchers and business leaders.

**Research Question**
How does food insecurity—insufficient access to safe and nutritious foods—affect core workplace outcomes (task performance, work engagement, and organizational citizenship behaviors), and through what psychological mechanism? Can providing food aid remediate these effects for food-insecure employees?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Food insecurity (manipulated via recall in Study 1; measured over four waves in Study 2; manipulated via distribution of food vs. matched nonfood packages in the Study 3 field experiment)
- DV(s): Task performance, work engagement, organizational citizenship behavior (OCB)
- Mediators: Anxiety
- Moderators: None formally hypothesized

Drawing on reinforcement sensitivity theory (Gray, 1978, 1982; Gray & McNaughton, 2003), the authors argue that food insecurity constitutes a diffuse, nonacute threat that activates the behavioral inhibition system and produces anxiety. Because cognitive capacity is limited, anxiety redirects attention toward monitoring and resolving the threat, leaving fewer attentional and cognitive resources available for work. This depleted bandwidth undermines task performance (which requires focused information processing), work engagement (which requires sustained mental immersion), and OCB (which requires noticing and responding to others' needs). Providing food aid is therefore expected to reduce anxiety and indirectly improve work outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends applied psychology into the grand challenge of food insecurity, challenging the field's implicit WEIRD-centric assumption that food is not a workplace-relevant stressor. It contributes to the food insecurity literature by moving beyond personal and physiological outcomes to show organizational consequences, building a business case for corporate engagement. It also enriches the workplace anxiety literature by identifying a stressor unique to economically disadvantaged workers struggling to meet basic physiological needs.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations have an evidence-based rationale to address employee food insecurity because it improves task performance and engagement through reduced anxiety. Employers can provide free or discounted food, partner with local food banks, host on-site pantries or community gardens, offer transportation to food banks (especially in food deserts), and educate employees about public food-assistance programs such as SNAP. Investing in employees' basic needs may yield higher returns than programs targeting higher-order needs (e.g., meditation, employee resource groups).

**Limitations**
In Study 3, only indirect (not direct) effects of food insecurity on work outcomes were significant, possibly because Pakistan's broader anxiety-inducing context diluted the food-specific signal and a single food package may have been insufficient to substantially reduce food insecurity. The studies rely heavily on self-reported measures, raising common-method concerns (though mitigated by the manipulated IV in Study 3 and within-person design in Study 2). The active nonfood control condition in Study 3 may have created expectancy effects. The research examined only three focal outcomes and did not capture other work-relevant consequences such as turnover or absenteeism.

**Future Research**
Future work should probe boundary conditions of food insecurity's effect on OCB—e.g., whether food-insecure workers extend more citizenship toward similarly situated colleagues than toward others. Researchers could test alternative or additional mediating mechanisms beyond anxiety, such as negative affect or regulatory focus, to map a more complete nomological network. Field experiments should also explore dose-response effects by varying the number and size of food packages, and examine downstream outcomes like turnover and absenteeism.
