# A CEO-Driven Process Model of Firm Responsiveness to Secondary Stakeholder Demands: The Roles of CEO Values and Passion

**Abstract**
Secondary stakeholder demands represent increasingly important strategic issues for firms. However, the principal drivers of firm response remain poorly understood. We extend research on firm responsiveness to secondary stakeholders by introducing a new theoretical perspective to detail the cognitive processes through which key CEO attributes—namely, personal values and passion—shape the trajectories of firm responsiveness. We first outline key aspects of secondary stakeholder demands to explain why CEO values and passion are important determinants of a firm’s response to secondary stakeholder demands. We then explain how a CEO’s values and passion influence the process of firm responsiveness. Finally, we extend our arguments to illustrate how values and passion-infused responses may influence social (dis)approval among key organizational stakeholders. By focusing on the cognitive processes of CEOs, we offer novel theoretical contributions to stakeholder theory, strategic leadership research, and the literature on firm social evaluations.

**Research Question**
What is the process by which a CEO's values and passion shape firm responsiveness to secondary stakeholder demands, and how do firms—and specifically CEOs—come to construct and enact divergent responses to similar, if not identical, secondary stakeholder demands?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): CEO personal values (Schwartz's self-transcendence, self-enhancement, openness to change, conservation dimensions) and CEO passion (harmonious vs. obsessive) toward the issue domain of a secondary stakeholder demand
- DV(s): Firm responsiveness to secondary stakeholder demands (degree of accommodation vs. denial and commitment to the chosen response posture); downstream social (dis)approval from primary stakeholders and other audiences
- Mediators: Socio-cognitive processes of stakeholder salience perception — field of vision, selective perception, and interpretation of the demand's moral relevance and urgency; identification/dis-identification with the stakeholder cause
- Moderators: Situational weakness of the secondary stakeholder context (ambiguous norms, limited dependencies, unclear strategic implications); congruence between CEO values and the values encoded in the stakeholder demand; harmonious vs. obsessive form of passion

The model argues that because secondary stakeholder demands arise in situationally weak, value-laden, and emotionally charged contexts, CEOs' personal values and passion — rather than purely rational stakeholder-salience calculations — drive firm responsiveness. Values determine which demands CEOs attend to and deem salient (what they engage with and why), while passion determines the intensity, persistence, and emotional investment with which CEOs engage (how). Harmonious passion tends to produce flexible, reflectively committed responses, whereas obsessive passion can yield rigid, defensive, or escalatory responses even when detrimental to the firm. Because audiences interpret responses through the lens of the leader, values- and passion-infused responses shape divergent social (dis)approval outcomes across otherwise similarly situated firms (multifinality).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions. First, it enriches stakeholder theory by incorporating top-executive characteristics — specifically CEO values and passion — into the conceptualization of when and whether a secondary stakeholder demand is perceived as salient and acted upon, moving beyond frameworks developed primarily for primary stakeholders. Second, it advances strategic leadership and Upper Echelons Theory by disentangling values from cognitive bases and specifying the distinctive socio-cognitive and motivational processes through which values (what CEOs engage with) and passion (how intensely they engage) jointly shape executive choice. Third, it offers a micro-foundational lens on firm social (dis)approval by explaining how CEO values and passion shape both firm responses and how audiences interpret those responses, accounting for why similarly situated firms can elicit divergent social evaluations.

**Practical Implication**
Strategic leaders should cultivate greater self-awareness of their natural tendencies to inject personal values and passion into decision-making about secondary stakeholder demands, because such reflexivity can help them evaluate these demands more effectively and avoid responses inconsistent with firm objectives or primary-stakeholder priorities. Boards and top management teams should monitor the extent to which a CEO's personal value commitments and obsessive versus harmonious passions are driving firm responses, and create processes for surfacing value-based assumptions before commitments become hard to reverse. Training and coaching interventions can help executives distinguish harmonious from obsessive passion so that value-driven responses remain flexible and compatible with broader firm strategy.

**Future Research**
The authors call for empirical tests of the model using CEO primary data (e.g., Schwartz Value Survey; Vallerand et al.'s passion scale; Cardon et al.'s entrepreneurial passion measure adapted to CEOs), as well as AI-based measurement approaches that apply NLP, sentiment trajectory modeling, and high-arousal language detection to CEO speeches, interviews, earnings calls, and social media to infer values and passion at scale. They suggest verbal protocol ("think aloud") studies in which CEOs or proxies respond to realistic secondary-stakeholder scenarios, and laboratory experiments in which participants assuming stakeholder roles evaluate alternative response postures to trace social (dis)approval and competitive-dynamics consequences. Future work could also examine how CEOs' personal values form a basis for firms' dynamic political management capabilities and non-market competitive advantage.
