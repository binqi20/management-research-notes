# Leadership and the Virtue of Humanity: Conceptual Clarity, Systematic Review, and Future Research Agenda

**Abstract**
Humanity – the virtue enabling meaningful human connection – is vital to the leadership we need to survive our polycrisis context. As a prerequisite to sustainable human community, the virtue of humanity is considered universal. It has been claimed as a 'higher-order virtue', comprised of and enacted by – but irreducible to – a suite of 'lower-order virtues' such as compassion and kindness. This claim is evident in widely-adopted virtues catalogues, but we lack consensus on how humanity is defined, and which attributes qualify as its virtuous dimensions. To address this, we develop a robust definition of humanity and apply criteria distilled from virtue ethics to theorize the lower-order virtues that comprise its virtuous dimensions. We thus determine the virtuous dimensions of humanity include empathy, compassion, forgiveness, love, kindness, and generosity. Our processual model illustrates how, when expressed by leaders and followers, these virtuous dimensions represent the fullest expression of the higher-order virtue of humanity and sustain human community. Our systematic review details what is known empirically about each virtuous dimension of humanity in relation to leadership. Drawing on this, we propose a research agenda to advance understanding of how the virtue of humanity might enable the leadership needed today and into the future.

**Research Question**
How should the higher-order virtue of humanity be conceptually defined, which lower-order virtues qualify as its virtuous dimensions, and what is empirically known about each of these dimensions in relation to leadership?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Humanity is a universal, higher-order virtue comprised of — but irreducible to — a set of lower-order virtuous dimensions that together enable meaningful human connection and sustain human community.
- Key constructs: empathy (primarily affective/cognitive), compassion, forgiveness, love (equally affective, cognitive, and behavioral), kindness, and generosity (primarily behavioral).
- Criteria (derived from Aristotelian virtue ethics) used to qualify a lower-order virtue: it must be inherently good, habituated, expressed in relation to others, and represent a golden mean between opposing vices.
- Processual model: leaders' and followers' expression of the six virtuous dimensions mutually shapes the relational co-creation of leadership, which in turn sustains human community (its telos).
- Boundary conditions: situations may demand other higher-order virtues (e.g., courage, justice); the effect of humanity on leader-follower relations is expected to vary with context.

The paper's processual argument is that when leaders and followers reciprocally enact empathy, compassion, forgiveness, love, kindness, and generosity, these dimensions operate in concert rather than isolation, producing the fullest expression of humanity as a higher-order virtue. Grounded in Aristotelian virtue ethics and Newstead's (2022) higher-order virtues framework, the authors argue that humanity — as the golden mean between cruelty and sycophantism — is habituated through practice and is uniquely suited to sustaining organizations through polycrisis because it is the virtue of meaningful human connection itself.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper resolves conceptual ambiguity around the virtue of humanity by providing a robust definition, theorizing humanity as a higher-order (meta) virtue, and distilling virtue-ethics criteria for determining which lower-order virtues qualify as its virtuous dimensions. It advances a processual model of leader and follower humanity and, through a PRISMA 2020 systematic review of 60 empirical studies, consolidates what is known about each dimension in relation to leadership and articulates a structured future research agenda.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that managers and HR practitioners should consider the virtuous dimensions of humanity in recruitment, selection, promotion, career development, training, and performance management — for example, by hiring, rewarding, and promoting individuals who demonstrate the capacity to lead in ways that express empathy, compassion, forgiveness, love, kindness, and generosity, because doing so promises to help sustain organizations through turbulent times and for the long term.

**Limitations**
The authors identify three limitations in the body of reviewed research: a dearth of empirical work on several dimensions (love, kindness, and especially generosity, for which no empirical leadership studies were found), an overreliance on cross-sectional and self-report data prone to social desirability bias, and a pronounced leader-centrism that neglects follower perspectives and the relational co-creation of leadership. The review itself was restricted to published, English-language empirical articles indexed in Web of Science and relied on title-only search terms.

**Future Research**
The authors propose exploring the outcomes and mechanisms (e.g., social exchange, reciprocity norms, emotional contagion, social identity, psychological safety climate) through which the less-studied dimensions — love, kindness, and generosity — influence workplace outcomes, and addressing leader-centrism by examining follower and relational expressions of humanity. They call for longitudinal, experience-sampling, and experimental designs to move beyond cross-sectional self-report, psychometric work to develop a valid higher-order measure of humanity, articulation of boundary conditions, and replication of the theorization exercise for the other higher-order virtues (courage, justice, prudence, temperance, transcendence, wisdom).
