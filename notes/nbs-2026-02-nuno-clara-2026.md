# Investments that make our homes greener: The role of regulation

**Abstract**
We study the investments triggered by a regulatory intervention requiring rented properties to satisfy minimum energy efficiency standards.

**Research Question**
What are the magnitude, nature, and financial returns of the investments triggered by a regulatory intervention requiring privately rented properties to meet minimum energy-efficiency standards, and to what extent are landlords compensated through rents for the capital expenditures they incur?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Introduction of the UK Minimum Energy Efficiency Standard (MEES) Regulations (approved March 2015) requiring privately rented dwellings to meet minimum SAP-based energy-efficiency thresholds; pre-regulation EPC status used as an instrument for post-regulation energy efficiency
- DV(s): Retrofit investments undertaken (type, capital expenditure, energy savings, internal rate of return); property-level rents; energy-efficiency (SAP) scores; CO2 emissions / carbon footprint of properties
- Mediators: Landlords' (in)ability to pass energy-cost savings on to tenants through higher rents; choice of low-capex retrofits (low-energy lighting, main heating controls) with higher IRRs; fuel-switching behavior (e.g., electricity to gas) driven by relative fuel prices
- Moderators: Whether a property was below vs. above the pre-regulation energy-efficiency threshold (compliance bindingness); sector (private rental vs. owner-occupied); main fuel type and its carbon intensity; whether carbon emission factors are updated

Building on the energy-efficiency gap and split-incentive literatures, the paper argues that in England and Wales utilities are paid directly by tenants, so landlords cannot internalize energy-bill savings from retrofits and — absent regulation — under-invest in energy efficiency. When mandated by MEES, landlords comply by selecting low-capex retrofits with the highest IRRs, but the DiD and IVDiD estimates show that they are unable to raise rents enough to recover these costs, confirming the friction. Because EPCs score properties on a cost-based metric that combines fuel quantities and prices, regulation incentivizes shifts toward cheaper rather than cleaner fuels, so energy-efficiency gains in the rental sector do not translate into commensurate reductions in carbon emissions, especially once outdated carbon factors are used.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides large-sample evidence on the types, capital costs, and internal rates of return of residential energy-efficiency retrofits undertaken by both homeowners and landlords, informing the climate-finance and energy-efficiency-gap literatures on barriers to private investment in decarbonization. It quantifies the cash-flow (rent) effects of a major energy-efficiency regulation and shows that landlords are not compensated for their capital expenditures, clarifying the nature of regulatory risks faced by real estate investors. It further demonstrates that cost-based energy-efficiency metrics can misalign private investment with decarbonization goals, distinguishing energy-efficiency from environmental-impact improvements.

**Practical Implication**
If energy-performance certificates and regulations are to be used to tackle climate change, they should report and target carbon emissions directly rather than cost-based efficiency metrics, and carbon factors should be updated regularly to reflect the decarbonization of electricity generation. Policymakers should recognize that landlord-tenant split incentives limit the pass-through of energy savings into rents, so mandating energy-efficiency upgrades will likely impose uncompensated costs on landlords and bias their compliance toward low-capex retrofits. Interventions should also coordinate consumption-side efficiency requirements with production-side decarbonization policies.

**Limitations**
The analysis is confined to England and Wales and to the specific MEES regulatory regime, so external validity to other jurisdictions and regulatory designs is uncertain. Carbon-footprint recalculations using updated emission factors are constrained by the EPC data, which do not allow the authors to split energy consumption by fuel type for multi-fuel properties. Estimated internal rates of return rely on the EPC-projected energy savings and lifespans, which may diverge from realized outcomes.

**Future Research**
Further work could examine whether regulations that target carbon emissions directly, rather than cost-based energy-efficiency metrics, deliver larger decarbonization benefits in residential real estate. Researchers could also study the interaction between consumption-side building regulations and production-side decarbonization of electricity, and extend the rent pass-through analysis to other countries and tenure arrangements. Additional studies could assess how landlord responses evolve over longer horizons as compliance thresholds tighten.
