# Beyond Rugged Individualism?: Exploring the Resilience of Black Entrepreneurs to Chronic Racism

**Abstract**
This study investigates how the resilience process unfolds for Black entrepreneurs in the context of chronic racism, employing a novel qualitative approach that combines Group Model Building (GMB) and semi-­structured interviews with 49 Black entrepreneurs. Drawing on the socio-­ecological theory of resilience and leveraging Critical Race Theory (CRT), the research finds that resilience, shaped by the persistent nature of racism, requires ongoing adaptation rather than a return to a pre-­adversity state. This continuous adaptation can lead to the depletion of coping resources. The study also illustrates how internal and external coping mechanisms interact, showing that over-­reliance on internal coping mechanisms arises due to insufficient institutional and social support. Our research contributes to the literature on Black entrepreneurship, resilience, and race in entrepreneurship, while offering a comprehensive policy approach to both support and empower Black entrepreneurs. We advocate for decolonizing research practices that not only study but actively benefit the communities involved, fostering engaged and transformative scholarship.

**Research Question**
How do individual capacities and systemic factors interact to shape the resilience of Black entrepreneurs in the context of chronic racism, and to what effect?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Chronic racism and racism-related stress (individual, systemic, and structural); availability of institutional and social (external) support
- DV(s): Resilience process and its dynamic outcomes — financial and psychological well-being, business continuity, and cyclical entry/exit/re-entry patterns
- Mediators: Stress appraisal and coping strategies (internal coping mechanisms such as self-reliance, faith, and persistence; external coping mechanisms such as co-ethnic and diverse partnerships); resource gain and depletion dynamics
- Moderators: Level of awareness/recognition of racism; level of institutional and community support; intersectional identities (e.g., gender, immigrant status)

Drawing on the socio-ecological theory of resilience and Critical Race Theory, the authors argue that Black entrepreneurs operate within systems built on White normative standards that provide lower foundational institutional support and, combined with in-group discrimination, limit external resources. Because racism is chronic rather than episodic, entrepreneurs cannot simply "bounce back" to a pre-adversity state; instead they must engage in continuous adaptation that repeatedly draws on internal coping mechanisms (self-reliance, hard work, faith). Over-reliance on these personal resources, triggered by the insufficiency of external support, leads to resource depletion, exhaustion, and fluctuating phases of well-being and duress. Strategic partnerships (co-ethnic and cross-racial) can replenish resources and enable periodic disengagement and re-entry, rendering resilience a cyclical, dynamic process whose outcomes are both enabling and costly.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the dominant "bounce-back" conception of resilience and theorizes resilience to chronic racism as a continuous, cyclical process of adaptation in which internal and external coping resources are gained and depleted over time. By integrating critical race theory and critical race methodology with the socio-ecological theory of resilience, it foregrounds racism-related stress as a core construct in entrepreneurship research and shows how insufficient institutional and social support compels over-reliance on individual coping, exposing a grimmer, compensatory face of resilience. In doing so, it advances efforts to decolonize entrepreneurship scholarship, moving beyond race-neutral framings and offering a process model that may transfer to other groups facing chronic, identity-linked adversity.

**Practical Implication**
The authors advocate a dual-strategy policy approach that simultaneously strengthens internal capabilities and addresses systemic barriers. Internally, they recommend building Black-entrepreneur-focused business-to-business networks and cross-group mentorship and sponsorship programs, alongside financial vehicles such as credit unions, Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs), and cooperative economic practices (e.g., sousous). Systemically, they call for transparent, race-conscious policies, greater representation of Black individuals and allies in decision-making, disaggregated data on grants and loans by race, and streamlined MWBE certification and government contracting processes to remove biases; the research team also partnered with a CDFI to launch a networking and referral program with forgivable loans as a tangible benefit to participants.

**Limitations**
The sample is drawn predominantly from the Northeast United States and over-represents women, which may constrain the regional and demographic generalizability of the findings. The emergent theory of resilience to chronic racism may be transferable to other marginalized groups facing chronic, identity-linked adversity, but its applicability should not be assumed without careful contextual consideration, as marginalized groups are heterogeneous. The study did not specifically theorize the experiences of Black immigrant entrepreneurs, whose relationship to racism and community support differs from that of African American participants.

**Future Research**
Future work should conduct similar socio-ecological, decolonizing studies with other marginalized groups (e.g., Latinx, women, refugee, or low-income entrepreneurs) to test the transferability of the process model and identify group-specific dynamics. Researchers should examine Black immigrant entrepreneurs in greater depth, including how ethnic-enclave community support reduces reliance on individual coping, and explore other intersectional identities. Empirical testing of specific elements of the resilience process model — particularly the compensatory dynamics between internal and external coping resources — could refine the theory and inform more tailored interventions.
