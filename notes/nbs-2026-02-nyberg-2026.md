# Carbon Populism and Representative Politics: On Why Fossil Fuel Firms Speaking for ‘The People’ Is a Bad Idea

**Abstract**
Despite growing recognition that countries around the world must transition to a low-carbon economy, global greenhouse gas emissions continue to rise. One way that decarbonization has been obstructed, we argue, is by fossil fuel firms intentionally conflating their agenda with ‘the people’, evoking notions of national identity, security and prosperity. We develop the concept of carbon populism to explain why big oil, coal and gas firms make representative claims typically associated with democratically elected governments. This adds a significant layer of difficulty for stakeholders endeavouring to enact decarbonization. Drawing on an in-depth study of fossil fuel industries in Australia (mainly coal and gas), we demonstrate how different forms of political representation in the public and political spheres influence national identity, expert knowledge and public policy. These artificial forms of representation legitimize fossil fuel industries’ political activities, authorize public policies that benefit it, and enable the industry to enjoy national dominance. We conclude by arguing that decarbonization can be achieved by rejecting carbon populism in favour of inclusive and participatory democratic representation.

**Research Question**
How do industries seek to represent ‘the people’ and what are the implications for political decision-making? Specifically, how do fossil fuel industries in Australia construct and represent ‘the people’ in (a) the political sphere where collective and binding decisions are made, and (b) the public sphere where common concerns are deliberated?

**Mechanism Process**
- Key constructs: Carbon populism; representative claim-making (Saward's maker-subject-object-audience schema); political sphere vs. public sphere; national identity; artificial/hegemonic representation.
- Forms of representation in the public sphere: (1) Simulated representation — industry acts ‘as’ the people (e.g., Glencore-funded astroturfing campaigns like ‘Energy in Australia’); (2) Symbolic representation — industry acts ‘on behalf of’ the people through advertising, public relations blitzes, and regional jobs narratives (e.g., the anti-carbon-tax campaign); (3) Staged representation — sympathetic media identifies the industry as standing for the nation.
- Forms of representation in the political sphere: (4) Claims about what counts as legitimate expertise and knowledge; (5) Authorization of policies favourable to the industry; (6) Institutionalized revolving-door and lobbying practices that reinforce industry voice.
- Process / loop: These six representations form a self-reinforcing loop — public-sphere claims about national identity legitimize the industry's political-sphere activity, which authorizes public policy, which in turn enables continued representative claim-making.
- Outcomes: Construction of ‘who we are’ (identity), ‘what we know’ (knowledge), and ‘what we decide to do’ (policy) around carbon-intensive interests; exclusion of citizen participation and voice; hegemonic (rather than legitimate) industry dominance over climate politics.

The central process is performative: by making claims that equate the fossil fuel industry with ‘the people’ and the Australian nation, firms and their allies manufacture an aggregated ‘unity of the people’ that mirrors industry interests. This artificial representation derives from hegemony rather than democratic legitimacy (Laclau and Mouffe), because citizens are neither the formal authorizers of nor accountable audiences for industry's claims. Once industry interests are rendered equivalent with the national interest, the need for extensive overt lobbying diminishes — decarbonization is structurally obstructed without overt prohibition.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces the concept of carbon populism — a form of populism in which the future of the nation is discursively and materially bound to carbon-intensive industries — and advances three contributions to management scholarship: (i) it extends Political Corporate Social Responsibility (PCSR) by explaining why firms cannot act as legitimate democratic representatives, since their ‘representative’ claims exclude citizen participation and rest on hegemony rather than legitimacy; (ii) it advances Corporate Political Activity (CPA) research by surfacing how artificial representation across public and political spheres shapes national identity and policy expertise, producing negative democratic externalities; and (iii) it contributes to emerging debates on corporate populism and nationalism and to political-theory discussions of the ‘constructivist turn’ by foregrounding corporate actors as claim-makers largely absent from those literatures.

**Practical Implication**
The authors recommend two measures to combat carbon populism and strengthen representative democracy for decarbonization. First, in the political sphere, expand citizen inclusion through public forums, citizen panels, and climate assemblies (e.g., the French Citizens' Convention for Climate), and devolve power to municipal councils, cooperatives, referenda, and recall elections. Second, in the public sphere, develop alternative digital platforms — not owned by private firms or single individuals — to support public consultation, online voting, real-time policy feedback, and ‘people's platforms’ for broad-based e-democracy. Together these measures would limit the industry's capacity to manufacture an ‘aggregated unity of the people’ that underwrites fossil fuel expansion.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The authors suggest extending the concept of carbon populism to other industries where industry prosperity is rendered equivalent to the national interest — including emerging ‘AI populism’ among US tech firms framing AI leadership as central to national identity amid US-China competition, as well as steel and other sectors facing foreign competition or legislative threats. They also call for further interrogation of the power relations underlying representative politics: who speaks for whom, why, and how social-media algorithms enable agents to reproduce representative claims from unwitting claim-makers.
