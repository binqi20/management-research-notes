# Guest Editors’ Introduction: Redefining Organizational Ethics Through the Lens of Life-and-Death

**Abstract**
We share the world we live and die in with others, in ways that are organized and disorganized. The authors of this special issue address life-and-death as a compound term, foregrounding the vital and deadly outcomes of (dis)organization and their (business) ethics implications as they play out in the context of growing inequalities and ongoing health, geopolitical, environmental, refugee crises and egregious war crimes. Organizations and organizing can shape such contexts by engaging in the ethics of care and politics of inclusivity, redefining “essential” or “front line” work, managing relationships between bodily health and work, or ethically relating to nonhuman forms of life. Considering the roles of organizations in terms of life-and-death can help scholars redefine organizations and/in/for/with the world by stressing the ethical dimensions of organizing for life which involves human and other-thanhuman relatedness and the obligation of care for all forms of life.

The guest editors advance a normative framing rather than a causal model. They argue that organizing is never ethically neutral: routines, metrics, and rhetorics of efficiency distribute livability and non-livability across human and other-than-human actors, shaping who is enabled to flourish and who is "left to die" or consigned to "slow death." They propose that an organizational ethics of life-and-death must hold two poles in tension — the existential givenness of mortality and the political contingency of how lives are organized — and must be grounded in care, relationality, and collective solidarity rather than in shareholder or narrowly stakeholder logics. The essay uses this frame to read the six articles of the special issue as illustrations of how organizational practices, discourses, memes, bodies, and multispecies entanglements materially produce vital or deadly outcomes.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The introduction reframes organizational and business ethics around "life-and-death" as a compound existential, collective, and posthuman category, extending Islam and Greenwood's (2021) call for a substantive (rather than narrow misconduct-focused) business ethics. Drawing on ethics of care, feminist relational thought, necropolitics, and more-than-human theorizing, the authors argue for an ethics that bridges micro-existential experience and macro-political organization, centers vulnerability and interdependence, and extends moral consideration beyond shareholders and even stakeholders to include nonhuman and not-yet-present others. They position this as a programmatic redefinition of the boundaries of business ethics in an era of intersecting ecological, geopolitical, and humanitarian crises.

**Practical Implication**
The editors urge business ethics scholars, organizations, and managers to treat life-and-death stakes as core rather than peripheral to organizational decisions, resisting the sanitizing vocabularies of efficiency, "wellness," and neutral measurement that obscure who lives and who dies as a result of organizing. They call for organizing practices centered on care, relationality, collective solidarity, and multispecies accountability, and for visibilizing (rather than tabooing) death as part of ethical organizational life. Academia itself is implicated and should reflect on how its own infrastructures participate in life-and-death processes.

**Future Research**
The editors sketch a research agenda organized around three lines of inquiry: principles of care and concern, collective solidarity, and accountability and relationality, including posthuman and multispecies extensions. They invite future work that broadens the geographies, voices, and scopes of the conversation, interrogates how measurement and datafication reconfigure life-and-death stakes, examines the role of academia in (re)producing livability, and develops organizational ethics capable of affirming life in the face of genocide, ecological collapse, and normalized "business as usual" forms of slow death.
