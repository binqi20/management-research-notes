# A stakeholder theory perspective on digital product passport implementation

**Abstract**
The Digital Product Passport (DPP) is expected to contribute to industry-wide sustainable development and digital transformation. However, implementing the DPP poses several risks, and non-compliance can lead to penalties and competitive disadvantages. This paper examines the requirements for DPP implementation from multiple perspectives by drawing on literature and an evaluation study. During the analysis, we assign stakeholders to DPP requirements through stakeholder mapping, identify necessary responsibilities, and facilitate collaboration to roll out DPPs. Following our qualitative analysis, we identified 315 requirements and mapped them to 14 specified stakeholder groups. The stakeholders and requirements can be found in our requirement catalog. Furthermore, we provide an overview of our requirement mapping to facilitate the usage of the requirement catalog. Overall, our study advances DPP research and practice by delivering nine explicit pathways to address uncertainties related to the DPP requirements. For instance, we elaborate on the need for DPP support for SMEs and the exploration of DPP definitions. The proposed requirement catalog informs about essential stakeholder groups and activities for implementing the DPP. It helps practitioners craft compliant DPPs and indicates the current boundaries of DPP regulation for policymakers.

**Research Question**
What are the stakeholder-specific requirements to implement Digital Product Passports?

**Mechanism Process**
- Data sources: EU regulatory texts (ESPR, EBR) and 41 academic DPP papers as the corpus from which candidate requirements are identified
- Gioia coding phases: 985 codes abstracted into first-order concepts (requirements), second-order themes (58 requirement categories), and aggregated dimensions
- Stakeholder classifications: 14 specified stakeholder groups grouped into three categories used by the paper — Regulatory Stakeholders (e.g., European Commission, National Authorities), Business Stakeholders (e.g., Economic Operator, Dealer, DPP Service Provider), and General Stakeholders (addressing all groups)
- Expert evaluation: eight DPP practitioners scored the interim mapping on a five-point Likert questionnaire adapted from Prat et al. (2015), leading to refinements of requirements and categories
- Emergent artifact: a requirement catalog and stakeholder–requirement map (visualized as a pie-chart wheel) that surfaces DPP-invisible groups (e.g., media, stake-watchers) where regulation is silent

Drawing on stakeholder theory, the authors read ESPR and EBR as a stakeholder-specific requirement system in which each interest group holds distinct responsibilities, capabilities, and risks for DPP rollout. By iteratively coding regulatory and literature passages into first-order concepts, consolidating them into requirement categories, and validating the structure with industry experts, the analysis produces 315 requirements assigned to 14 stakeholder groups across the three classifications. The resulting catalog operationalizes inter-organizational collaboration, shows where the Economic Operator bears the bulk of obligations, and marks gaps where the regulation currently leaves stakeholder requirements unspecified.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances DPP research by using stakeholder theory to systematically map a fragmented requirements space, showing that legal text (ESPR, EBR) can be read as a stakeholder-specific requirement system in which each group holds distinct responsibilities. It extends stakeholder theory applications to digital-sustainability technologies by specifying how stakeholders demand value in return for meeting regulatory requirements and by identifying DPP-invisible groups (e.g., media, stake-watchers) whose requirements are currently missing. The resulting requirement catalog provides a structured artifact that bridges stakeholder theory, information-systems requirements engineering, and circular-economy policy.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers can use the catalog to refine upcoming ESPR acts, clarify vague requirements (e.g., General Stakeholder obligations), and design incentives and enforcement tools, particularly for SMEs that lack resources to comply. Practitioners, especially Economic Operators, gain transparency over the DPP requirements and stakeholder map, helping them identify capability gaps, plan collaboration with dealers and service providers, and communicate obligations along the value chain. The catalog thereby supports compliant DPP design and guides investments in data-sharing infrastructure.

**Limitations**
The requirements were derived through manual investigation and subjective interpretation of the ESPR, which text mining could complement to improve coverage and objectivity. The Gioia coding method introduces researcher subjectivity in forming categories, mitigated here by three-person cross-review. The study focuses on the already-published ESPR and EBR, so later sector-specific working plans and additional product regulations fall outside its snapshot, and the evaluation involved only eight experts.

**Future Research**
Future work should broaden the evaluation with more experts and additional stakeholder interviews (e.g., consumers) to surface requirements for DPP-invisible groups such as media and stake-watchers. Researchers should consolidate existing DPP definitions, develop judicial and support tools (especially for SMEs), and investigate how emerging technologies like artificial intelligence and blockchain can support DPP data management. Examining upcoming delegated acts and sector working plans can extend the requirement catalog as the DPP regulation matures.
