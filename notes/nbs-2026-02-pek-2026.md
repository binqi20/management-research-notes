# Advancing Employee Voice Through Insights From Democratic Innovations

**Abstract**
Employee voice has long attracted interest from academics, policymakers, and practitioners in light of its myriad benefits. To date, employee voice researchers have identified a wide array of voice practices that are organized and understood through a core set of dimensions. However, there are growing calls for more interdisciplinary research to ensure the field's repertoire of practices is effective in terms of addressing employees' and firms' needs while also being attuned to the contemporary nature of work, which includes increasing concerns about inclusion and employee polarization. In this conceptual paper, we seek to expand the field's understanding of existing and potential voice practices by considering whether and how core dimensions and families of practices from democratic innovations scholarship have been applied in employee voice research. We identify four understudied dimensions and three understudied families of practices, illustrate how they could be applied in the context of employee voice, and articulate contributions to research and practice.

**Research Question**
Whether and how have the core dimensions and families of practices in Elstub and Escobar's (2019a) framework of democratic innovations been applied in employee voice research, and how could underused dimensions and families expand the field's repertoire of voice practices to address contemporary challenges like inclusion and employee polarization?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: Employee voice and democratic innovations share an "ineliminable core" — improving opportunities for individuals to influence decision-making — so the political-science typology of democratic innovations can productively extend the voice field's conceptual repertoire.
- Key constructs (dimensions): Six existing/emerging voice dimensions (depth, level, scope, form, formalization, reach) are complemented by four understudied dimensions imported from democratic innovations — participant selection method, stage of the policy process, mode of participation, and mode of decision-making.
- Key constructs (families of practices): Collaborative governance already overlaps with voice; three understudied families — deliberative mini-publics, participatory budgeting, and referenda and citizen initiatives — are theorized as new or reimagined voice practices.
- Boundary conditions: Firms differ from polities in the legitimation of authority (ownership-based hierarchy vs. democratic rights), and adoption is shaped by managers' cost-benefit assessments, employees' perceptions of efficacy, and the institutional setting (CME vs. LME varieties of capitalism).

The paper's central process is conceptual translation: it maps Elstub and Escobar's seven-dimension framework onto the voice literature, identifies points of overlap and neglect, and argues that attending to the four understudied dimensions (selection, stage, participation mode, decision-making mode) reveals how the three understudied families could be adapted for workplaces. The authors illustrate applications — for example, using stratified random selection to improve "reach," using deliberative mini-publics to address complex or polarizing organizational issues, using participatory budgeting for benefits or philanthropic allocations, and using binding referenda and citizen initiatives for high-stakes decisions — while arguing that these adaptations jointly improve both epistemic quality of decisions and inclusion of marginalized voices.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances a more comprehensive framework of employee voice by adding four understudied dimensions — participant selection method, stage of the policy process, mode of participation, and mode of decision-making — to the six dimensions already prominent in voice research, yielding an expanded ten-dimension framework that highlights both independent and interdependent design choices. It further introduces three families of democratic innovation practices (deliberative mini-publics, participatory budgeting, and referenda and citizen initiatives) as underused voice practices, and positions democratic innovations as a productive interdisciplinary resource for addressing voice failures, inclusion, and employee polarization.

**Practical Implication**
Managers can use the expanded ten-dimension framework as a diagnostic tool to assess their current suite of voice practices and redesign them — for example, adding deliberative modes of participation and decision-making to town-hall meetings, or switching from self-selection to stratified random selection to improve reach across a diverse workforce. Policymakers can draw on the understudied families of practices when updating statutory voice provisions, using "hard" mandates (e.g., referenda for mergers and acquisitions) or "soft" incentives and funding supports to diffuse practices such as participatory budgeting and binding initiatives, particularly in coordinated market economies where public support for voice is stronger.

**Limitations**
The paper is explicitly conceptual and illustrative rather than empirical, so the proposed applications of mini-publics, participatory budgeting, and referenda to employee voice are not tested. The authors note space constraints that forced them to foreground only particularly salient contemporary issues (inclusion and polarization) and acknowledge that important differences between firms and polities — especially around ownership-based authority and managerial discretion in non-statutory settings — mean democratic-innovation insights must be carefully translated rather than directly transplanted into workplace contexts.

**Future Research**
The authors call for empirical research that investigates how different selection methods shape employees' perceptions of representatives' autonomy and legitimacy, and how shifts in mode of participation (e.g., from polling to deliberation) affect outcomes like organizational creativity and job satisfaction. They also encourage work that leverages the four understudied dimensions and three understudied families of practices to study relationships with longstanding voice outcomes, and that examines the institutional conditions (CME vs. LME, statutory regimes) under which these practices diffuse and achieve their intended effects.
