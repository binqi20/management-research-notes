# Are they really just words? Investigating the prevalence and impact of physical disability biased language in job advertisements.

**Abstract**
language is used in recruitment, it could impact application rates of diverse and qualiﬁed applicants. This article examines the prevalence and impact of biased (ableist) and inclusive (equal employment opportunity [EEO] and accommodation statements) language on individuals with and without disabilities. In Study 1, a content analysis of 1886 job ads found that ableist language was present in 84.09% of the job ads, whereas EEO and accommodation statements were only present in 19.25% and 9.28% of job ads, respectively. Study 2 examined the effect of biased and inclusive language and found that individuals with and without disabilities were (a) negatively affected by ableist language and (b) positively affected by EEO and accommodation statements. Ableist language was associated with lower perceptions of personorganization ﬁt and intentions to apply. In contrast, EEO and accommodation statements were associated with higher intentions to apply. Accommodation statements were also associated with increased ﬁt perceptions. These effects were more pronounced for individuals with disabilities. Study 3 sought to replicate these results while manipulating the desirability of jobs through pay levels. Ableist language continued to have a negative impact on ﬁt and likelihood to apply, and EEO and accommodation statements maintained a positive impact. However, unlike Study 2, these effects were not stronger for those with physical disabilities. This research demonstrates the negative effects of ableist language in recruitment materials and the positive effect of EEO and accommodation statements.

**Research Question**
How prevalent is ableist (disability-biased) language in job advertisements, and how do ableist language and inclusive cues (EEO and accommodation statements) affect perceived person-organization fit and likelihood to apply among individuals with and without physical disabilities, including when job desirability (pay) varies?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Ableist language in job ads; EEO statements; accommodation statements; pay level (Study 3)
- DV(s): Perceived person-organization (P-O) fit; likelihood/intentions to apply
- Mediators: Not formally modeled
- Moderators: Physical disability status of the applicant

Grounded in signaling theory (Spence, 1973), the authors argue that because job seekers work under imperfect information, the specific words used in job ads act as signals about the organization's culture, values, and the kinds of people who "fit." Ableist phrasing (e.g., "fast paced," "stand out in a crowd") signals that individuals with physical disabilities may not belong, lowering perceived P-O fit and intentions to apply, whereas EEO and accommodation statements signal inclusivity and raise both fit perceptions and application likelihood. These effects are expected to be amplified for individuals with disabilities, who are more attuned to such cues, and may be only partially offset by otherwise desirable job features such as higher pay.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper provides a seminal investigation of ableist language in recruitment materials, extending prior work on gendered and ageist job-ad wording to physical disability, and documents that ableist cues are both pervasive and consequential. It advances signaling theory by showing that subtle, likely unintentional signals of bias in everyday job-ad wording can carry as much weight as more overt signals of inclusivity (EEO and accommodation statements) and can rival the influence of pay on applicant reactions.

**Practical Implication**
Recruiters and organizations should audit job advertisements for ableist wording and substitute more inclusive phrasing while still accurately describing job requirements, because such language deters both disabled and non-disabled applicants from applying. Adding EEO and accommodation statements, which remain rare in practice, is a low-cost way to raise perceived fit and intentions to apply. Higher pay does not fully compensate for the negative effects of ableist language, so pay-based strategies alone cannot offset biased wording.

**Limitations**
The ableist word dictionary was developed with subject-matter experts with lived experience of physical disability but is not exhaustive and may overlap with age- or health-related bias; the ableist experimental conditions contained a large dose of ableist language, making it hard to test smaller, more realistic amounts. Pay manipulation checks were imperfect, the likelihood-to-apply and P-O fit measures may conceptually overlap, and the experimental context cannot capture real-world pressures such as the need to secure income. The authors also could not fully explain why participants without disabilities were affected by ableist cues.

**Future Research**
Researchers should refine and extend the ableist-language dictionary to cover other disabilities and test thresholds for how much ableist language is needed to produce negative effects. Future studies could use more differentiated measures of P-O fit and likelihood to apply, stronger pay manipulations (e.g., explicit comparisons to median salary), and field or longitudinal designs that capture real job-search constraints. Further work is also needed to unpack the mechanisms by which ableist language affects applicants without disabilities.
