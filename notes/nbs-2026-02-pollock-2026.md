# When Opposites Don’t Attract: The New Information Environment, Polarization, and What Happens to Social Evaluations When ‘The World Goes to Hell in a Handbasket’

**Abstract**
Organizations today operate in a new information environment, where changing technologies have enabled fundamentally different forms and pathways of information creation and dissemination while also generating unintended consequences – chief among them increased polarization. In combination, the new information environment and polarization have profoundly altered the social processes underpinning social evaluations. To animate social evaluations research on these issues, we consider how information environment shifts and increased polarization affect the value and durability of five social evaluation types – legitimacy, reputation, status, celebrity/infamy, and stigma/esteem. We highlight the implications for both theorizing about and empirically studying social evaluations in an ever more polarized world.

**Research Question**
How do shifts in the new information environment and increased polarization alter the value and durability of five types of social evaluations — legitimacy, reputation, status, celebrity/infamy, and stigma/esteem — and what does this mean for future theorizing and empirical study of social evaluations?

**Mechanism Process**
- Core argument: The new information environment (multi-directional, many-to-many, without editorial gatekeepers) plus algorithm-driven polarization fragment the shared sociocognitive content on which social evaluations depend.
- Key constructs: new information environment; polarization / echo chambers; value and durability of social evaluations; five social evaluation types (legitimacy, reputation, status, celebrity/infamy, stigma/esteem).
- Boundary conditions: effects vary by social-evaluation type because each draws on different sociocognitive content (cognitive-normative-regulative for legitimacy; rational assessments for reputation and status; emotions for celebrity/infamy; moral judgments for stigma/esteem).
- Emergent outcomes: societal-level value and durability of rational, consensus-based evaluations (legitimacy, reputation, status) erode while subgroup-level value and durability may intensify; emotion- and morality-based evaluations (celebrity/infamy, stigma/esteem) amplify within polarized in-groups and intensify against out-groups.

The process the authors articulate is that consensus-dependent evaluations collapse when audiences no longer share information sources, facts, or values, so aggregate evaluations give way to simultaneous, opposing subgroup evaluations. Because celebrity, infamy, stigma, and esteem are grounded in emotion and moral judgment rather than analytical assessment, polarization's constant emotional supply makes them more durable and more extreme within polarized communities, while the tribal nature of polarization also means the same actor can simultaneously attract celebrity from one group and infamy from another.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The essay contributes to social evaluations research by foregrounding how the traditional and new information environments differ, and by articulating how the resulting polarization reshapes what is — and is not — different in theorizing and empirically studying social evaluations. It calls for moving legitimacy scholarship from a consensus to a conflict model, for rethinking whether "generalized" favourability in reputation remains possible, and for developing esteem as a positive moral analogue to stigma.

**Practical Implication**
The authors signal that organizations can no longer be managed as if audiences were an undifferentiated "general public"; instead, managers and scholars must recognize that actors can simultaneously hold opposing social evaluations with different polarized audiences, and that attention-grabbing and emotional intensification reward the loudest and most extreme voices. Navigating polarized and fragmented social landscapes requires attending to specific audiences rather than averaged assessments.

**Future Research**
The authors propose three priorities: (1) broaden social evaluations' theoretical foundations by integrating values psychology (e.g., Schwartz value framework) and individual/group identity research; (2) revise sample construction and measurement by abandoning central-tendency aggregation, adopting spectrum or stratified sampling, and capturing evaluations within specific audiences and time periods; and (3) attend to context, language, and generalization through ethnographic and digital-ethnographic methods that illuminate how vocabularies and frames shape social evaluations in different polarized communities.
