# Is Gen Z willing to pay for sustainability? An advanced market segmentation analysis

**Abstract**
Purpose – The purpose of this study is to develop a segmentation model that uncovers distinct segments of Gen Z respondents based on their perceptions related to sustainability issues, with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as a framework, along with willingness to pay a premium for sustainable products. Design/methodology/approach – Drawing from two national samples of Gen Z individuals and utilizing state-of-the-art econometrics techniques – Finite Mixture Modeling – the authors apply perceptions of the importance of SDGs and related societal issues, along with demographic and psychographic factors including willingness to pay a premium for a brand that makes the world a better place, across two longitudinal studies to determine differentiated segments of Gen Z individuals. Findings – These findings explore Gen Z’s perspectives related to sustainability with implications for managers and policy makers. Results reveal segmentation models with two segments of Gen Z consumers with differing model characteristics across the two studies. Findings highlight how changes in public sentiment can substantially alter the characteristics of consumer segments over relatively short periods of time, posing challenges for traditional assumptions about segmentation model generalizability and stability. Originality/value – As demand for sustainable goods and services grows, brands continue to incorporate sustainability and social responsibility within their marketing practices. Existing research supports the rationale (market-, policyor ethics-based reasons) for firms to take a leadership role in addressing important environmental and societal issues. However, there remains a gap in research that explores perceptions related to sustainability and social justice initiatives from a segmentation perspective related to Gen Z consumers. This study reveals the impact of recent socio-political shifts in sentiment on the generalizability and stability of segmentation models. It contributes to existing research by advancing the understanding of when and why segmentation models may need to be reassessed or adapted in dynamic cultural, social and political environments.

**Research Question**
What are the characteristics of differentiated segments of Gen Z individuals, based on demographic and psychographic factors, related to their willingness to pay a premium for brands that make the world a better place through their products or services, their stand on social justice, and support of their employees? Further, given the nature of the politically sensitive issues investigated, to what extent do recent changes in public sentiment related to sustainability and societal issues alter the characteristics of these segments?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Perceived importance of 14 United Nations Sustainable Development Goals and 12 related societal issues (e.g., climate change, LGBTQ rights, racial justice, body image, mental health); demographic and psychographic factors (gender, race, financial well-being, education)
- DV(s): Willingness to pay a price premium for brands perceived as making the world a better place through their products or services, their stand on social justice, and support of their employees
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Socio-political shifts in public sentiment between Study 1 (June 2023) and Study 2 (February 2025)

Drawing on cohort theory, the authors argue that Gen Z — having come of age during climate activism, Black Lives Matter, and #MeToo in an always-on media environment — shares values and beliefs that shape sustainability perceptions and purchase motives. Finite Mixture Modeling uncovers two latent classes in each study whose willingness to pay a premium is differentially driven by specific SDGs and societal issues. The authors invoke self-efficacy theory (Bandura, 1977) to explain why Gen Z may prefer more personal and controllable issues (e.g., LGBTQ rights) over abstract global issues (e.g., affordable clean energy). Because socio-political sentiment shifted between studies, segment composition and the mix of issues driving willingness to pay changed, challenging the stability and generalizability of segmentation models over time.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes four contributions to the marketing sustainability literature: it focuses on Gen Z as a consumer segment; incorporates an extended set of issues spanning the UN SDGs and Gen Z-relevant societal issues; reveals distinct segments of Gen Z consumers based on willingness to pay a premium for sustainable products; and assesses the impact of recent socio-political shifts in public sentiment on the generalizability and stability of the initial segmentation model. By doing so, it advances understanding of when and why segmentation models may need to be reassessed or adapted in dynamic cultural, social, and political environments.

**Practical Implication**
Marketers should recognize that Gen Z is not a one-size-fits-all segment and that the sustainability issues driving their willingness to pay a premium are sensitive to socio-political shifts. Brands should craft narrower, more personal, and locally actionable initiatives — e.g., circular-fashion messaging around climate action, size-inclusive products, accessibility features, or neighborhood-level LGBTQ and racial-justice partnerships — rather than abstract global SDG commitments. Brands targeting Gen Z should regularly reassess segmentation models and pair sustainability claims with measurable, credible commitments (e.g., supplier wage upgrades, low-carbon operations, women's advancement programs).

**Limitations**
One limitation is that the use of ranking data for the SDGs and related societal issues could limit the depth of the findings, as ranking does not capture the varying magnitudes of importance respondents attach to each issue. Study 2's usable sample (N=266) is substantially smaller than Study 1's (N=1,223), which constrains comparisons of segment composition across studies. The study relies on single-item measures for willingness to pay and financial well-being, and was conducted exclusively with U.S.-based Gen Z respondents, limiting cross-cultural generalizability.

**Future Research**
Future research could employ rating scales (rather than rankings) to capture varying levels of importance for SDGs and societal issues, and further explore the impact of socially, politically, and culturally sensitive topics on the stability of segmentation models during times of rapid change. Researchers could investigate the underlying mechanisms or mindsets driving Gen Z segment perceptions, compare attitudes across cohorts (Millennials, Baby Boomers), and replicate the study in other countries using different social-media platforms such as Instagram and TikTok. Finally, applying social cognition theory — specifically self-efficacy — could illuminate the role of specific motivations influencing sustainable consumption across groups defined by age, gender, race, and other background variables.
