# Balancing, Shielding, Filtering: Three Models of Role Morality

**Abstract**
How does a role—whether in business, law, government, or some other institution— change what is morally permissible or obligatory? Here I present three options and argue for the third. On the balancing model, a role simply gives its occupant additional normative reasons, to be weighed against all other normative reasons. On the shielding model, a role comes with its own moral code, blocking the force of all role-external reasons. On the filtering model, a role selectively filters its occupant’s reasons for action, creating obligations or permissions to act on a narrowed range of considerations. I argue that the filtering model offers a superior analysis of the ethics of roles, including the concepts of professional integrity and discretion. I focus on three difficult cases: a nuclear safety regulator, a criminal defense lawyer, and a corporate lobbyist. I conclude by discussing the implications of the filtering model for business ethics.

**Research Question**
How does occupying a role within a social institution (business, law, government) change what is morally permissible or obligatory for its occupant, and which model of role morality—balancing, shielding, or filtering—best captures the moral structure of roles and the ethics of professional integrity and discretion?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Model of role morality (balancing vs. shielding vs. filtering); legitimacy and mandate of the role
- DV(s): Range of normative reasons on which role actors are required or permitted to act; scope of appropriate moral criticism of role actors
- Mediators: Not reported in paper
- Moderators: Whether filters of the role are obligatory vs. discretionary (strict requirement vs. permission to exclude certain first-order reasons)

Russell argues that a legitimate role functions as a set of second-order reasons that selectively filter out (rather than add to, or entirely displace) the first-order normative reasons that otherwise apply to persons. Because role actors are required or permitted to act on a narrower range of reasons, a correspondingly narrower range of moral criticisms is appropriately directed at their conduct. The filtering model explains why a nuclear safety regulator may not weigh medical-isotope supply against safety, why a defense lawyer may withhold information about buried bodies, and why a lobbyist may exercise "moral flexibility"—while still preserving space for critique when role filters are exceeded or abused.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper develops and defends the filtering model of role morality as a superior alternative to both the balancing model (which treats roles as merely adding reasons to be weighed against all others) and the shielding model (which treats roles as self-contained moral codes blocking all role-external reasons). By distinguishing obligatory from discretionary filters and articulating how roles create second-order permissions, the article provides a more expressive vocabulary for evaluating professional integrity, discretion, and hard cases in business ethics. It situates Joseph Heath's market failures approach as the leading filtering view in business ethics and shows how filtering better captures the moral experience of acting in a role than balancing-style stakeholder theories or shielding-style Friedmanite defenses.

**Practical Implication**
For business ethics, the filtering model implies that critiques of managers, lobbyists, and other role actors should be framed in terms of the narrowed range of reasons their legitimate role permits them to act on, rather than by appeal to the full range of ordinary moral considerations. It also suggests that some objectionable conduct (e.g., wage-squeezing, adversarial lobbying) may fall within the discretionary permissions of a role yet still warrant moral criticism as greedy or heartless, because discretion does not erase the ethical vocabulary that applies to persons.

**Future Research**
Russell suggests that the most promising direction is to challenge Heath's market failures approach from within the filtering model—arguing in a systematic rather than ad hoc way that a broader but still limited range of first-order reasons (such as fairness-based reasons in labor relations) should escape the second-order scope of the filters of the managerial role. More work is also needed on how to distinguish obligatory from discretionary filters in specific business roles.
