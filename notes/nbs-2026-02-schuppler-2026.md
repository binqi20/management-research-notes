# What do circular cities do? Insights from 26 case studies

**Abstract**
Cities produce the largest share of global carbon emissions yet are well-positioned to lead the circular economy (CE) transition. Research on circular cities (CC), a relatively new field, is characterized by conceptual ambiguity, limited empirical depth, and a lack of nuanced understanding of the concept. This medium-N study expands the concept of CCs, demonstrating that urban circularity is not monolithic but a continuum of locally tailored strategies. It analyzes the circular strategies of 26 cities, complemented by 18 expert interviews. Based on target sector scope and intervention scale, it identifies four CC archetypes: niche community optimizers (NCO), implementing promotional and educational measures in a few highimpact sectors, mainly built environment and procurement; diversified transformative enablers (DTE), pursuing innovative transformative measures across a high number of sectors, notably food, built environment, and procurement; diversified collaborative loopers (DCL), incrementally upgrading multiple “traditional” sectors through cooperation with public and private actors, notably built environment, waste, food, and procurement; and niche conformers (NC), refining existing practices mainly in the built environment and food sectors. This proposed typology advances the understanding of the CC concept in the literature to at least four archetypes and provides city managers with a tool to understand their city's position and to compare approaches with similar cities, promoting focused learning and knowledge exchange.

**Research Question**
How can cities be systematically and conceptually grouped based on the empirical reality of their CE measures? The study aims to advance theoretical understanding of circular cities by proposing an empirically grounded typology and to offer practical guidance for cities developing or strengthening their circular initiatives.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Scope of target sectors (few vs. many sectors covered by a city's circular strategy) and scale of intervention (incremental refinement vs. transformative ambition expressed through core principles, enablers, and actions)
- DV(s): City circular-city archetype classification (NCO, DTE, DCL, NC) and the corresponding pattern of CE measures (sectors, 10R principles, enablers, and actions)
- Mediators: City governance arrangements, availability of public-private cooperation, and sector-specific enablers/actions (e.g., procurement reform, materials banks, education, infrastructure)
- Moderators: Local context and resources, political leadership, external funding, and whether the city frames its strategy around higher-R (Rethink/Reduce/Reuse) or lower-R (Recycle/Recover) principles

The authors argue that because urban circularity depends on locally available resources, institutional capacities, and sectoral priorities, cities translate generic CE principles into divergent strategic configurations. By coding 26 strategies against sectors targeted, 10R principles, enablers, and actions and cross-validating them with 18 expert interviews, the authors inductively derive two orthogonal dimensions — sectoral scope and scale of intervention — that partition cities into four archetypes. The archetypes differ in which sectors they prioritize, whether they emphasize higher- or lower-R principles, and whether they proceed through incremental cooperation or transformative innovation, producing distinct pathways for translating CE principles into urban practice.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study expands the theoretical conceptualization of the circular city from a single, ambiguous construct to at least four empirically grounded archetypes, demonstrating that urban circularity is not monolithic but a continuum of locally tailored strategies. It clarifies key CC dimensions and boundaries — notably sectoral scope and scale of intervention — supporting more rigorous theoretical development in a field previously marked by conceptual ambiguity, limited empirical depth, and the absence of a structured typology.

**Practical Implication**
The four archetypes offer city managers a lens for understanding where their city stands within the broader landscape of circular transition, enabling them to benchmark their approach against similar cities and to identify transferable practices from detailed examples in the supplementary appendices. The authors also provide a self-assessment tool (Appendix A of SI 1) that allows practitioners to determine which archetype best fits their city, making strategic pathways more tangible and accessible for cities beginning to explore circularity.

**Limitations**
The analysis predominantly covers European and North American cities, limiting global representativeness of the typology. The data reflect strategic intentions expressed in publicly available documents rather than on-the-ground outcomes, so whether a city ultimately reduces resource consumption or emissions may depend on political leadership changes or external funding not captured in the strategies. An initial classification into one archetype should not be seen as prescriptive or permanent, since cities operate in dynamic contexts and may transition between archetypes.

**Future Research**
Future research should use the four archetypes as a starting point to investigate archetype-specific barriers, opportunities, and development trajectories, ultimately testing whether these strategic classifications translate into tangible resource and emission reductions. Additional work is needed to extend the typology beyond European and North American contexts and to examine transitions between archetypes over time as cities' conditions, priorities, and capacities evolve.
