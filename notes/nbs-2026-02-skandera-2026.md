# Corporate Strategy and the Portfolio-Based View of CSR: Toward a Theory of Optimal CSR Diversification

**Abstract**
Prior research suggests firms maximize the financial returns from corporate social responsibility (CSR) by focusing on how stakeholders reward their CSR investments. This demand-side view of CSR’s financial returns overlooks the costs tied to each CSR investment, neglecting that returns also depend on the efficient use of resources. We integrate insights from corporate strategy research on firm diversification with the portfolio-based view of CSR to take a step toward a theory of optimal CSR diversification that balances demand-side and supply-side considerations. Our theory treats CSR investments as a diversification problem in which managers must choose from among three investment options when allocating additional resources to CSR: CSR portfolio scaling, within-cause diversification, and cross-cause diversification. Which option yields the highest marginal financial return at any point in a CSR portfolio’s evolution depends on the diminishing marginal rewards from stakeholders and the increasing marginal diversification costs associated with each option. Crucially, demand-side (e.g., demand complementarities) and supply-side (e.g., resource fungibility) factors shape the rates at which marginal rewards and costs change over successive CSR investments. Our framework reveals new ways for firms to diversify CSR portfolios and enhance the financial returns from CSR investments by optimizing resource allocations across the initiatives comprising CSR portfolios.

**Research Question**
Given additional resources for CSR, how should managers allocate these resources within their firm's CSR portfolio to maximize the portfolio's financial performance?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Allocation choice among three CSR investment options—CSR portfolio scaling, within-cause diversification, and cross-cause diversification
- DV(s): Marginal financial returns from CSR investments (CSR portfolio financial performance)
- Mediators: Diminishing marginal stakeholder-based rewards; increasing marginal diversification costs (from resource sharing and redeployment across CSR initiatives)
- Moderators: Demand complementarities (demand-side); resource fungibility (supply-side)

Drawing on corporate diversification theory and Penrosean resource-based logic, the authors argue that CSR's financial returns depend jointly on how stakeholders reward a firm's CSR efforts (demand side) and on how efficiently the firm can leverage and redeploy resources across initiatives (supply side). As firms add CSR investments within an existing initiative, within-cause domain, or unrelated cause, stakeholder rewards diminish at the margin while diversification costs rise due to coordination, integration, and resource-reallocation frictions. The relative steepness of these reward and cost curves is shaped by demand complementarities across initiatives and by the fungibility of the firm's underlying resources, which together determine which of the three investment options yields the highest marginal financial return at each stage of a CSR portfolio's evolution. Optimal CSR diversification therefore occurs at inflection points where marginal rewards equal marginal costs, yielding a set of decision rules for orchestrating CSR portfolios.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper reframes CSR portfolio management as a diversification problem, complementing and challenging demand-centric views of CSR by integrating supply-side resource constraints and portfolio-level inefficiencies with stakeholder-based rewards. It distinguishes three CSR investment options (CSR portfolio scaling, within-cause diversification, and cross-cause diversification) and parses CSR portfolio depth into scaling versus within-cause diversification, adding granularity to prior breadth/depth conceptualizations. In doing so, it develops propositions and decision rules showing how demand complementarities and resource fungibility jointly determine when each option maximizes financial returns, opening a new "portfolio-based view of CSR" research agenda.

**Practical Implication**
Managers should approach CSR investment decisions with the same diversification logic they apply to selecting product markets—evaluating each new initiative against both expected stakeholder rewards and the costs it creates within the existing portfolio. The framework advises knowing when to scale existing initiatives, diversify within a social issue area, or diversify into a new cause; strategically sharing and redeploying resources across initiatives; and considering cross-initiative complementarities in both resource requirements and stakeholder demand. External stakeholders (investors, customers, CSR evaluators) should likewise look beyond aggregate CSR scores to assess the synergies, coherence, and resource alignments across a firm's CSR portfolio.

**Limitations**
As a conceptual paper, the framework is not empirically tested and its scope is bounded to CSR portfolio assembly, leaving CSR contractions/divestitures and dynamic portfolio management outside the model. The theorization focuses on resource fungibility as the key supply-side factor without incorporating other resource characteristics such as decomposability and scalability, and treats social issue characteristics (scale, salience, urgency, polarization) only in stylized form.

**Future Research**
Future work should empirically test the propositions and extend the framework to CSR portfolio divestitures and dynamic, intertemporal CSR portfolio management, as well as incorporate additional resource characteristics such as decomposability and non-scale-free resources that may create sharper diversification trade-offs. Researchers could also examine how social issue attributes (scale, salience, urgency, stakeholder polarization) shape the tipping points between scaling, within-cause, and cross-cause diversification, and explore the societal-level implications of CSR "division of labor" across firms with distinct resource profiles.
