# A Design for All: De-Neurotypicalizing Business Schools and Achieving Substantive Performativity

**Abstract**
Neurodivergent people experience significant disadvantages obtaining and maintaining employment. Locating our analysis at the intersection of the performativity, neurodiversity, and role of business school literatures, we argue that business schools exacerbate these issues by being designed and operated around neurotypical culture, curriculum, and teaching practices. Substantively redressing these issues and making business schools neuroinclusive requires more than the symbolic performativity that is typically the case with diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives. Instead, there is an urgent need to move to substantive performativity through a process that we call de-neurotypicalizing the business school. De-neurotypicalization involves challenging and changing the implicit neurotypical assumptions that pervade business school education and work practices. A key mechanism is applying universal design principles to both learning and work. By taking substantive actions toward de-neurotypicalization, we argue that business schools will make business education genuinely open to both neurodivergent and neurotypical people. Our analysis also advances theory on DEI in business schools by illustrating that universal design offers an inclusive solution to broader equity concerns.

**Research Question**
The essay addresses the open conceptual challenge of how organizations can realize the radical implications of performativity and transition from symbolic to substantive performativity, focused specifically on how business schools can move beyond symbolic DEI gestures to substantively redress the exclusion of neurodivergent students, staff, and faculty.

The paper articulates a conceptual mechanism rather than an empirical one. The authors argue that applying universal design principles — Universal Design for Learning (UDL) in the classroom and Universal Design for Work (UDW) in the workplace — drives a process they call de-neurotypicalization, which challenges and changes the implicit neurotypical assumptions embedded in business school culture, curriculum, teaching, and work practices. De-neurotypicalization, in turn, enables business schools to move from symbolic performativity (declarations, awareness campaigns, narrow accommodations) to substantive performativity that alters core structures and processes. The mechanism is theorized as one in which deep analysis of an underrecognized problem (neurotypical business schools) surfaces anomalies, generates paradigm shifts (e.g., regarding effective leadership), and enables practice shifts (e.g., new pedagogical practice and broader conceptions of accommodation through universal design).

**Theoretical Contribution**
The authors develop the novel concept of de-neurotypicalization to illustrate how specific mechanisms (e.g., universal design) enable organizations to intentionally design for substantive performativity, building upon Marti and Gond (2018) to show how a new approach challenges existing practices, surfaces anomalies, and generates paradigm and practice shifts. They advance theory on DEI in business schools by illustrating the importance of intentional design and creating capabilities (e.g., universal design) that enable DEI efforts to be bundled rather than fragmented. The essay also provides the foundations for a fuller theory of the double empathy problem by showing how design choices, content choices, and norms can exacerbate it and how ad hoc, individualized interventions fail to redress it.

**Practical Implication**
Business schools should apply universal design principles to both learning (UDL) and work (UDW) — including transparent workload allocation, flexible work arrangements, explicit feedback structures, multiple means of participation, and varied forms of assessment — to move from symbolic to substantive performativity on neuroinclusion. By embedding these practices and aligning internal workplace practices with classroom pedagogy, business schools can simultaneously improve outcomes for neurodivergent students, staff, and faculty, benefit neurotypical members of the community, and better prepare future leaders to build neuroinclusive organizations. Doing so also positions business schools to meet AACSB and stakeholder expectations for meaningful DEI commitment.

**Future Research**
Future empirical research should examine how symbolic performativity (e.g., declaring support for neurodiversity) and substantive performativity (e.g., neuroinclusive curriculum, pedagogy, and workplace practices) affect disclosure, neurodivergent enrolment and employment, and the growth of neurodivergent employment over time. Further conceptual and empirical work is needed to test the proposed mechanism of universal design. Researchers should also examine the extent to which the proposed benefits of de-neurotypicalization and inclusive universal design extend to other marginalized groups as well as to neurotypical students, and whether and how spillover benefits across minoritized identities (e.g., LGBTQ+) materialize.
