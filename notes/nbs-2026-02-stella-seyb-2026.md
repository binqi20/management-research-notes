# Scale, scope, speed, and customization (pick two): Resourcing drives trade-offs in local compassion venturing effectiveness

**Abstract**
Building on previous work explaining why some individuals respond entrepreneur­ ially in the face of an adverse event, we focus on the post-launch phase of venturing to theorize about how locals’ heterogenous resourcing approaches influence the effectiveness of their new ventures. Our resourcing framework of local compassion venturing effectiveness outlines four pathways through which locals navigate the conditions imposed by the adverse event to develop effective entrepreneurial responses. We offer four propositions explaining how locals’ varying perspectives on resourcing lead them to engage in resource acquisition and deployment in ways that produce different tradeoffs between the four formative dimensions of response effectiveness (scale, scope, speed, and customization). By explaining these tradeoffs, our theorizing contributes to the entrepreneurship literature by providing new insights into why and how compassion ventures differ in effectiveness and highlighting situations in which the aggregate effectiveness of individual ventures may not accurately reflect the community-level impact of compassion venturing.

**Research Question**
When local individuals launch new ventures to alleviate suffering after an adverse event, how can they navigate abrupt resource constraints to develop effective responses?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Locals' personal resourcing mindset (scarcity vs. slack); locals' social resourcing orientation (insider- vs. outsider-focused); resulting resourcing pathway (scarcity-insider, scarcity-outsider, slack-insider, slack-outsider)
- DV(s): Four formative dimensions of compassion venturing response effectiveness — scale, scope, speed, customization — and the trade-offs among them
- Mediators: Resource acquisition and deployment activities (how locals obtain and allocate material, social, and knowledge resources after the adverse event)
- Moderators: Not reported in paper

Adverse events abruptly deplete resources while simultaneously creating urgent, geographically bounded needs for entrepreneurial action. Local compassion venturers vary along two resourcing perspectives (personal mindset and social orientation), which shape whom they turn to for resources and how they deploy them. Each of the four resulting pathways privileges two of the four effectiveness dimensions at the expense of the remaining two, producing predictable trade-offs rather than convergent, uniformly effective responses. Because ventures in the same community face similar conditions, these venture-level trade-offs aggregate into community-level patterns of complementarity or cannibalization in suffering alleviation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper challenges the implicit assumption in the crisis and prosocial organizing literatures that the dimensions of response effectiveness (scale, scope, speed, customization) co-vary, and instead theorizes systematic trade-offs among them driven by heterogeneous resourcing perspectives. It also challenges the assumption that community-level suffering alleviation equals the sum of venture-level effectiveness, exposing potential cannibalization and complementarities across ventures. Finally, by showing how scarcity-insiders and scarcity-outsiders can achieve unexpected advantages in customization, speed, or scale, it elaborates the boundaries of entrepreneurial resourcefulness and reframes previously disadvantaged locals as uniquely positioned to alleviate others' suffering in specific ways.

**Practical Implication**
Because no single compassion venture is likely to achieve all four dimensions of effectiveness simultaneously, community recovery depends on a portfolio of complementary ventures and actors whose strengths and weaknesses offset one another. Entrepreneurs, funders, NGOs, and government coordinators should clarify which effectiveness dimensions are most urgent in a given recovery phase and deliberately form partnerships that compensate for predictable deficiencies, rather than pushing every venture to maximize all four. Increasing visibility of other actors' activities and lowering coordination costs can reduce duplication and help locals situate their ventures within a broader, synergistic response.

**Future Research**
Future scholarship can examine the value and trade-offs of simultaneously mobilizing local embeddedness for customization while leveraging non-local pipelines for scale, including coordination costs, legitimacy or authenticity threats, and mission drift, and explore the feasibility of achieving scalable customization of responses. Researchers could also integrate the framework with humanitarian logistics and supply-chain management to study how complementary rather than duplicative efforts can be encouraged, and extend it to explore the dark side of compassion venturing (e.g., virtue washing and exploitation of brokering positions). Further work could examine how path dependence in early resourcing choices shapes ventures' longer-term viability and their role in system-level recovery.
