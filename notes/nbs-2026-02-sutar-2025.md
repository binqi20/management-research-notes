# BOP’s dilemma: is jugaad an innovation or an invisible burden?

**Abstract**
Purpose – This study aims to explore the consequences of jugaad at the Bottom of the Pyramid (BOP) and to examine the motivations behind continuance intention (CI) following its adoption. Design/methodology/approach – This qualitative phenomenological study used 29 in-depth interviews with BOP consumers. Thematic analysis yielded 26 categories and nine themes. The expectation-confirmation model (ECM) and bounded rationality (BR) serve as the theoretical lens through which the authors understand the rationale behind the CI of jugaad. Findings – Despite its utility, jugaad carries safety risks, frequent failures, and social stigma, putting BOP consumers in a dilemma of whether to persist. The study reveals a paradox where certain jugaad repurpose waste, while certain other jugaads pollute the environment. The study found that social unacceptance often outweighs functional benefits, showing that jugaad’s CI is shaped by nuanced, context-specific trade-offs. Originality/value – This study extends ECM by showing that BOP consumers may continue with jugaad despite dissatisfaction, driven by BR and limited alternatives. Satisfaction alone does not ensure CI. Safety concerns, social stigma, and aspirations shape CI. The findings offer key insights for policymakers and businesses engaging with BOP markets.

**Research Question**
RQ1: What are the consequences of practicing jugaad at the BOP? RQ2: What motivates jugaad practitioners to continue with its usage despite adverse consequences?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Post-adoption consequences of jugaad (perceived usefulness; safety and health risks; environmental harm; social unacceptability/stigma; aspirations; absence of viable alternatives)
- DV(s): Continuance intention (CI) of jugaad among BOP consumers
- Mediators: Satisfaction / confirmation of expectations (from ECM)
- Moderators: Health risks, safety risks, social unacceptability, and structural constraints (financial scarcity, limited alternatives) operating through bounded rationality

Drawing on the expectation-confirmation model, the authors argue that BOP consumers form expectations about a jugaad solution at adoption and then evaluate post-use performance, with confirmation of expectations shaping satisfaction, perceived usefulness and continuance intention. However, they show that in BOP jugaad contexts this chain is disrupted in two ways: satisfied users may nevertheless abandon jugaad when health, safety or social-unacceptability risks materialize and override functional benefits; and dissatisfied users may persist because bounded rationality, financial scarcity and the absence of safer alternatives force them to satisfice rather than optimize, cognitively recalibrating expectations downward so that compromised functionality is accepted as "good enough." Jugaad is therefore reinterpreted as a necessity-driven, context-specific practice in which continuance reflects structural deprivation and social negotiation as much as positive evaluation.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends the expectation-confirmation model (ECM) to resource-constrained BOP settings by demonstrating a satisfaction–CI mismatch: satisfaction is a necessary but insufficient condition for continuance because health, safety and social-unacceptability consequences moderate the relationship, and dissatisfaction does not trigger discontinuance when viable alternatives are absent. It also extends bounded rationality theory into informal, survivalist BOP economies, showing that persistence with hazardous jugaad reflects satisficing under structural constraints rather than attitudinal endorsement. Finally, it unveils the social-acceptability and stigma dimensions of jugaad at the BOP, reframing it as a socially negotiated practice marked by aspiration and image management rather than a purely functional adaptation.

**Practical Implication**
Businesses serving BOP markets should design affordable products that address both functional and aspirational needs, and can leverage the resourcefulness of jugaad for circular-economy branding and sustainability-driven segments. Policymakers, labor unions and social-initiative groups can reduce risks by running awareness campaigns, conducting safety audits in informal workplaces, training BOP consumers in safer alternatives, and institutionalizing affordable substitutes such as solar energy, low-cost water filtration and safer transport in place of hazardous electrical, water and vehicular jugaad. Municipal waste departments and urban planners can integrate jugaad practitioners into formal recycling and waste-management systems, while design-led capacity-building programs can elevate the cultural value of repurposed products and mitigate the stigma of jugaad as "the poor's way."

**Limitations**
The study did not develop and validate measurement scales for the proposed constructs (environmental impact, social acceptability, maintenance costs), leaving the propositions untested in a structural model. The two regional sites (Odisha and West Bengal) turned out to be culturally similar, so intended cross-cultural comparisons were not achievable, and the sample is male-skewed because women were more reluctant to participate, limiting gendered insight. The cross-sectional design also cannot capture how jugaad practices and continuance intentions evolve as technologies, incomes and infrastructure change over time.

**Future Research**
Future work should develop measurement scales and test the propositions as hypotheses in a structural model, and replicate the study in culturally distinct BOP regions to examine cross-cultural differences in jugaad. Researchers should pursue greater gender balance and examine gender as a moderator of jugaad practices, motivations and challenges in BOP communities. Longitudinal and comparative (urban vs. rural) designs are needed to track how jugaad and its social acceptability evolve as access to technology, education, infrastructure and awareness changes.
