# Designing Dual-Purpose Organizations: The Role of Soft Governance

**Abstract**
Although the phenomenon of dual-purpose organizations (DPOs)—market-based organizations that pursue both private (commercial) and broader social objectives simultaneously— has been explored extensively in prior literature, the role of organization design in addressing the distinct coordination problems inherent to DPOs has been largely overlooked. We develop a theoretical framework that conceptualizes how design considerations can influence organizational outcomes for DPOs. We identify two central coordination challenges faced by DPOs: an aggregation problem, in which heterogeneous individual preferences and organizational goals must be effectively aligned; and a consolidation problem, in which the dual-purpose nature of DPOs prevents the consolidation of goals into a single organizational meta-goal, resulting in nonhierarchical decision-making structures. We argue that formal organization design processes and mechanisms alone may fail to resolve these coordination problems, and we conceptualize the role and notion of soft governance, which we define as the agentic deployment of informal design mechanisms that shape an organization’s informal decision-making structure. Our core contribution lies in developing an integrative model of dual-purpose organizing that demonstrates how soft governance may complement formal organization design in alleviating the coordination problems inherent in DPOs, thereby supporting their stability and growth.

**Research Question**
How can organization design—and in particular the agentic deployment of informal mechanisms (soft governance)—address the distinctive coordination challenges (an aggregation problem and a consolidation problem) that arise in dual-purpose organizations pursuing commercial and social objectives simultaneously?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Formal organization design choices across sorting, structuring, and socialization; agentic deployment of soft governance mechanisms (informal roles, informal authority, recognition-based rewards, informal interaction structures).
- DV(s): Coordination within DPOs, alignment between individual preferences and organizational goals, synchronized decision-making, organizational stability and growth.
- Mediators: Reduction of goal heterogeneity (via self-selection and tacit signaling during sorting); informal feedback loops during structuring; diffusion and convergence of decision rules through peer-to-peer and cross-level interactions during socialization.
- Moderators: Degree of goal non-collapsibility; opacity of decision rules; persistent ambiguity of feedback and action-performance mappings.

DPOs are conceptualized as facing two unique coordination problems: an aggregation problem rooted in heterogeneous individual and organizational preferences, and a consolidation problem arising because dual purposes cannot be collapsed into a single meta-goal, producing nonhierarchical and opaque decision rules. Formal design mechanisms (incentives, supervisory structures, task allocation) are necessary but insufficient under these conditions. Soft governance—the intentional, agentic deployment of informal mechanisms—complements formal design by shaping the informal decision-making structure of the organization across the three organizing processes: during sorting it attracts and screens members with aligned preferences through tacit signaling; during structuring it supplies informal feedback and authority where formal rules are ambiguous; and during socialization it diffuses and converges local decision rules via peer interactions, enabling micro-level choices to cohere with macro-level dual-purpose goals.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper introduces and develops the construct of "soft governance," defined as the deliberate, agentic deployment of informal mechanisms (informal roles, authority, rewards, and interaction structures) that shape an organization's informal decision-making structure. By extending the scope of organization design to encompass intentionally cultivated informal elements, the authors challenge the dominant assumption that informal structures merely emerge as byproducts of formal design and show that soft governance can complement formal processes to resolve coordination challenges that are endemic to dual-purpose organizing. The integrative process model bridges the hybrid-organizing, goal-multiplicity, and organization-design literatures, recasting informal structure as a "designable" component of the organization.

**Practical Implication**
The framework offers managers of DPOs a diagnostic lens for identifying when formal mechanisms are likely to fail and a categorization of informal mechanisms that can complement or substitute for them. Achieving coordination under goal ambiguity, the authors argue, requires not just formal structure but also the strategic, agentic construction of informal scaffolding such as informal roles, recognition-based rewards, and deliberately cultivated interaction patterns. The insights may also extend to other pluralistic organizational settings, including multinational firms and nonmarket forms of organizing, where goal divergence and decision ambiguity are endemic.

**Future Research**
The authors call for comparative empirical studies that examine the relative effectiveness of specific soft governance mechanisms—for example, when and why informal roles outperform recognition-based rewards, or how different interaction patterns affect the convergence of decision rules across levels. They further invite research into the coevolution of individual and organizational goals over time, and into how soft governance operates in other pluralistic or contested settings beyond DPOs, such as multinational firms and nonmarket organizations.
