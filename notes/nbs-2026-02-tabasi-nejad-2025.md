# Beyond Dangling Carrots: The Effect of Policy Maker Motives on Their Response to Corporate Political Activity

**Abstract**
Management scholars have predominantly theorized corporate political activity (CPA) as an exchange wherein a firm offers resources to a policy maker in return for favorable policy. While the CPA literature has extensively explored what drives firms to engage in CPA, it remains largely silent on the question of why a policy maker would accede to a firm’s attempt at influence. Drawing on social influence theory, we develop a framework that extends current CPA theory and centers a policy maker’s perspective when faced with CPA. We introduce a policy maker’s noninstrumental motives and identify novel types of CPA that appeal to these motives. We also consider the role of the bureaucrat—a heretofore largely ignored but important type of policy maker whose motives differ from those of politicians in key respects that shape whether and how a given policy maker is influenced. Lastly, we examine how third-party lobbyists affect the CPA process, identifying key pathways through which they are likely to enhance a firm’s CPA. By theorizing around the motives of the policy maker faced with CPA, we present a novel policy maker-centered framework that helps us better understand this socially and economically important strategy.

**Research Question**
Why would a policy maker be influenced (or not) by a firm's CPA efforts? The authors develop a more comprehensive framework of CPA that centers the motives of the target policy maker rather than treating the policy maker as a solely instrumentally driven actor in an exchange.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Type of CPA (instrumental, relational, moral); policy-maker motive profile (instrumental, relational, moral); policy-maker type (politician vs. bureaucrat); third-party lobbyist involvement
- DV(s): CPA effectiveness (whether and how the policy maker is influenced), including the possibility of negative influence
- Mediators: Monitoring, relational salience, and moral outrage as determinants of influence effectiveness; moral identity threat under incongruent CPA-motive pairings
- Moderators: Policy-maker type (politician vs. bureaucrat) shapes motivational profiles and responsiveness to each CPA type; lobbyist characteristics enhance the determinants of effectiveness

Drawing on social influence theory, the paper argues that policy makers are driven not only by instrumental (self-interest) motives but also by relational motives (connection, belonging) and moral motives (values, identity). Firms can therefore engage in three corresponding CPA types, and effectiveness depends on the match between CPA type and the policy maker's dominant motive. Mismatches can backfire, as when instrumental inducements trigger moral identity threat in a morally motivated policy maker. Bureaucrats and politicians differ systematically in their motivational profiles and in how they process monitoring, relational salience, and moral outrage, so the same CPA tactic is not equally effective across policy-maker types. Third-party lobbyists enhance CPA by amplifying monitoring capacity, relational ties, and moral credibility, going beyond their currently theorized roles of resource aggregation and access provision.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends CPA theory beyond its exclusive focus on instrumental motives by integrating relational and moral motives, thereby identifying new noninstrumental types of CPA and explaining when CPA may succeed, fail, or even produce negative influence. It introduces policy-maker type (politician vs. bureaucrat) as a core theoretical component of CPA, arguing that bureaucrats constitute a motivationally distinct class of policy maker who have been largely neglected. It also expands the conceptualization of lobbyists from resource aggregators or access providers to key intermediaries who enhance influence determinants, and in doing so extends social influence theory to account for intermediaries and for influencee type.

**Practical Implication**
For firms designing CPA strategies, the framework suggests that "dangling carrots" is often not the most effective tactic and can be counterproductive when policy makers are driven by relational or moral motives. Firms should diagnose the dominant motive profile of the target policy maker and match it with an appropriate CPA type, taking special care when targeting bureaucrats, who tend to differ from politicians in their motivational orientations. Employing third-party lobbyists is valuable not only for access but also for monitoring, relationship building, and enhancing moral credibility.

**Future Research**
The authors invite research on CPA that simultaneously combines multiple influence types (e.g., relational and moral) and on which combinations are more likely to be used and succeed. They encourage empirical work on the relationship between corporate social responsibility and CPA when both speak to moral motives, and on how noninstrumental motives shape outcomes in settings beyond politics (e.g., incentive use in knowledge-sharing communities). They also call for research on how influencee type (e.g., bureaucrat-like, technically oriented stakeholders) shapes the effectiveness of relational influence, and on how intermediaries beyond lobbyists (e.g., lawyers serving activist shareholders) enhance influence in other contexts.
