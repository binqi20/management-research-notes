# Getting Away with Murder: Legitimizing Unethical Organization Practices via Memes

**Abstract**
Research on the ethics of life and death in organizations has overlooked the growing role of social media in shaping the ethical implications of organizational regulations on death. More specifically, while recent research suggests that memes are increasingly used to influence the reputation and perception of organizational actions, research on their roles in legitimating the organization of death is scant. This paper analyzes Internet memes legitimizing police slaughter in Brazilian favelas. Three primary discourses were identified: denying life worthiness, establishing actors deserving death, and upholding the police organization as executioners of death. This paper contributes to research on the ethics of organizational death in three different ways in a context of political polarization and growing authoritarism. First, it discusses how social media creates a legitimate discourse that makes death visible and gloats over its victims. Second, this discourse is enabled by social media memes, which divert the ethical debate on life and death, replacing it with superficial and transient engagement. Third, we discuss how memes can reinforce the role of the state police enforcement apparatus as an expression of racialized inequalities.

**Research Question**
How do social media memes legitimize organizational actions related to the ethics of life and death? The authors investigate this in the context of rising authoritarianism through the extreme case of Brazilian police massacres in Rio de Janeiro favelas.

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Circulation of Internet memes (as assemblages of images, texts, and cultural artefacts) by police, politicians, and right-wing influencers on Instagram following lethal police operations
- DV(s): Legitimation of organizational killing / organization of death (making death visible, celebrated, and unchallenged)
- Mediators: Three discursive moves — denying life worthiness, establishing actors deserving death, and upholding the police as executioners of death — combined with humorous derision, abjection, and triumphant deification of state organizations
- Moderators: Political polarization and rising authoritarianism; platform affordances of Instagram (feeds and ephemeral stories); intertextual linkage to traditional media news and cultural artefacts (movies, comics, photos)

Drawing on necropolitics and necrocapitalism, the paper argues that state organizations and their supporters use memes as a new legitimation technology that departs from the traditional discourses of normalization and invisibilization. Instead of hiding or routinizing killing, memes render deaths hyper-visible and celebrate them: by ridiculing human-rights critics, framing favela residents as deserving of death, and sanctifying police as rightful executioners, the memetic discourse constructs favelas as "states of exception" where lethal violence is morally warranted. The humorous, rapid, and ephemeral nature of memes short-circuits rational ethical debate and replaces it with superficial, affective engagement, thereby supporting racialized necropolitical regimes within a broader authoritarian drive enabled by social media platforms.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper makes three contributions to the literature on the ethics of the organization of death. First, it identifies a third legitimation discourse alongside normalization and invisibilization — one that makes death visible and celebrates it, particularly when directed at marginalized groups. Second, it theorizes memes as a distinctive legitimation technology whose speed, humor, and ephemerality void ethical debate and replace it with vacuous engagement, linking social media affordances to the reproduction of authoritarian and necropolitical discourses. Third, it foregrounds the state's law-enforcement apparatus as an expression of racialized colonial inequalities, connecting business ethics to the critical study of state violence and the complicity of Big Tech platforms that allow such content to circulate.

**Practical Implication**
The authors argue that ethical corporations, particularly Big Tech platforms, must account for the memetic content they allow to circulate, since permitting such material makes them complicit in the regulation of death and the terror inflicted on vulnerable populations. They also suggest that social media can be mobilized in the opposite direction — to combat inequalities in the organization of life and death, as seen in the George Floyd case — and call on business ethics practitioners, regulators, and civil society to contest memetic legitimations of state violence rather than treat them as harmless humor.

**Limitations**
Not reported in paper

**Future Research**
The authors call for further research on the organizational ethics of life and death in the post-truth era, particularly on the role of social media platforms and Big Tech complicity in enabling legitimations of killing. They invite studies that examine how memes and other pictorial technologies operate in other authoritarian and necropolitical contexts, and how counter-memes and alternative discourses might contest the celebration of death directed at marginalized and racialized populations.
