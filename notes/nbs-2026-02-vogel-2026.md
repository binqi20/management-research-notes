# Ecosystem initiation: a key policy challenge for circular economy transition

**Abstract**
The circular economy (CE) is increasingly viewed as a key pathway to sustainable development, but its uptake remains behind expectations. We explore the concept of circular economy ecosystems to advance our understanding of the market sector conditions necessary for the adoption of circular economy practices by producers, consumers and other stakeholders within an ecosystem. Our methodology is a multiple case study with data collection through semi-structured qualitative interviews of key informants instrumental in the initiation of the ecosystem cases, enriched by secondary data. We identify the special conditions necessary for establishing a circular economy ecosystem and the implications for stakeholders, with a special focus on local government. We deduct a novel framework to analyse and understand the agency involved in a circular economy ecosystem. Our framework depicts seven functions with actors from each function potentially involved in the circular ecosystem. Finally, the article outlines the limitations of our work, and testable propositions and methodological avenues for longitudinal case studies, and agent-based modelling to empirically validate and refine the proposed concepts, advancing the research frontier on circular ecosystems.

**Research Question**
Why and how might local government initiate and orchestrate a circular economy ecosystem?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Local government agency and engagement in ecosystem initiation and orchestration; convening of heterogeneous stakeholders meeting conditions of autonomy, complementarity, and modularity
- DV(s): Formation and functioning of a circular economy ecosystem that can materialize a focal CE value proposition for mutual stakeholder benefit
- Mediators: Coordination across seven ecosystem functions (initiation and orchestration; CE innovation; CE knowledge building and diffusion; structure and supply; public sector services, policy and regulation; dominant values, norms and culture; demand for goods and services)
- Moderators: Market sector characteristics; presence of dominant values, norms and culture (hegemonic linear-economy culture); public procurement levers; motivational triggers of initiators

Local government, acting through empowered officers, can serve as initiator, orchestrator, and context-specific participant in a circular economy ecosystem. By convening autonomous but interdependent stakeholders around a focal CE value proposition, local government helps establish the market-sector conditions (innovation, knowledge diffusion, supply structure, regulatory support, cultural legitimacy, and demand) required for circular practices to scale. The mechanism works when actors in each of the seven functions cooperate and co-create value, and when dominant values, norms and culture shift to support circularity; absent such agency and cultural alignment, CE initiatives remain confined to niche markets.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper advances ecosystem theory for the circular economy on two fronts. First, it identifies that local government has the agency to initiate and orchestrate circular ecosystems and may take up other context-specific functional roles beyond regulation. Second, it proposes a novel circular ecosystem framework with seven functions as the organizing principle (rather than stakeholders/actors), categorizing business and technology, social and behavioral, and policy and regulation perspectives, and introducing "dominant values, norms and culture" as an explicit function — offering a more nuanced conceptual and definitional basis for analyzing circular ecosystems.

**Practical Implication**
For local governments, the guidance framework clarifies the range of roles — initiator, orchestrator, knowledge partner, procurer, cultural advocate — available to facilitate a CE transition within their jurisdictions. For firms, investors, and civil-society actors, the framework shows how to purposefully assume or shift between ecosystem roles to maximize collective value creation, de-risk investment, and capture first-mover advantages in emerging circular economy sectors.

**Limitations**
The findings are based on a parsimonious set of case studies, all of which are mature circular ecosystems with successful outcomes, introducing a survivorship bias. Although the authors purposefully sought diversity across market sectors, the selection is limited to two developed economies (Australia and the Netherlands) and to the specific sectors sampled, constraining transferability of the findings.

**Future Research**
The authors recommend (1) investigating circular ecosystems that have failed and the reasons for failure; (2) examining which market sectors are best suited for local government to establish circular ecosystems; and (3) testing the proposed analytical framework — particularly whether foregrounding societally dominant values, norms, and culture usefully explains circular ecosystem dynamics — through longitudinal case studies and agent-based modelling.
