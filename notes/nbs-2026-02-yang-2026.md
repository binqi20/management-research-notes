# Mitigating Disability Bias in Hiring: The Role of Inclusion-Focused Generative AI in Complex HR Decisions

**Abstract**
This research investigates how inclusion‐focused generative AI (GAI), designed with diversity, fairness, and inclusion princi ples, mitigates disability bias in hiring—particularly under complex, cognitively demanding conditions. Drawing on Construal Level Theory, two experiments with HR professionals (N = 117; 238) compared standard, inclusion‐focused, and control conditions. Inclusion‐focused GAI significantly reduced bias by emphasizing job‐relevant competencies and lowering psy chological distance. The study introduces a fairness‐oriented AI design that advances accountability and inclusion in HR systems, offering implications for equitable hiring, bias auditing, and the strategic use of ethical AI in talent acquisition.

**Research Question**
How can organizations harness the potential of generative AI while ensuring equitable, inclusive hiring decisions? Specifically, does decision complexity amplify disability bias, and does inclusion-focused GAI (designed with diversity, fairness, and inclusion principles) reduce such bias more effectively than standard GAI, particularly under complex decision conditions?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Decision complexity (complex vs. simple hiring decisions); AI assistance type (no AI / standard GAI / inclusion-focused GAI)
- DV(s): Disability bias in hiring, operationalized as the hiring rate of disabled vs. non-disabled candidates against a 50/50 neutrality benchmark
- Mediators: Psychological distance and construal level (theorized; not directly manipulated)
- Moderators: Decision complexity moderates the effect of inclusion-focused GAI (H4: stronger bias-reduction under complex conditions)

Grounded in Construal Level Theory, the paper argues that complex, ambiguous hiring decisions induce greater psychological distance and higher-level (abstract) construals, triggering stereotype-consistent evaluations that disadvantage disabled candidates. Inclusion-focused GAI, designed with diversity-fairness-inclusion prompts, functions as an external cognitive scaffold (per the Extended Mind perspective) that reduces psychological distance and redirects evaluators toward concrete, job-relevant competencies. This competency-anchored construal displaces heuristic stereotype use, mitigating disability bias most strongly in high-complexity contexts where bias risk is greatest.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The study extends Construal Level Theory by demonstrating that psychological distance—typically treated as an individual cognitive tendency—can be externally regulated through AI framing, operationalizing CLT in a technologically mediated context. Drawing on the Extended Mind perspective, it reconceptualizes inclusion-focused GAI as an active cognitive intervention and ethical scaffold rather than a passive decision aid, positioning AI as a "fairness infrastructure" alongside structured interviews and standardized rubrics that embeds equity and accountability into hiring systems.

**Practical Implication**
Organizations should embed diversity, fairness, and inclusion prompts into GAI hiring tools to guide evaluators toward competency-based judgments, particularly in complex, cognitively demanding decisions where bias risk peaks. Inclusion-focused GAI should be deployed as a strategic partner rather than a replacement for human judgment, enabling bias auditing through logged prompts and outcomes; however, calibration is needed to avoid overcompensation (e.g., the 70.2% hiring rate for disabled candidates in COMdk).

**Limitations**
Psychological distance was theorized but not directly manipulated, leaving the CLT mechanism inferred rather than empirically isolated. The controlled lab setting with hypothetical candidates may not reflect real-world hiring conditions involving time pressure, team dynamics, or organizational norms. The analysis focused solely on disability bias and did not include an AI-only condition to isolate algorithmic judgment from human decision-making.

**Future Research**
Future work should design GAI prompts that explicitly manipulate CLT mechanisms such as temporal and social framing, and test their interaction with organizational culture and individual cognition. Field studies in naturalistic recruitment contexts are needed, as are extensions to other bias domains (race, age, gender) and to alternative human-AI configurations that address privacy, transparency, and unintended effects. Calibration research is needed to prevent inclusion-focused GAI from producing inverted bias or overcompensation.
