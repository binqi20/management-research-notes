# The power of consumer solidarity framing in scaling up social impact

**Abstract**
This distributed process of scaling up social impact unfolds outside the venture's direct control, relying on community enactment as well as engagement and energizing from the media, incumbent retailers and competitors, as well as politicians. We contribute to research on scaling up social impact by explaining how and why entrepreneurial framing can mobilize diverse ecosystem actors. We also advance entrepreneurial framing research by offering a social-emotional explanation of how framing can enable change.

**Research Question**
How do social entrepreneurs use entrepreneurial framing to mobilize ecosystem actors to scale up social impact?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Energized solidarity framing by the social entrepreneur (combining consumer-solidarity content with emotional energizing across diagnostic, prognostic, and motivational frames)
- DV(s): Distributed scaling up of social impact (community mobilization, market adoption, legislative change)
- Mediators: Mobilization of ecosystem actors in sequence — community/followers, media, market actors (retailers and competitors), and policymakers — with reinforcing feedback loops of shared emotion
- Moderators: Salience of a societal crisis (milk crisis), product concreteness and cultural familiarity, founder media skills, and openness of media and political environments

The paper develops a process model in which an entrepreneur's energized solidarity framing — articulating a societal problem (diagnostic), a collaborative consumer-based solution (prognostic), and a motivational call to fairness — infuses a mundane purchase with shared moral meaning. Positive and negative emotional energizing generates collective momentum first within the community, which is then amplified by the media, engaged by market actors seeking legitimacy, and taken up by policymakers. Reinforcing feedback loops between entrepreneur, community, media, market actors, and politicians sustain engagement over time and drive nationwide scaling outside the venture's direct control.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper contributes to research on scaling up social impact by theorizing a distributed process in which a social entrepreneur's framing of a solution is progressively taken up and energized by heterogeneous ecosystem actors, enabling nationwide impact under resource scarcity. It also advances entrepreneurial framing research by introducing a social-emotional perspective — showing how shared emotions and energized solidarity framing, not cognition alone, mobilize diverse ecosystem actors and enable change.

**Practical Implication**
Rather than confronting incumbents NGO-style, social entrepreneurs can combine consumer-solidarity content with emotional energizing to maneuver between community, media, retailers, and policymakers, adopting fair pricing even in price-competitive sectors. Skillful framing that avoids direct attacks and cultivates both social and traditional media engagement can help small ventures trigger systemic change, as illustrated by cases such as Fairphone, Fairafric, and Yuka.

**Limitations**
The findings rest on a single exemplary case (CQLP) in France, so the distributed scaling-up process may unfold differently in other settings. Boundary conditions include the publicized nature of the French milk crisis, the concreteness of milk as a consumer product, and founder Nicolas Chabanne's skill with media and willingness to cooperate with incumbents; framing may be less effective where crises are not publicized, media is state-controlled, or founders lack such skills.

**Future Research**
More work is needed on cognitive and emotional dynamics around social and environmental problems, including conditions under which emotional energy dissipates rather than accumulates. Comparative and cross-national studies could examine how energized framing interacts with deep versus up scaling, whether it transfers across product categories, and how authenticity is preserved when powerful actors co-opt the frame. Future research could also build on identity-based typologies to ask which social entrepreneurs are most likely to pursue distributed scaling processes.
