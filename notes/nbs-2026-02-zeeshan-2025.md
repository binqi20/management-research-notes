# Participative pricing, food affordability, and nutritional adequacy: evidence from India

**Abstract**
Purpose – This study investigates the impact of participatory pricing mechanisms, specifically pay-what-you-want (PWYW) and set-your-own-price (SYOP), on household food expenditure and affordability in India. This study aims to understand how these pricing models affect consumer behavior across various food categories and income groups in both urban and rural settings. Design/methodology/approach – Using data from the NSSO 2022–23 Consumption Expenditure Survey (CES), this study uses log-level ordinary least squares regressions, generalized linear models with a log link and logistic regressions. These models incorporate interactions between pricing scenarios (for PWYW, as well as SYOP quintiles) and income groups (measured by monthly per capita expenditure quintiles). A comprehensive set of control variables at the household level was included to ensure robust estimates. Findings – The results indicate that PWYW pricing leads to nonlinear changes in food expenditure. Urban households exhibit elastic responses, with higher-income groups reallocating their spending more significantly in response to discounts, whereas rural households display inelastic behavior under similar pricing conditions. Under the SYOP model, lower-income households face severe affordability constraints, whereas higher-income consumers demonstrate increased spending on nutrient-dense foods. These heterogeneous responses suggest that price flexibility alone may not uniformly improve food affordability and nutritional adequacy across different socio-economic contexts. Originality/value – This research extends the existing literature on pricing strategies by applying participative pricing models to food expenditure and nutritional adequacy in a developing country context. The study offers actionable insights for policymakers and industry stakeholders, underscoring the need for targeted interventions that integrate flexible pricing with complementary social and economic support measures.

**Research Question**
How do participative pricing mechanisms (PWYW and SYOP) influence household food expenditure patterns across income groups; can they improve affordability for diverse and nutritious food categories; and how do participative pricing responses differ across urban and rural households in India?

**Mechanism Process**
- IV(s): Participative pricing scenario (PWYW pricing fractions of 50%, 75%, 100%, 150% of baseline; SYOP household-specific prices drawn within +/-20% of baseline, summarized by quintiles)
- DV(s): Household food expenditure (total and by food category); food affordability (binary: monthly per capita food expenditure <=30% of MPCE)
- Mediators: Budget reallocation across food categories; expenditure share of MPCE
- Moderators: MPCE income quintile; urban vs. rural sector; household controls (size, education, social group, ration card, PMGKY benefits)

Participative pricing shifts part of the pricing decision from the seller to the buyer, introducing consumer discretion over prices. Under PWYW, simulated fixed-fraction discounts interact with existing affordability constraints: lower prices do not automatically raise quantities because households reallocate their budgets across food categories, with urban and higher-income households being more elastic than rural or low-income ones. Under SYOP, consumer-set prices within a narrow acceptance band allow higher-income consumers to channel spending toward nutrient-dense foods, but binding affordability constraints prevent lower-income households from doing the same. The heterogeneity across income and sector reflects differences in budget slack, market access and price elasticity rooted in Engel's-law reasoning about food shares and household welfare.

**Theoretical Contribution**
The paper extends participative pricing research beyond digital commerce and cultural industries to food affordability in a developing-country context, formalizing PWYW and SYOP as counterfactual pricing regimes over national survey microdata. It reframes the policy question from "how much to cut prices" toward "how to allocate pricing discretion and complementary access supports," showing that bounded consumer discretion (SYOP) outperforms uniform discounts (PWYW) for affordability when budgets are tight. In doing so, it integrates participative pricing with nutrition-transition and food-security literatures in low- and middle-income countries.

**Practical Implication**
Policymakers and retailers should pair flexible pricing with complementary interventions: tiered "community pricing" bands anchored by a suggested price, simple eligibility (e.g. ration-card verification) for reduced bands and brief SYOP windows to learn willingness-to-pay. Urban, higher-income households respond to PWYW-style discounts, while rural and low-income households require structural supports (market access, subsidies, income transfers) to benefit. Programs should track affordability rates (<=30% MPCE), basket diversity and sector-specific take-up, iterating floors and bands accordingly.

**Limitations**
Price variation is simulated through PWYW fractions and SYOP bands rather than observed in live markets, so external validity depends on how floors, reference prices and eligibility are implemented in practice. The analysis focuses on expenditure rather than directly mapping quantities to nutrients, which would require additional assumptions beyond the scope of the study. The design is cross-sectional, precluding observation of learning, persistence or seasonal adjustment in consumer behavior.

**Future Research**
Future work should run live pilots or staggered rollouts to capture dynamic learning, persistence and seasonal effects under actual participative pricing. Short panels could identify how floors, reference prices and eligibility rules shape affordability and basket composition. Extensions could map expenditure changes more explicitly to nutritional intake and calorie adequacy, and test community pricing bands in field experiments across regions.
